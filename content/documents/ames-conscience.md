An English translation of William Ames's *De Conscientia et ejus Jure vel Casibus* (1631), printed at London in 1639. The work is a systematic treatment of casuistry — the application of moral and theological principles to practical cases of conscience — divided into five books. Book One addresses the general nature, power, and cases of conscience; subsequent books treat specific areas including the state of man before God and his duties toward God and neighbor. Ames drew on Reformed theology and Ramist method to produce one of the most influential Protestant works on practical divinity in the seventeenth century, widely read in Puritan England and New England.
