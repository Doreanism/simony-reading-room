As is read in the Historia Tripartita \[*Tripartite History*\]: Perigines was ordained bishop at Patras; and because the citizens were unwilling to receive him, the bishop of the city of Rome ordered him to be enthroned at Corinth, the metropolis, upon the death of its bishop, and he presided over that church as long as he lived. Sisinnius too, the bishop of Constantinople, consecrated Proclus at Cyzicus; but before \[Proclus\] departed for Cyzicus, the citizens, coming out in procession, ordained for themselves the monk Dalmatius as bishop. Proclus therefore remained, having no church of his own, yet he was greatly flourishing in the teaching of the churches of Constantinople, and was afterward enthroned in the see of Constantinople. It is apparent, therefore, that neither of these men was elected by the clergy or the people, since it is manifest they were not received. Moreover, the same history, making mention earlier of the Second Ecumenical Council, namely the Council of Constantinople, says:

The fathers confirmed again the Nicene faith and established patriarchs, distributing the provinces so that the bishops of each diocese should not go to foreign churches. For this had previously been done without distinction on account of the time of persecution. Indeed, for those ordained in this manner another synod proposed such a rule: “If any bishop who has been ordained does not proceed to the parish in which he was ordained — not by his own fault, but either because the people have rejected him or for some other cause not made by necessity — let him share only in the honor and ministry, and let him not mix himself in the affairs of the church in which he celebrates the assembly, but let him endure what the provincial synod shall determine by its judgment.” From this, therefore, let it be weighed that our Catholics, in former times and afterward, obtained a valid consecration even without those three requisites, whereas all heretics and simoniacs named in particular obtained none at all — neither then nor afterward — even when those three requisites were present for them, namely the election of the clergy, the request of the people, and the judgment of the metropolitan; because no purity or grace can adhere to unbelievers until their hearts are cleansed by faith. Nevertheless, in saying this we so venerate the ordination of our holy fathers out of the necessity of the time and their very great authority that we testify to our successors and to ourselves in every respect that the ecclesiastical rules will continue to be observed, because necessity excuses the ancients and their own freedom; but the moderns are accused by their own will and rashness alone.

## Chapter VII.

Corruptor \[i.e., the opponent being refuted\].

You have also heard that, just as no one ought to be baptized again, so no one can be consecrated again in the same order.

Corrector \[i.e., Humbert’s voice\].

Who says or holds that anyone can be consecrated again in the same order? Far be it from us to think so. Among the other things we have received from the holy fathers on this matter, we also receive with due reverence the proposed chapter of blessed Pope Gregory. For as he himself testifies in that passage, the one who dared to do such a thing was justly judged — namely, because he first deposed a priest who had lapsed, removed him from office, and subjected him to penance, and when this was completed, consecrated him again in the same order, as if he had never been a presbyter.
