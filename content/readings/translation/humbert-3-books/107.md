Then that deranged man, terrified by such thunder and the lightning bolt of those words, sought to have so great a divine indignation removed from himself through the intercession of the apostles, saying: “Pray to the Lord for me, that none of the things you have said may come upon me.” Yet he deserved no reply from them on this matter, neither then nor afterward, so that those who came after might learn what a terrible precipice he had fallen into — one to whom even the holy apostles themselves did not presume to extend the hand of their intercession, judging it more just to leave him, cut off from their communion, to the irrevocable wrath of God, because they perceived his offense to be inexpiable. And if, as Chrysostom says, the ruin of those who went before ought to be a warning to those who come after, we ought to consider, as that same teacher adds, that if we fall into it repeatedly, those who fell first deserve more forgiveness than we who fall second. Therefore, I urge the simoniacs and their intimate chamberlains and faithful attendants finally to awaken and, seeing what a ruin their first founder fell into, what a precipice he was hurled down to, to reconsider and at last — however late — recognize where they lie, and that they do not stand as the second from him in that ruin, but as the more than thousandth; for they have dared to believe that through money they can become priests or hold any other rank of ecclesiastical order, or merit it, or give to anyone freely or for a price what it is certain they could in no way have obtained through money — as the prince of the apostles testified to their prince Simon: “There is no part or lot for you in this word.” In this presumption, the error of the magician is more pardonable — who merely estimated or thought that the gift of God could be possessed for money — than that of these men, who believe and are confident, and moreover declare, that they have nonetheless received, possess, and can give that gift to anyone for money. For if they did not believe this, they would themselves preach along with us that it was impossible to become bishops through money, and they would cease to usurp an office not their own, and would not communicate to others the condemnation of their perdition rather than any ordination or consecration of episcopal ministry. But if they do believe it, they are manifestly heretics, worse than that man who only estimated or thought this first. If however they do not believe it, why do they presume upon an office belonging to another, and why do they not acknowledge that what they do not possess for themselves they also cannot grant to others in ministry? And in this respect as well they are manifestly heretics, and they despair, or at least in their hope are adding daily blasphemies to their first accursed blasphemy against the Holy Spirit, being worse than that master of theirs who, after his repulse, neither believed that he had received or possessed the gift of God — namely, the Holy Spirit — nor laid hands on anyone for this purpose; for he had sought from the apostles only this power, that whoever he laid hands on might receive the Holy Spirit. That man was wicked because he offered money for this; these are worse, because they not only offer but also persistently confer it. That man was wicked because, not yet having completed half a year from his conversion, he fell into this sacrilege once; how much worse are these, who from the womb of their fleshly mother, having received the faith of Christ, from childhood to near old age seek to think or do almost nothing else? That man was wicked because, though rebuked and warned, he did not repent; these are far more wicked, because, made incorrigible and impenitent, moved by no rebuke or warning, they do not abandon the foreign office or place they have presumed upon for themselves, nor do they submit themselves to continual penance, lest perhaps there be forgiven them at some point what they have brought: they have led many others to ruin and infected the Church of Christ with the contagion of their damnation.
