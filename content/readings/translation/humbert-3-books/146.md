The dove, content with simple foods, covets no plunder, but rather, despising deceitful kites and raptors of that kind, carefully avoids them. Indeed, “the simplicity of the righteous will guide them, and the supplanting of the perverse will lay waste to them.” Hence the righteous, living by faith, whether in the course of their duty they lay hands on someone in simplicity, or do any other thing in the name of the Lord, all things work together for good for them. On the contrary, the perverse, because they insatiably lust after mutual supplanting, whatever they do is sin, since it is done without faith. For where there is supplanting, there is also fraud. Where there is fraud, there too is unfaithfulness and a curse, as the prophet warns: “Cursed is he who does the work of the Lord deceitfully.” Therefore, among other things, let attention be paid to that supplanting of the simoniacs by which they supplant one another through buying and selling. For indeed, the buyer, by giving money, supplants the seller when he defrauds him of the grace of God, so that the seller may not have it; and the seller, by receiving the money, equally supplants the buyer when he defrauds him of the grace of God, so that he may not receive it. By this mutual supplanting — since each defrauds the other of the grace of God — the blessing is driven far from them, so that they then condemn others along with themselves. Whoever is tainted by the laying on of their hands, whether for payment or without payment, is thereby made complicit; and thus it comes about that the supplanting of the perverse lays waste to them and leaves in them no root or shoot of spiritual grace. For how dangerous a business it is for even laymen to engage in commerce in worldly goods, this proverb of the wise man shows: “Two kinds of dealings appear to me difficult and dangerous: a merchant is not easily absolved from negligence, and a tradesman will not be justified from the sins of his lips. Many have sinned for the sake of poverty, and he who seeks to grow rich turns away his eye. As a stake is driven into the joint of stones, so sin will be wedged between buying and selling.” But if the buying and selling of laymen in their common and private interests is so difficult and dangerous, who can declare how difficult and dangerous it is for clergy in ecclesiastical ordinations? And certainly it is lawful for you to sell what is yours, since according to the words of blessed Augustine, what may lawfully be bought may be lawfully sold. For the Lord himself said to a certain young man: “Go, sell all that you have.” And elsewhere, when the Lord said to Judas the betrayer: “What you do, do quickly,” some of the disciples thought he was speaking in the usual way, saying: “Buy us the things that are needed for the feast day.” And yet even the lawful and wholly necessary selling of one’s own goods by very many mortals scarcely fails to fix in them some sin — which scripture declares is wedged and driven tight between buyer and seller like a stake between the joints of stones. How great, then, do we suppose is the crime incurred by those who presume to sell another’s property by illicit and destructive sale, against the will of its owner? They are plainly violent men and robbers, of whom the Apostle says: “Nor shall robbers possess the kingdom of God.” In these men, if they are able, let them rightly consider what great robbery is presumed by those who desire wickedly to traffic in God himself — the Lord of all things visible and invisible — when they do not hesitate to sell and buy his grace (which he commanded to be given freely, having been freely received) to the perdition of many souls. But in this they are not only robbers and sacrilegious men, but also murderers, perjurers, apostates, and heretics, for whom the various and innumerable torments of the lower depths of hell would scarcely be sufficient punishment. Not content with the aforesaid crimes, they heap up for themselves everlasting damnation by blaspheming the Holy Spirit, whom they proclaim and believe to cooperate so thoroughly with their sacrilege, robbery, and other wickedness that whatever they — leprous and accursed — touch with mouth and hand is thereby sanctified and blessed.
