He was an implacable adversary of Berengar, and at the pope’s command composed the confession which Berengar was compelled to subscribe at the Roman Easter synod of 1059. At that same synod Humbert subscribed the decree of Nicholas II on the election of the supreme pontiff. The last bull issued by Humbert is dated the 30th of April 1061. If we trust John of Bayon, Humbert died at Rome on the 5th of May 1061, and his body was given honorable burial by the pope.

Humbert was celebrated in the judgment of medieval writers, including his own contemporaries. Wibert praises the brilliance of his mind; Lanfranc his knowledge of both sacred and secular literature; Lanfranc and likewise Richer, among his other virtues, praise Humbert’s humility. In accord with these testimonies is what Othloh reports about his humility, and two passages in Humbert’s pamphlet against the simoniacs may be referred to the same quality: for humility is evident in his speaking on behalf of the lowly and the poor against lords and princes, and in his description in Book III, chapter 35, of good pastors as those rulers of the Church who, content with plain food and clothing, exact from their flocks only what they need to avoid destitution.

If one examines Humbert’s philosophy, he can be found in several passages of the pamphlet against the simoniacs to follow the opinions and statements of John Scotus Eriugena. It is worth presenting some examples. Humbert II, 41: “the interior part of man, which is reason”; John Scotus, On the Division of Nature II, 31 \[Migne, PL CXXII\]: “our interior nature, that is, the rational soul.” Humbert III, 2, where the discussion is of accidents that both contain their subject and are contained by it; cf. John Scotus I, 24. 34. 37. 39. Then Humbert in the same place, that in the sale of a tree it is rather the quantity and quality of the tree — in themselves invisible — that are sold than the matter subject to the senses through its forms; John Scotus III, 14: “it is more rational to say that formless matter appears in colors and forms than that forms and colors appear sensibly in matter” (cf. also I, 56). — Humbert III, 24, where measure, number, and weight are said to be common to all creatures; cf. John Scotus II, 28. III, 11; furthermore Humbert in the same place says that those three, though they inhere equally in all creatures, yet make a single thing. Cf. John Scotus III, 14: that quantities and qualities, coming together into one, produce formless matter. The word *perioche* \[summary/excerpt\] occurs in Humbert III, 42 and in John Scotus’s work I, 25.

The monad, which is the foundation of Scotus’s reasoning and method, is also of the greatest importance to Humbert, who in Book III, chapter 24, calls the monad the principle of subsistence and the dyad the beginning of dissolution. Finally, John Scotus II, 31, following in the footsteps of the fathers, sees the image of the Trinity in the rational soul alone; and indeed, according to Humbert in the same place, the incorruptible Trinity is said and held to be in each and every creature possessing those three — that is, measure, number, and weight — either one spirit or one body.
