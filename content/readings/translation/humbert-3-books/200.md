For this reason let the simoniac brigands cease to whitewash the gallows of their damnation — or rather, since they are parricides and fratricides, let them cease to smear in vain the sack in which they are to be sewn up and punished, and let them learn at last, if belatedly, that they have purchased not ecclesiastical possessions but only the license to possess. This license is the episcopal blessing, as it were the title-deed of the possession. Surely if in some place a decree were proposed and enforced without exception that nothing there might be possessed by anyone without the subscription of the royal hand, what would anyone be said to have rightly purchased save only the royal subscription, whether he obtained it for free or not? For without that license the possession, however purchased, would not pass into his legal right. And so the license — whether given gratis or purchased — transfers the lordship of the possession to another more essentially than the conceded or purchased possession transfers the license. Thus episcopal dignity claims possession for itself more than possession claims episcopal dignity. And yet such is the nature of episcopal office that without those on whom it ought to be bestowed and applied, it is no office at all — just as if someone were said to have the license to cultivate a field, yet lacked a field to cultivate. Is that license not superfluous and void? By the same analogy, a bishop, who is called an overseer \[*superintendens*\], will be nothing if he lacks those over whom to exercise oversight. And why say more? Whatever anything contains inseparably, or is inseparably contained by, cannot be sold or purchased except along with the other. Now episcopal dignity contains the things consecrated to God, and is in turn contained by them — indeed, it exists in them, being itself consecrated by God — just as any subject contains its accidents in such a way that it is in turn contained by them more essentially. Hence just as in all visible things, whenever they are sold, not so much their visible matter is sold as their accidents, which are in themselves invisible, are sold substantially — though neither can be sold without the other — so too in things consecrated to God, in which the consecration or sanctification of those things — which is the grace of the Holy Spirit, invisible in itself — is sold, so to speak, even though the things themselves are also sold. Finally, to take an example: whoever purchases a tree purchases its form more than anything else — that is, its height, its girth, and, to put it briefly, its quantity and quality, which are in themselves invisible — rather than the material underlying the senses through its forms. The same applies to whoever purchases a horse, an estate, or anything of that kind.

## CHAPTER III.

### On the judgment of the Council of Chalcedon against the aforesaid.

But now let the followers of Simon Magus, manifoldly caught out and refuted, set aside the frivolous tricks by which, as if behind masks, they conceal their true and natural faces, and with impunity mock and delude all they can. Let them either repent or perish alone, destined to feel that God himself will mock the mockers. Although in the foregoing we have sought to convict them with arguments drawn from human knowledge, we nonetheless have ready at hand the divine judgments of the holy fathers, which alone suffice to stop their mouths and bring their evasions to a halt. For the holy and universal Council of Chalcedon \[451\] directed the same sentence against a steward \[*oeconomus*\], that is, a dispenser of church goods, promoted through money, as against a bishop, a presbyter, and the other ministers of the clerical order — namely, that the promoter be deposed, and the one promoted be excluded from the charge he sought to purchase with money, and that the brokers of such a base and wicked transaction, if they are clerics, be deposed; and if laymen or monks, be anathematized.
