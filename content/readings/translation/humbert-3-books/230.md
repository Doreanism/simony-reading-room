Since, therefore, from these and many other testimonies of holy scripture it is clear that it pleases the Lord God supremely, in the whole of his creation, to have the universal and common good, and that conversely it displeases him to have the particular and private: whose knowledge — or rather whose brazenness — belongs to those who assert that anything sanctified to God can without penalty and without sacrilege be divided from the very sanctification by which it cleaves to God? The old law, which is a type of the new, shows in many ways how these things are sanctified: either by obligation, or by offering, or by vow. By obligation, as with firstborn, firstfruits, and tithes, which are required as things already sanctified to the Lord before they are born; by offering, as with the breast, the shoulder, an animal, or anything whatsoever that is sanctified to the Lord when it is duly offered; by vow, however, whatever is freely vowed — for from the very vow these things are known to be sanctified to the Lord. These are briefly encompassed together in Leviticus, where it is commanded thus: “No man may sanctify and vow a firstborn, which pertains to God, whether it be ox or sheep; it belongs to the Lord. But if it be an unclean animal, he who offered it shall redeem it according to its valuation, and shall add a fifth part of the price; but if he will not redeem it, it shall be sold for whatever it is valued at. Everything consecrated to the Lord, whether it be man or animal or field, shall not be sold nor redeemed. Whatever has been once consecrated shall be most holy to the Lord. All tithes of the land, whether from the fruits of the field or from the fruits of trees, belong to the Lord and are sanctified to him. If anyone wishes to redeem his tithes, he shall add a fifth part. Of all tithes of ox and sheep and goat, whatever passes under the shepherd’s rod at the tenth, it shall be sanctified to the Lord. He shall not choose whether it be good or bad, nor shall he exchange it for another. If any man makes a substitution, both what he changes and what he changes it for shall be sanctified to the Lord and shall not be redeemed.” \[Leviticus 27:26–33\] If you examine this carefully, you will find that the tabernacle and all its vessels, anointed with the oil of sanctification, are called the holy of holies, while the other external things that pertain to it or to its ministers are called simply holy. Hence it is understood that these latter have received the sanctification by which they are holy from the communion whereby through the devotion of the faithful they are connected to the holy of holies. From this connection they derived and possessed so great a sanctification that, according to the degree of the admonition \[i.e., their closeness to the sanctuary\], some of these things were due to the priests alone within the sanctuary, others also to the males of their lineage, others again to their entire household whether purchased or home-born — excluding every foreigner, the sojourning guest of the priest, the hired servant, and also the leper and the unclean. If a transgressing soul sinned by mistake against the ceremonies concerning these sanctified things, he was to offer for his trespass a ram without blemish from the flocks, obtainable for two shekels according to the weight of the sanctuary; and he was to restore the loss he had caused, adding a fifth part on top, and hand it to the priest — and thus it was forgiven him. And if anyone ate of the sanctified things in ignorance, he was to add a fifth part to what he had eaten and give it to the priest at the sanctuary, “so that they not defile the holy things of the children of Israel, which they offer to the Lord, lest they perhaps bear the iniquity of their trespass, who eat of the sanctified things. I am the Lord who sanctifies them.” \[Leviticus 22:15–16\] From all this it is clearly proven that the Lord sanctifies his tabernacle and his ministers in such a way that whatever belongs to them remains sanctified. From this it follows that if anyone, however slightly, presumed to take from them by mistake or ignorance, he would either add to it in restitution or perish from the midst of his people; but if through pride, he would by no means escape the sentence of dreadful death. Hence, since the Lord, the sanctifier of all things, establishes — now by gentle warning, now by harsh deterrence — that his sanctification is inseparable from those whom or which he sanctifies: who are those who speak iniquity against the Most High, asserting that they have bought or received as a gift the sanctified things apart from their sanctification?
