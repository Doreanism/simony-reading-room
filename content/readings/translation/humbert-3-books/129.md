For a uniform sentence is not to be pronounced on all blasphemers, because blasphemy is not uniform — as Truth itself attests: “Every sin and blasphemy will be forgiven men, but blasphemy against the Spirit will not be forgiven” \[Matthew 12:31\]. Do not the prophet, the apostle, and the doctor appear to have pronounced the same and uniform sentence as the Lord himself on this blasphemy against the Spirit? For the prophet says: “The leprosy of Naaman the Syrian shall cling to you and to your seed forever” \[2 Kings 5:27\]. And the apostle: “Your money perish with you” \[Acts 8:20\]. And a little later: “Repent therefore of this wickedness of yours, and pray to God — if perhaps the thought of your heart may be forgiven you” \[Acts 8:22\]. The doctor, assuredly following the authority of both, says: “The guilt of a sold mystery is inexpiable.” The blessed Augustine likewise, in his commentary on the Gospel of John: “Simon wanted to buy the Holy Spirit because he wanted to sell the Holy Spirit, and he thought the apostles were merchants of the same kind as those the Lord drove from the temple with a whip. For he was of that kind himself — he wanted to buy what he had to sell. He belonged to those who were selling doves; for the Holy Spirit appeared in the form of a dove. Who, then, brothers, are those who sell the dove? Those who say: ‘We give the Holy Spirit.’ But why do they say this, and at what price do they sell it? They receive the price of their office, the price of temporal sees, so that they appear to be the sellers of doves. Let them beware of the whip made from small cords. The dove is not for sale; it is given freely, because it is called grace.” The same, from the same commentary: “In the Acts of the Apostles we read that many believed in Samaria through the preaching of Philip — and by ‘Philip’ is understood either one of the apostles or one of the deacons, since we read that seven deacons were ordained there, among whom Philip’s name also appears. Through the preaching of Philip, then, the Samaritans believed, and Samaria began to abound with faithful people. There was this Simon, \[a practitioner\] of magic arts, who had demented the people through his magical tricks so that they thought him the power of God. Moved, however, by the signs that were being done by Philip, he himself also believed; but the subsequent events showed how he believed. Simon too was baptized. The apostles who were at Jerusalem heard this, and Peter and John were sent to them; they found many baptized, and because none of them had yet received the Holy Spirit — as he then descended to show the sign of the nations who would believe, so that they spoke in tongues, upon those upon whom the Holy Spirit descended — they laid hands on them, praying for them, and they received the Holy Spirit. Simon, who was not a dove in the Church but a crow — because he sought the things that were his own, not the things of Jesus Christ, and hence had loved power among Christians more than justice — saw that the Holy Spirit was given through the laying on of the apostles’ hands, not because they themselves gave it, but because it was given in answer to their prayer; and he said to the apostles: ‘What do you want me to give you in money, so that through my laying on of hands the Holy Spirit may be given?’ And Peter said to him: ‘Your money perish with you, because you thought the gift of God could be purchased with money’ \[Acts 8:18–20\]. To whom does he say ‘Your money perish with you’? To one already baptized. He already had baptism, but he did not cleave to the inner life of the dove. Hear how he did not cleave, and observe the very words of the apostle Peter. For it follows: ‘You have no part or lot in this faith; for I see that you are in the gall of bitterness’ \[Acts 8:21, 23\]. The dove has no gall. Simon had it, and therefore he was separated from the inner life of the dove. What did baptism profit him?” In these words of that most eloquent and discerning doctor, those who shamelessly and impiously strive to assert that the dove — that is, spiritual grace — can be sold must necessarily take note of what he says: “The dove is not for sale; it is given freely, because it is called grace.” And likewise of this: that Simon saw that the Holy Spirit was given through the laying on of the hands of the apostles,
