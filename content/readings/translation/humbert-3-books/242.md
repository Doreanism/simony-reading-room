Therefore, this argument being rejected as truly frivolous, let arguments be drawn from cases that are joined to the matter and analogous to it. Consider: suppose someone, through a judge corrupted by money and through that judge’s favor and unjust sentence, obtains the property of another. If a just judge subsequently discovers this and ejects him, and the property rightfully passes to the original owner or his heirs — does the legitimate owner retain the portions distributed \[*sortes*\], or things sold or donated, or the titles, ministries, and rights conferred by the intruder in that place? Or does he rather, looking out for himself and his posterity, expel them from there? Otherwise, if he permits these things out of reverence or favor for the intruder — as though the intruder had legitimately arranged anything there — his own negligence weakens and alienates his right, while confirming and reversing it in favor of his adversary. By this argument of unjust acquisition and just loss, or just recovery, one can be taught what is sold by simoniacs, what is bought, what is conferred by those promoted by them whether freely or not, and what is profitably arranged and transacted in another’s possession either for themselves or others. For even though the intruder professes, with an unjust judge as his witness, that he received his just claim, held it, and donated or sold it to others — or that it was afterwards unjustly taken from him — it is nonetheless established that neither he nor anyone through him ever acquired anything in such estates beyond sheer injustice and his own loss. When they are expelled, penalized by the laws and fined with multiple restitution of the things presumed upon, both the actors and their abettors alike are not deprived of any justice, for they had none; but only of the injustice they incurred. Finally, the judge — to be stripped of the crime of perjury and falsehood through penance — is cast down; while the intruders are no less cleansed of the leprosy of falsehood and rapine; otherwise, to the last farthing, what is contracted here in time shall be repaid in eternity. For “a false witness shall not go unpunished” \[Proverbs 19:5\]; and “No one long enjoys what he gains through an unjust judge.”

Furthermore: if someone is caught having stolen any thing, shall he not, as Solomon testifies, restore it fourfold and deliver the whole substance of his house \[cf. Proverbs 6:31\]? And if someone knowingly purchases stolen goods, he both loses the price paid and is nonetheless liable to the penalty due a thief. But if someone buys unknowingly, he also loses the price paid and must restore the purchased goods without the thief’s stigma — provided he proves by oath to those who bring the charge that he did not know, at the time of purchase, that it had been taken by theft; otherwise, if he tries to keep it by force or delays in clearing himself of the theft, he will bear the liability of a thief together with the stigma and the penalty. This the person who received stolen goods freely must of necessity also suffer. From all this one may conclude that, if no one who acquires stolen goods from any thief escapes without disgrace, loss, and punishment — whenever those goods are found in his possession — how much more so those who acquire from simoniac thieves the dove of God, stolen as it appears, whether knowingly or unknowingly, for a price or as a gift; and all the more so, by how much divine things are greater and more precious than human. Therefore, either let it be proved that simoniacs, like other heretics, are not thieves and robbers — or let it be conceded that nothing received from them, or receivable from them, can be had without disgrace, loss, and penalty, which assuredly cannot be escaped without legal satisfaction. But if it is still insufficiently established whether simoniacs are thieves and robbers, let this be carefully considered and faithfully believed: that if they are heretics, they are beyond all doubt thieves and robbers; if, however, they are Catholics, they will be either shepherds or hirelings. But lest we remain stuck on this ambiguity any longer, let the supremely Good Shepherd himself be heard, resolving this point expressly. For in his own words, as by an index, he shows shepherds, hirelings, thieves and robbers, sheep and wolves, one by one.
