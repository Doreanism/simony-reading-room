And its land will be measured with a line when ecclesiastical property is alienated from the lot of the Lord’s priesthood and recalled to the lot and dominion of laymen. If these men were defenders of the church and not plunderers and invaders, they would without doubt not neglect to defend it for whatever fault or negligence of the priests, nor would they claim its rights for themselves. For the act of an individual, as blessed Gregory teaches, is not to be turned to the detriment of the church; rather the individual is to be corrected in such a way that the ecclesiastical estate is preserved entirely unharmed. For the church has its own laws, its own judges, by whom — with the counsel of the devout and the wise — the faults of its rulers and ministers are either rebuked or corrected. If ecclesiastical men overlook or disregard these laws, then secular rulers and faithful laymen must at last press forward in defense and recovery of what they owe to their mother the church, and must meet them in season and out of season with warnings, lest by silence and indifference they favor the destroyers of God’s churches and the undisciplined; rather, they should rise together with their canons to reform her. But if even after warnings the wicked are not checked, let the lay power at last do what good sons of a free and beloved mother would do — sons who, if they know their mother’s chastity to be taken captive by adulterers, gird themselves without delay to avenge her, even if their father, warned, should neglect to avenge so intolerable a wrong against himself. For guarding against a stain upon their lineage and no small disgrace upon their name, good sons do not desist, even when their father is idle, from avenging the wrongs done to their mother. But if, having rescued her from the adulterers, they do not restore her to her lawful marriage but prostitute their mother’s chastity to their own lust, would they not be worse and more detestable than any adulterers? Would it not have been more tolerable not to have defended her at all than, having defended her, to seize and appropriate her in this way? I ask that Christian rulers and laymen consider this with Christian piety, and recognize at last how much more guilty they are if they defend their spiritual mother in such a way that they do not restore her to her Father, but merely prostitute her to themselves. And to what Father? To him, certainly, to whom no earthly father, let alone is he to be preferred, but cannot even be compared — according to the saying: “He who loves father or mother more than me is not worthy of me” \[Matthew 10:37\]; and even if we have them as adversaries on the path of the spiritual Father, we must hate them and flee from them.

## Chapter XII.

### On the Presumption of Women and the Dignity of Sacred Things.

But why do we complain only of lay men, when even lay women — to whom it is not permitted to speak in church nor to exercise authority over a man — not only entrust ecclesiastical affairs to laymen for their management, but furthermore invest clergy with pastoral staffs and rings for bishoprics and abbacies, men whom favor, secular service, or temporal payment brings to their attention and commends? These women likewise exercise dominion over every ecclesiastical council and judgment, so that at their nod, even in their absence, some are set over others and some are deposed, some though guilty are passed over or defended. Thus by this most perverse abuse, as the impious Porphyry tauntingly accuses, our senate is made up of women, by whom sacerdotal insignia are moreover granted; nor is it permitted to ecclesiastics to assume what is theirs save by their gift — things which not only women, but not even any man who is not a clergyman ought to touch or carry, as blessed Pope Stephen the martyr thus declares: “The ecclesiastical vestments in which service is rendered to the Lord must be both sacred and seemly; no one should make use of them for any purpose other than ecclesiastical and worthy divine offices, nor should they be touched or carried by any persons other than those consecrated to God, lest the punishment which struck Belshazzar \[Daniel 5\] come upon those who transgress and presume such things by divine judgment, and cause them to fall to the depths.” By the decree of so great a pontiff, laymen and laywomen must take care not to defile ecclesiastical vestments by their touch, nor, having been barred from lesser things, to presume to greater.
