Gregory greatly praised this law, saying: “One who, while still wearing secular dress, hastens to take up ecclesiastical office does not wish to leave the world, but merely to change it.” This same blessed pontiff likewise enlisted industrious men from his own church as rectors of its patrimonies in various provinces, for the diligent governance of holy religion and the affairs of the poor — among them Cyprian the deacon for the Sicilian patrimony, Pantaleon the notary for Syracuse, and others named there, down to Candidus the priest for Gaul. He also wrote to Januarius of Cagliari, saying among other things: “It has been reported to us that you have entrusted the care of your patrimony to certain laymen. Your fraternity must henceforth take care to avoid this, and the affairs of the church are not to be committed to secular men who do not live under your rule, but to proven clergymen from your staff, so that if any wickedness should be discovered in them, you may be able, as their superior, to correct what has been done unlawfully — men, that is, whose manner of life befits their office rather than excusing them from it.” Writing also to the Emperor Maurice concerning priests, he said among other things: “Our Lord should not be quickly displeased with priests on account of earthly power, but in his sublime wisdom should govern them with due regard for him whose servants they are, even rendering them the reverence they are owed. For in the divine scriptures, priests are sometimes called gods and sometimes angels. Through Moses it is said of one who is to be brought to an oath: ‘Bring him before the gods,’ that is, before the priests. And again it is written: ‘You shall not revile the gods,’ meaning the priests. And the prophet says: ‘The lips of the priest shall keep knowledge, and they shall seek the law from his mouth, for he is the angel of the Lord of hosts.’ What wonder, then, if your piety deems them worthy of honor, when God himself in his own words honors them by calling them either angels or gods? Before the Emperor Constantine, the pagan rulers of the republic, knowing not the true God and worshiping gods of wood and stone, nevertheless paid the greatest honor to their priests. What wonder, then, if a Christian emperor deems the priests of the true God worthy of honor, since pagan rulers, as I have said, knew to show honor to priests who served gods of wood and stone?” It is likewise shown from other writings of this same pontiff that episcopal ordinations were celebrated by his judgment and consent — a fact that appears beyond all doubt from two cases, for which he wrote to the people and clergy, commanding: “As the Apostolic See, by God’s authority, stands above all churches, so among many concerns it greatly troubles us whenever our decision is awaited for the consecration of a bishop. The Bishop Laurence of the church of Milan having died, the clergy reported to us by letter that they had unanimously agreed in electing our son Constantius, their deacon. But since that letter lacked signatures, lest we omit anything pertaining to caution, it is necessary that you, supported by the authority of this directive, travel to Genoa. And since many Milanese are gathered there, compelled by the savage barbarity of the Lombards, you must summon them together and carefully inquire into their wishes. And if no disagreement separates them from unity in the election, and if you find that the will and consent of all remains fixed upon our aforementioned son Constantius, then, with the support of our authority, cause him to be consecrated by his own bishops, as ancient custom requires, so that, this practice being preserved, the Apostolic See may retain its proper vigor and not diminish the rights it has granted to others.” But when Constantius, after some years of distinguished service in the episcopate, had fallen asleep in the Lord, the clergy and people of Milan elected Deacon Deusdeditus, yet were threatened by King Agilulf to elect the man whom Lombard bar\-
