And since each person becomes what he worships, one who worships a statue becomes a statue, just as one who worships God becomes God — by grace, not by nature — as the psalmist says: “I said: you are gods” \[Psalm 82:6\]. Thus the most avaricious men, worshipping money as a god, esteem it as all-powerful after the manner of pagans worshipping their various gods — to such a degree that they believe it capable of subduing even the Holy Spirit to itself. Measuring out immense majesty from their own experience — for they themselves turn pale at the sight of a coin and at its nod perform both what is lawful and what is unlawful — they utterly believe that whatever cannot fail to please themselves must please God. A satirist rebuked their madness in these words:

O souls bowed down to earth and empty of all heavenly things!

What good does it do to bring our ways into the temples

And to infer the gods’ good gifts from this depraved flesh?

Now if even a pagan, though abusing reason, believed that idols were omnipotent gods and the authors of all good, yet being a rational creature he was led by reason to conclude that those whom he believed supremely good could not be pleased by the vicious affections and passions of human beings — for supreme virtue must of necessity be entirely free from all vice — can those be thought Christians who consider the Father of lights, from whom comes down every good gift and every perfect gift \[James 1:17\], so imperfect that by means of money, which they love above all things, they believe they can conciliate and acquire for themselves his supreme gift, and that what thus pleases themselves cannot fail to please God?

## CAPUT XVI.

### The Judgment of Saint Ambrose on the Ordinations of Simoniacs.

Let us now add certain words of the great Ambrose, by which simoniacs — being proven to be greedy — are shown to be usurers as well, and therefore, as it is written, accursed above all men. For every greedy person is a usurer, and every usurer is greedy. The blessed Ambrose, therefore, speaking of the life and ordination of bishops, says among other things: “Although the length of this little book already presses us, and time urges us to reach the conclusion of this work, the solicitude of brotherly love delays us — especially on behalf of those priests who, dulled by torpor of mind, dishonor the priestly office. Those who are known to have given birth prodigiously to a new rule and storm, following Gehazi’s example and Simon’s sentence, have purchased the grace of the holy episcopate with money, fearing not at all that, in buying the priestly office with money, they have been condemned along with Simon by the blessed Apostle Peter, who says: ‘Your money perish with you, for you thought you could buy the gift of the Holy Spirit with money’ \[Acts 8:20\]. For they must believe that they have contracted leprosy along with Gehazi from the holy Elisha, since they suppose they have purchased the priestly rank with money. Since it should have sufficed for us to tell them these things — but they went on to possess their purchased leprosy. For just as in more serious diseases, when the head is corrupted, the rest of the body is necessarily flooded to death by the overflow of the superior malady, so also these men, who appear to be the head of the Church, corrupt the body of the brethren with pestilential disease, so that nothing in the whole body’s frame can escape being wounded without being mortally infected by the virulence of negligent priests’ corruption. Thus you may see in the Church, on every side, those whom not merit but money has raised to the episcopal order.
