But why is it said that they cannot now have the one they can no longer even invoke? This was signified by the dreadful end of Judas, who, having betrayed Christ, hastened to the noose and perished by a punishment befitting his crime: with those very jaws which had dared to open and lend their passage to the deadly voice of his betrayal bound up and wholly constricted, he could neither truly nor falsely invoke God after the true utterances of his evil scheme or after the feigned ones of a still worse supplication. Not undeservedly are his airways so constricted that they can no longer breathe out the vital breath — airways that had rashly sent forth the voice that dared to sell the Redeemer of the world. Hence “he burst open in the middle and all his bowels gushed out.” Nor did he deserve to have the common and natural departure of the spirit shared by other men, he who had conspired against the common Lord of nature. Thus, hated by heaven and earth and loathed by every creature, he perished in the space between them, while the earth recoiled from receiving his most foul and fetid corpse, and the air from receiving his most impious and impure spirit. Let the simoniacs therefore learn from their own beginnings what they are, what sort of men they are, and what they deserve. And if the first-fruits are ground down by twofold contention, how often shall such great heaps as theirs be ground? Do they not see that, after the very utterance of the sale, they are unable to invoke the Lord, since their throats are strangled by the inextricable noose of treachery? Indeed, how shall they call upon one in whom they have not believed? But perhaps, still stiff-necked and shameless with a brow of brass, they stubbornly insist that they believe in God with a Catholic mind, even though they sell the Holy Spirit. Let them trace out, therefore, by every means they can, all the hiding-places of that first author of theirs in the selling of the Lord God, and let them show whether they can find him having believed in Jesus from the time that Satan entered his heart to betray him. For if he believed in him in any way, he would never have attempted such a thing. He would have recognized that the Lord cannot be sold, and that no one can resist his will and power. But the wretch knew in him only what was human and visible. Nor is it a wonder that he did not fully know even what he saw, if perhaps, corrupted by Pharisaic leaven, he ascribed the Savior’s miracles to the prince of demons. For if he had known, he surely would have believed; for no one believes what he in no way knows, even though by believing one merits to know more perfectly, as the Apostle says: “Now I know in part, but then I shall know even as I am known.” And elsewhere: “Having known God” — namely through faith — “you are known by him.” But who would doubt this of the carnal and mortal Judas, when the same Apostle asserts that none of the princes of this age — even those of a more subtle and immortal nature, such as the spiritual powers of wickedness in the heavenly places — knew the incarnate wisdom of God the Father? “For if they had known, they would never have crucified the Lord of glory.”

## CHAPTER XXV.

### On the fact that Judas repented without faith, and that those who consented to the chief priests were deprived of faith.

But lest anyone suppose he believed when we read that he repented and returned the money, let them know that the repentance there spoken of is used in an improper sense, since it was in truth the sorrow of the world which produces death — not that which, as the Apostle says, is according to God and produces a steadfast repentance leading to salvation. Indeed, praiseworthy repentance is not without hope, and its substance is faith. Hence, since Judas’s repentance was without hope, it is clear that he had no faith at all; and therefore, seeing himself condemned, led by the sorrow of the world, he preferred to destroy his own throat in despair rather than humbly to offer worthy fruits of repentance in hope. Had he done the latter, his error would not have harmed him. But the faithless one, who had earlier blasphemed the Son of God by believing that he could be sold, blasphemed in the end also the Holy Spirit, believing that he could not grant him pardon for so great a sin.
