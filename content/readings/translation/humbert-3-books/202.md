And one who offers a gift to the Lord of hosts’ — understand: ‘May the Lord destroy him who shall wish to offer a gift at the altar on behalf of men of this kind, whose only remedy is not to do what they have done.’” In this strict yet stringent sentence of so learned a man, let those take heed of what they deserve who defile things consecrated to God by trafficking in them for their own profit. Through their trafficking, what is sacred becomes secular, as Blessed Gregory teaches. Hence, because they read — that is, steal — sacred things, they are rightly called sacrilegious \[*sacrilegi*\]. For they steal the sanctification when, by withdrawing or invading the things which that sanctification was hallowing, they purloin and carry away that sanctification with such guile that, like an unwary man who has suffered theft, they affect themselves to be rich with ecclesiastical affectation while having nothing of it any longer. That such sacrilegious persons are to be guarded against, Pope Pius, a holder of the apostolic see, declares thus: “All usurpation by him \[the invader\] must be repelled with reproach, lest the estates dedicated to the secrets of heaven be harassed by certain marauders. And if anyone shall presume to do this, let him be held and judged as a sacrilegious person. Moreover we have heard that those who do this pursue and defame the clerics and priests of the Lord, so that they may add evil upon evil and become worse, not understanding that the Church of God consists in priests and grows into a temple of God. And just as one who lays waste the Church of God, despoils and invades its estates and offerings, becomes a sacrilegious person, so he who persecutes its priests is guilty of sacrilege and is judged a sacrilegious person.” And Blessed Pope Urban: “Ecclesiastical properties,” he says, “are called the offerings of the faithful, because they are offered to the Lord. They ought therefore not to be converted to any uses other than ecclesiastical ones and those of brethren wishing to live a common Christian life or of the needy, because they are the vows of the faithful and the price of sins and have been handed over to the Lord for the fulfillment of the aforesaid purpose. But if anyone, which God forbid, shall act otherwise, let him see to it lest he receive the condemnation of Ananias and Sapphira \[cf. Acts 5:1–11\] and become guilty of sacrilege, as were those who defrauded the price of the aforesaid things.” And shortly after: “These things, brethren, are greatly to be guarded against and feared, because the properties of the Church are not to be dispensed faithfully, as if one’s own, but as common properties offered to the Lord, with the utmost fear, for no purposes other than the aforesaid ones, lest those who withdraw them from the place to which they were dedicated incur the guilt of sacrilege — and what is worse, become anathema maranatha, and, if not bodily as Ananias and Sapphira did, who fell down dead, yet in the soul, which is more important than the body, die and, cut off from the fellowship of the faithful, fall and sink into the depths of the abyss. For if anyone shall do this, after the sharpness of the punishment justly to be inflicted on sacrilegious persons, let him be condemned to perpetual infamy and handed over to prison, or thrust into the exile of perpetual deportation, since according to the apostle such a man ought to be delivered to Satan, that his spirit may be saved on the day of the Lord \[cf. 1 Corinthians 5:5\].” Blessed Pope Lucius likewise: “The properties of your churches,” he says, “and the offerings of the faithful, which you report to be harassed by certain marauders and taken from you and your churches, are beyond doubt a very great sin, as scripture testifies, which says: ‘Whoever takes anything from his father or mother and says “this is no sin” is the companion of a murderer \[Proverbs 28:24\].’ Our Father therefore is without doubt God, who created us, and our mother is the Church, who spiritually begat us in baptism. And therefore whoever takes, defrauds, or seizes the money of Christ and the Church shall be reckoned a murderer in the sight of the just judge. Hence it is written: ‘Whoever seizes the money of his neighbor commits iniquity; but whoever takes the money or property of the Church commits sacrilege.’ Hence also Judas, who defrauded the money being distributed for the uses of the Church — that is, for the poor whom the Church ought to feed — by command of the Savior, whose role bishops hold, became not only a thief but a thief and a sacrilegious person.
