For the Lombards, although then barbarians and Arians, and not even their king Agilulf, however greatly hostile to Roman liberty and dignity, dared to resist the Roman pontiff in his establishing a metropolitan for the Milanese church according to the ancient canonical privileges of his apostolic see. This privilege he retained not only in Italy, then invaded by barbarians and Arians, but also in Germany and throughout the Gauls, as is clearly declared in his deeds and writings.

## Chapter XI.

By what means and by whom the churches were deprived of their privileges.

And this canonical authority, solid and special, was reserved to the Roman pontiffs over all metropolitans, and likewise to all metropolitans over all their suffragans, as anyone who searches will find in the deeds of blessed Boniface, Archbishop of Mainz. Nor did Pippin the king then, nor his son Charles the Great, the first emperor of the Franks, attempt to infringe upon this canon — nor indeed did any of the great-nephews or heirs of their realm and empire, down to the time of Otto. For as these rulers grew in power and advanced, the strength of the Roman pontiffs began to diminish and fail, so greatly that through their own laziness and foolishness, and through the arrogance of the newly empowered rulers, every ecclesiastical office and right was gradually seized and wrested from their hands, while they labored to retain barely the empty name of their former dignity, and not the dignity itself. With the head of the clerical order thus weakened and cast down, secular power at once usurped the right to subjugate the entire body of that order to its own will with impunity; and it did not at first seize all ecclesiastical things together and at once, but in the manner of other wickednesses, separately and step by step. For at first it favored the ambitious who coveted ecclesiastical dignities or possessions with a petition, then with threats, then with concessive words; and seeing in all of this that no one contradicted it, nor moved a pen, nor opened his mouth and objected, it advanced to greater things. And now, under the name of investiture, it began to grant first tablets or mere sticks of any kind, then lay staves, and finally episcopal ones. This enormous wickedness has so taken root that it alone is now believed to be canonical, and no one knows or heeds what the ecclesiastical rule is. And indeed I recall having seen some men invested with pastoral staffs and rings concerning bishoprics and abbacies by secular rulers, while their metropolitans and primates, though present, were neither consulted nor dared to utter a word against it. For these men, caught in so great an adultery of their wives and slaying of their sons, deservedly incurred, through their own negligence and transgression, that Mosaic curse which says: “You shall take a wife, and another shall sleep with her before your eyes” \[Deuteronomy 28:30\]. Concerning this, as blessed Jerome attests in his commentary on the prophet Zechariah, nothing more cruel, nothing more wretched can be found than that men, through fear of their own death, dare not defend even the safety of their sons or the chastity of their wives. The prophet Amos likewise speaks this threat to Amaziah the impious priest: “Your wife shall play the harlot in the city, and your sons and daughters shall fall by the sword, and your land shall be measured with a line” \[Amos 7:17\]. For a priest’s wife is indeed violated by others whenever the church is administered or governed by laymen to whom it does not belong.
