### Refutation.

In this position, what understanding or what spark of reason can be found? O God and Lord, Holy Spirit, who are eternal justice — according to the teaching of these preachers, or rather of these lost souls, you can be unjustly acquired, unjustly held, unjustly conceded. And where is justice then to be sought, if justice can be unjustly acquired and held? Wretched and insane men, who esteem you as though you were some beast stolen by a thief, kept by the thief, and then knowingly purchased from him. But let them first become like the horse and the mule, which have no understanding, since men, though they were in honor, did not understand, but were made equal to senseless beasts and became like them. For although they were created by God as rational beings, yet in abusing reason they do not understand that justice cannot coexist or be compatible with injustice, or virtue with vice. These men, as blessed Jude the apostle says, *blaspheme whatever things they do not know; and in those things which they know naturally, as brute animals, they are corrupted.* Therefore, with the Holy Spirit having fled from such thoughts and departed, as was said above, they are seized — that is, wholly taken over — by oncoming iniquity: namely the evil spirit, who is so iniquitous as to be iniquity itself, as the glorious Paul declares: *The mystery of iniquity is already at work.* And a little later: *And then that Iniquitous One will be revealed* — who is finally so much a murderer that he is death itself; so ruined and a ruiner that he is ruin itself. Seized entirely by this iniquity, they are now altogether carnal men and *do not perceive the things that are of the Spirit of God. For they are foolishness to them, and they cannot understand the things that are spiritually examined.* Moreover, as the same apostle adds: *But the spiritual man judges all things, and he himself is judged by no one. For who has known the mind of the Lord, who will instruct him? But we have the mind of Christ.* For, as is stated a little earlier: *To us God has revealed them through his Spirit. For the Spirit searches all things, even the deep things of God. For what man knows the things that are of a man, except the spirit of the man that is in him? So also, what is of God no one has known except the Spirit of God. But we have received not the spirit of the world, but the Spirit who is from God, that we may know the things that have been given to us by God — which we also speak, not in words taught by human wisdom, but in the teaching of the Spirit, comparing spiritual things with spiritual.* Among the apostle’s words, one thing above all is now to be considered: his statement, *We have received not the spirit of the world, but the Spirit who is from God.* For the spirit of the world, as blessed John the apostle writes, is the evil spirit dwelling in wickedness \[*in maligno positi*\] — whom the Lord in the gospel calls the prince of this world, as in: *The prince of this world will be cast out.* And: *The prince of this world has been judged.* And: *The prince of this world comes, and in me he has nothing.* It is plainly evident that those who know the things given to them by God have not received him. This knowledge is worked in them by the Spirit who is from God, whom they are said to have received for this purpose: that they might know the things given to them by God. The proof, therefore, of having received the Spirit who is from God is the recognition of the things given by him. That we retain this perfectly is confirmed by the fact that we profess and, to the measure of our ability, defend — comparing spiritual things with spiritual — that the gift of God is to be given freely, that is, received and given freely, according to the evangelical and apostolic tradition. From the opposite side it is gathered that those have received the spirit of the world who — contrary to evangelical and apostolic discipline — suppose, believe, profess, and strive with all their might to defend that the gift of God can be possessed for money, comparing carnal things with spiritual things. If they but recalled what this same spirit of the world is, and where it is, and what it has already suffered, they would with great contrition of heart seek to be delivered from the power of darkness and — while there is still time — transferred into the kingdom of the Son of his Father’s brightness. For that spirit is the prince of this world, the ruler of these present darknesses — that is, of unbelieving men — in whom, as in sons of disobedience, he himself, the ancient serpent who is the devil and Satan, already judged and condemned and cast out, reigns and works: cast out, that is, not out of the fabric of heaven and earth, but out of the hearts of the faithful, whom Christ made a kingdom and priests for God his Father. And if the spirit of the world has already been condemned and cast out, it is plain that the world over which he rules has already been condemned and cast out with him, and is no longer the world that God loved and, having redeemed it with the blood of his Son, cleansed it with the washing of water in the Word, so that God himself might be with them as their God. For *what part has a believer with an unbeliever?* Therefore, whoever holds wrong views about the Holy Spirit should take note of what blessed Pope Damasus, writing among other things to Paulinus, patriarch of Antioch, says: *If anyone has rightly believed concerning the Father and the Son, but has not held right views concerning the Holy Spirit, he is a heretic.* And that all heretics who hold wrong views concerning the Son of God or the Holy Spirit are found to be in the company of Jews and pagans. Indeed, to the Jews saying: *We are not born of fornication* — that is, of idolatry — *we have one Father, God,* the Lord Jesus answered: *If God were your Father, you would surely love me. For I have come forth from God and I came; for I did not come of myself, but he sent me. You are of your father the devil.* And to Simon Magus, who sought to purchase the Holy Spirit for money, Peter said: *You have no part nor lot in this faith.* But lest this sustained refutation — though entirely necessary — if prolonged further should press and assail our adversary so relentlessly that our weary fellow soldier falls in the struggle, the writer’s intention ought to provide the delicate reader with a moment to breathe, and accordingly let this be the end of the first little book.
