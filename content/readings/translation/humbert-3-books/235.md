## CAPUT XXVIIII.

The opinion of Saint Augustine on the power of the soul and a reconsideration derived from it, and the character of those promoted by laypeople and of their metropolitans.

Whether the sanctification of this Spirit sanctifies and governs the whole body of the Church — if this is still doubted — or how it does so — if this is questioned — let it be known that this is ineffable and beyond human comprehension. It is therefore necessary to believe and understand it piously, lest if it is impiously examined, offense be given. Yet the soul, insofar as a creature is permitted, can preach to man so great an artisan Spirit, that from whatever little the mind can grasp, it may form some conjecture of that ineffable diffusion, through which it gains some degree of approach to a tenuous contemplation of the incomprehensible power of the Savior. For the soul, as blessed Augustine writes to the holy Jerome, extends through the whole body which it animates — not by local diffusion but by a kind of vital intentionality. For it is present as a whole simultaneously in all the body’s parts: not smaller in the smaller parts nor larger in the larger, but more intently in some places, more laxly in others, and in all as a whole and in each one as a whole. Nor indeed does it perceive what happens in the body in any other mode than wholly, yet it perceives as a whole. For when something touches the tiniest point in living flesh — though that place is not only not the whole body but can scarcely be seen in the body — yet the whole soul takes notice of it. Nor does what is perceived course through all the members of the body, but is perceived only where it occurs. How, then, does what happens not throughout the whole body reach the whole soul immediately, unless because the soul is also present where it happens, and does not abandon the rest in order to be wholly present there? For those other parts also live with it present, as though nothing had occurred. But if that were to happen, and both things happened simultaneously, both simultaneously would equally not escape the whole soul. Thus it could not be wholly present simultaneously in all and each of the body’s individual parts, if it were diffused through them in the way we see bodies diffused over spatial extents, occupying smaller portions with their smaller parts and larger portions with their larger parts. That the soul does this is attested both by the opinion of so great a doctor and by daily experience. Who among the faithful can any longer doubt, then, that the Holy Spirit fills the whole Church far more ineffably with his sanctification, sanctifying each thing connected to it and owed to it in proportion to the quality of its ministers and affairs? For the clerical order in the Church is preeminent like the eyes in the head, of which the Lord says: “Whoever touches you touches the apple of my eye.” And the lay power is like the chest and arms, vigorous and outstretched to obey and defend the Church. Then the common people are like the lower or more peripheral members, equally subject and entirely necessary to both ecclesiastical and secular powers. Furthermore, the possessions of the Church can fittingly be taken as the hairs of the head, and the resources of the secular world as the nails or the other hairs of the body. He who removes these indiscriminately or violently from the outer man must needs gravely offend the inner man, through the injury of dishonor or presumption. This is what the Holy Spirit, who in the universality of the Church operates in all things far more excellently than the soul, must undoubtedly do — distributing to each as he wills and announcing to the disciples many things which before the passion of Christ they could not bear. Hence, just as when the soul departs the body is at once seized by corruption and worms, which dissolve it into ash with the greatest loathing and rejection by men, so too when ecclesiastical things are deprived of their sanctification they are at once abandoned to the dominion of demons under the supreme curse of God and the holy fathers, and perish. Wherefore, according to what the Lord says in the gospel to the man demanding the division of an inheritance: “Take heed and beware of all covetousness.” O you who are divided, dividers — learn at last, if belatedly, that what cannot be divided cannot be divided, and cease to yield to those who would divide, if you desire to take counsel for your souls and theirs according to God. For by pursuing things that are seen and despising things that are not seen, you grasp for a time the reed of vain honor, and do not look ahead a little to the court of supreme blessedness or the prison of extreme misery.
