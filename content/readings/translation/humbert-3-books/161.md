## CHAPTER XVIII.

### On the fact that simoniacs are possessed by Satan, after the example of Judas setting about the betrayal of the Lord.

Having now demonstrated by clear and manifold authority — on the occasion of secular traders — how far our carnal Christians, even with their dead faith, differ from heretics, who have not even a dead faith remaining but none at all, as if uprooted and emptied out entirely, it remains to show in order in what respects the sellers of ecclesiastical ministry transgress. They too, of course, seek to enrich themselves temporally, but they transgress so much the more than other traders as eternal things surpass temporal, and heavenly things surpass earthly — or, to put it more briefly and plainly, as the Creator of all things is preferred above the most worthless of creatures. When anyone seeks to enrich himself by trafficking in God’s sacraments, he is undoubtedly plotting, like a second Judas, to betray to the faithless the very God who is essentially present in those sacraments and cooperates through them. It is therefore necessary that those whom the same intention equals to impious and cruel Judas should be bound by the same guilt and enveloped by the same condemnation. And indeed the devil, who stands at the right hand of Judas — not any common thief, but a sacrilegious one — entered his heart to seduce him toward evil for the mere contemplation of betrayal, as the Gospel of John attests: “And during the supper, when the devil had already put it into the heart of Judas, son of Simon Iscariot, to betray him,” and so on. From this diabolical instigation, what the wretched Judas attained Luke shows most plainly as follows: “Satan entered into Judas, who was called Iscariot, one of the Twelve, and he went away and spoke with the chief priests and officers about how he might betray him to them. And they were glad and agreed to give him money, and he pledged \[himself\]. And he sought an opportunity to betray him away from the crowd.” In these most truthful words of truthful evangelists it is plainly suggested how great a guilt was incurred by that most wicked traitor even in the conception of his betrayal. Although he had long before been a sacrilegious thief — when he stole by theft from the purse the Lord’s money that he carried as minister, money intended to benefit the poor — yet the devil is not recorded to have entered him until the moment when it was put into his heart to betray the Master. If he merited such a tenant from the mere affection of betrayal, what tenant, we may ask, did he merit from the act itself?

For it must be known that just as goodness has its degrees, by which it advances gradually upward, so too does wickedness have its own, by which it continually falls lower and lower. Nor is this surprising, since the prompting toward good is the seed of God, and the prompting toward evil is likewise the seed of the devil. And just as the seed of God cast into good ground, according to the parable of the evangelical farmer, first puts forth a shoot, then produces the blade, then the ear, then the full grain in the ear, which is at once cut with the sickle and, winnowed by the fan, is laid up in the heavenly barn — so too the seed of the devil strives to grow by its own increases in bad ground, until it reaches its own place, namely the eternal fire. For no one falls suddenly to the lowest point in evil, just as no one rises suddenly to the summit in good.
