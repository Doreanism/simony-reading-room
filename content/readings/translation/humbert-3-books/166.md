Since these \[foxes\] divide \[the vineyard\] among themselves to possess according to the quality or rank of their most wicked power, Isaiah, concerning Edom, prophesies thus under the names and significations of various harmful birds, trees, plants, and unclean beasts: “The pelican and the hedgehog shall possess it, and the ibis and the raven shall dwell in it.” And shortly after: “And thorns and nettles shall grow up in its houses, and brambles in its strongholds. And it shall be the haunt of dragons and a pasture for ostriches, and demons and the wild goat shall meet there, and the shaggy one shall call to his fellow. There the night-hag has lain down and found rest; there the hedgehog has its lair and reared its young and dug around \[it\] and brooded in its shadow. Thither have kites gathered, one to another.” The little foxes of whom we now speak — having become equally the sharers and participants of such and so shape-shifting foxes — are possessed and possess by the merit of their duplicity, multiplied by the multiform wickedness of demons, and still more multiply to be possessed; and they divide and will divide among themselves those very \[domains\] to be possessed by them, \[domains\] which are in turn portioned out among them by those by whom they themselves are to be possessed. Concerning these likewise, a certain wise man says: “Foxes inhabit souls mindful of malice, and beasts lie down in the turbulent heart.” But the little sheep of Christ are blessed by the merit of their simplicity, whose lord is their God, who is wholly the unique portion of their glorious inheritance — an inheritance which he himself will restore wholly to each of them, and each of them in turn wholly in his whole \[self\], that all may be consummated into one, and God may be one and all things to all. Thus truly shall they be his people, and he God with them shall be their God. Because the aforesaid little foxes abhor their \[i.e., the sheep’s\] unique simplicity, they inhabit the solitudes of dragons and the desert of ostriches, deprived of the glorious and populous inheritance of the one fold and the one shepherd. And because they sometimes occupy the mind appointed for pastoral care or divine contemplation, when by their presumption of Church governance or sacred Scripture study they dissipate both through perverse action and interpretation, Jeremiah rightly laments concerning them thus: “Our heart has been made sorrowful in grief, our eyes have been darkened, because of Mount Zion, for it has perished — foxes have walked upon it.” But even though on account of such speculators Mount Zion at times perishes, we have some consolation in this: that they are said not to have dwelt or stood upon it, but only to have walked upon it. For the episcopate or teaching authority of heretics in the Catholic Church or in canonical Scripture will not be able to rest or stand for any length of time. Indeed, because they do not speak the truth in their heart, because they practice deceit with their tongue, because they accept bribes against the innocent — they cannot dwell in the tabernacle of God or rest on his holy mountain. So also, because they take up their souls in vain and swear deceitfully to their neighbor, they cannot stand in his holy place. And as for the saying that they shall not stand — they do not even ascend to that \[place\]. What sort of person ascends, the Psalmist declares, saying: “The innocent of hands and pure of heart,” and so on. Hence it is clear that these foxes — so cunning and rapacious — have not even walked on the mountain of Catholic Zion, which they were not able to ascend. Yet according to the estimation of the common people believing that such men hold authority in the Church of God or scrutinize her teaching, they are said to have walked \[upon it\], as the Lord says through the prophet: “They reigned, but not from me. They became princes, and I did not know them.” And elsewhere: “All her princes shall come to nothing.” Of these, Samson the mighty — as a figure — captured three hundred foxes, and as the story tells, tied their tails to tails, and bound torches in the middle, and setting them on fire let them go, so that they ran hither and thither. They straightway made their way into the fields of the Philistines, and when \[the fields\] were set ablaze, both the grain already gathered and the grain still standing in the stalk were burned, so much so that the flame also consumed their vineyards and olive groves.
