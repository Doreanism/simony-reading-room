## CHAPTER II.

### On Godliness and Ungodliness, and on the Presumption of Cain and Balaam.

Let no one consider it superfluous that we have drawn so extensively from the apostolic letters and inserted them here in continuous sequence, for they mark out various heresies — and above all that one which, in pursuit of shameful gain, deems godliness a means of profit. Now godliness here is not to be understood as what is commonly understood and improperly called mercy, but as what the Greeks call *eusebia* — that is, right worship — or rather *theusebia* — that is, the worship of God — according to which one who worships God or worships rightly is called *pius*, that is, devoted and religious toward God in right faith. So, on the contrary, *impius* is whoever, being ignorant of the true God — faithless or unbelieving — is everywhere given over to the spirits of error and enslaved to their pleasure. For faith is the doorkeeper of godliness, without which neither is God pleased, nor is God appeased, nor is he worshiped. Hence, keeping in mind the proper meaning of this word, all of us who are believers truthfully and wholesomely acknowledge ourselves to be sinners while we are on pilgrimage from the Lord, yet we utterly shrink from and avoid calling ourselves ungodly in any sense. And from this it follows that every ungodly man is also a sinner, but not every sinner is also ungodly — for any believer, so long as he lives here, is not without sin, yet is nonetheless without ungodliness, that is, without unfaith. But the unbeliever is without neither sin nor ungodliness — such are the ungodly Jews and pagans, of whom the Lord says in the Gospel: “Whoever does not believe is already judged” \[John 3:18\], just as the prince of this world is already judged. Hence according to the Psalmist: “The ungodly will not rise in the judgment” \[Ps 1:5\]. If, therefore, according to the apostle, those who deem godliness — that is, the worship of God — a means of gain are deprived of the truth, how much more are the simoniacs, who so thoroughly reckon godliness a means of gain that they hold for sale, for the sake of profit, all the offices of that very godliness — namely, the orders of divine worship — and are even confident that the Holy Spirit himself, the author of that same godliness, the gift of God who operates chiefly in those very ecclesiastical orders and sacraments, can be purchased with money?
