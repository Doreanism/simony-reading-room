For the priests received from the offerings the shoulder, that is the arm, and the breast, and the tongue, and the stomach, and those things described in Leviticus. He declares that he will throw all these away on account of their sins and hurl them in the faces of the priests, so that those who offer such things may be just like the things they offer. ‘And it shall carry you away with it,’ he says, ‘that is, the dung of your solemnities, so that your stinking faces may be confounded by the putrid mixture of dung.” To these proofs, from the words also of blessed Gregory — whose improper figures of speech the defenders of simoniacs take as proper — it is shown how many things are spoken improperly. For he says in his homily on the Gospel of Epiphany: “All the elements bore witness that their Author had come. To speak of these things in the manner of human usage: the heavens recognized this one as God, because they immediately sent a star. The sea recognized him, because it offered itself to be trodden under his feet. The earth recognized him, because it trembled at his death. The sun recognized him, because it hid the rays of its light. The rocks and walls recognized him, because they were split at the hour of his death. Hell recognized him, because it gave back the dead whom it held.” And yet this one, whom all the insensible elements perceived as God, the hearts of the unbelieving Jews still refuse to acknowledge as God, and — harder than rocks — they are unwilling to be broken open in repentance, and they deny confessing the one whom the elements, as we said, proclaim as God by their signs and their splitting. Accordingly, the simoniacs and their obstinate defenders must confess that blessed Gregory here spoke improperly. Otherwise, if they maintain he spoke properly, they must profess that he was a Manichean, who believed and taught that insensible things possess not only a vital soul but a sensory soul as well. But if they fear to profess this, they must confess with us that neither the heavens, nor the sea, nor the earth, nor the sun, nor the rocks, nor the walls, nor hell, recognized or proclaimed their Author, nor did all those things that are said to have been done in his service truly occur — but that by a sudden and unprecedented transformation or shaking of themselves, he was recognized and proclaimed by human beings, according to the trope of metonymy \[*kata tropon metonomian*\], by which that which is made is usually signified by that which makes it; as man is called “dust,” and the sky “air.” By this same trope God is said to have been recognized when he brought it about that he was recognized by human beings. By this same trope blessed Gregory admits that spiritual grace, or the dove, was sold when it was thought to be sold. For he himself, as he asserts, spoke in the manner of human usage — as he declared that insensible things had sense perception, so likewise he declared that what cannot be sold was sold.

## CAPUT XXXII.

### On Criminal Catholics Promoted or Having Lapsed After Promotion, and on Their Ministry and Deposition.

And so, from these and very many other arguments, it is established that simoniacs have obtained nothing of ecclesiastical dignity — although their defenders shamelessly labor to persuade others that the honor received by them endures, unless the head of the Roman and Apostolic See or the metropolitan bishops of their province have cast them down by synodal action. For how can that which was never received endure in them? As for what they add — that the same consideration must apply to all crimes, so that if a priest commits adultery he should be deposed — they should know that this case must be understood and treated very differently. For heretics differ from Catholics so greatly that however criminal a Catholic may be, he is more acceptable to God and more tolerable to men than however just, if one can say so, a heretic may be. Hence whatever good a Catholic, even a criminal one, does or possesses is, beyond all doubt, good by virtue of faith — without which it would not exist.
