And though he sometimes offers certain things rightly, he nonetheless sins in that he does not rightly divide, and is deserving of Cain’s curse and sentence, to be cast away from the face of the earth, that is, from the Catholic Church. For indeed, by twisting and perverting the holy scriptures and the sound teachings of the holy fathers to bring about his own and many of Christ’s little ones’ ruin, he curtails some of them and extends others with words of his own. And finally, with his bow violently drawn, he has prepared his arrows in the quiver to shoot in the dark at the upright in heart. And what is still worse, puffed up and blinded by the arrogance of his learning or whatever continence he professes, he has a zeal for God, as it is thought, but not according to knowledge; and he supposes that he renders service to God when, like a cancer, he creeps with his pestilential words, infects and corrupts, and cuts away the sensibilities of simple brethren from the chastity that is in Christ. Having truly abandoned the fountain of living water, he digs for himself cisterns — broken cisterns that can hold no water — and he takes no care to draw living water from the wells that the sons of Isaac dug, but rather pours mud into them. And like that garrulous and wandering woman of Proverbs, restless and intemperate, foolish and clamorous and full of allurements, knowing absolutely nothing, unable to remain at home in her house, and in every way destitute of true bread, — now outside, now in the streets, now lying in wait at the corners — now she sits at the door of her house in a high place of the city, to call out to those passing by on their way: “Whoever is simple, let him turn in to me.” And she speaks to the foolish man: “Stolen waters are sweeter, and hidden bread is more pleasant.” But they do not know that giants are there, and that her guests are in the depths of hell. Against the brothel — nay, the wallowing-place — of this harlot, and against her persistent shamelessness and shameless persistence, there is nothing by which we might more aptly and cautiously deter and call back our little ones than by these invectives and rebukes of Jeremiah, who was sanctified in the womb: “Now what do you want on the road to Assyria, that you should drink the water of the river? What is it to you on the road to Egypt, that you should drink its turbid water?” And by such dissuasions of the vessel of election, the blessed Paul: “You cannot be partakers of the Lord’s table and of the table of demons. You cannot drink the cup of the Lord and the cup of demons.” And: “Do not be unequally yoked with unbelievers. For what partnership has righteousness with iniquity, or what fellowship has light with darkness? What accord has Christ with Belial, or what part has a believer with an unbeliever? What agreement has the temple of God with idols? For you are the temple of the living God, as God says: ‘I will dwell in them and walk among them, and I will be their God, and they shall be my people. Therefore come out from among them, and be separate, says the Lord, and touch not what is unclean, and I will receive you, and I will be a father to you, and you shall be to me sons and daughters, says the Lord Almighty.’” Having therefore these promises, beloved, let us cleanse ourselves from every defilement of flesh and spirit, perfecting holiness in the fear of God. In these words of the Apostle, even if it is clearer than light that we are warned to be separated from unbelievers in marriage and carnal union, much more so in spiritual union; and not to touch what is unclean, so that we may present to our God a glorious Church, having no spot or wrinkle or any such thing; so that he himself may dwell in us and he himself receive us, perfecting our sanctification. Moreover, when you hear “unbelievers,” understand not only pagans but also heretics, whom the blessed Jerome and the entire chorus of holy doctors declare to be worse than pagans — because in pagans there is still hope of faith, but in heretics there is already the conflict of discord.
