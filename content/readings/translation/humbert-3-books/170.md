“What is that to us? See to it yourself” — as if to say: “What does that sin of yours concern us, who only bought what you sold? See to it yourself what you have done, for we who have already received and hold the one bought and handed over will not release him.” Do not the simoniacs daily use this very same reply whenever their sacrilegious ordination is condemned? “What does the crime of the simoniac heresy concern us? Let those see to it who sold and handed over spiritual grace to us; for what we have once received we hold, and we will not lay down our office.” Lest they suppose themselves exempt from the fate of such men — though those who have received the grace of ecclesiastical orders gratis, as it were, from buyers of grace, are far more tolerable — let them consider that the chief priests were unable to give Jesus, whom they had purchased from the crowd whose favor they had won for themselves, back to the crowd; but in accordance with what they had persuaded them to ask, they obtained from the judge the release of Barabbas, a seditious robber and murderer. So that miserable people, persuaded and seduced by the Pharisees, deserved to become Pharisee — that is, separated from God the Father and Teacher — and to become sons of their father or teacher, so that henceforth they would be brothers to those who were from the devil as their father or from his instruction. For this is what Barabbas, or Barrabbas, means. By this act they incurred so great a loss of their former faith that those who shortly before had gone out to meet the Lord with palms, had spread their garments and branches of trees in his way, and had cried out “Hosanna to the Son of David, blessed is he who comes in the name of the Lord, the King of Israel, Hosanna in the highest,” suddenly cried out for Barabbas. Whenever the judge sought to restore them to their senses and correct their error, nothing came of it; rather the tumult grew greater, their voices grew stronger, and they wanted nothing other than to cast Jesus away from themselves and to demand Barabbas, crying: “Not this man, but Barabbas”; and “Away with this man, release Barabbas for us”; and “Away with him, away with him, crucify him, crucify him”; and “His blood be upon us and upon our children” — and other things like these. By this conspiracy and sentence they so bound themselves and their posterity to the devil that scarcely a few from that innumerable multitude could be freed. For very few were those among them who believed, in comparison with those who did not believe and took no care to deliver themselves and their posterity from eternal death. Hence they also incurred, together with the death of the soul, a most shameful and bitter death of the body — as happened through Vespasian and Titus and through Aelius Hadrian. Concerning this people, it is also to be considered that however many of them were saved, they did not merit to be perfectly reconciled to God by baptism alone — which beyond all doubt grants remission of all sins — unless they had also first labored to appease him through repentance. For it was thus said to those stricken with compunction at the evident outpouring of the Holy Spirit and at the preaching of Peter: “Repent, and let each of you be baptized in the name of Jesus Christ for the remission of your sins, and you shall receive the gift of the Holy Spirit.” Nor could ignorance excuse them from the need to repent, even though Peter himself bore testimony to them concerning it in the following words: “And now, brothers, I know that you did this through ignorance, as did your rulers also. Repent therefore and be converted, so that your sins may be blotted out.” And what those sins chiefly were he had already shown in what preceded, saying: “The God of our fathers glorified his Son Jesus, whom you indeed betrayed and denied before the face of Pilate when he had judged him to be released. But you denied the Holy One and the Just, and asked that a murderer be given to you, and you killed the Author of life.” If then those are said to have denied Christ who, not believing or doubting that he was God, handed him over to the judge to be killed as a mere man, in exchange for a mere man — will it be doubted whether those deny the Holy Spirit who claim to believe him utterly God and Lord and yet sell him for gold? And how much more tolerable this denial would be if it were done even for a man’s sake — tolerable by as much as a man is more valuable than a stone.
