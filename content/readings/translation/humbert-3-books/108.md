What then do they give? Certainly what they have. But what do they have? Certainly the spirit of falsehood. How do we prove this? ## Chapter V.

But now let us return from here to the examination of the proposed chapter and show that blessed Leo, in that passage commemorating pseudo-bishops, laid down such rules not against simoniacs or any other heretics, but against certain Catholics. And indeed that he does not speak there of simoniacs or other heretics of any kind has been sufficiently demonstrated above; now, however, it remains to be shown that he speaks of pseudo-bishops who are nonetheless Catholics. Whoever is consecrated a bishop must, according to the decretal rules of the holy \[fathers\], first be elected by the clergy, then sought by the people, and finally consecrated by the co-provincial bishops together with the judgment of the metropolitan. For he cannot otherwise be called or considered a certain, established, or true bishop unless he has a definite clergy and people over whom he presides, and has been consecrated by his co-provincial bishops by the authority of the metropolitan, to whom the care of that province belongs in place of the apostolic see. Whoever has been consecrated without any one of these three requisites must be called and considered not a certain, established, or true bishop but a pseudo-bishop, and must not be counted among canonically planted or made bishops; for since a bishop is called a superintendent or overseer, over what clergy or what people does such a man superintend, who had the election of no clergy and no people for him to oversee, and was moreover lacking the authority of the metropolitan and the co-provincial bishops? As for what follows in the proposed chapter — “hence, since a question often arises concerning an honor wrongly accepted, who would doubt that what is shown not to have been conferred ought in no way to be attributed to these men” — it is to be understood thus: the episcopal honor, that is, the consecration, was indeed received, but wrongly — not because it was received through money or from simoniacs (for in that manner no consecration could precede or be had, as is shown sufficiently above), but because it was \[conferred\] without clergy and people and the authority of the metropolitan; and therefore there should be no doubt that bishops of this kind are unworthy to have attributed to them what is shown not to have been conferred on them when they were consecrated — namely, a certain and proper parish and the authority to ordain their own proper clergy — or, as is read in the African council concerning presbyters ordained without a specific title, they may minister or ordain nowhere, to the injury of those who ordained such men non-canonically.
