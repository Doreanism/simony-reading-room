Let us attend to deeds, not the clamor of the tongue; let us ask why Christ came in the flesh, and we shall find who denies that he came in the flesh. For if you attend to tongues, you will hear many heresies confessing that Christ came in the flesh, but the truth refutes them. Why did Christ come in the flesh? Because it was necessary to bring us the hope of resurrection. God therefore came in the flesh so that he might die for us. But in what manner did he die for us? “Greater love than this no one has, than that someone lay down his life for his friends.” Love therefore brought him to the flesh. Whoever therefore does not have love denies that Christ has come in the flesh. Now I ask all heretics again: Did Christ come in the flesh? He came; this I believe, this I profess. But in fact you deny it. How do I deny it? You hear me saying this? No — I prove that you deny it. You say it with your voice; you deny it in your heart. You say it in words; you deny it in deeds. How, you ask, do I deny it in deeds? Because Christ came in the flesh precisely so that he might die for us; he died for us precisely because he taught great love. You do not have love, because for love of yourself you divide unity. Understand from this the spirit that is of God. Knock, tap the clay vessels, lest they have cracked and sound hollow. See if they ring whole; see if love is there. You tear yourself away from the unity of the whole world; you divide the church from its unity; you rend the body of Christ. He came in the flesh to gather; you cry out in order to scatter. Therefore that is the Spirit of God which says Jesus Christ came in the flesh — which says it not with the tongue but with deeds; which says it not by making a sound but by loving. That, however, is not the Spirit of God which denies that Jesus Christ came in the flesh. It too denies — not with the tongue but with its life; not in words but in deeds. It is manifest, then, brothers, by what means we may know. Many are inside, as though they were inside; but no one is outside unless they are truly outside. So that you may understand that he has referred it to deeds: “And every spirit,” he says, “that dissolves Jesus who came in the flesh is not of God.” By “dissolves through deeds” you are shown what he means — what does he show you, who denies, since he said “dissolves”? He came to gather; you come to dissolve. You wish to separate the members of Christ; how do you not deny that Christ has come in the flesh — you who, for your own honor, rend the Church of God which he gathered? You come against Christ; you are an antichrist. Whether you are within or without, you are an antichrist. But when you are within, you hide; when you are without, you are manifest. You dissolve Jesus and deny that God has come in the flesh; you are not of God. He therefore dissolves who does not do what he teaches us — what does this show us, except that we should question deeds and not believe \[mere\] words? All deny that Jesus has come in the flesh who violate love. For Jesus had no need to come in the flesh except for love. How could the Son of God lay down his life for us unless he were clothed in flesh in which he could die? Therefore, whoever violates love — whatever his tongue may say — his very life denies that Christ has come in the flesh; and such a person is an antichrist, wherever he is, wherever he enters. “We are of God. Whoever knows God hears us; whoever does not know God does not hear us. By this the spirit of truth and the spirit of error are known.” Now then he has made us attentive: he who knows God is the one who hears; he who does not know \[God\] does not hear, and this is the discernment of the spirits of truth and of error. Let us see what he is about to recommend, in what matter we should hear him. “Beloved, let us love one another, for love is of God.” And so on. Again from the same author and source: The violators of love made a schism; as they hate love itself, so they hate the dove. But the dove refutes them; it comes forth from heaven, the heavens are opened, and it rests upon the head of the Lord. Why this? So that he might hear: “This is he who baptizes.” Withdraw, you robbers; withdraw, you invaders of Christ’s possession. Upon your possessions, where you wished to hold dominion, you have dared to affix the titles of the powerful one. He himself knows his own titles; he reclaims his own possession — he does not erase the titles, but enters and possesses. So when someone comes to the catholic \[church\], baptism is not erased, lest the emperor’s title be erased; rather, the title is recognized for what it is in the catholic \[church\].
