There are certain divine precepts which, if someone transgresses them out of piety, he incurs not only no guilt but earns great reward — as did that glorious transgressor Paul, who, though he had a precept from the Lord (as did his fellow apostles) to live from the gospel while preaching it, used none of these arrangements and instead lived by the labor of his own hands, so as to give no occasion of offense to the gospel of Christ. There are also some precepts which, if followed continuously and never set aside for a time, lead to no small deviation, as with the matter of the purse and the knapsack — whether to leave them behind or take them, depending on the time of peace or persecution. For he who first said “Carry neither purse nor knapsack” later said “Now let him who has a purse take it, and likewise a knapsack” (Luke 22). When such dominical precepts are set aside with discernment, the one who thus sets them aside is rightly regarded as blessed. There are also others which trouble even the perfect by their excessive subtlety — such as “Do not covet,” and the strict prohibition not only of contemptuous speech but even of idle words; when these are transgressed, a person’s faith is not wounded, but the holiness of his life is diminished, though it can be restored and improved when, by not following those concupiscences and by harshly judging himself for his words, he daily professes before God his Father: “Forgive us our debts as we also forgive our debtors.” There are yet others which, though they invite the less perfect to perfection, nonetheless weigh upon their will by necessity — as: “If you wish to be perfect, go, sell all that you have” (Matthew 19), and: “Let him accept who can accept” (Matthew 19). These four categories of divine precepts belong to the spiritual and truly catholic, who, though they do not hear all God’s commandments in such a way as to carry them out, nevertheless have their sound faith made bright by right conduct. There remains a fifth category, which guides the faithful who are carnal and uneducated and restrains them from vices and crimes — such as the Decalogue and similar things: “You shall love the Lord your God with your whole heart,” and “Honor your father and mother, you shall not kill, you shall not commit adultery.” If such persons turn from evil but do not do good, they are indeed faithful, but their faith is idle. But if they do not even turn from evil, and moreover ruin themselves with criminal sins, then their faith is reckoned as a dead tree — though not uprooted, since it somehow still lives through the hope by which they hope for forgiveness, and by their love, however slight, of God, from whom they hope for that forgiveness, and of their neighbor. Finally, as the apostle says: “Faith is the substance of things hoped for” (Hebrews 11). From this it is clear that faith is the substance of hope. But where there is hope, there is also love, because one must necessarily love the goods one hopes for. And so the saying of blessed Gregory is established: that each person hopes and loves as much as he believes. Furthermore, hope is properly said to be only of good things, to such a degree that grammarians treat it as a fault — called in Greek acyrologia, or in Latin improper speech — whenever it is used otherwise, as in the poet’s line: “If I could have hoped for such great grief.” Hence they explain: “he said ‘hoped’ in place of ‘feared.’” Preserving the proper meaning of the word, another says: “It is permitted to hope while fearing.” Therefore, although faith is of good things and evil things alike, hope is only of good things — because we believe both the good things God promises and the evil things he threatens, yet we only hope for the good things and only fear the evil things. And although blessed James says “You believe that God is one; you do well; the demons also believe and tremble” (James 2), yet the belief of wicked men differs from the belief of demons in this respect: wicked men believe and hope; demons indeed believe but do not hope — rather, they despair, and therefore tremble.
