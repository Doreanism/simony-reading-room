## CAPUT XXXI.

### On the proper meanings of the words in the above-quoted prophet and in blessed Gregory

We have inserted these words of the prophet here in order, because even if all other arguments were lacking, these alone would suffice for the confutation of heretics and especially for the rebuttals of the simoniacs’ objections. To pass over the rest of the foregoing prophecy, let us touch briefly on the one point that is of value to the present work — namely, the wall built by the people, which the false prophets smear with mortar without straw. For in many regions, especially in the East, bricks are made by mixing straw into the clay, which binds the material together so that the heat cannot later dissolve what the moisture had congealed. Hence the straw is there called the “tempering” \[*temperatura*\], because it tempers the dissolvable clay lest it crumble. The people therefore builds itself into a wall when it strives to become the Church of Christ. But since it is smeared by false prophets — that is, by heretics — with the clay of visible sacraments without the tempering of invisible grace, it is about to fall. This wall is precisely said to be no wall, and the plaster no plaster, and the prophets no prophets, and the souls made alive \[to be\] not living, and the souls put to death \[to be\] not dying. There too is foretold the punishment of those who grieve the heart of the righteous whom God has not saddened, and who strengthen the hands of the wicked lest he turn from his evil way and live. Moreover, that the things pertaining to visible sacraments found among heretics are not only detrimental but even refuse is shown by the words of the apostle to the Philippians, where he proclaims how highly he had previously valued, and how highly he now values, the sacrifices and ceremonies which heretics of Judaism were observing after the passion of Christ: “As to the righteousness which is in the law, I was blameless; but what was gain to me, these I have counted as loss for Christ’s sake. Indeed I even count all things as loss because of the surpassing worth of knowing Jesus Christ my Lord, for whose sake I have suffered the loss of all things and count them as refuse, that I may gain Christ.” The Lord too, through Malachi, rejecting the Jewish priesthood, says: “Behold, I will throw to you the arm and scatter dung over your faces from the dung of your solemn feasts, and you shall be taken away with it.” This blessed Jerome expounds, adding: “Because he is speaking to priests: ‘I will throw,’ he says, ‘your faces’ — that is, those things which you consider the holier things in the law and which have been granted to you by God as a reward for virtuous acts from the sacrificial offerings. For from the offerings the priests received the shoulder — that is, the arm — and the brisket and the tongue and the stomach and those things described in Leviticus. All these things, on account of their sins, he protests he will cast away and throw into the faces of the priests, so that those who offer such things may be of the same quality as the things which are offered.” \[He adds:\] “’And he shall take you away with him’ — that is, with the dung of your solemn feasts — so that your fetid faces may be confounded by the putrid mixture of dung.” To this, from the words also of blessed Gregory — whose improper expressions the defenders of simoniacs take as proper — it is shown how many things are said improperly. For he says in his homily on the gospel of Epiphany: “All the elements bore witness that their creator had come. To speak of them in terms of human usage: the heavens recognized him as God, because they immediately sent a star. The sea recognized him, because it offered itself walkable beneath his feet. The earth recognized him, because it trembled at his death. The sun recognized him, because it hid the rays of its light. The rocks and walls recognized him, because they were split at the time of his death. Hell recognized him, because it gave back the dead it was holding.” And yet him whom all these insensible elements sensed as God, the hearts of the faithless Jews still do not in the least recognize as God, and — harder than rocks — they refuse to be split for repentance and deny confessing him whom the elements, as we said, proclaim as God by signs or splittings. Therefore simoniacs and their obstinate defenders must admit that blessed Gregory here spoke improperly. For if they contend that he spoke properly, they must confess that he was a Manichean who believed and taught that insensible things possess not only a vital soul but even a sensitive soul. But if they are afraid to confess this, they must in the end confess along with us that the heavens, the sea, the earth, the sun, the rocks, the walls, and hell neither recognized nor proclaimed their creator, nor performed all those things which are said to have been performed in obedience to him — but that by a sudden and unusual transformation or commotion of theirs he was recognized and proclaimed by men *kata tropon metonomian* \[by the trope of metonymy\], through which what is accomplished is customarily indicated by that by which it is accomplished — as a man is called dust, and the sky is called air. By this same trope God is said to have known something when he brings it about that it is known by men. By this trope blessed Gregory admits that spiritual grace or the dove was sold, when it is thought to be sold. For he, as he himself asserts, spoke in the manner of human usage — just as he said that insensible things perceived, so also he said that what cannot be sold was sold.
