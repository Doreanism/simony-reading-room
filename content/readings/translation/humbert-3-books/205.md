## CAPUT VI.

### On the staffs and rings conferred by the hands of secular powers.

Since all these venerable and supreme pontiffs have thus decreed, by the dictation of the Holy Spirit, that the election \[of bishops\] be confirmed by the judgment of the metropolitan and the desire of the clergy confirmed by the assent of the prince, and the request of the people and order \[confirmed\] by the same — in contempt of the sacred canons and in the trampling down of all Christian religion, everything is done in reverse order, and the first are last and the last are first. For it is the secular power that is first in electing and confirming, and whether it will or not, the consent of the order, people, and clergy follows upon it, and the judgment of the metropolitan comes last. Wherefore those so promoted, as was argued above, are not to be counted among bishops, because their appointment hangs upside down, since what ought to have been done for them last has been done first, and by those who have no part in the matter at all. For what concern is it of laypeople to distribute the ecclesiastical sacraments and the episcopal or pastoral grace — namely the cambered staffs and rings, in which the whole episcopal consecration is chiefly accomplished, exercised, and grounded? Indeed in the cambered staffs, curved and bent at the top to attract and invite, but sharpened and armed at the bottom to repel and strike, is signified the pastoral care entrusted to bishops; and their very shape and construction admonishes pastors to be upright and level, and to lower and bend gently the lofty and rigid summit of their action and contemplation in order to invite and attract the flock of God toward themselves, yet in such a way that they also always attend to themselves and never turn the gaze of their mind away from consideration of their own \[souls\]. The tip of the staff signifies that they should terrify the undisciplined with stern rebuke, and if these prove obstinate, repel them from the Church by the ultimate sentence. All of this the apostle \[Paul\] briefly implies when he says: “We beseech you, rebuke the unruly, comfort the fainthearted, support the weak, be patient toward all” \[1 Thessalonians 5:14\]. Furthermore, the ring signifies the seal of heavenly secrets, admonishing preachers to unseal and speak, with the apostle, the hidden wisdom of God among the perfect, while keeping it sealed, as it were, from the imperfect, who still have need not of solid food but of milk alone — or else to display and commend without ceasing, as friends of the bridegroom, the pledge of faith to his bride, who is the Church. Whoever, therefore, initiates anyone with these two things unquestionably claims for himself by that presumption the entire pastoral authority. For after these inaugural gifts, what free judgment about such rulers already installed can the clergy, people, and order, or the metropolitan who is to consecrate them, possibly retain — unless they simply acquiesce? Thus one already inaugurated by \[a secular lord\] invades the clergy, people, and order with violence, in order to domineer over them before he is known, sought, or requested by them. Thus he approaches the metropolitan, no longer to be judged by him but to judge him; for he no longer seeks or receives the metropolitan’s judgment, but only demands and extorts the service that is all that remains to the metropolitan — namely his prayer and anointing. For what further relevance or benefit is there to him in rendering up the staff and ring that he carries? Could it be because they were given by a lay person? But even baptism administered by a layman is not to be repeated, only supplemented by the priest’s prayer and anointing if \[the recipient\] survives; without which supplements, if one does not survive, the kingdom of heaven is without doubt entered, since no one \[enters\] without the washing of water. Whence it is manifest that the entire episcopal office was given to them in the staff and ring, without whose initiation and authority they cannot exercise episcopal functions, since without visible anointing this was conceded to the holy apostles in the sole reception of pastoral care, which is visibly signified and conferred by the staff and ring. I ask, then, why is \[the staff and ring\] surrendered when one already has it — unless it is either that the ecclesiastical office may once again be sold under the guise of this command or gift, or that the metropolitan and his suffragans may subscribe to confirm the prior sale, or at least that the presumption of lay ordination may be concealed under the color and veil of some clerical discipline? If neither of these has been done or is being done, let someone prove me wrong on this point.
