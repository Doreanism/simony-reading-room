Otherwise the Council of Nicaea is convicted not of having cut off error but of having propagated it, and the holy fathers who followed its authority — imposing hands upon whatever heretics came over to the Catholic Church — would have made the bad worse, both in this life and in the next. Hence that slanderer, if he is not blind on both sides, can now clearly recognize that neither heretics’ laity ought to be received into communion nor their clergy into the ranks of the Catholic clergy without the imposition of a Catholic priest’s hand after the one they are known to have received from their own people. For if they were laypersons, they are confirmed in the faith through the laying on of hands and the anointing of chrism; if clergy, they remain none the less in the order they had previously appeared to hold, through the laying on of hands. Hence it is clear that the second laying on of hands is valid and absolutely necessary — provided, however, it is performed by Catholics after \[an ordination by\] heretics, and not after \[one by\] Catholics. Yet this laying on of hands, if one considers the matter carefully, is rightly called not a second or additional one but simply the correct one. For the blessed Pope Innocentius declares that the laying on of hands by heretics is corrupt \[*prava*\], while that of Catholics is correct \[*recta*\]. If, then, the heretics’ laying on of hands is corrupt and the Catholics’ correct, it is plain that the corrupt one is only abusively called “the first” by a certain vain resemblance to the correct one, while the correct one is equally called “the second” only abusively — since they bear no substantial or accidental relation to each other in any reckoning. For if what is abusively called the second were equally corrupt or more corrupt, it would not be called “second” abusively but truly. Since, however, the one is corrupt and the other correct, it follows that in two things utterly contrary and dissimilar to each other one should not speak of a “first” and a “second,” but call each simply “one.” The same must be understood of ordinations, or, as detractors contend, “reordinations.” Finally, if a heretic lays hands on someone after \[another\] heretic, the reordination is corrupt or still more corrupt, and therefore not an ordination at all but a compounded damnation. But if a Catholic does so after a heretic, this ordination is held to be correct and singular, and therefore not a reordination but a unique purification. If, however, a Catholic presumes to repeat it after a Catholic — as is alleged — he confounds what is correct with what is corrupt without any ambiguity, and is therefore not to be said to have reordained but to have seduced or drawn both himself and the other from the correct to the corrupt. For this reason, with the apostolic canon cited above in view, both are to be judged — that is, both are to be deposed — because in thinking that a Catholic ordination should be repeated they incurred an equal danger. Since these things are so, the aforesaid rule of the fathers is thought to be in no way applicable to simoniacs alone — as if simoniacs were either not to be counted as heretics, or were certainly of such a kind that no spiritual grace could be conferred upon them by the Catholic Church, inasmuch as the fullness of the Holy Spirit obeys and attends upon them at will, whether they wish to bestow it freely or not.

## CAPUT IX.

But since Catholics cry out against heretics, while both sides profess themselves Catholic and each calls the other heretics — so that in these palpable darkness of this plague-ridden Egypt of our time it is not easy to discern who belongs to which spirit — setting aside everything about which there can at present be doubt as to whether they are to be held Catholic or heretic, let our pen be turned to only two \[groups\] from the most ancient times, whom no one doubts were certain and notorious heretics, condemned from the beginning by the holy fathers with unanimous judgment. The first were exposed and condemned by the holy apostles, the second by the venerable fathers of the holy Council of Nicaea and by all who have been or are defenders of the Catholic faith after them. We speak of Simon Magus with his Simonians, and Arius the Alexandrian priest with his Arians. Of these two, though the former is deemed worse by the holy fathers, yet since we have conceded above that they are equal in heresy, let us hurl and level against the liquid shield and armor of the faithless and resurgent Simon the weapons of the holy fathers by which the cunning armor of the mad Arius — who is no longer to be found anywhere — was attacked and overcome.
