From this it follows that those who seek sacred orders study not to correct their lives nor to reform their conduct, but to amass the riches by which the sacred office is bought. Hence it also comes about that the innocent and the poor are barred from sacred orders and recoil in contempt. And when the poverty of the innocent gives offense, there is no doubt that payment there commends wrongdoing, for where gold finds favor, so does vice. Hence, therefore, not only is a deadly wound inflicted on the souls of the ordainer and the ordained, but the kingdom of your Excellency is also burdened by the fault of the bishops, by whose intercessions it should rather have been supported. For if he is judged worthy of the priesthood whose claim rests not on the merit of his conduct but on abundance of payment, it follows that neither uprightness nor diligence has any weight in ecclesiastical office, but that the profane love of gold obtains everything. And when honor rewards vices, the one who perhaps deserved to be punished is brought to the place of punisher, and thus priests are shown not to advance but rather to perish. For when the shepherd is wounded, who will apply the cure to the sheep? Or when will he protect the people with the shield of prayer, who exposes himself to be struck by the enemy’s darts? Or what fruit can he produce, whose root is infected with a grievous plague? Greater therefore is the calamity to be feared in those places where such intercessors are brought to positions of governance who provoke God’s wrath against themselves rather than placating him before the people.

### Likewise, to Syagrius, bishop of Autun.

A report carried by rumor among us made it known that in the regions of Gaul sacred orders are conferred through the simoniac heresy, and we are seized by heavy grief and sorrow if money has any place in ecclesiastical offices and what is sacred becomes secular. Therefore, whoever strives to receive \[holy orders\] by paying a price desires not to be a priest but only to be called one, vainly. For what, pray, is accomplished by this, except that no proof of conduct is required, no concern for character, no examination of life, but only he who can pay the price is deemed worthy? From this, if weighed on the scales of right judgment, since he rushes improperly to seize for empty glory a place of humility, he is by that very act the more unworthy the more he seeks honor. Just as he who, when invited, declines, and when sought, withdraws, is to be brought to the sacred altars, so he who presses forward unbidden or thrusts himself in importunately is without doubt to be repelled. For he who thus strives to ascend to higher things — what does he do but grow outwardly while diminishing, and by ascending externally descend inwardly into the depths? And so, dearest brother, in ordaining priests let sincerity prevail; let the consent be simple and without venality. Let pure election be preferred, so that one advanced to the summit of the priesthood may be believed to have been elevated not by the votes of those who sell but by the judgment of God. For that it is altogether a grave crime either to purchase or to sell the gift of God, the authority of the Gospel is our witness. For our Lord and Redeemer entered the temple and overturned the chairs of those selling doves. What else is it to sell doves but to receive payment for the laying on of hands and to traffic in the Holy Spirit whom Almighty God grants to men? That their priesthood falls in the sight of God was clearly signified by the overturning of their chairs. And yet the wickedness of iniquity still thrusts forth its powers. For it compels those whom it has deceived into buying to sell in turn, and since no heed is paid to the divine command “Freely you have received, freely give,” the result is that the condition of both buyer and seller grows worse, redoubled in one and the same sin.
