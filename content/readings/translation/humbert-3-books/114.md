And first, by adducing the testimonies and rules of the Catholic fathers, let us show how highly they regarded the baptism of Arians and other heretics, and what they thought it to be. In the canons of the holy apostles — which our adversary here said above, on the authority of blessed Isidore, ought to be received, while we \[accept them\] rather on the authority of the blessed Pope Clement and his many successors — these two canons are found without any doubt.

*We ordain that a bishop or priest who receives the baptism of heretics be condemned. For what accord has Christ with Belial, or what part has the faithful with the unbeliever?*

The second follows of the same kind.

*A bishop or priest who presumes to rebaptize one who has true baptism, or who does not baptize one polluted by impious heretics, is to be deposed, as one who mocks the cross of the Lord and does not at all distinguish true priests from false ones.*

This rule, because its considerable subtlety offended and scandalized many, was removed by the successors of the apostles and wrapped in a certain accommodation: they allowed baptism — that is, immersion in water — under the invocation of the Trinity to be valid, and decreed that it was by no means to be repeated if it was established to have been performed in the name of the same Holy Trinity. In this matter the most blessed Leo also used this word “allow”: “We allow,” he says, “the baptism of heretics to be valid.” And the glorious martyr and bishop Cyprian — what he thought about the baptism of heretics can be clearly known from his own council. For he decreed against it the same things as the aforesaid apostolic canons did. But the later rule prevailed, because it reclaimed more people, removed disputes among the learned, and restrained the stubbornness of the unlearned populace. Moreover, what is permitted, even though it may be lawful, is not for that reason considered altogether good, as the blessed John Chrysostom says, discussing at greater length the proper meaning of this word in his commentary on Matthew: “Moses saw,” he says, “that the Jews were devoted to the practice of sacrifices out of carnal delight, and if he had commanded them to withdraw from sacrifices, he would not have been heeded, because a longstanding disease is not cured by a momentary remedy. He commanded them to sacrifice, but no longer to demons as in Egypt, but to the living God. He permitted half the evil, lest he lose all the good. For this reason he also permitted them to give a certificate of divorce, because he judged it better that under the permitting law marriages be dissolved than that hatred compel homicide. He permitted them to do the lesser evil, lest they do worse. Therefore by permitting this he did not show them God’s justice, but with the sin he took away the guilt of sinning, so that their sin would not seem to be sin when they acted in accordance with the law — just as if someone has a slave who steals and gets drunk, and the master, considering that if he commands him to observe both at once nothing will be gained, commands him to drink as much as he wants, but in public, so that he may gradually learn to obey commandments. So too divorce was permitted — an evil, yet lawful. For this reason the apostles also commanded second marriages on account of the incontinence of men. For to take a second wife is lawful according to the apostle’s instruction, but according to the truth of reason it is truly fornication. But when it is committed publicly and lawfully with God’s permission, it becomes an honorable fornication; therefore the Lord blessed it, because Moses permitted it and did not command it.
