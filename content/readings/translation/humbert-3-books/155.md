This makes clear that those to whom harmful things are given have asked for harmful things, and have asked nothing from the Father of lights — from whom every good gift and every perfect gift comes — nor have they asked in the name of Jesus; otherwise Christ himself, who promised otherwise, is contradicted: “Amen, amen I say to you, if you shall ask the Father anything in my name, he will give it to you” (John 16). And: “Whatever you shall ask the Father in my name, he will give it to you.” It is established, therefore, that these heretics receive what they ask, from him whom they ask. And whom do they ask? Their father and lord, mammon. And what do they ask for? Bread. What bread? Surely the kind that tends to taste sweet to them, as Solomon says: “Bread of deceit is sweet to a man” (Proverbs 20); and lest anyone doubt that it is a stone, he adds: “but afterwards his mouth shall be filled with gravel.” Do you see how here in place of the bread of charity, a stone of hardening is given — by whose dreadful effect it comes about that when they ask for the fish of faith, treachery is given to them like a serpent in its place? For where the bread of falsehood is eaten and the wine of iniquity is drunk, there is no doubt that treachery alone holds sway. Hence it comes about that, possessed by so pestilential a serpent, if they ask for the egg of hope, they deserve the scorpion of extreme despair. In them is fulfilled what the Wise Man says: “When the wicked man comes into the depths of evils, he despises” (Proverbs 18). These three principal evils, contrary to the three principal virtues of the soul, proceed from avarice alone. Of avarice’s wretched offspring someone once composed this couplet:

The miser is always in want, for nothing is more wicked than he;
The miser always loves lies, thefts, and plunderings.

He incurs such great poverty and misery because, asking for bread in faithlessness, he has earned a stone. Hence, having surrendered the fleshly heart in which the spirit of piety and mercy might rest, he finds in its place one of stone — insensibly hardened against every duty of charity and humanity. This heart the Lord neither gives to man nor makes, but in just judgment permits to come to be when, as he withdraws, it is subject to voluntary hardening: he does not soften it with his breath, but leaves it hardened by spiritual wickedness and to be hardened yet further. But if the spirit of charity were truly sought, he would freely bestow it, and by its warmth the cold of avarice would be dissolved. For if, as the Lord says, earthly fathers — though evil — know how to give good gifts to their children, how much more will the Father from heaven give the good Spirit to those who ask him. From this we understand that since evil men are generous when asked by their children, how much more and incomparably more generous is the supremely good Father to children who ask him; and just as good and evil are incomparable, so the fact that any heretics, and in particular simoniacs, do not receive the good Spirit is established because they do not ask the heavenly Father for the good Spirit. What then do they ask for, and from whom? They ask for an evil spirit from their evil father — that is, the author and inventor of all evil. But an objection is raised: Do not such people appear to call upon the heavenly Father and to ask the good Spirit for themselves or others? To these an apostolic answer is given: “How shall they call on him in whom they have not believed?” (Romans 10). And lest it be said that they truly and catholically believe in God, the text continues: “How shall they believe in him whom they have not heard?” And indeed, setting aside the daily failures of human frailty and sudden concupiscence, how do they believe in him whom they have, with long premeditation and deliberate purpose, transgressed by falling into the crime of this heresy, despising and refusing to hear him who says: “Freely you have received, freely give” (Matthew 10)? It is established, therefore, that they do not believe in him whom they do not hear when he decrees and establishes inviolable and perpetual privileges for his sacraments.
