Following their examples, we together with you \[the assembled bishops\] repel and condemn by apostolic authority all such presumptuous persons — plunderers of the Church and alienators of her goods — as excommunicated from the threshold of holy mother Church, and we judge them to be sacrilegious. Concerning such persons, the blessed pope and martyr Stephen also says: “We declare those persons to be infamous who are marked with infamy for any fault — that is, all who reject the rule of Christian law and despise the statutes of the Church, likewise thieves, sacrilegious persons, and all those ensnared in capital crimes,” and so forth. ## CAPUT V.

How the simoniac faction assaults the Church of God through its trafficking, and that nearly all laymen and clergy continually seek to profit from the Church.

By the decretal sentences of these blessed pontiffs and martyrs, subscribed with their blood, it is shown more clearly than the sun that invaders and plunderers of ecclesiastical property are infamous, thieves, sacrilegious, murderers too, excommunicated, and condemned to perpetual imprisonment or exile. Let the hypocrites go, then, and shamelessly contend that they have purchased not an ecclesiastical consecration but the properties of the Church. And lest they still labor to disguise themselves by asserting that they have not broken in, invaded, disturbed, or laid waste to ecclesiastical properties, let them learn that they do or have done all these things whenever they break through the immunity and privileges of those properties — confirmed by the holy fathers — with their money, and presume to claim for themselves the use of those properties that was owed to ecclesiastical persons and to the poor. For just as robbers assault the houses of others with their weapons, and thieves with ladders or other devices, and seize for themselves what belongs to another’s right either by force or by fraud, so too do these men assail and overcome the churches of God with their money as with their own weapons and machines, and claim for themselves what was owed to the ecclesiastical household. If some wicked man were to seize another’s bride by giving or promising money to her most villainous and avaricious guardian, along with all her household goods, would he not truly and justly be said to have assailed and conquered her along with all that was hers? The guilt of such presumption is punished under the sacred canons with excommunication until death for the ravisher, the guardian, and all their supporters alike, and by civil law with blood or perpetual exile. From this judgment we understand how abject and ignoble the bride of Christ is reckoned to be, when no one is found to grieve over her injuries, when no one defends her chastity — prostituted, sold, and given over to sacrilegious ravishers — no one who might at least snatch her away from them and restore her, even defiled, to her spouse. And it is supposed that Jesus will endure this forever — he whom no ecclesiastical or lay person deigns to regard or hear as he complains about the rape of his bride — which even the most abject of men would not bear with equanimity, since, if his complaint is spurned by judges and advocates, he avenges the rape of his bride by whatever means he can, even by fire. What, I ask, remains for the bride of Christ?
