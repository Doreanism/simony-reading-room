Indeed, in order to freely devour and destroy the people of God, simoniac promoters strive first to extinguish the Holy Spirit in them, or to make him captive and subject to themselves. Are they not seen to drag him bound when they presume they can sell his grace to whomever they will as if it were a captive? Are they not seen to disregard him as if extinguished by themselves, when through their perfidy they extinguish him in the sheep whom he was giving life? For the Holy Spirit is said to be extinguished when the faith over which he presides — which is his gift — is extinguished by the approach of perfidy. Hence the Apostle warns: “Extinguish not the Spirit; despise not prophecy.” These thieves plainly extinguish the Holy Spirit in the sheep when, through the offense of their impious communion or assent, they compel him to withdraw and flee from them; and once he is extinguished, all prophecies — that is, the holy scriptures — are scorned. For with what reverence will they now receive the pronouncements of others, who so stubbornly trample upon the very decretal sentence of the great Prophet and Lord of prophets, who, giving his assent to the institution of his sacraments, says: “Freely you have received, freely give”? Such persons, even if they appear to be within the Church, it is no wonder that they are also thieves — for thieves too, when they steal, appear to be within the sheepfold. They would certainly be less condemned if they stole from outside. Do not Jews and pagans sometimes appear within the material walls of the church? And yet they enter as they are and depart as they are; indeed they are worse while inside, by reason of their presumption and the dishonoring of the sacraments and the contamination of others. “For what fellowship hath justice with iniquity?” Yet are they held to be Christians as long as they appear to be within? So undoubtedly, simoniac promoters, while they appear to be within the Church, are then more to be repudiated and regarded as thieves. And they are finally to be believed not to be thieves and robbers only when it shall be proved that they have entered the sheepfold through the door and have not climbed in from elsewhere. Otherwise — not excepting them, nor any heretics whatsoever — all who have come by any way other than the door are, as the Lord testifies, thieves and robbers. Lest perhaps they should be thought in some respect useful like the hired hands, it is added: “The thief cometh not but for to steal, and to kill, and to destroy.” Where, then, are those who preach that what good men have received from wicked simoniac promoters is not only not harmful but even beneficial — with the Lord declaring in protest that thieves come only to steal, kill, and destroy? Hence those who have only these things in them and come for these things alone — what good can they give even to any good person, unless perhaps they first come to their senses, change themselves, and receive the good which they might give to others? Of such persons I will not speak of their deeds, but their very voice is so deadly that if the sheep hear it they cannot but perish. For as soon as they hear it they cease to be Christ’s sheep — the Lord himself bearing witness that “the sheep heard them not.” It is therefore plain that those who heard the thieves are no longer sheep of the good shepherd. And what are they? Seized, slaughtered by the thieves, and destroyed — and thus nothing now but victims of robbers. They would not have miserably succumbed to these evils if they had not heard their voices. For even if robbers sometimes prevail in breaking in upon the sheep, they cannot steal, slaughter, or destroy them unless the sheep first hear them; and thieves do not accomplish what they attempt so long as the sheep do not lend them their hearing. What destruction could simoniac promoters inflict on Catholics, if the sheep — forewarned by their good shepherd Christ — were to flee from them as from shepherds and withhold their favor and consent from such thieves? For this reason, the multiplication and boldness of thieves and robbers proceeds chiefly from the wretched docility of the sheep, who perish by a just judgment of God, because — warned and instructed in advance by two signs, as many witnesses — they either neglected or despised the need to beware of thieves. Indeed the good shepherd set before his sheep two signs by which to recognize thieves, so that if perhaps one of them came about without their knowledge or will, they might at least avoid the other. The first sign of thieves and robbers is proposed from the manner of entry into the sheepfold: if it is not through the door but from another way. The second is from the voice unknown to the sheep — which must now be briefly impressed upon us, since the matter of perverted entry is judged sufficiently impressed.
