For how shall he obtain the gift of Almighty God who despises having it by his bounty, and further strives to take it by storm and plunder with money laid down? For he wretchedly loses his mind who thinks that any violence can be done to Almighty God so that he would grant his grace to one who despises receiving it as his gift. To these arguments, those mentioned above, multiplying their already conceived blasphemy in defending it, add: “In another place also, the glorious Gregory, expounding the words of the Gospel, says among other things: ‘I speak in mourning to you priests, because we have learned that some of you perform ordinations with payment, selling spiritual grace and, to the loss of sin, accumulating temporal profits from the iniquities of others. And a little later: The dove is sold, because the laying on of hands, through which the Holy Spirit is received from heaven, is offered at a price.’” Now in these words of the blessed Gregory, if they accept what he says — that some sell spiritual grace — why do they disregard what he adds: that they accumulate temporal profits from the iniquities of others, to their own sinful loss? And how can spiritual grace be sold and bought in a transaction where another’s iniquity — namely the buyer’s — piles up temporal profit for the other, that is the seller, at the cost of sin? For what spiritual grace can be given and received where iniquity is shown to be at work on both sides, since, as the beloved of the Lord John affirms, “everyone who commits iniquity commits sin, and sin is iniquity”? Far be it, far be it, then, that the Holy Spirit should be considered any kind of mediator, or \[that any grace should be transmitted\] in such foul and wicked transactions where iniquity accompanies and dominates on both sides. The dove, therefore, is not sold, is not bought, but is thought or seen to be sold or bought by the sacrilegious and the foolish, whenever their wicked laying on of hands is offered at a price. Yet through it, whenever it occurs, the Holy Spirit is received from heaven by those for whom it pertains — those \[acting as\] traffickers, as we have described, the Spirit deigns to reward with no portion of his grace through any laying on of their hands, but rather equally execrates \[them\] from the sole damnation received in this manner.

## CHAPTER XV.

### On proper and improper expressions, and the vanity of simoniacs.

If, therefore, they had drunk in these and similar sayings of the orthodox fathers with a pious ear, and ruminated them with the mouth of a Catholic heart, they would at last proclaim with us that the Holy Spirit can neither be sold nor purchased. But neglecting or feigning to ignore their own damnation, they indeed accept the holy fathers speaking improperly and in ordinary usage, yet reject them when they expound themselves properly and according to rule. In ordinary or improper usage, the blessed Gregory speaks when he says that sacred orders are conferred by such persons; but he expounds himself regularly and properly — making clear what he wishes to be understood of those orders — when he inserts: “through the simoniac heresy,” for no spiritual grace is involved where wicked heresy is the mediator. So also in ordinary usage he speaks when he says that men obtain the Holy Spirit by means of payments, but he expounds himself according to rule — making clear what is to be understood — when he inserts: “whom they disdain to have as a divine gift.” For who can have the Holy Spirit, that is, God’s gift, without God? Nor is God one thing and his gift, that is the Holy Spirit, another — although he may deservedly be called, and is, another \[Person\]. This mode of expression is found so frequently in divine and human writings, as well as in our everyday conversations, that ancient grammarians assigned it a proper figure of speech, called *catachresis*.
