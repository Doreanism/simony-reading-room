## CAPUT XII.

### That Heretics Are to Be Dreaded More Than Vipers, and on the Nature and Typological Significance of Vipers.

Wherefore, if we shudder at vipers who, led by the laws of nature, contract no guilt from their ferocity, how greatly must heretics be abhorred, who, driven solely by the breath of demons, shamelessly commit whatever vices and crimes lie scattered everywhere? Not inappropriately do we take them for Chaldeans — that is, those who take captive — or, as it were, for demons themselves; for although by nature they are men, yet by taking the faithful captive through their vice they are demons. They are all the more carefully to be guarded against in that the most wicked of beasts injure not only with teeth but — what is more pitiable — also slay with their tongue countless hordes of men, whereas other beasts customarily assail what is set before them only with teeth, horns, or hooves: and these are so much the less to be dreaded in proportion as what they seek is external and their savagery is the more open. Those \[heretics\], by contrast, are all the more to be dreaded in proportion as what they seek is internal and their savagery is more hidden. Of them the Psalmist says: “The sons of men: their teeth are weapons and arrows, and their tongue a sharp sword” \[Psalm 57:5\]. How greatly this tongue prevails against the unwary is shown by blessed Job, saying: “The tongue of the viper slays him” \[Job 20:16\]. The viper’s very nature is so abhorrent to men that, though no one has firsthand knowledge of it, it can scarcely be heard about, when it happens to be recounted, without the mind being unsettled. For as naturalists \[*physiologi*\] assert: when a viper is about to conceive, it seeks its mate with open mouth; the male, in mad love, inserts its head into her mouth, which she, in still madder fashion, severs with a bite and swallows. Once the male has perished in this manner, the female conceives and, when the time of mature birth is at hand, is killed by her young in their haste to enter the world, who gnaw through her flanks, for nature has denied her the normal means of conceiving and giving birth common to all. Thus do these worst of beasts perish — the male while mating, the female while giving birth; the male at the female’s hand, the female at the hands of her young, who are themselves fated to perish not long after.

If this account from natural history is examined typologically — for nothing happens on earth without a cause — it fittingly applies to a soul goaded by its evil concupiscences and driven by the flesh, and thus desirous of fornicating away from God, its lawful spouse. To such a soul, as to a viper, its mate draws near — that is, the devil — who by reason and by apostasy, like an adulterer entering first, inserts into the mouth of her concupiscence, as it were, his head — that is, his suggestion or persuasion — which the miserable soul, taking excessive delight in it, takes from him and conceals within herself. Conceiving immediately from this, she gradually and stealthily fashions and nurtures within herself the seeds of perversity; which, once matured, cannot find natural and fitting channels or outlets in the perverted mind — for it is not the nature of the soul or of an angel to bring forth the works of vices — and so they kill their wretched mother, the soul. For when they sense that her nature is an obstacle to their birth, they are so enflamed that, gnawing through her vitals and flanks — that is, through her immortality and strength — they destroy their mother utterly, achieve their end, and burst forth. “Thus sin, when it is consummated, brings forth death” \[James 1:15\]. For “the soul that sins shall itself die” \[Ezekiel 18:4\]. But the soul dies in bringing forth; its seducer, the devil, dies in begetting, that is in urging evil; for the devil’s head is his persuasion, which, once received, becomes the seedbed and beginning of all sin. And lest anyone suppose that by suggesting evil one can avoid dying, let him consider what the apostle John says: “You know that no murderer has eternal life abiding in him” \[1 John 3:15\]. For the devil, that spiritual viper, dies as many times as he suggests evil to men. Since he was created immortal, though he always lives, he nevertheless always dies — both from his own sin incurred from the beginning, and from the malicious suggestion by which he always desires the perdition of souls.
