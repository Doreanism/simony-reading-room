Beyond these things let them attend to this: in the land of the Franks, where kings arise from a lineage, which king of the past hundred years and more is remembered as having reigned in his sons even to the fourth generation? Indeed the Ottos, who surpassed all kings before them in presuming upon priestly prerogatives, scarcely reached the third. After them, the first Henry \[left\] none at all. Anyone who investigates will find that the same has happened in other kingdoms and principalities. And yet with a marvelous blindness these princes imagine that they render service to God if, out of money or estates thus acquired, they build new churches or other pious places — when not only does this profit them nothing, but it greatly harms them as well, as is shown sufficiently above from the words of the blessed Gregory. And indeed what good does it do princes to destroy a thousand churches and build one, after the example of those princes we have named, who, from the spoils of many churches and especially of the provinces of poor Italy, constructed two bishoprics — so that they might at least be approved by human judgment when they are condemned by divine — and, as if they had done something great, handed them over in name only to the right of the apostolic see, while retaining for themselves and their successors all dominion over them? Surely these men, and whatever other imitators of theirs there may be, either deceive or are deceived, if they believe that such offerings or money can now please the Prince of the Apostles, contrary to his sentence against Simon Magus — as if he had repented of his earlier verdict. And why do they not consider that it is not their business to make churches of God, but to defend ones already made? For this purpose they receive the sword from Christ’s priests; for this they are anointed: to fight in defense of God’s churches and to wage war wherever it is needed — not to institute churches, nor to sanctify them or anything belonging to them, nor to presume upon such things. But if sheepfolds must be built somewhere for the rational sheep, let princes be devoted and vigorous helpers of those sheep’s pastors — and let them be built not from the milk and wool of other flocks, but only from those whose concern it is; for, as the wise man says, “he who builds his house at another’s expense is like one who gathers stones for himself in winter.” And certainly how highly is it regarded by God and by men of sound mind if someone, for the sake of almsgiving alone, robs or kills any rich or middling man so as to clothe the naked or revive the dying? And how much more praiseworthy would he be if he neglected the wretched rather than showing mercy to them in such a manner? Therefore, those who hold worldly power, lest they suffer mighty torments, should among other ecclesiastical matters take care not to invest anyone with ecclesiastical rings and staffs, knowing that this pertains not to their office but to priests. For they themselves, like household servants in the gospel parable, at the command of the father of the family bring forth the first robe for the returning prodigal son — that is, they catechize him; they clothe him — that is, they baptize him; they \[put\] the sandals of imitation of the earlier fathers on his feet; and all the more they place the ring of preaching or of ecclesiastical betrothal on his hand. For those who have no business baptizing and preaching have still less business giving the ring, by which the preaching and authority of the rulers of the church are most specially commended — it is also their business to bring the fatted calf and kill it, that is, to prepare the sacraments of the body and blood of Christ for his household about to feast. But if the princes of this age desire to provide for the reward of their souls in the defense and administration of ecclesiastical persons and their resources, let them support the preservation of priestly duties, residences, and expenses through priests wherever the ecclesiastical order is found intact — following the example of the most holy king David, who, in order that the priesthood might then be disposed intact before the Lord through appropriate and due offices according to the priestly lots, took pains and, from the spoils of the Lord’s enemies and from resources within his own right, provided and prepared for his son Solomon the expenses for building the temple of the Lord, as the end of the first book of Chronicles sets out more fully.
