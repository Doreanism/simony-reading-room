But it must be known that just as it is evil for men to sin by despairing, so too it is evil to defer repentance day after day by hoping beyond measure, heaping sins upon sins and weaving from them, as it were, a long rope by which, when bound hand and foot at the end, they are cast into the outer darkness. Such persons, as long as they neither despair nor defend their sins, we by no means condemn by final sentence from the paradise of the catholic church, though we sometimes remove them for a time from our communion for the sake of healing. And so it is established that such persons are still counted among the faithful — criminal though they may be — and are not rightly called impious; for “the impious man,” that is, the unbeliever, “when he has come into the depth of evils, despises” (Proverbs 18). Thus these too have not heeded the Lord forbidding crimes, and yet they do believe in him, as is clearly shown above; and the same must be said of those who sometimes fall in like manner from the summit of virtues to the depths of vices.

## CAPUT XVI.

### Where it is shown in opposition that heretics do not hear God even as criminals do within the church.

But lest the aforementioned heretics assail us with our own weapons while we are off guard — asserting that they too believe in God after the example of those we have mentioned, even though they do not hear him commanding: “Freely you have received, freely give” — let them know that our case is incomparably different from theirs. For our people fail to hear certain commands of Christ, as it were, either because they pass them over readily out of the grace of supreme perfection, or because they do not approach them, terrified by weakness of mind, or because they sometimes heedlessly transgress them led by human frailty, or because they succumb to them when overcome by diabolical temptation, or else they return from them. The first of these are not only to be praised and imitated but even beatified; the middle group are to be praised but also pitied; the last are to be pitied and reproved alike, yet, depending on time and circumstance, if they allow themselves to be corrected and amended, they are to be patiently tolerated and awaited. Even if they have apostatized from God by perpetrating wicked deeds, they are still with him and stand for him by their profession of faith, which they retain in heart and mouth. For though they depart from God in deed, they do not depart in faith, since they believe and confess him faithful in all his words and holy in all his works, even when they neglect to confirm that faith by their actions. They hear Christ the Lord when he speaks to move them to belief: “This is the work of God, that you believe in him whom he has sent” (John 6), and “You believe in God, believe also in me” (John 14); but they do not hear him when he speaks to move them to action: “Love your enemies, do good to those who hate you, and pray for those who persecute and slander you” (Matthew 5), and “Do not store up treasures for yourselves on earth” (Matthew 6), and many other similar precepts. Of those who live or flourish by faith and die or wither in their works, that prophetic word is fittingly spoken: “One part was rained upon, and the part on which it did not rain dried up” (Amos 4). They are green with the sap of faith, which they received by hearing it for belief and still retain; and they are dry through neglect of the good work, which they heard for doing but do not retain. To them, as regards faith, that sentence of the Lord rebuking the unbelieving Jews does not fully apply: “If I speak the truth, why do you not believe me? Whoever is of God hears the words of God; therefore you do not hear, because you are not of God” (John 8). For all carnal persons in the church believe that Christ speaks the truth and hear his words, even if not to their profit. Hence they both are of God, insofar as they hear the words of God, and are not of God, insofar as they do not hear. But heretics neither hear God nor his words in either way, because faith being neither admitted nor retained, not only do they do nothing good, but even if they appear to do something, it is all sin — namely, because it is without faith. For otherwise, if they do not lack catholic faith, which alone is faith, they are not heretics. But in the meantime, setting aside other groups, simoniacs have from the very first rudiments of Christianity been called and regarded as heretics. Therefore they are without faith; if they are without faith, they cannot have hope either, because faith is the substance of hope. If they have no hope, neither have they charity, since no one can love what he neither hopes for nor believes — as was demonstrated sufficiently just above.
