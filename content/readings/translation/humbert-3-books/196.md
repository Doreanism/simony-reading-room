And he said: ‘The synod has defined nothing new, O emperor. For thus I myself received it of old, from the times of the apostles — both the term of faith and the time of the paschal feast.’ Then when the emperor asked again, ‘Why then do you separate yourself from communion?’ he recounted what had happened under Decius during the time of persecution and brought forward the rigor of his most severe rule: that those who sin after baptism a sin which the divine scriptures pronounce to be unto death ought not to be made worthy of the communion of the saints, but should indeed be invited to repentance, yet must sustain the hope of remission not from priests but from God alone, who has the power to forgive sins.” But enough of this.

From here, wishing to provide both for the reader’s refreshment and the writer’s recreation, we have judged it necessary to fix the second boundary of this little work at this point.

HERE BEGIN THE CHAPTER HEADINGS OF THE THIRD BOOK.

I. Against those who say they have purchased not a consecration but the properties of the church.

II. Proof that possession without its proper right is of no avail.

III. On the sentence of the Council of Chalcedon against the aforesaid.

IIII. On the sanctification that sanctifies ecclesiastical things, followed by testimonies of the holy fathers on this matter.

V. How simoniacs through their trade assail the Church of God, and that nearly all laity and clergy seek continually to traffic in the church.

VI. On the staffs and rings given by the hands of secular powers.

VII. On the time of the Ottos and the zeal of Emperor Henry and the wickedness of the king of the Franks.

VIII. On the fourfold sale among the Latins and on the devotion of Constantine the Great toward the clergy.

VIIII. On the difference between clergy and laity and on the zeal of blessed Gregory in such matters.

X. How much freer the church of the Greeks is from the power of the laity than that of the Latins.

XI. How and by whom churches have been deprived of their privileges.

XII. On the presumptuousness of women and the dignity of consecrated things.

XIII. On the dignity of the priesthood under the old law, and on the presumptuousness and punishment of the kings of Judah and Israel.

XIIII. On the presumptuousness of Saul and his punishment.

XV. How great the scourges that afflict princes together with their peoples on account of presuming upon priestly office.

XVI. On the zeal of holy kings for divine worship, and the recompense, the sin, and the punishment of peoples.

XVII. On the merits of Israel and Judah as a type of heretics and Catholics.

XVIII. On the difference between the priesthood of Catholics and heretics, and on the first-fruits of the simoniacs.

XVIIII. How the priests themselves under the law avoided communion with the impious, and how they purified what had been contaminated by them.

XX. On the danger of priests and the undisciplined ambition of the clergy of our time.

XXI. On the chief evil of Italy, and on a certain vain bishop, and on the duty of priests, princes, and peoples.

XXII. On the harmful silence of priests and the talkativeness of flatterers.

XXIII. On the danger of priests who are wrongly silent and those who speak wrongly, and on the indivisible unity of the Godhead and its members.
