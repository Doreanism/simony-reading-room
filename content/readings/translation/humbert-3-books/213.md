For those to whom not even the little key of the doorkeeper — by which he locks and unlocks the doors of the holy temple — nor the rope of the bell, by which he marks the fixed hours of the divine office, is granted, to them it is far less granted to touch the staff and ring of a bishop, by which he who is adorned with these as his principal insignia consecrates to almighty God not the doorkeepers alone but the other ecclesiastical orders and the temple itself with all its furnishings, as well as the altar with all its utensils. By the same reasoning, to those who are not permitted to touch the girdle or the maniple of the subdeacon who is to carry the offerings to the altar, it is still less permitted to touch the bishop’s ring, with which he is adorned when he stands at the altar and sanctifies and immolates the Lamb of God, or when he consecrates the chrism of the Holy Spirit, or when he blesses the people of God. Nor is it any wonder that we should so greatly revere the truth of Christ in the New Testament, since the Lord decreed that even its shadow in the Old Testament should be reverenced and honored thus — so that in the book of Numbers he says through Moses: “The boards of the tabernacle and the bars, the pillars and their bases, and everything pertaining to worship of this kind — the pillars of the court with their bases all around, the pegs with their cords — shall be under the care of the Levites. The camp shall be pitched before the tabernacle of the covenant, that is, on the east side: Moses and Aaron with his sons, having charge of the sanctuary in the midst of the children of Israel. Whatever stranger approaches shall be put to death” \[Numbers 3:36–38\].

Then, to show how greatly the Lord wishes the vessels of the altar to be held in reverence, he says a little further on: “They shall cover the fire pans, the forks and three-pronged hooks, the flesh hooks and shovels, and all the vessels of the altar, with a covering of blue leather, and shall put its carrying poles in place. And when Aaron and his sons have finished covering the sanctuary and all the vessels of the sanctuary as the camp sets out, then the sons of Kohath shall come to carry them; but they shall not touch the holy things, lest they die” \[Numbers 4:12–15\]. And warning again of this peril, he says to Moses and Aaron: “Do not cut off the tribe of the families of the Kohathites from among the Levites; but do this for them, that they may live and not die, when they approach the most holy things: Aaron and his sons shall go in and assign each of them to his task and to his burden, but they themselves shall not go in to see the holy things even for a moment, lest they die” \[Numbers 4:18–20\].

This was what the Levite Uzzah incurred long afterward, when he attempted to support the ark of the Lord as it tilted, an act permitted only to priests. From these examples let them weigh what they deserve who, with mere brazenness, handle and carry the vestments and vessels of the ecclesiastical ministry — if those who were merely led by curiosity to look upon these things, or moved by sudden necessity to support them, were slain by God’s judgment. And those were Levites who had been consecrated, yet so great a chastisement restrained them: with how much greater severity, we may think, did this deter laymen? But lest anyone suppose these things were to be observed only by the people under the Law and not by us under grace, let him hear the Apostle saying: “Whatever was written was written for our instruction” \[Romans 15:4\]; and likewise blessed Pope Stephen decreeing similar things above; and our Lord himself in the Gospel impressing upon his people the perfection of greater righteousness, having prefaced it thus: “Unless your righteousness exceeds that of the scribes and Pharisees, you shall not enter the kingdom of heaven” \[Matthew 5:20\]. And indeed the Law does not punish with death mere anger, mockery, or verbal abuse directed at a brother, but only actual homicide; whereas grace punishes not only homicide itself but also its kindling and seed — namely anger and open insult — and with a death so much graver as the soul surpasses the body. By bodily death, which alone those under the Law felt, men were restrained from excess like frightened slaves; whereas we, like free persons, ought to work the righteousness of the eternal Father by love alone. If we cast off that righteousness — his discipline, as it were — and rebelliously resist his will in our disorder, we shall not only be disinherited but shall moreover be punished forever with the death of the soul, which, believing it to be imminent, we have disdained to flee. And yet in this too we differ from them, for to us as free persons the opportunity of satisfaction is reserved; to them, however, as slaves, the punishment of temporal death was applied without delay — a death which was beyond all doubt the shadow of eternal death.
