## CAPUT X.

### On the Difference in Excess Between Those Who Build on Christ’s Foundation and Those Who Build Without It.

Now because among Catholics as well, one who despises money is reckoned a rare bird — though there is a great difference between those who rightly seek money for necessary use alone and those who seek it even for superfluities — yet the merchants under discussion ought not to compare themselves in any respect with those \[Catholics\] who prefer to suffer temporal want rather than put the grace of God up for sale, trusting that “nothing is lacking to those who fear him.” If these \[Catholics\] sometimes go too far even in the affairs of earthly things, they can certainly appease the Lord quickly enough through almsgiving and other good works, because they preserve the foundation of the Catholic faith whole in their minds. By the merit of that foundation they are saved — yet so as through fire — even if they have built upon it wood, sometimes hay, and stubble. This is believed of all sinners as well, from whom that foundation is retained, provided they have in this life brought forth fruits worthy of penance. But the purchased slaves of mammon, his leaders and standard-bearers in wicked madness — those who not only offer their own souls for sale but even labor to sell the very creator and sanctifier of souls — what will they do? And whither shall those most deceitful kites flee from the face of the sword of that dove whom they think for sale, men who have deprived themselves of the foundation that is Christ Jesus — that is, faith — upon which he himself builds his Church as upon a firm rock? For neither by almsgiving nor by any other good works can they be helped, since all such things are entirely absent from them once that foundation itself is taken away; nay, whatever in others works salvation is reckoned to them as sin. Nor is it any wonder, since they seek neither their own salvation nor anyone else’s in the ecclesiastical offices they wrongly arrogate to themselves — which is proved by the fact that money alone rules them, money alone operates in all their actions: it is money that is petitioned, it is money that is sought from the seller, it is money that is knocked at, it is money that is put forward, it is money that is adored, it is money that is invoked, it is money that is given, it is money that is received, it is money that is held, it is money that is possessed. If money is removed from the midst, or when it is demanded but refused, it is without doubt clear that God is neither petitioned nor sought nor knocked at nor put forward nor adored nor invoked nor given nor received nor held nor possessed.

Thus, conversely: it is money that petitions, it is money that seeks, it is money that knocks; money is what the buyer puts forward, what he adores, what he invokes, what he gives; through money he receives what he receives, through money he holds what he holds, through money he possesses what he possesses. But what? Outward whitewashing and inward blackening — that is, perdition, damnation, malediction, sacrilege, lying, apostasy — these they give, receive, hold, and possess in common. Nay, they are possessed by as many demons as there are ways in which, by their wicked trafficking, they trip up and are tripped up. In particular they are possessed by the malignant mammon, whose chief usurers they are, and to whom their every intent and action is in service. For neither of them — seller or buyer — sets God before his eyes; on the contrary, each utterly sets God behind him, putting a coin before him. The result is that by setting behind them God who is first and last, the Ancient of Days whose kingdom has no end, they set before themselves a new god; and forsaking their own, they worship a foreign god — as the Psalmist speaks against them: “O Israel, if you would hear me, there shall be no new god among you, nor shall you worship a foreign god” \[Psalm 81:9–10\]. But because he was not heeded, he adds: “So I let them go according to the desires of their heart.” And shortly after: “The enemies of the Lord lied to him.” Of them he complains elsewhere: “But they thought to repel my price” — that is, my God and me. To what conclusion that thought has brought them he shows, adding: “With their mouth they blessed, but with their heart they cursed” \[Psalm 62:4\]. If therefore those who only thought to repel God from a holy man already curse with their heart, though they bless with their mouth — what, do you suppose, do those do who labor not by mere thought alone, but by word and deed, to repel the Lord from themselves and from others? Their oral blessing, whatever it may be, becomes before God an unspeakable curse, for God weighs men’s words and deeds solely by the intention of the heart; he does not consider what good they say, but what end they pursue in saying it.
