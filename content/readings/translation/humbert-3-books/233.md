It was he who made the apostles witnesses of Christ in Jerusalem and from there in every direction to the ends of the earth. He also visibly enkindled them and bestowed upon them the gift of all tongues and of his sevenfold grace. It was he who, working through the apostles — ordinary and unlettered men — withstood the chief priests and rulers of the Jews, so that when forbidden to speak they replied with constancy: “We must obey God rather than men,” and: “Whether it is right in the sight of God to listen to you rather than to God, you judge. For we cannot but speak of what we have heard and seen” — and they went away from the council rejoicing that they had been counted worthy to suffer dishonor for the name of Jesus. It was he who, when they prayed, shook the place where they were gathered and filled them so that they spoke the word of God with boldness. He performed many signs and wonders through the hands of the apostles. He made the multitude of believers one heart and one soul. He slew with the word of Peter those who had defrauded the community of money owed to it, Peter saying: “Ananias, why has Satan tempted your heart to lie to the Holy Spirit? You have not lied to men but to God.” And indeed it is fitting that you should tempt the Spirit of the Lord. In these words it is plainly declared that the money with which those communal members were supported in temporal sustenance — so that no one among them should be in want — belonged not to men but to the Holy Spirit. Those who are accused of lying to him and who promptly die are those who attempted to divert from that money what the Holy Spirit had consecrated to the support of his community members in their very vow of dedication, and because they sought fraudulently, while outwardly presenting a portion for the poor of Christ’s food and clothing, to retain something secretly for themselves. He condemned Simon Magus by the mouth of Simon Peter with an irrevocable condemnation, because he supposed that the grace of the Holy Spirit could be bought and sold. In this irrevocable sentence it is worth considering how much less grave was the guilt of Ananias in defrauding the community of his own transitory money than the guilt of the faithless magician in merely entertaining the thought of fraudulent traffic in eternal grace. Hence the former, tempting the Holy Spirit in defrauding him of an outward and visible thing which he had consecrated, is punished only by the death of the outer man; but the latter, having plotted fraud against the inner and invisible grace, is struck down by the death of the inner man. Thus, although both sinned against the Holy Spirit, yet in proportion to the measure of their guilt Ananias, punished by temporal death, is thought to have escaped the everlasting death, while Simon, condemned to everlasting death, merely deferred the temporal, since the blasphemy against the Holy Spirit was not to be forgiven him in this age or in the age to come. Ananias was not permitted to live in the flesh for a single moment, so that his death might serve as a hidden remedy for himself and a manifest example for the living; but Simon, dead in soul, is permitted to live for a while in the temporal realm, to the increase of his future torments and for the exercise of the good, even as he was removed, crushed and destined for crushing, from among men. In their punishment it must be observed, as blessed Prosper says: “Nothing is more wretched than the good fortune of sinners, since their impunity fosters guilt, and the evil will is strengthened as an inner enemy. For that which is neither punished nor cut away by temporal chastisement is reserved for eternal torment.”
