You are trampling on me.” You would not say “You are trampling on my head,” because the head was being honored; but the head would cry out more on behalf of the trampled members than on behalf of itself, because it was being honored. Does not the head itself cry out: “I do not want your honor — stop trampling on me now”? You then say, if you can: “Why did I trample on you?” Tell the head: “I wanted to kiss you, to embrace you.” But do you not see, O fool, that what you wish to embrace — through a certain bond of unity — extends down to what you are trampling? You honor me above; you trample on me below. The trampling pains me more than the honoring pleases me, because even he whom you honor grieves for those whom you trample. How does the tongue cry out: “It pains me”? It does not say “It pains my foot” but says “It pains me.” O tongue, who touched you, who provoked you, who pricked you? No one — but I am joined to those who are trampled. How do you wish me not to feel pain, since I am not separated from them? The head, about to ascend into heaven, saw that many would honor him because he ascended into heaven; and it saw that their honor was useless if they trampled his members on earth. Lest anyone err, adoring the head in heaven while trampling the feet on earth, he commended his members on earth; he said where his members were; he spoke his last words and departed. After those very words he spoke no more on earth. You will no longer find Christ speaking on earth; you find him speaking, but from heaven. And why from heaven itself? Because members were being trampled on earth. For to Saul the persecutor he said: “Saul, Saul, why do you persecute me?” I have ascended into heaven, yet I still lie on earth; here I sit at the right hand of the Father; there I still hunger and thirst and am a stranger. How then did he commend his body on earth as he was about to ascend? He answered his disciples: “You will receive the power of the Holy Spirit coming upon you, and you will be my witnesses in Jerusalem and in all Judea and Samaria and to the ends of the earth.” Behold where I lie, I who ascend — for I ascend because I am the head; but my body still lies. Where does it lie? Throughout the whole earth. Take care not to strike it; take care not to violate it; take care not to trample it. These are the last words of Christ as he was about to go into heaven. Brothers, consider with christian hearts: if for earthly heirs the last words of a father about to die are sweeter, more welcome, and of greater weight as he is about to be laid in the tomb — what ought the last words to be for the heirs of Christ, who is not about to enter a tomb but about to ascend into heaven? And if the last words of a dying father, whose corpse lies without sensation in the tomb, are the more carefully kept by his sons — what must they hope for themselves, those who do not keep the last words of one sitting in heaven, looking down from above to see whether they are contemned or not — of him who said: “Saul, Saul, why do you persecute me?” — who keeps for judgment whatever he sees his members suffering, for whom the sword of the tongue of heretics is heavier and more intolerable than the sword of iron of the pagans.

## Chapter XXX.

### Recapitulation of the foregoing words of Saint Augustine.

In the light of these clear judgments of the foremost doctor, it is necessary that transgressors of the new commandment return to their heart — all heretics and above all simoniacs, who take the power of the Holy Spirit they have received and direct it solely to secular business, not to any witness for Christ — Christ who, as he was about to ascend, affirmed that his disciples were to receive that very power of the Holy Spirit for this purpose alone: that they might be witnesses of his resurrection and of his love throughout the world. But those most perverse of men, turned like a twisted bow against him who bent them, attack and strike him when they presume to distribute for a price the riches of his grace — which they were commanded to dispense and give out freely to all, as they received them freely. Thus, ungrateful to Christ, they rise up against him to fight him with his own gifts, and they inflict upon his faithful ones, as members of Christ himself, the worst and incurable wounds — for they cannot reach Christ himself, who is the head of his own. Even if they sometimes pretend with their lips that they wish to honor him, all that they do is an insult to him, not reverence, so long as they with faithless perfidy destroy his faithful ones like thieves and robbers.
