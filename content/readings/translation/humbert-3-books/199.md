“Far be it from us to purchase an ecclesiastical consecration; we purchase only ecclesiastical property” — as if, treading in the inverted footsteps of the Pharisees, they were striving to maintain that only the sanctifier ought to be honored, while the sanctified things are nothing. Against these men, because in our foregoing narrative we labored with all our strength to defend the Sanctifier, let us now undertake, with the same Holy Spirit as our guide, to go and defend the sanctified things. And first, let us sift out the nature of that argument by which a party of the aforesaid traffickers tries to cover and defend itself, while at the same time establishing and confirming their heresy — asserting, that is, that they have purchased by price not the invisible grace of the Holy Spirit but only the visible properties of the churches, because it happened that they received their consecration from a metropolitan for nothing. They would not make such an assertion if they possessed even a spark of any prudence or the face of any shame. But because they neither understand nor blush to make excuses for their sins, they stitch together from the leaves of the fig tree, which the Lord cursed, brief and flimsy loincloths like the first parents who sinned, and place them before their shameful parts — they who are to be cast out of the paradise of God under anathema. And because this cloak of their lying is short, it cannot cover both ends at once: for if they try to go about with their head covered by it, they immediately bare their feet; and if they cover their feet, they at once uncover the head. For their action or intention is stripped bare either at the beginning or at the end. If they wished to purchase only the patrimony or the revenues of the church, why is it that immediately after the sale and purchase — even before being consecrated — they invade the bishop’s seat and his authority? Why do they leap from a lower to a higher rank? Why do they pronounce episcopal judgment and counsel over offenders? By what obligation do they demand that consecration be given canonically to those elected to the episcopate — unless it is because they hold onto the ecclesiastical property they first bought, and trust that no one will occupy the church over them? And if they purchase ecclesiastical resources only in order to use them, why are they not content with their use but impudently demand on top of it the blessing which they say they did not purchase? But perhaps — though I do not think so — they will say that when they bought the ecclesiastical estates they did not know that for this reason they would also have to be consecrated, but thought they would possess what they had bought as laymen. Let them then show to everyone that they did not know this at the time, and let them cease demanding the episcopal grace which, in paying a price for ecclesiastical properties, they were neither thinking of nor demanding. And although it is with sacrilege that they hold the things they alone have purchased, let them not on that account presume to the rank properly owed to bishops, but let them, as laymen, render to their own bishops everything that belongs to bishops — not only from benefices but from their estates as well. Let them leave others to receive consecration, episcopal authority, and the care of souls, which are beyond doubt spiritual and \[pertaining to\] invisible grace. But they recognize — as surely they knew before they began to traffic — that the properties of the churches belong to bishops, and that they themselves cannot retain those properties except under the guise and title of a bishop; and so they demand the consecration, which they had previously bought as one of the church’s assets, as though it were theirs by right. When a layman buys fields or ponds, does he not simultaneously purchase whatever will come to him from them gratis? Does he not also purchase the very cultivation by which he works the property?

## CHAPTER II.

### Proof that possession is of no benefit without its privilege.

For if he does not purchase the cultivation itself — indeed the license to hold and to work the land — what advantage does the possession he has purchased afford him? It seems he wished to do nothing other than to suffer the loss of his money, and moreover to have purchased harsh injuries and violence against his person and his people, whenever he presumes to exercise the cultivation he did not purchase along with the purchased estate. From this it is established in every way that none of the powerful — indeed none of mankind — purchases in visible things anything but what is not seen. To take an example: someone purchases numerous cities, provinces, kingdoms. What has he purchased in these, if not the license to hold and to rule?
