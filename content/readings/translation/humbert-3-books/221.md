For a simoniac can do nothing other than transfer his fellow countrymen — that is, Christians — to pagan custom, which is certainly a wicked and unheard-of crime. Hence the same Jason is condemned not as a priest but as an impious man, by which name he is properly shown to be a pagan. Consider at the same time that in this first conspiracy of the simoniacs — namely of Antiochus, Jason, and Menelaus, who was born of the tribe of Benjamin and had wrested the high priesthood from the aforesaid Jason by money — no fewer than eighty Jews were slaughtered in Jerusalem and forty-two were sold into bondage. Then too, with Menelaus as guide, Antiochus, like antichrist led by simoniac leaders, entered the temple — which together with its sacraments prefigured the Catholic Church and its sacraments — and took the golden altar, the lampstand with its lights and all its vessels, the table of showbread, the libation cups, the bowls, the golden mortars, the veil, the crowns, and the golden ornament that hung before the face of the temple; and he smashed all these things, ordering the temple to be defiled and renamed in honor of Jupiter Olympius. And he took the silver and gold and the desirable vessels and the hidden treasures that he found, and having carried off everything he departed to his own land. Each of those ornaments of the temple prefigured the sacraments of the Church, as the many writings of orthodox fathers attest. Among these, the golden ornament that was on the face of the temple openly signified the title of faith by which we are known to be God’s and through which entry is made to God. This is foreshadowed as having been smashed and taken captive by heretics following antichrist, in the smashing and captivity of that golden ornament; for with it captive, the Lord repels his tabernacle where he dwelt among men, and delivers their strength into captivity and their beauty into the hands of the enemy, and shuts up his people with the sword and despises his inheritance. Fire devours their young men, and their virgins are not lamented. Their priests fall by the sword, and their widows are not mourned. Why is this? Because, according to the lamenting Jeremiah, the Lord has rejected his tabernacle and cursed his sanctuary. Hence even the temple itself is no longer called the Lord’s but that of Jupiter Olympius, to indicate that whatever of visible sacraments was accustomed to belong to the Lord in it has yielded to the prince of the power of the air. But Jason, the author of so great a crime, expelled from the priesthood by Menelaus, fled from city to city, hated by all, as an outcast from the laws and an execrable fugitive, as an enemy of his country and his fellow-citizens, and he who had driven many from their homeland perished in a foreign land; and he who had cast out many unburied was himself cast aside unmourned and unburied, having the use neither of a foreign burial nor of his ancestral tomb. His supplanter Menelaus, after the murder of the most holy priest Onias and of many other saints, was himself supplanted by his brother Lysimachus, who was killed not long afterward by a popular uprising near the temple treasury. Scripture establishes him as sacrilegious not only because he obtained the priesthood by money, but because beyond that he smuggled out sacred vessels and the furnishings of the sanctuary by theft — a crime that no one doubts belongs more specifically to simoniacs. For they press forward not only to purchase the priesthood, but plunder and steal whatever might remain to the churches of God. Nor did the seller escape unpunished, he who, struck by an incurable and invisible plague, consumed by worms and filled with a stench intolerable to his army and to himself, died a miserable death in the mountains in a foreign land — fittingly a type of him whom the Lord Jesus will slay in the holy mountain with the breath of his mouth, according to the prophecy of Daniel.
