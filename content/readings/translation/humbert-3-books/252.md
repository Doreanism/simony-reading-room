And if anyone dares to contradict this, asserting that thieves and robbers know how to do or can do something else that is good, let him dare to dispute and contend in judgment with the Lord himself — and, if he can, prove him a liar. But since no Christian dares to do this, one must concede that thieves and robbers of this kind do nothing but harm to themselves and to others. For this reason, why are simoniacs thought to need repeated and almost daily synodal deposition, when they were once, simultaneously, and irrevocably deposed from the beginning by the princes of the apostles \[cf. Acts 8:20–23\] — just as other heretics likewise were, each at their very inception, by the orthodox and catholic fathers? For whenever an evil tree is uprooted entirely, it is vain to suppose that its branches live or will live, unless they too are torn out — since in the uprooting of their mother they are reckoned to be uprooted along with her. So too, the deposition of any heretic, as an act of excision, is not something to be sought person by person as if branch by branch, once the founder of the heresy as the root of an evil tree is established to have been thoroughly uprooted. This is certainly not the case with Catholics, however badly they may live, since their root — namely the catholic church — is never uprooted, even when some of its branches are pruned away on account of their barrenness or disorder. Such branches, for as long as they are sustained by their mother tree, either improve or at least serve to give shade to men or rest to birds. So too, wicked ministers of the good Catholic church, however badly they live for their own part, yet as long as their deposition is deferred, it either happens that they improve, or at least that some benefit is conferred on certain people in the meantime. From this it can be observed that the deposition of badly-living Catholic ministers is often deferred according to time and circumstance, and is sometimes carried out; whereas the deposition of heretics is deferred neither on account of time nor on account of any circumstance for any length of time, but is done as soon as they become known. Hence, in the case of badly-living ministers of Christ, the Catholic flock ought to await a Catholic synod and not reject them until the judgment of their fellow ministers or their superiors rejects them — in accordance with what Solomon teaches: “Let your eyes look straight ahead, and let your eyelids guide your steps” \[Proverbs 4:25\]. For it often happens that what seems intolerable to the imperfect seems tolerable to the perfect, and on the other hand, what is held intolerable by the perfect seems tolerable to the imperfect. Lest the church perhaps be misled or scandalized by this ambiguity, one must wait patiently and wisely until synodal judgment discerns and determines the matter. I do not know whether someone who thinks that judgment must still be awaited in the case of heretics, after councils have once been held concerning them, could truly be called a Catholic; for anyone to whom any heresy in the church seems tolerable has without doubt been shipwrecked or fallen from the Catholic faith.

## CHAPTER XLIV.

Illustrated by examples: heretics were not tolerated by the Catholics of old even for an hour, while hirelings were tolerated.

Did the Catholics anywhere, in the time of the Arians or other heretics, tolerate the ministry of any heretic given to them as bishop, or hold it to be holy, until he should be synodally deposed? That this was never tolerated by Catholics is clear from many historical sources, and especially from the *Historia Tripartita*, which recounts that some boys expiated with fire a ball they had been playing with, because it had accidentally rolled out among the feet of a donkey on which the heretic bishop of their city at that time was riding. The same Catholics are said to have so loathed his baths that they refused to bathe there not only with him but even without him, until the very facilities had been purified with fresh water. And lest anyone think that this admirable Catholic zeal, set before all Catholics for imitation, was displayed in a time of peace and some measure of temporal Christian power, let it be known that this happened in the time of Arian emperors and princes, by whom that very city was put to the sword on this account, and the greater part of its citizens were slaughtered. Whoever desires to know more thoroughly with what alacrity they hastened to death for the confession of the Catholic faith, and how the executioners gave out from slaughtering before the devout citizens ceased to offer and press themselves forward to those who would kill them, let him turn to the history mentioned above.
