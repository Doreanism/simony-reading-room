I do not regard the Laurentian codex, though written in a consistent hand with distinct letters and almost no errors, as the archetype. It is regrettable that it breaks off defectively at chapter 45 of Book III, of which only the rubric survives, so that the last nine chapters of Book III are missing. It is unlikely that more has been lost.

Humbert’s work, elaborated to refute the assertion that orders conferred by simoniacs are valid, rests on two principal theses: one theological — namely that simoniacs are heretics — and the other, so to speak, political: that the temporal possessions of churches are inseparably annexed to the clerical order (III, 26. 29). These theses are treated so abundantly and at such length with authorities and arguments that the author not infrequently returns to the same arguments, as is demonstrated before our eyes by the notation of the words “as above” and similar phrases at Book III, chapters 21, 30 (twice), 32, and elsewhere. Since therefore he could hardly have brought forward anything new, I would not readily believe that he wrote more than three books. Another manuscript, no. 104, is preserved in the chapter archive of the Spanish city of Vic. It is written on parchment in letters of the eleventh or twelfth century, but already in chapter 1 of Book II it breaks off at the words “if anyone teaches otherwise and does not assent to the sound words of our Lord Jesus Christ and to him who.” Humbert composed his disputation to refute the “Spinosus” \[the Thorny One\] (Book I, chapter 7); E. Martène, who first published the treatise, believed this to be a proper name, but he was wrong, for it is clear from the opening of the preface that the name of the author to whom Humbert was responding had been suppressed; that the name “Spinosulus” \[the Little Thorny One\] was applied merely on account of his thornier arguments was rightly noted already by A. Fabricius in his Bibliotheca Latina, Book VIII, under “Humbert.” The diminutive form is used contemptuously, just as in Book I, chapter 1 the expression *sententiola* \[petty opinion\], in Book II, chapter 19 *summula* \[trifling summary\] and *xeniola* \[paltry gifts\] are used. But it seems worthy of note that Peter Damian in the *Liber gratissimus* had already employed several of the arguments and reasons that Humbert attributed to his adversary, because that work likewise in chapter 33 cites canon 68 (67) of the Apostles and a passage from a letter of Gregory I \[MG. Epist. I, 145; Jaffé, 2nd ed., no. 1198\] in the same wording that Humbert (Book I, chapter 7) charges his adversary with corrupting. And the intention that Humbert there attributes to Spinosulus is the same intention that underlies the *Liber gratissimus*. Although therefore Humbert was spurred on by another’s book, yet far surpassing the measure of defense and refutation, with all the vehemence of his ardent spirit he attacks the simoniacs, so that one might better call the work an invective than a treatise.

The two arguments I noted above correspond to the two positions Humbert had proposed to himself to prove. First, from the fact that simoniacs, as heretics, had never at any moment possessed a genuine order, he concluded that they need not be deposed, since they cannot be deprived of what they do not have (Book I, chapter 19; III, chapters 32 ff.), and accordingly declared the synodal sentence, which Humbert’s adversary is shown to have demanded (from III, chapters 32 and 43), wholly superfluous. Then he required that ecclesiastical rules contrary to the right of investiture be known and observed (III, chapter 11); he summoned ecclesiastical men from sloth to the vindication of the Church’s liberty — indeed its sovereignty (III, chapter 29); and finally he called upon secular princes and the lay people, insofar as they had rights of their own (III, chapter 16), to stand against the despoilers of the churches (III, chapter 11).
