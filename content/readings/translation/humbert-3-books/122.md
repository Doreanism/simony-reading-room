From the decrees of the same \[Gregory\], on the same subject:

Following the ancient rule of the fathers, I establish that nothing is ever to be received in connection with ordinations — neither on account of the conferring of the pallium, nor the handing over of documents, nor for that which the new artifice of ambition has invented under the name of the *pastellus* \[a gift of bread or food given as a pretext for payment\]. For since the pontiff lays his hand on the bishop being ordained, the deacon reads the Gospel lesson, and the notary writes the letter of his confirmation, just as it does not befit the pontiff to sell the hand which he lays on, so the deacon and notary ought not to traffic in voice or pen at the ordination. I therefore forbid the one who is to be ordained to give anything at all for the ordination, or for the use of the pallium, or for documents and *pastelli*. If anyone among those named above shall presume to demand or seek any payment from him for any of the aforesaid things, he will be subject to judgment in the strict examination of Almighty God.

Likewise, to the same Virgilius, bishop of Arles:

From those who have reported to me I have learned that in the regions of Gaul and Germany no one attains to sacred orders without payment. If this is so, I say in weeping, I announce in mourning: since the priestly order has collapsed within, it will not long be able to stand without. For we know from the Gospel what our Redeemer did by his own hand, because upon entering the temple he overturned the chairs of those selling doves. To sell doves is to receive temporal payment for the Holy Spirit, whom Almighty God, consubstantial with himself, grants to men through the laying on of hands. From which evil, as I said before, what follows is already foreshadowed: because those who presumed to sell doves in the temple of God had their chairs overturned by God’s judgment. This error, evidently, spreads with increase among those subjected to it. For the one himself who is advanced to sacred honor is already corrupted at the very root of his advancement and is the more ready to sell to others what he has bought. Where is what is written: “Freely you have received, freely give”? And since the simoniac heresy arose first against the holy Church, why is it not guarded against, why is it not seen that he who ordains someone with a price, in advancing him, causes him to become a heretic?

The same \[Gregory\] to John, bishop of Corinth, on the same subject:

Since the holy universal Church utterly condemns this most wicked perversity, we exhort your brotherhood to suppress with every effort of your diligence so detestable and monstrous a sin from all places under its authority. For if we learn that anything of the sort is done hereafter, we will no longer address this with words but with canonical punishment, and we shall begin to hold a different judgment about you — one which is not fitting. Your brotherhood knows that previously the pallium was not given without a payment being made; since this was inappropriate, having held a council before the body of the blessed Peter, prince of the apostles, we have forbidden under strict prohibition the receiving of anything for this as well as for other ordinations. It is therefore proper that you neither consent to nor permit any persons to be brought to sacred orders through payment, through favor, or through the entreaties of certain persons. For the sin, as we have said, is grave, and we shall not allow this to continue without correction.
