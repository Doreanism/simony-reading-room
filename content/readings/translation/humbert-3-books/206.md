## CAPUT VII.

On the times of the Ottos, and on the zeal of the Emperor Henry, and on the malice of the king of the Franks.

To pass over earlier centuries, the memory of many still retains how this frenzy of reciprocal selling raged through Germany and Gaul and all of Italy from the times of the Ottos down to the Emperor Henry, of august and blessed memory, the son of Conrad. In his own day he to some degree removed this great sacrilege both from himself and from the ecclesiastical persons of the empire entrusted to his care, although he pressed hard and desired to remove it entirely. Forestalled by untimely death in the midst of this excellent desire of his heart, he was translated — as we believe — to the kingdom of eternal life for this intention alone, as it were, on account of the simplicity of his eye, though he was also praised for many other virtues. His contemporary and namesake — the destroyer of western Francia and a tyrant against God — by contrast, as a son of perdition and an antichrist, is the adversary of Christ, assailing and ceaselessly striving to overcome his grace in every part of his dominion. For this deed he is reckoned the tail of the dragon whose follower he is — a tail that has dragged and continues to drag innumerable and illustrious stars of heaven, that is, the churches of Francia, casting them and continuing to cast them into the dark land covered with the shadow of death. May the Holy Spirit, against whom he sins without ceasing, more than Simon Magus \[ever did\], either correct him soon through compunction or swiftly remove him by death, lest he continue to heap up still more blasphemies and injuries against the Holy Spirit himself, to the increase of his own damnation and the destruction of holy Christendom. God has permitted him to reign on account of the sins of the people, so that through him those who bore the name of Christ only in name may be thoroughly drawn to the lot of the devil. Against Christ, this ruinous man openly and obstinately resists — another Julian \[the Apostate\] — so that, though admonished and rebuked many times by the pontiffs of the apostolic see of revered memory, the lord Leo and Victor, he grows worse from day to day, and in rebellion against God and his priests scorns to put an end to his wickedness; harmful likewise to the land he occupies like a barren tree. Those who imitate his wickedness or who are his peers and companions in this profane money presume to approach and frequent the threshold of Simon Peter, believing they can please him with money acquired against his own verdict, after the example of Simon Magus, and do not fear that the prince of the apostles himself will say to them as well: “Your money perish with you” \[Acts 8:20\]. Their money, however great it may be, becomes unclean the moment that simoniac money, approaching, touches it. But let us return to our subject.

## CAPUT VIII.

On the fourfold selling practiced among the Latins, and on the devotion of Constantine the Great toward the clergy.

Thus ecclesiastical office is sold far and wide and scattered about — first by princes and their associates, then by metropolitans and their associates. For neither sale takes place without intermediaries. How much more tolerable it would be if it were sold only by metropolitans and their associates, following the example of the Eastern Church and the Church beyond the sea, since it would be sold twice rather than four times.
