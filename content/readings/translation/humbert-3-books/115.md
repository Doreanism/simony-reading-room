## CAPUT X.

But in what follows it will become more evident how imperfect the baptism of heretics is. The blessed Pope Siricius responded as follows to the bishop of Tarragona who was making inquiry about baptism: “On the first page of your letter you noted that many who had been baptized by the impious Arians are hastening to the Catholic faith, and that certain of our brethren wish to baptize these again — which is not lawful, since the apostle forbids it to be done and the canons contradict it, and since the general decrees sent to the provinces by my predecessor Liberius of venerable memory, after the Council of Rimini was annulled, prohibit it. We receive these people, together with Novatians and other heretics, into the fellowship of Catholics — as it has been established in synod — through the invocation alone of the sevenfold Spirit and the laying on of hands by a Catholic bishop. This the whole East and West observes. From this path it is likewise fitting that you not deviate henceforth, if you do not wish to be separated from our college by synodal judgment.” In this decretal chapter it is openly indicated that Catholics from the time of the apostles up to Pope Liberius maintained not a uniform but a twofold rule for receiving those coming from heretics; and that Liberius himself, after the annulment of the Council of Rimini, first prohibited by general decree that those baptized by Arians should be rebaptized by Catholics, but should be received into the fellowship of Catholics through the episcopal laying on of hands. But the most blessed Pope Innocentius plainly shows how imperfect those baptized by heretics are held to be in the Catholic Church, citing the ancient and authoritative rule of the holy Roman Church: “It is the law of our Church,” he says, “for those coming from heretics who have been baptized there to grant them lay communion only through the laying on of hands, and to raise none of them even to the lowest clerical honor.” From this rule it is clear what the holy fathers think about those baptized by heretics — persons whom they will not allow to be promoted not merely to any rank at the holy altar, but not even to the honor of the clergy alone, excepting, as was said above, the Cathars.

What the most distinguished doctor of the faith, Leo, thought about the baptism of heretics — though this has been set out at length above — we have not been reluctant to insert his chapter on this subject here once more: “Those who received baptism from heretics when they had not been baptized before are to be confirmed only by the invocation of the Holy Spirit through the laying on of hands, because they received only the form of baptism without the virtue of sanctification. And this rule, as you know, we preach to be observed in all churches: that the washing once initiated be violated by no repetition, as the apostle says: ‘One God, one faith, one baptism’ \[Ephesians 4:5\]. That ablution is not to be violated by any repetition, but, as we have said, only the sanctification of the Holy Spirit is to be invoked, so that what no one receives from heretics, one may obtain from Catholic priests.” And from the same doctor above: “There are those who do not know that they have not been baptized, but profess that they do not know the faith of those who baptized them.
