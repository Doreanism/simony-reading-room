The intent with which those hired hands perform their works the Lord makes clear when he says: “All their works they do for the sake of being seen by men” — showing in sum that they seek nothing through their preaching or their ministry except to become known to people and to please them. He then discloses what works of theirs he means: “They bind heavy and insupportable burdens and lay them upon men’s shoulders, but with their own finger they will not move them. And they widen their phylacteries and enlarge their fringes. And they love the first places at feasts, and the first chairs in the synagogues, and salutations in the marketplace, and to be called by men, Rabbi.” By these words of our Redeemer they are not reproached for laying the burdens of God’s commandments upon people — for that is recognized as the function of their office — but for making them insupportable, and for not relieving them by so much as a finger of their own effort. Nor for possessing phylacteries and fringes, but for widening the one and magnifying the other; nor because as masters they recline first, or sit in the first place, or are greeted, or called Rabbi — but because they love these things alone. And yet, even though very many ministers of Christ are of such a kind that they seek nothing of Christ’s — only the world’s advantage — in the ministry of Christ which they administer, Christ nonetheless wills that what belongs to them be faithfully rendered: that is, that they be heard, honored, and remunerated with the tithes and offerings of the faithful. Such men who are so corrupt in the sight of people — in the sight of God, what must they be? And yet God tolerates them, both in secret and openly, whether truly evil or merely feigning goodness, and he does not defraud them of the wage they seek. For since they are ministers of Christ, though they do not preside well, they are worthy of simple present honor. “For presbyters who rule well,” as the Apostle says, “are worthy of double honor, especially those who labor in word and doctrine.” “For the scripture says: ‘Thou shalt not muzzle the ox that treadeth out the corn’; and: ‘The laborer is worthy of his hire.’” As for the Apostle’s judgment concerning the hired hands — should it be required — his letter to the Colossians \[i.e., Philippians\] shows it thus: “Some indeed preach Christ even out of envy and contention, and some also out of good will — some out of charity, knowing that I am set for the defense of the gospel; and some out of contention preach Christ, not sincerely, supposing that they add affliction to my bonds. But what then? So that by all means, whether by occasion or by truth, Christ is preached; and in this I rejoice, yea, and will rejoice.” From the judgment of so great a teacher we learn how necessary the hired hands are; of those who preach Christ even insincerely, he expresses such joy that this great Apostle testifies he rejoices and will rejoice, whether Christ is preached out of envy — even through contention and disparagement of good men — so long as Christ is proclaimed. And who does not know how great a crime envy is? Yet if Christ is proclaimed even through envy, as through contention and disparagement of the good, the Apostle rejoices. And how great an imperfection it is to seize upon an occasion for preaching solely for bodily advantage! And yet the Apostle rejoices no less over such preaching. Therefore, let neither good preaching and ministry occasion the endorsement of wicked works, nor let wicked works occasion the contempt of holy preaching and ministry’s goods. Hence, if a cluster of grapes hanging from a hedge is to be gathered, the thorns of the hedge must be avoided.

## CHAPTER XXXIX.

On those promoted knowingly or unknowingly to evil, and on their ministry: the judgment of Paschasius.

For in order to show that such persons would exist in his Church until the end of the age, and that they are sometimes to be overlooked by the masters of the Church — whether in being promoted or in ministering — the Lord willed to advance the sacrilegious thief Judas to the rank of apostle, even though from the beginning he knew what was in the man. Likewise the holy Apostles unknowingly promoted to the diaconate Nicholas — chosen from among so many thousands of holy monks, a pig from Epicurus’s herd, to so speak. In these two figures it was clearly prefigured that some are known and others not known when they are promoted in the Church, yet whose office is either approved on its own account or tolerated in the meantime for the sake of others. Whatever such persons do in the divine sacraments by virtue of the office entrusted to them in the meantime is entirely holy — just as Judas \[acted\] before the conspiracy of betrayal, and Nicholas before the invention of his abominable sect. This is further confirmed by the treatise of Paschasius on the Body of the Lord, where it reads as follows: “Even if the priest be guilty, the office of his ministry is sacred, and the rank of priesthood is sustained by Christ the supreme pontiff, so that this mystery is in no way diminished — as regards the flesh and blood — by any unworthy person, provided that the consecration is performed in accordance with the institution of the apostolic lineage; for it is fulfilled not from the priest himself but from his office, by the supreme pontiff, with the grace that truth itself has promised, and the priesthood and the pontifical office are not diminished in any respect by the merit of any individual. For the power of consecrating resides in what he who is ordained receives. Therefore those who return, having previously been baptized, are not rebaptized; just so, those who have previously been ordained are not re-ordained when they return to Catholic unity after having departed. But either they minister, if the utility of the Church demands it, or if not, they nonetheless carry the sacrament of their ordination, and therefore hands are not laid upon them among the laity. There remains in them, therefore, the power of the sacrament — unprofitably for themselves, even when they consecrate while guilty — yet sufficiently profitable in its power and ordination of consecrating for those who communicate with faith, hope, and charity in the grace of the blessing and administration they have received. Nor do these life-giving sacraments flowing from a heavenly fountain benefit the perverse unto salvation, since there is no participation of justice with iniquity, just as there is none of light with darkness. And yet such persons — even if they are adulterers and murderers and have committed many things on the devil’s side — while they sacrifice without yet having been cut off by the evangelical sickle, one must not consider who and what they are, but what they give and what those who communicate in faith receive — nor who possesses the power of consecrating, but what he possesses. For just as in the name of Christ, as he himself testifies, even by members of the devil, demons are driven out and many mighty works are done through them, so also by the power of the same Christ the body and blood are consecrated into a sacrament by the wicked, even though it is said that God does not hear sinners — for over the water of baptism, and over the oil, and over the Eucharist, and over the heads of those upon whom hands are laid, all these things are celebrated and accomplished in the sacrament. Therefore they are valid even through murderers; and even if such persons do not have the Holy Spirit, yet God is the one who gives the same Holy Spirit through their ministry and consecrates from the office the gift of his own generosity.” Lest perhaps the objection be raised against us from this same man’s judgment — since it is said here at the end that the Holy Spirit is given through the ministry of criminals, even though they themselves do not have him — let those who raise it take note of what he set out at the head of his argument, where he speaks hypothetically thus: “This mystery is in no way diminished by any unworthy person, provided that the consecration is performed in accordance with the institution of the apostolic lineage; for it is fulfilled not from the priest himself but from his office, by the supreme pontiff, with the grace that truth itself has promised.” Whence it is clear that they possess the Holy Spirit for ministry — that is, through the ministry which they received by the institution of the apostolic lineage — yet do not possess him for themselves. They possess it for ministering good things to others, but not for turning aside evil from themselves. They possess it for believing rightly, but not for living rightly — as has been shown abundantly above, along with the demonstration that the ministry of heretics is not of Christ, nor was it received through the institution of the apostolic lineage.
