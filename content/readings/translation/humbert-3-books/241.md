And if these two things are openly absent, one may nonetheless surmise that in the supposedly gratuitous promotion of noble or religious persons they are covertly angling for their favor or authority — so that those already promoted by them may be more secure about their own promotion, those yet to be promoted may be readier to buy, and the simoniacs themselves may be bolder to sell. Thus either the present transaction is practiced by them, or the past is confirmed by the present, or at the very least the future is invited. and damned together with Simon Magus himself. Moreover, it does not seem out of place here to preemptively meet yet another argument of those who speak in vain. For they say:

The law of the emperors decrees that, if someone does this or that, he shall be punished by death. Now it happens that some layman commits that crime for which, by the given law, he ought to suffer capital punishment. But so long as the judge, whose business it is, delays carrying out the sentence — whether out of mercy or negligence — does he not live in the meantime? Does he not carry the head which he justly deserves to lose? And if, in the meanwhile, he should beget a son, will the son be guilty of bloodguilt on account of the father’s crime? Certainly not. For he who committed the crime alone will bear the sentence of punishment.

### Refutation.

This argument is shown to work in our favor, not against us — in favor of the “headed” \[*cephali*\], that is, those who still have their head, not in favor of the “headless” \[*acephali*\]. For it was shown sufficiently a little above that those promoted freely by Catholics, even if found to be criminal before or after their promotion, are not without a head; and also that those promoted by them before their deposition do not perish on their account. Hence it is established that the foregoing argument provides no defense for the headless, nor for their offspring — because they neither have nor have had a head, that is, ecclesiastical office, of which they could be deprived. For a law is shown to be superfluous by which someone is decreed to be deprived of what he does not possess. It is as if one were to say: “If someone who has been beheaded does this or that, let him be deprived of his head.” Indeed, every heretic is beheaded, cut off from the head of the Church, Christ, by his own heresy; and so too are all those who are subsequently seen to be promoted by them. Hence the dead are born from the dead, and therefore no one receives from them anything but a dead thing \[*morticinum*\]; and what is taken away from them at their deposition has been amply shown above.
