Thus these men who are called Christians sell the entire world of Christians — insofar as it lies within them — the world which God so loved that he gave his only-begotten Son as its redemption. And to whom do they sell it? To the devil. And to what end are they sold to him? That they may again be his and do evil at his pleasure, and that no sacraments of Christianity may profit them — since those sacraments, received freely, are neither given nor administered freely. And even if they appear to do good things, those things are no longer good, because they are reckoned to him to whom they are bound as slaves by the ill-drafted bond \[chirographum\] of their masters. Like the vessels of the good shepherd girded \[for service\], they deceitfully draw back for the wicked lion those whom they persistently snatch away from the gentle lamb. And therefore they are to be lamented without consolation, because they perish through their own negligence or ignorance alone, while they either neglect or fail to know how to avoid the malice of such great and such terrible wolves. If the rabidity of Judas is compared with their rabidity, it will be found more tolerable in many respects. For he, driven by sudden fury, sold his master once — the master whom he saw as mortal and did not doubt was capable of suffering. But these men, inflamed by long premeditation, sell the Holy Spirit a thousand times over — him whom they profess in words to be immortal and impassible. Judas, though for a time he disturbed a few disciples for a little while, did not thereby take Christ away from them, even slain. But these men both disturb the universal Church without ceasing and banish his grace from innumerable peoples of Christ. Judas, when one man betrayed one \[person\], ruined himself and a few accomplices, but nonetheless served the salvation of the entire world, though unwillingly and unknowingly. But these men, when countless individuals betray one, ruin themselves and their supporters, ravage the destruction of the whole world, and resist the salvation of all the faithful — and can be of benefit to neither themselves nor anyone else unless they perhaps repent at some point. Judas sold his master alone and without stripping him of his and his followers’ belongings. But these men sell not only the Paraclete Spirit but also everything that is his and his people’s, wherever it may be, down to the smallest coin. Judas presumed to seek no mediator or witness for himself apart from himself. But these men very frequently summon and make not only themselves and their household but also outsiders into mediators and witnesses of their trafficking. Judas, led by repentance, returned the thirty pieces of silver to the buyers even when they were unwilling. But these men, unrepentant, not only do not return however small a price, but would wish to have received more. And indeed, lest they be thought in any respect inferior to the lost Judas and his accomplices — they who are shown to surpass those men in so many crimes — these men too rejoice when they have put a price on the Holy Spirit, just as those who priced Christ rejoiced; and as those sought an opportunity for betrayal, so these seek out their trafficking.

## CHAPTER XXI.

### On the fact that no almsgiving of any kind profits simoniacs.

Indeed, just as a most covetous merchant anxiously waits and breathlessly seeks out the annual fairs, so too do these men await ordinations to be held in their season, either to redeem their possessions pledged in hope of such a price, or to fill an empty purse, or to pour more into one already full. From that money which they love so much as not to fear selling the Holy Spirit, they will reap no fruit — even if they spend it on the poor and on holy places — just as Judas reaped nothing from his money, with which a field was bought for the burial of strangers: that \[field\] profited neither the seller of Christ nor its buyers toward the merit of their souls, so much so that the profane Judas received not even a mean grave there, nor lay unburied upon it. Hence if one considers that the chief priests judged and professed at the same time that the money of the betrayal, refunded to them, was not lawful to put in the treasury \[corban\], that is, to be stored with the votive offerings of the holy temple, because it was the price of blood — do we think how much more wicked than the Jews are those whom we judge to be more criminal, who sometimes think that the price of the Paraclete is to be stored together with the offerings which the devotion of Christians has brought or brings to the churches of God?
