What the Church even visibly sometimes suffers on account of the sins of such princes and priests and their supporters is plainly declared by the prophet Micah: “Hear this, you heads of the house of Jacob and rulers of the house of Israel, who abhor justice and pervert all that is right; who build Zion with bloodshed and Jerusalem with wickedness. ## CAPUT XXX.

### A recapitulation concerning the deposition of heretics and the testimony of Ezekiel

Now, although I have with the Lord’s help for the most part either repelled, deflected, or blunted the darts hurled against the Catholics by their adversaries, I think it still necessary at the close of this book to blunt that dart steeped in the deadliest poison, with which the ecclesiastical rule is stubbornly assailed — namely, that if according to that rule it follows that heretics are to be deposed, it should likewise follow according to their assertion that these same heretics have acquired ecclesiastical grace, from the height of which it is necessary that they be deposed. Wherefore, on this point too, I ask those who weigh words to bear with me for a moment, and to tell me: from what degree of divine grace are they deposed, against whom the psalmist prays thus: “Scatter them in your power and cast them down, O Lord my protector”? And again, from what ecclesiastical degree are they deposed, for whom the Catholic \[Church\] prays thus on the day of the Lord’s passion: “Almighty and everlasting God, who savest all and willest that none should perish, look upon the souls deceived by diabolical fraud, that laying aside all heretical depravity the hearts of those who err may return and come back to the unity of your truth.” Behold, here the Catholic \[Church\] prays that heretical depravity may be deposed from the souls deceived by diabolical fraud. The psalmist likewise, above, \[prays\] that his enemies may be cast down. In which casting down we can understand no demotion from the heights of any grace or dignity they previously held, but only the presumption of heretical falsehood, which they pertinaciously claim for themselves under the appearance of Catholic truth. So too when simoniacs or any heretics are commanded to be deposed, they are commanded to be deposed not from any grace of ecclesiastical ordination that they have hitherto held, but only from the outward appearance of ecclesiastical grades, which they abuse for their own perdition and the deception of the Christian people by means of imposture. To them the fable of the little donkey clothed in a lion’s skin may most fittingly be applied — a donkey who, having assumed (or, more aptly, simulated) the lion’s vigor, was troubling the frightened cattle through his fields, when he could not in any way acquire for himself or anyone else any of the lion’s actual strength from those hides and spoils, beyond mere empty terror.
