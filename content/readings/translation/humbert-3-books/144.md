“Take the censer and, having taken fire from the altar, put incense upon it, and go quickly to the people to make atonement for them; for wrath has already gone out from the Lord and the plague has begun.” And when Aaron had done this and run into the midst of the congregation, which the fire was already devouring, he offered incense and, standing between the dead and the living, he made intercession for the people, and the plague ceased. Now those who were struck down were fourteen thousand and seven hundred, besides those who had perished in the revolt of Korah. In this act it is clearly shown that even the misguided common people are dreadfully cast down by divine indignation when they lament the death of those same perverse men whom they had falsely believed and called the people of God. Nor is this surprising, since, as Solomon says, a companion of fools will become like them — a companionship he warns must be avoided when he writes: “My son, if sinners entice you, do not consent. If they say: Come with us, or cast in your lot among us, let there be one purse for us all — for their feet run to evil. Do not envy wicked men, nor desire to be with them, for their lips speak deceit.” And: “Do not mingle with detractors, for their destruction will rise suddenly, and who knows the ruin of them both? Give not your honor to strangers, nor your years to the cruel, lest strangers be filled with your strength, and your labors be in the house of another, and you groan in the end, when you have consumed your flesh and your body, and say: Why did I hate instruction, and my heart did not yield to rebukes, nor did I hear the voice of those who taught me, nor did I incline my ear to my masters? I was almost in every evil, in the midst of the church and the assembly.” These passages from Proverbs we have gathered more fully because the brutish and sluggish multitude of people, prone to evil, disdains to know the teaching of truth, eager only to imitate the evil deeds of its superiors and rulers, and strives to excuse itself by its own ignorance — to which it willingly submits — when Paul says: “He who does not know will not be known.” Therefore, let the Christian people cast off the ignorance that is the mother of vices, and learn who is the shepherd, who the hired hand, who the thief and the robber; let it follow and heed the shepherd entirely, merely heed and for a time endure the hired hand, but never hear nor endure the thief and the robber, for they come only to steal and kill and destroy. And alas, how lamentably corrupt is the condition of our humanity, which scarcely ever, even when wearied by long meditation and daily warnings, comes to know what things it should seek or flee, whereas all other animals from the very beginning of their existence, by nature’s instinct alone, seek what is suitable and flee what is unsuitable — just as doves and similar birds flee hawks and birds of that kind. Furthermore, in the surface meaning of the narrative set before us, it is shown beyond doubt how great is the difference between those who take honor upon themselves and those who are called by God. For Korah and his accomplices, by offering incense and prayers to the Lord, benefited no one but harmed themselves in every way, so that they straightaway deserved to be consumed by fire. But Aaron alone, by offering incense and interceding for those who were dying — standing between the dead and the living like some impregnable and impenetrable wall — himself remained unharmed amid so great a conflagration and turned it away from the survivors. Thus the oblation and prayer of one lawful priest appeased the indignation of the divine majesty, whereas that of two hundred and fifty presumptuous men provoked it against themselves and others. Simoniacs and their defenders must hear this, if they have ears to hear, and at last cease by their prayers and sacrifices to kindle unquenchably the wrath of God against themselves and against the people.

## CAPUT V.

### On Jannes and Jambres, who are surpassed in wickedness by the simoniacs.

There remain Jannes and Jambres, whose presumption and avarice are manifest. These sorcerers, driven by a twofold avarice, presumed to imitate the signs of the divine, whether so that, being esteemed equal to Moses or superior to him by the people, they might be magnified, or certainly so that they might be loaded with royal gifts.
