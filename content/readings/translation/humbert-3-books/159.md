It is the first and distinctive characteristic of bishops and other clergy whose office it is to administer the divine sacraments to offer and expose for sale a heavenly commodity only; whereas laypeople and other practitioners of secular trade offer one that is earthly and worldly only. It is likewise proper to the former always to offer, price, and sell something that is not their own, over the objection of its owner; while the latter sell only what is their own, with no one’s objection. Just so, it is the former’s lot always to sell cheaply what is not theirs; the latter’s to sell their own cheaply only rarely. It is likewise the former’s lot to not give freely what they have received freely; the latter’s, to not give freely what they have not received freely. For if one considers carefully, man never gains anything of benefit from another man without cost — he receives what he receives either in exchange for service, or for human favor or goodwill, or in return for a gift of money; and if he receives something from a man for God’s sake alone, he is all the more not receiving it for free, because even that the man himself received from God so that he might give it to another, and by giving it he lays up for himself good merit before God, who alone is therefore the true God and Lord, because, as the Psalmist says, “he has no need of our goods” (Psalm 16). Of whose infinitely full and uniquely complete fullness even the eminent Prosper \[of Aquitaine\], following the understanding of the most learned father Augustine, first in prose and then in verse, writes thus: “God commands nothing that benefits himself, but rather the one he commands. For this reason he is a true Lord who has no need of a servant, and of what the servant has need.”

God commands nothing so that it might profit himself,
Nor does the true Lord need the service of a servant.
He who serves is increased by his commandments,
And whoever disregards his rule becomes the lesser.
\[God\] loses nothing of his own, gains nothing that will perish.
For God omnipotent, power that holds all things at once,
Never lacked what he has, giving whatever it profits to have,
And receiving only what he himself gave.

## CAPUT XVIII.

### That carnal members of the church are not without faith, after the likeness of Reuben and Gad and the half-tribe of Manasseh.

But to return to the merchants set before us: it is proper to the former \[i.e., simoniacs\] to believe that God and Lord himself — namely the Holy Spirit — is for sale and to treat him as such; while the latter \[i.e., secular merchants\], if they have reached the depths of the evils of avarice, hold their own souls for sale and yet do not believe that they are able to sell or give them to any man. With this in mind, let us with keen mental discernment re-examine and individually distinguish the common and peculiar lines of these merchants, which we have sketched above, and determine who sins more grievously, or who comes closer to the Scylla or Charybdis of perfidy. Let us first consider the practitioners of secular trade, in what they are accustomed to transgress. Plainly they transgress in seeking to enrich themselves temporally — but who is found not desiring to enrich himself more than is just? Such a bird is certainly extremely rare. They transgress also in this: that out of desire for gain they defraud brothers in business, selling cheap things to them at a higher price or carrying off costly things from them cheaply. There remains for some of them that supreme crime of avarice by which they even hold their souls for sale, slaying them in pursuit of gain through various crimes and sins — yet though their souls die from these \[sins\], faith is not entirely wrested from them. For they not only do not attack or refuse the sound faith; they actually labor to defend it in heart and mouth, even if it is dead for them. These are fittingly signified by the sons of Reuben and Gad and the half-tribe of Manasseh, who cross the Jordan armed before their brothers, and by powerful arm and fighting with the sword win and subject to them the land of promise.
