From the highest rank of the Church down to the lowest, all without exception busy themselves trafficking in ecclesiastical goods for their own profit. Emperors, kings, princes, judges, and all who hold any power in the world practice this above all else and seek it out — men who ought to defend ecclesiastical goods for the Church’s rights, some by the spiritual sword and others by the material sword. Nor do they weigh how wicked and detestable before God and man they become by squandering what they received to protect on behalf of the poor of Christ. For if the guardian of any orphan were to sell for his own profit, or give away at his pleasure, the little patrimony entrusted to his protection, or were knowingly and deliberately to hand over a guardianship once accepted to some spendthrift who would strip away the substance left to the orphan, he would be considered and judged by all as abominable and as a murderer. How abominable, we may suppose, and guilty of how many murders are those who dissipate at their pleasure the patrimonies of churches, whose guardians they have been appointed by him to whom the poor man is left — who is also the father of orphans and the judge of widows? Hence it comes about that the kings and princes of this age, assigned as guardians and defenders of the churches of God, having abandoned the duty entrusted to them from on high, carry the sword — which they brandish as ministers of God and avengers of wrath against all evildoers — without purpose, since they procure vengeance against wrongdoers and praise for the good never or scarcely ever. And would that they were so cold or lukewarm in this their duty, rather than inflamed and burning to seize what belongs to others! For having abandoned the military function by which they should have protected the commonwealth and the fatherland against external attacks, and having abandoned likewise the tenor and terror of their own laws, by which they should have restrained transgressors of divine and human justice against internal and daily excesses, they transfer all their power, all their terror, all their ingenuity, all their energy toward attacking and entirely appropriating for themselves the ecclesiastical properties of which they had been made guardians. And not content with their own sphere, they encroach upon the ecclesiastical sphere, presiding and holding authority in synod, and through earthly power making themselves so formidable that everything proceeds and is determined by their nod and judgment. They even take precedence over primates and metropolitans in the election of bishops, when they ought only to confirm with their own assent, according to God, the consent and desire of the clergy, people, and order of each city — as the blessed pope Caelestinus teaches: “Let not strangers and foreigners, who were previously unknown, be placed over the retiring clergy of their own churches in order to exclude those who merit the favorable testimony of their fellow citizens. Let no bishop be given against the will of \[the people\]. The consent and desire of the clergy, people, and order must be sought. Let one be chosen from another church only when no one worthy can be found — which we do not believe can happen — among the clergy of the city for which a bishop is to be ordained. For those candidates must first be rejected before persons from other churches deserve to be preferred. Let each man reap the fruit of his own service in the church in which he has spent his life fulfilling every office. Let no one usurp the rewards of another’s service in a foreign church, and let no one dare to claim for himself a reward owed to another. Let the clergy have the ability to resist if they see themselves burdened, and let them not fear to refuse what they recognize as irregular; for if they cannot have the reward that is their due, they ought at least to have free judgment concerning him who is to govern them.” In agreement with this, the blessed pope and doctor Leo says: “No reason permits that those be counted among bishops who have neither been elected by the clergy nor sought by the people nor consecrated with the judgment of the comprovincial bishops together with the metropolitan,” etc.
