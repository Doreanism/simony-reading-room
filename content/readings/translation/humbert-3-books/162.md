When they rejoiced at this and bargained for money, he brought forth as it were an ear; it ripened while he sought an opportunity for the betrayal; and he showed the full tare in himself when he consummated the betrayal with the sign of a kiss. At last the scythe came, when, hanged by a noose, he burst asunder in the middle and departed hence to be consigned to his own place. The simoniacs, who have become his closest followers, are drawn down by no lesser avarice to the point of attempting to betray the Holy Spirit. With this his instigation the devil enters their hearts by force, the doors of faith — that is, fear and love — having been broken, though he had hitherto stood outside them, tripping up those whom he had lured through other evils of avarice, without yet altogether banishing whatever faith in God remained in their hearts. But as soon as they do not fear to think perversely about God and to strive, even if secretly, \[against him\], they lose faith entirely; and so, as Christ withdraws — he who had previously dwelt in their hearts through faith — the devil, that guest of faithlessness, succeeds to possession. Then, pregnant with the conception of such great wickedness and laboring to bring forth \[their intentions\], they cleverly seek out those like themselves, and when they have found them, whisper to them: “What will you give us? And we will hand over the Holy Spirit to you.” Is this not a voice that was once Judas’s for a single day, yet is nearly continuous and daily for these men? Would that these kites — gathered to their own Babylon, one to another, as Isaiah \[says\] — whispered this nefarious voice to one another slowly and softly, and did not proclaim it boldly and loudly! But what is worse: the practice of this trafficking has grown so ingrained that now, as if by the authority and rule \[of custom\], the Holy Spirit in his sacraments is openly set up for sale before all, openly assessed, openly his price demanded, openly — so it is believed — sold, and openly bought. In this they are more wicked than the most wicked Judas, who attempted in secret what should on no account have been attempted by anyone at all. If among other things he offended the Lord God monstrously even by leaving what was beyond valuation to be priced at the Jews’ discretion, and by esteeming the Incomparable so little as to hand him over to profane men for a number of denarii — where will these men flee from the Spirit of God and from his face, where will they escape, they who are accustomed to put a price on the Holy Spirit himself, now by their own judgment and now by another’s, and esteem him so lightly that they do not hesitate to sell him not only for a small sum of coins but even — ah, it shames and grieves one to say — for a dog, a pig, a hawk, and other such trifling gifts of every kind? For it is a guilt beyond measure to wish to sell the Creator of heaven and earth for however great and precious a commodity. Therefore, as was said above, they commit an intolerable crime whenever they attempt to sell cheaply what is precious and dearer than all things. For what is dearer and more precious than God? The wise man commends his incomparable excellence under the name of wisdom, saying: “I counted nothing comparable to her, and I did not compare any precious stone with her, for all gold in comparison with her is a little sand, and silver will be esteemed as mud in her sight, for her light is inextinguishable. For she is an infinite treasure to men.” And shortly after: “For in her is a spirit, intelligent, holy, unique, manifold, subtle, mobile, eloquent, undefiled,” and so forth. Blessed Job also says of her: “Man does not know her price. Fine gold shall not be given for her, nor shall silver be weighed as her exchange. She shall not be compared with the dyed colors of India, nor with the most precious sardius stone, nor with sapphire. Gold or crystal shall not equal her, nor shall vessels of gold be exchanged for her. High and eminent things shall not be mentioned in comparison with her. But wisdom is drawn from hidden places. The topaz of Ethiopia shall not equal her, nor shall she be compared with the purest dyeing.” He then briefly discloses, a little later, what wisdom is, speaking from God’s own mouth to man: “Behold, the fear of the Lord, that is wisdom, and to depart from evil is understanding.” Hence blessed Jerome in his commentary
