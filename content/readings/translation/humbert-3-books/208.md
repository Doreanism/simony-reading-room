What secular affairs are, the holy canons make clear and forbid thus: “Since it is manifest that certain persons who appear elected to the clergy, for the sake of shameful gain, become lessees of others’ properties and take secular affairs under their management — despising the ministry of God, running about the houses of secular men, and taking upon themselves anxiety for patrimonies out of greed — the aforesaid synod has decreed that henceforth no cleric shall lease property or mix himself in secular affairs, except in the case of orphans, minors, and widows, or unless perhaps the bishop of the city directs him to take charge of ecclesiastical affairs.” From which it is plainly apparent that secular affairs are one thing and ecclesiastical affairs another. Was not Moses in the world when he continually entered and departed from the tabernacle — he who was inwardly caught up in contemplation, and outwardly pressed by the affairs of the infirm; inwardly considering the hidden things of God, outwardly bearing the burdens of the carnal? Priests in the Church should enact his type, so that when they go outside to conduct affairs on behalf of the needs of their subjects, they return inwardly to themselves through contemplation of the commandments. Like Paul, who is inserted into heavenly secrets and yet, through the depths of his condescension, searches out the dwelling place of carnal men. Like Jacob, who saw the angels ascending and descending, because the rulers of the Church not only seek God through contemplation but also descend with compassion toward his members below. And when priests imitate the deeds of these men, they both guard themselves and carry the burdens of their subjects, and appear to be such as that same eminent Paul commands — those who use this world as though they do not use it, and who rejoice as though not rejoicing, and who buy as though not possessing \[1 Corinthians 7:29–31\].

## CAPUT VIIII.

On the distinction between clergy and laity, and on the zeal of the blessed Gregory in such matters.

From these same texts we are taught equally that, just as clerics are prohibited from presuming to engage in secular affairs, so laymen are prohibited from presuming to engage in ecclesiastical affairs. And just as clerics are excluded from the sphere of laymen, so laymen are removed from the sphere of clerics — as much from the permission of mutual accusation as from that of testimony. And just as clerics are distinguished from laymen by habit and profession, so they must be distinguished in action and manner of life, so that neither usurps the office or the hereditary portion of the other, but each attends to the boundaries set by the holy fathers and the orthodox princes. For just as clerics are separated from laymen within the walls of basilicas by places and offices, so too they must be distinguished and known outside \[those walls\] by their pursuits. Therefore let laymen manage and attend to only their own affairs — that is, secular affairs — and let clerics manage and attend to only their own affairs — that is, ecclesiastical affairs. Each group has rules set before it both by the holy pope and martyr Stephen and the other pontiffs and by the councils of the holy fathers, as well as by catholic princes. This is sufficiently shown in the deeds of the blessed Gregory, where we read: “No layman managed any ministry of the sacred Lateran palace or any ecclesiastical patrimony, but all functions of ecclesiastical right were likewise discharged by men of ecclesiastical standing, with laymen assigned solely to the military service of arms or to the continuous care of fields.” On account of this, some of the nobles began to have themselves tonsured first under the pretext of religion. The Emperor Mauricius wisely countered their evasion and issued a law through his chamberlain Longinus, commanding that whoever had been involved in public administrative duties should not be permitted to enter upon an ecclesiastical office.
