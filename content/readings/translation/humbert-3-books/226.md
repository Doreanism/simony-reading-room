The authority of the holy fathers impresses this upon priests in these terms: The people are to be taught, not followed; and if they do not know what is permitted or what is not permitted, we ought to admonish them, not give them our consent. Furthermore, concerning kings and princes of the world it says: The princes of the world sometimes hold the heights of power they have obtained within the Church, so that through this same power they may defend ecclesiastical discipline. Otherwise, powers would not be necessary within the Church, unless it were that what the priest cannot accomplish through the sermon of teaching, this power might command through the terror of discipline. Often through the earthly kingdom the heavenly kingdom makes progress, so that those who, placed within the Church, act against the faith and discipline of the Church, may be crushed by the rigor of princes, and so that the discipline itself which the utility or mildness of the Church cannot exercise may be imposed by princely power upon the necks of the proud. Let the princes of the world recognize that they must render an account on behalf of the Church, which they undertake to protect from Christ. For whether the peace and discipline of the Church are increased through faithful princes or dissolved, he who entrusted his holy Church to their power will demand an account from them.

## CAPUT XXII.

### On the Harmful Silence of Priests and the Loquacity of Flatterers

Since no Christian dares to contradict these things, woe to us priests, for we are silent, for we are men of unclean lips. For if the most holy Isaiah incurred the pollution of lips because, when the king was attempting the priestly office even a single time, he did not refute him with a free voice as Elijah did, and could not be perfectly purged from that very pollution — according to the judgment of the blessed Jerome — until he had been savagely cut down and slain: I ask, when will our lips be made clean, we who not only do not rebuke princes who presume upon ecclesiastical matters without ceasing, but even praise them? And with such lips do we dare to call upon or name the Lord? And what does it profit to say daily: “Lord, deliver my soul from lying lips and from a deceitful tongue,” and: “Lord, open my lips, and my mouth shall proclaim your praise”? Are not lips iniquitous that do not rebuke iniquity, or that do not defend equity? From these \[lips\] all Christians must pray to be freed, but far more so priests, who must devote themselves to prayer and preaching not only for themselves but for all, lest perchance when they multiply prayers, God — offended by their unclean lips — does not hear them. For the law of truth, as the blessed Jerome expounds on Malachi, is the teaching of peoples in the mouth of the priest, which in the priest ought to be tainted by no falsehood but must proceed entirely from the fount of truth; and so that he himself may have peace and make peace for others, he must accept no person’s \[face\] in judgment, and must turn many away from iniquity by reproving others. “The lips of the priest shall guard knowledge.” And he did not say “shall put forth” but “shall guard,” that he may speak at the opportune time. “And they shall seek the law from his mouth, for he is the angel of the Lord of hosts” and a watchman given by the Lord to the house of Israel. From his mouth teaching and rebuke shall be required, so that unless he has announced to the righteous man not to sin, and to the unrighteous man to convert, the blood of both shall be required from the hand of his tongue. If lips are stricken as iniquitous through silence alone by this punishment, by what punishment, do you think, will deceitful lips be stricken — those that not only keep silent about the iniquity they are commanded to reprove, but moreover labor by lying to show that it is equity?
