How extensively he was able to deploy ecclesiastical authorities and canons would take long to discuss; one thing only I do not wish to pass over in silence: in the treatise against the simoniacs, Book I, chapter 8, he cites canon 68 (67) of the Apostles, although both there and in the response against Nicetas (chapter 16) he states that only the fifty canons are received and the rest counted among the apocrypha; yet the text of the canon as cited both by the corrupter and by the corrector differs from the genuine text.

Humbert does not seem to have been entirely ignorant of secular law, if one considers what he cites from secular laws in Book III, chapters 5 and 34, and his remarks at the end of chapter 23 about the peculium \[private property\] of a slave.

But that he had a command of Greek, I am not convinced. What Wibert says in his Life of Leo IX, II, 9 \[Watterich, Vitae pont. I, p. 161\] — that a calumny published in Greek was translated into Latin by Humbert’s efforts — seems to me rather to prove that this translation was made not by Humbert himself but by another at his instigation; then too, in Book I, chapter 8, Humbert cites canon 68 (67) of the Apostles in such a way that he must be supposed only to have discovered afterwards that it had been translated from Greek; and when he inserts Greek words into his text — for example, *acyrologia* \[improper expression\] II, 15; *gazophilatii* \[treasuries\] 21; *synmista* \[co-initiate\] 38; *plastes idoli* \[idol-maker\] 43; *apollueen* \[destruction\] 44; *gnatonico officio* \[filial service\] III, 20 — he was displaying his learning, just as he also used unusual words from Latin, such as *abstemii* \[abstainers from wine\] II, 27; *cretata ambitio* \[chalked ambition\] 35; *camyrus* III, 6.

As for Humbert’s works, mention is made of poems in which, while still a monk, he celebrated the praises of several saints.

There survive a dialogue against the Greeks and a refutation of Nicetas Pectoratus, composed by Humbert in 1054. But his most important work is without doubt the Response against the Simoniacs, which stands in the first rank among the works by which the contest between priesthood and empire over lay investiture has been transmitted to us. It survives in Florence in codex 34 of pluteus XIX of the Laurentian Library. That this is the same manuscript which B. de Montfaucon, Bibliotheca bibliothecarum I, p. 236, lists among the codices of pluteus XIX as containing the sermons of Cardinal Humbert, there can be no doubt.
