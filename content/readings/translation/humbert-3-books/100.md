Thus it must be said that Humbert, refusing any accommodation, sounded the signal to give battle with these books against the simoniacs.

That being so, this work must nonetheless be reckoned among the historical monuments especially on account of those chapters in which simony is connected with investiture (III, chapters 6 and 11) and on account of what the author relates about his own time (II, chapters 19, 35, 36, 43; III, chapters 5, 8, 10, 12, 20, 21).

But let us now consider at what time Humbert composed the anti-simoniac treatise. From Book III, chapters 8 and 10, where the condition of the Eastern Church is discussed, it is clear that he had already completed the legation to Constantinople of 1054 when he wrote that book. Furthermore, it appears that he wrote it between 1057 and 1060 — that is, after the deaths of Emperor Henry III and Pope Victor II (in 1056 and 1057), but while Henry I, king of the Franks, was still living. This was already noted by E. Martène in the preface to his edition.

Within these two limits established by Humbert’s own testimony, a precise year cannot be determined except by conjecture. H. Halfmann \[loc. cit. p. 31\] holds it certain that Humbert completed the treatise before the 23rd of May 1059, the day on which Philip was crowned king of the Franks in the presence of two legates of the apostolic see. But in the chapter cited, after the pontiffs Leo and Victor, there is silence about Stephen IX. From this I would infer that the disputation was brought to completion while Stephen was still living — that is, before the 29th of March 1058.

Moreover, there is not even the slightest mention of Benedict X, who succeeded Stephen IX; on the contrary there is an allusion to a pontiff whom Peter Damian plainly charges with having been tainted by simony. Given all this, it seems more probable to me that the book was composed before Benedict’s enthronement (5 April 1058) than during or after his pontificate. This accords well with the account of John of Bayon, who records that Humbert published the codex of books against the simoniacs while residing at Florence; for it was reported above (p. 96, notes 12 and 14) that Humbert was resident in Florence both before the election of Benedict X and after it, but in the same year.

In the past century these books, retrieved from the Laurentian Library by Mabillon, were published by E. Martène in the Thesaurus anecdotorum, vol. V, p. 629 ff. \[Migne, PL CXLIII, col. 1005 ff.\]; but he satisfied his undertaking poorly, misinterpreting some things and omitting others entirely. Fr. Thaner.
