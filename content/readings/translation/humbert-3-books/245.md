## CHAPTER XXXVII.

### That Catholics promote no one on account of crime, whereas simoniac promoters promote all on account of crime alone.

In this matter, the simoniac promoters neither give nor promise any payment to those who ordain them; rather, they receive it — after the manner of worldly hired hands, who will bind themselves to serve no one unless they have been assured of the agreed wage. For otherwise they are charged with plotting theft from another’s property, which they scheme to appropriate for themselves under the pretense of another’s benefit. Whether dealing with the one type or the other, it is out of necessity and not superfluity, by free request and not by price, that they are summoned and bound to serve others’ interests. Hence, whether they are regarded as criminals or as innocent, they must necessarily be placed in charge of those to be governed without payment. That this does not occur among simoniac promoters, no one can be ignorant — for among them it is not the necessity or devotion of the people, but money alone, that commends candidates, whether criminal or blameless. For such promoters cannot truly say to those they are about to promote: “Come, that you may be promoted, because you are necessary or acceptable to the Church” — but only: “because you have given or will give us a hundred gold coins, or however many.” Has it ever been heard, even among Catholics of whatever wickedness, that anyone was told before ordination: “Come, that you may be promoted, because you have committed adultery, perjury, and the like”? God forbid! Rather: “You are necessary to the ministry and acceptable to the Church.” Nor does the candidate say: “Give me an ecclesiastical rank because I am a fornicator or a sacrilegious man” — but “because I wish to render service to the Church of God, so that I may merit the stipend owed from it.” Finally, in the manner of the prince of demons, who — as we read — promotes and crowns his own followers for the deception by which he hurls good people into sin, simoniac promoters likewise advance their accomplices. Their impudence is so great that they proclaim their guilt openly before all, saying to the seller: “We have given you the price; now give us the rank we sought, or return the price you received.” It is therefore established, according to the judgment of blessed Gregory, that among simoniac promoters vices are rewarded — namely, avarice, the root of all evils, and heretical perfidy — which are so much graver than fornication or adultery, as it is graver to sin against God than against one’s own body or against another person. Indeed, by such simoniac promoters the sinner is praised for the desires of his soul, and the wicked man is blessed. God forbid that this should be done in any way by Catholics, even when sinners are promoted by them.

## CHAPTER XXXVIII.

### In what respects the hired hands are to be heeded and in what respects not.

From this point, let the sheep themselves learn from the shepherd in what respect they ought to heed even the hired hands. Concerning those hired hands, the Gospel says: “The scribes and Pharisees have sat upon the seat of Moses. All things, therefore, whatsoever they shall say to you, observe and do.” Behold what great obedience the supreme good Shepherd requires to be paid to wicked hired hands — that the people should not only keep their words in mind, but also fulfill them in deed. Immediately afterward, he adds in what respect they are not to be attended to: “But according to their works, do ye not.” Why this, O Lord? Did you not command your people to observe and do all that they say? He adds: “For they say and do not.” As if to say: “Attend to the fact that they speak, and do not attend to the fact that they do not do what they say.” Here he briefly admonished that the teaching of their mouth and their ministry — which they received from God — is to be honored and received for the salvation of others; and that their carnal works, which they have from the evil one, are to be guarded against only for one’s own protection from perdition. Either let the sheep not follow the works of the hired hands, lest, since those hired hands say and do not, the sheep likewise hear them and do not act, because they observe that those hired hands speak and do not practice. As for the intention with which these hired hands do their works, the Lord makes clear when he says: “All their works they do for the sake of being seen by men” — thus showing summarily that they seek nothing, whether through preaching or through their ministry, except to be known and to please men. Then he discloses what works of theirs he means: “They bind heavy and insupportable burdens, and lay them on men’s shoulders, but with a finger of their own they will not move them. And they widen their phylacteries, and enlarge their fringes. And they love the first places at feasts, and the first chairs in the synagogues, and salutations in the market place, and to be called by men, Rabbi.” By these words of our Redeemer they are not reproached for imposing the burdens of God’s commandments upon people — for that is recognized as belonging to their office — but for making them insupportable, and for not relieving them by even the smallest act of their own. Nor because they have phylacteries and fringes, but because they widen the one and magnify the other; nor because they recline first as masters, or sit, or are greeted, or called Rabbi — but because they love these things alone. And yet, although so many ministers of Christ are of this kind — seeking nothing of Christ’s but only the world’s advantage in Christ’s ministry which they administer — Christ nonetheless wills that what belongs to them be faithfully rendered: that is, that they be heard, honored, and remunerated with the tithes and offerings of the faithful. Such men who are so corrupt in the sight of people — what must they be in the sight of God? And yet God tolerates them, both openly and secretly, whether they are truly wicked or merely feigning goodness, and he does not defraud them of the wage they seek. For since they are ministers of Christ, though they do not preside well, they are worthy of simple present honor. For “the presbyters who rule well,” as the Apostle says, “are worthy of double honor, especially those who labor in word and doctrine. For the scripture says: ‘Thou shalt not muzzle the ox that treadeth out the corn’; and: ‘The laborer is worthy of his hire.’” As for what the Apostle’s judgment is of the hired hands, if it is required, his letter to the Colossians \[i.e., Philippians\] shows it thus: “Some indeed preach Christ even out of envy and contention, and some also out of good will. Some out of charity, knowing that I am set for the defense of the gospel. And some out of contention preach Christ not sincerely, supposing that they raise affliction to my bonds. But what then? So that by all means, whether by occasion or by truth, Christ is preached: and in this I rejoice, yea, and will rejoice.” From the judgment of so great a teacher we learn how necessary the hired hands are; concerning those who preach Christ even insincerely, he expresses such joy that so great an Apostle testifies that he rejoices and will rejoice, whether Christ is preached out of envy, through contention and deriding of good men — so long as Christ is proclaimed. And who does not know how great a sin envy is? Yet if Christ is preached even through envy itself, as through contention and disparagement of the good, the Apostle rejoices. And how great an imperfection it is to seize upon the occasion of preaching for the sole benefit of the body! And yet the Apostle rejoices no less over such preaching. Therefore, let neither good works be assumed on account of good preaching and ministry, nor let the goods of holy preaching and ministry be despised on account of evil works. Hence, if a cluster of grapes hanging from a hedge is to be plucked, the thorns of the hedge must be avoided.
