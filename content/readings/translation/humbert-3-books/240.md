Now, either through ignorance of, or through disregard or neglect of, the sins and failings of human frailty, the dignity of the priesthood is sometimes granted to unworthy persons; through whose promotion, either the necessity or benefit of their neighbors in the Catholic Church is temporarily served, or their wishes are complied with. Since these persons belong to the Catholic faith, it must be believed that when they are promoted by Catholics, the Holy Spirit is conferred upon them — whom they possess, if not for their own salvation, yet for the perfecting of the salvation of others. The Holy Spirit is therefore present to them in their ministry and absent from them in their transgressions. He is present to the ministry, which he acknowledges as belonging to their faith, recognized by the faithful; he is absent from their iniquity, which he does not acknowledge. He deigns to be present in his sacraments to those who serve him therein, and he disdains to be present in the corruption of morals to those who resist him. And so, by whatever small measure of faith in their hearts the Holy Spirit is invited and poured out through the ministry of their mouth and hands for the sanctification of things unto the salvation of his own people, yet he is provoked to condemn the wicked, being offended by the faithlessness of their crimes. Since they are ministers and yet wicked, he accepts their ministry, despises and punishes their crimes — present to them as ministers, absent from them as wicked. To such persons that saying of the prophet can fittingly be applied, where he says to the Lord: “You are near to their mouth and far from their heart.” For the Holy Spirit, presiding in the hearts of such ministers according to the faith of which he is the author, makes petition before God on behalf of the saints and obtains whatever is fitting for them before God. This is indeed said metonymically to be his action, because he causes the petition to be made and obtained, since he himself, together with the Father and the Son, is petitioned and gives — even through the ministry imposed upon criminals, but yet Catholics, by Catholics — so that the necessity or benefit of Catholics may be served in that time and place. These ministers, to be sure, while many evils accuse them, yet certain goods excuse them: namely faith, which is capable of hope and charity in its own measure; then necessity and utility; the devotion and petition of the Church; and finally, their ordination free of charge \[*gratuita ordinatio*\] by those who received it free of charge. If in their case the innocence of life required by canonical discipline is absent, yet obedience is present, or at least their promotion alone to ecclesiastical ministry without any trafficking — in accordance with the rule given by the Lord Jesus Christ himself — by which the whole is certainly confirmed on one side, even if it is weakened on the other. If this dominical rule were absent, no one, however innocent in other respects, could give or receive any grace at all. It is clear, therefore, that by this rule alone even the wicked are directed in some measure, so long as they consent to it; and without it even the upright are not a little corrupted, so long as they dissent from it. Accordingly, in ministers promoted in the manner described, or those who have fallen into some crime after promotion, the received honor endures so long as the Church either does not know of their faults, or disregards them and tolerates such persons, out of hope for their repentance, for the benefit of others. So long as their deposition is for any reason delayed by those whose concern it is, they are permitted to perform the office which they no longer deserve; and everything that they do in the meantime is held sacred by virtue of the ministry previously entrusted to them, or subsequently permitted. This, though it redounds to their own punishment, yet profits others unto life. Such persons are truly deposed when they are deposed, because, even if not by merit, they are recognized to have received a true promotion in terms of office. Nor are those who are canonically promoted by them before their deposition condemned or deposed along with them, nor are the things administered by them in a Catholic manner invalidated.
