## CAPUT XXXV.

### A Recapitulation of the Lord’s Teaching on Good Shepherds and Hirelings.

Although the Lord’s words suffice for the full discernment of the good and the bad, we think they should nonetheless be briefly recapitulated for the sake of some slower minds. First, concerning the good shepherds, we note only this: that they are members of the Supreme Shepherd himself, in whom alone, as in a single head, all are made perfect — through him, under him, with him — one shepherd of one fold. These, whether bishops or other rectors and ministers of the Church, as sons and household-born servants \[*vernaculi*\] of the great Father of the household, feed his sheep not for themselves but for him; not seeking their own gain but his, content with simple food and clothing, which the Father of the household has allotted them from the milk and wool of his own sheep. For they exact tithes and firstfruits and the offerings of the faithful, and the reverence of due subjection, for this purpose alone: that they may be sufficiently able to feed the sheep, lest they fail along the way through hunger and nakedness. They go before the sheep and know them and are known by them. They provide the sheep with wholesome and life-giving pasture, and for the season now coax, now rebuke. They diligently guard the sheep that are fat and strong, and assiduously labor to heal, restore, and strengthen those that are weak and feeble or broken. They drive wolves away from the sheep with staves and loud cries, opposing their vigilance, in defense of the sheep, against spiritual or earthly powers — lest the devil, tempting spiritually, or any secular aggressor, persecuting carnally, seize and scatter them. Hence they sometimes bravely face grave dangers in resisting such forces, and cheerfully lay down their own bodies’ lives for their devoted sheep. By this sign above all they are recognized as good shepherds: that they do not flee when they see the wolf coming. Such persons evidently enter the sheepfold through the gate, Christ — freely chosen and promoted to this ministry, they freely and sincerely preach Christ in word and deed. For they do not receive temporal stipends from the sheep because they preach the Gospel, but in order that they may be able to preach it; and not so that they may grow rich or shine in temporal things, but that they may provide for their temporal necessity unto the everlasting salvation of Christ’s sheep. For them, in their homeland \[*in patria*\], nothing is an obstacle on account of the expenses they bore on the way, since they did not seek from those expenses to fill their own temporal purses, but to live temporally among their brethren in Christ — having made use here of the stipends due them in such a way that they may there enjoy their full reward. We have spoken of the very few elect; let us now speak of the many who are called to the ministry of pastoral care. And first, something must be said about the name “hireling.” “Hireling” \[*mercennarius*\] is a name compounded from “wage” \[*merces*\] and “year” \[*annus*\], because he is hired for an annual wage to manage affairs or to feed the sheep of his employer. Although he feeds them, he is declared by the Lord not to be a shepherd, because the sheep are not his own. For he does not seek their salvation and increase in them as in his own property, but only his own wage from them as from another’s. This the coming wolf makes plain, because the moment it is seen it puts the hireling to flight; he would not flee if the sheep were his own, or if he were himself the shepherd. But because he is a hireling, he flees; for he has no concern for the sheep, whose life and good health he does not seek to profit by, but only the wage for pasturing — and since it is this that concerns him, and not the sheep, he does not free them or defend them at any risk to himself from the dangers that press in upon them, but reserves himself only for the agreed wage, on account of which occasion he was pasturing the sheep.
