But if one considers the foregoing words of the Lord, one will find that thieves and robbers are described in more explicit terms by John than pseudo-prophets and pseudo-Christs are by the other evangelists — even though those writers call the same men pseudo-prophets or pseudo-Christs whom John calls thieves and robbers. The other evangelists gave no mark by which to distinguish them, except that they would perform great signs and wonders — a criterion that not only fails to draw the unwary back from error, but can even lead the cautious into error. John, however, pointed them all out as if with a finger, by their manner of entry and their voice, so that henceforth no one could in any way excuse his own ruin by pleading ignorance, if he did not fear to hear and follow thieves who had been thus clearly identified for him. And indeed, if the sheep are at times deprived of free judgment concerning the manner of entry of one who is to rule them, they can nonetheless refuse to hear the voice of strangers and refuse to follow them, but flee from them instead. And if they see someone clothed in the appearance of a true shepherd, let them not dare to hear and follow him until they have ascertained from his manner of entry and his voice whether he is a shepherd or a robber — and let them say to him with the praiseworthy curiosity of Joshua son of Nun: “Are you one of ours, or of our adversaries?” \[cf. Joshua 5:13\]

## CHAPTER XLIII.

### On the difference between hirelings and thieves, and that heretics are not to be tolerated by Catholics in the way that badly-living Catholic ministers are.

From what has gone before it is now sufficiently clear how great is the distance between thieves and robbers on the one hand and hirelings on the other. Hirelings are to be obeyed in all that they say, as the Lord commands concerning them: “All things whatsoever they shall say to you, observe and do” \[Matthew 23:3\]; but thieves and robbers may not even be heard, because as soon as they are heard, one is lost. And indeed hirelings are such that even the Apostle rejoices in their preaching \[cf. Philippians 1:18\]; but thieves and robbers are such that the same Apostle, weeping, calls them corrupters of the word of God and enemies of the cross of Christ \[cf. 2 Corinthians 2:17; Philippians 3:18\]. Hirelings, too, though they seek their own interests and not Christ’s, yet seek for themselves no more than what is owed and commanded to be rendered to those who feed Christ’s sheep — namely, tithes and due reverence — and in demanding these they do not transgress, because they are seeking to render to God what belongs to God. They do transgress, however, in seeking these things for themselves alone, claiming them as entirely their own; yet even so, insofar as they temporarily feed the sheep of God’s pasture for the sake of these rewards, they are in this respect rendering to God what is God’s. For that tithes and other offerings of the faithful are owed and rendered to the Lord alone, and that they are divinely appointed for the temporal support of his ministers of whatever quality, blessed Jerome expounds thus on Malachi: “Who is there among you, O priests, who would shut the door and kindle my altar gratuitously? For there is no one among you, down to the last of the ministers — not the pontiff, I say, not the priest and the rest, but not even the doorkeeper or the one who lays fire to the altar to burn the holocausts — who does not receive a wage from me for his labor.” \[cf. Jerome, Commentary on Malachi 1:10\] He says this signifying the tithes of all the produce offered by the people — which, when robbers exact them, they are not demanding what is their own. For they are owed no stipend for divine ministry, of which they are entirely devoid, but only the gallows or extreme punishment for their banditry. Yet these men, alas, very often receive not only what they find set aside for the ministers of Christ, but over and above that what is forbidden to them — since they come and are capable of nothing other than to steal and slaughter and destroy. And who in his right mind considers that thieves and robbers of this kind are to be tolerated by Catholics, when the Lord judges that they are to be fled, testifying that those who even hear them are not his sheep? And with what face does anyone preach that the ministry of such men must be tolerated and accepted until they are deposed by a synod — and that everything done in the sheepfold by their ministry in the meantime is good and holy — when their only ministry is one of stealing, slaughtering, and destroying?
