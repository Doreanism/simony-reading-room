After all, the Arians, though they err most greatly in asserting that the Son is lesser than the Father and a creature, and likewise the Holy Spirit lesser than the Father and the Son and a creature, nonetheless call both God, and do not contend that either of them is subject to any creature whatsoever, save only to God the Father, the creator of all things visible and invisible. But simoniacs, not content with such great sacrilege as the Arians commit, believe that the omnipotent Holy Spirit is not merely lesser than the Father and the Son, or subject only to the Father and the Son, but actually inferior to themselves and subject to themselves — as if he were some purchasable and worthless chattel — so that, whether he wills or not, he obeys their commands and voices, and, drawn by the cord of their money or as if bound by their golden chains, he ministers the fullness of his sanctification and grace to their sacrilegious offices and execrable consecrations, and cooperates with their trafficking and their actions. To entertain this view even for a moment is an intolerable and perhaps unforgivable blasphemy, since it is to sin against the Spirit or at the very least to speak a word against him. It was by this view alone that Simon, the founder and standard-bearer of their sect — from whom they take their name — was irrecoverably condemned. He could indeed in some way be excused by the novelty of the religion or by ignorance amid such a manifest outpouring of the Holy Spirit; dazzled and astonished by it, being still a novice in baptism and a beginner in the knowledge of the faith, he suddenly, like an eager and impatient disciple, deemed that so inestimable a gift of knowledge was to be sought and obtained from its effective masters, even at the greatest cost of money. But he was found inexcusable by the blessed apostles Peter and John — so much so that the most gentle Peter, son of Bar-Jonah, that is, son of the dove, the most simple and innocent of men, with no gentleness held in reserve, no modest rebuke given in advance as is customary with beginners, no satisfaction demanded, no amendment awaited, no warning added that he should beware of attempting any such thing thereafter, burned with such great zeal against that most wretched man, and raged with such severity, that, without preserving the order of judgment, he first delivered the irrevocable sentence, striking the accused with an anathema, saying: “Your money perish with you in damnation” — and then made known to him the entire reason for so great and sudden a condemnation, adding: “Because you thought that the gift of God could be possessed with money.” Presently, denying him any share whatsoever in spiritual grace, he says: “You have no part or lot in this word” — that is, in this gift. And finally, having thus execrated his wickedness and the depravity of his heart, he rebuked him vehemently: “For your heart is not right before God; for I see that you are in the gall of bitterness and in the bond of iniquity.” And lastly, urging him to penance, he made it uncertain and doubtful whether so great a blasphemy could be forgiven, saying: “Repent therefore of this your wickedness, and pray to God, if perhaps the thought of your heart may be forgiven you.” In Peter’s final sentence against Simon, nothing is condemned but the thought of his heart, just as in the first sentence only the opinion is condemned.

## CHAPTER IV.

It is therefore clearer than light to all men of sound mind that Peter — a man of excessive patience and rustic simplicity — in no way pronounced so sudden and so strict a sentence out of his own impulse and human passion or emotion, but rather that the Holy Spirit himself, dwelling in Peter as in his own temple, could not endure such great injury, blasphemy, and insult, and, utterly indignant — whose wrath no one could now withstand — was moved, as is read metaphorically in Genesis, with inward sorrow of heart.
