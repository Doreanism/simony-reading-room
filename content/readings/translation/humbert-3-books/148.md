Therefore he was not only a defrauder but also a sacrilegious thief, because what had previously been within his power to retain or sell had, by his vow and profession, begun to be sacred; and since he stole something sacred from what was sacred — defrauding the saints of his wealth — he was rightly subject to a harsher punishment than if he had taken something sacred from what was not sacred, or what was not sacred from what was sacred. Let those who profess the common life take careful note of this, lest after \[such\] prohibitions they defraud one another — even of the last portion of what was once their private wealth — and so that they, along with all who presume to commit any fraud or robbery against the church, may suffer in their soul the damnation that those \[Ananias and Sapphira\] endured in their bodies, which is all the more severe in that the soul is better than the body. Let them also consider that every thought, word, and action of theirs in the course of the fraud and after it — even if it may seem good to men — is nonetheless held by the Holy Spirit to be a lie and a temptation, as Peter said to Ananias: “Why has Satan tempted your heart to lie to the Holy Spirit and to defraud from the price of the land? Why have you conceived this thing in your heart? You have not lied to men, but to God.” Here it is clearly shown that by placing the fraud in his heart he lied to God. And a little later he says to his wife: “Why have you agreed together to tempt the Spirit of the Lord?”

## CAPUT VIII.

### On the fact that what is vowed to the Lord must not only be vowed but also rendered.

But lest anyone of little faith say to himself that if what is vowed is required so strictly, one must be careful henceforth not to vow such things at all — let him hear the Psalmist, forewarning in the Holy Spirit: “Vow.” And lest you think it sufficient merely to vow, he immediately adds: “And render.” And as if you were to ask to whom it is to be vowed and rendered, he answers: “To the Lord your God.” And lest perhaps you believe that you or others who offer external gifts — for example, oil, wax, and incense, fasting, and many other such things — are thereby excepted, he adds: “All you who round about him offer gifts” — as if to say: I do not criticize you for offering your external gifts to the Lord, but I exhort you to offer yourselves as well — that is, your souls and bodies as a living sacrifice, holy and pleasing to God — not conformed to this age but reformed in the renewal of your understanding, so that you may discern what the will of God is, what is good and well-pleasing and what is perfect. And lest you neglect this vow, he impresses upon you to whom you have vowed, saying: “To the terrible one.” How terrible, you ask? He adds: “And to him who takes away the spirit of princes.” And, striving to reveal the full measure of his terror — truly ineffable — to mortal men, he concludes thus: “Terrible among all the kings of the earth.” Therefore, since it is altogether unworthy that we should be ungrateful to our Lord Jesus Christ, who has accomplished and suffered such great things in us and for us, let us vow and render to him all that we have, all that we live, and all that we are. Otherwise, it is better not to vow than, after vowing, not to render; since even through Moses it is shown that it is a sin if we are slow to render a vow. If it is a sin to delay in rendering, how much more so if we fail to render at all? For if we vow something to a fellow human being and do not render it — having as it were defrauded him of what belongs to him — we will feel his opposition as much as he is able. But if, having undertaken to render a vow to God, we fall short, we turn back to Satan and become as a dog returning to its vomit and a sow washed and wallowing in the mire.
