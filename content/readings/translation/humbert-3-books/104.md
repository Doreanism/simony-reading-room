Corrector.

It is the manner of poisoners to coat the rim of a deadly cup with a little honey, so that the sweetness tasted first may cause whatever lethal remainder lies beneath to be swallowed without hesitation. This is what that slanderer is charged with having done, when, weaving his snares of chapters — which he had corrupted by begging them from every quarter and posting them at the door of his disputation — he begins:

Corruptor.

After this, illustrious testimonies follow, by which the ordination of Pope Formosus is shown to be valid and legitimate.

Corrector.

In order to secure for this little proposition the greatest favor and to make it appear venerable to all, he falsely captioned it as from the authority of the greatest and first synod — from the Council of Nicaea. In this presumption, what sort of man he should be considered became apparent in what followed: one who has defiled the very gateway of his own narrative with a lie — or rather, with a sacrilege. For that first chapter, or that verse, is not to be found in the entire Nicene council, nor in any council after it; nor is anything about whether the ordination of Pope Formosus ought to be considered valid or void treated or demonstrated in the Nicene council or in any council after it — since even to suspect any such thing in the slightest is reckoned the height of absurdity. For between the Nicene synod and Pope Formosus there are found five hundred years and more — namely, the interval between the great Constantine and Emperor Arnulf, by whose assistance Formosus prevailed in retaining the Roman pontificate. Moreover, Arnulf is reckoned the sixth or seventh emperor after Charlemagne. But if he thought that chapter ought to be captioned as from the Nicene council because some of the chapters he subsequently attached are indeed from that council, let him be told, now that he has been corrected, that he has attached no chapter from that council except the single one on the Cathari. From this, therefore, it is apparent what sort of slanderer he is — one who, overcome by error, labors impudently even through lying to appear victorious against the greatest, most universally known, and venerable council. But now let his statements be sifted and examined.

## CHAPTER II.

Having set forth a chapter of Pope Leo — the blessed and great doctor — he attempts to corroborate his error from it, as if some mention had been made there of simoniacs, or at least of other heretics, and not rather of Catholics, if perchance the presiding officers of the church had on occasion consecrated or did consecrate someone through ignorance, presumption, or necessity, not in strict accordance with the canons. For it could happen and can happen that one or two of the three required elements, or all three together, be lacking to those who are consecrated — namely, that they were not elected by the clergy, not sought by the people, and not consecrated by the provincial bishops with the judgment of the metropolitan — and yet their consecration is not lacking, since it was performed without the selling and buying of money, even if it may waver, be unsettled, or go astray in respect to the three aforesaid elements. Otherwise, if there were no episcopal consecration in them at all, there would be absolutely no ordination by them — even if they were to ordain clerics in churches belonging to their own bishops, with the customary judgment of those presiding over them, so to speak. For no church whatsoever, and no judgment of those presiding over it, could or will ever be able to bring it about that anyone could give to another what he himself is shown in no way to possess — namely, to minister to others a valid consecration by his prayer and action, when he himself could in no way receive or hold it for himself. Would not the ordination of a Catholic layman in this respect be considered more praiseworthy than that of any heretical bishop, if one could give by the mere judgment of bishops presiding over churches what one does not have?
