Nor is someone immediately to be reckoned a heretic if he has entertained some view contrary to the faith, but only when he has obstinately and irrationally defended his own opinions and views. And therefore he is to be shunned without delay, as the same Apostle says: “After a first and second warning, have nothing more to do with a heretic. For such a person is warped and sinful, being self-condemned.” But if a heretic with such abomination and such swift condemnation is to be damned and rejected, what does his defender deserve? For one who defends the error of others is far more damnable and notable than those others, because he not only errs himself but prepares and confirms stumbling blocks of error for others. Hence, because he is the teacher of error, such a person is rightly called not only a heretic but also a heresiarch — as one who is first in error.

Let the defenders who obstinately champion the cause of simoniacs — who are certainly the disciples or followers of Simon Magus, the first heretic after grace was revealed — consider therefore at what rank or by what name they should be held among the faithful. But now, Holy Spirit, come and join yourself to our voice. Apply rebuke, and if it be possible, correction to the simoniacal madness of him who speaks a word against you and blasphemes you, and through me — your mute beast of burden — speaking by your omnipotence in a human voice from heaven, restrain the foolishness of the false prophets who desire to be teachers of the law but understand neither what they say nor what they assert. Defend, O most free God and Lord, equal in all things and co-eternal with God the Father and the Son, your singular freedom against the sacrilegious merchants, against the money-laden heirs of Simon of Samaria, lest against the judgment given by the great Simon Peter at the very dawn of Christianity even your little and simple ones be led into such a pernicious error as to believe — or what is considered more tolerable, to suppose — that you, the supreme and incomparable gift of God, can be possessed with money; which is even a crime to say, as though you were a purchased slave, or a saleable beast of burden, or a cheap chattel. And to what end will come that liberty of ours, in which through you we already presume and boast in hope, in the liberty of the children of God, with the Apostle proclaiming: “Where the Spirit of the Lord is, there is liberty”? And “As many as are led by the Spirit of God, these are the sons of God” — if you yourself are made subject, an addicted slave to the money-changers and servants of mammon, and you, O dove, either by necessity or by will offer yourself to be torn apart by kites? But if at the nod of such wicked servants and most vile merchants you descend or are poured out upon the sanctifying of creatures, then it must be said that you are either violently compelled to this by fear of such masters — as though the instruments and deeds of your purchase were displayed before you — or certainly that you are pleasurably drawn, by a kind of love and reverence for money, into harmony and agreement with your perverse masters. And thus grace is no longer grace, since it is not received freely. If therefore you are compelled by fear, your omnipotence and freedom perish, because you no longer breathe where and when you will. If however you are drawn by love, then blessed are not those poor in spirit, but wretched ones — since you are found to be a persistent pursuer of money, which you falsely command to be despised and scattered without any future reward. But let nothing of the sort be even lightly suspected of you, O inviolable and irreproachable Spirit of truth, whom God has given us as a pledge of faith and hope — that, obligated by the pledge of money, you should be proclaimed to simoniacs not as Lord of lords but now, three degrees below, or even a thousand degrees below, as the slave of the worst servants of a worse master, namely mammon. Therefore let us now refute the words of those who blaspheme you and who presume to claim for themselves an undeserved priesthood, with counter-words, your fire going before us and burning up the reckless ones; and at last let their alien fire, once scattered here and there, be hammered into bronze censers — sanctified, as it were, by the deaths of sinners — and affixed to the altar of the Lord, so that from their words, which are vocal yet obscure, the pure sayings of the Lord, like silver and gold compared to bronze, may become better known and shine more clearly and evidently. For “there must be heresies,” says the Apostle, “so that those who are approved may be made manifest among you.”
