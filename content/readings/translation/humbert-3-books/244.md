## CAPUT XXXVI.

### On the Wage of Hirelings and Their Promotion.

Without doubt all are of this kind who, bearing the name of shepherd and sitting upon the pastoral throne, govern the Church of Christ, yet seek only their own interests and not Christ’s. And what are their interests? The first places at banquets and the chief seats in synagogues, and greetings in the choir, and to be called “rabbi” by men \[cf. Matthew 23:6–7\]. Then the wool and milk of the sheep — that is, all the revenues of the faithful people, which they convert to their own use and profit, which they exact with such violence out of devotion to their own luxury and avarice, that they sometimes wring and extort from the sheep not only milk and wool but their very blood as well, compelling the destitute by tyrannical lordship to render what they do not have. Such men thrust themselves forward against no attack on behalf of the sheep, nor do they even murmur if they sense that the wolves might harm them in any way, or if they fear that the wool and milk may for a time be withheld by sheep in poor health that refuse to be healed. Yet they are not blamed for justly receiving what the Lord has delegated to them, but because they love and seek only the giving and use of these things, not their fruit — and pasture the sheep for no other reason than these things, which are so owed to shepherds that it goes ill for those who do not render them, even if it does not go well for those who receive only them. Since such men preach Christ by Catholic profession, they enter through the gate into the sheepfold, to lead the sheep out and bring them back; yet because they are servants and not sons, they do not remain in the house forever. For they are not to be said to have climbed in from elsewhere, or to pasture the sheep outside the Church, or to have come in order to steal and slaughter and destroy; but for this purpose alone, invited by others, that they may guard and pasture \[the sheep\] in order to receive their wage. Yet because they do this more out of love of temporal wage than of everlasting \[reward\], they are not shepherds but hirelings — because, not content with food and clothing as household servants of the Father, they seek annual wages by which to stuff their private purses, lest if at some point they are removed from care of the sheep — which are not their own — they be left empty-handed. To such persons, since they receive in this life everything they seek from the sheep, it will be said in the homeland: “Amen, I say to you, you have received your reward” \[Matthew 6:2\]. These, then, are certainly not among the few who are saved, because they do not strive now to enter through the narrow gate — but among the many who afterwards seek to enter and will not be able. For when, as Luke testifies, the Father of the household has entered and shut the door, they will begin to stand outside and knock at the door, saying: “Lord, open for us”; and he who answers will say to them: “I do not know you or where you come from.” Then they will begin to say: “We ate and drank before you, and you taught in our streets” — or, as Matthew says: “We prophesied in your name, and cast out demons in your name, and performed many mighty works in your name” \[Matthew 7:22\]. And then he will declare to them: “I never knew you. Depart from me, all you workers of iniquity” \[Matthew 7:23\]. Yet these hirelings, so long as they are permitted to preside over the faithful, are necessary — because they dispense and preach the sacraments of the Church and the Catholic faith, on the occasion of their wage. Hence their corrupt life is tolerated, and the office by which necessary things are provided to the flocks is honored. That consent was given to their promotion — since the common necessity, and not private greed, was the intermediary — must not be doubted: it was granted by many of the faithful consulting their own salvation according to the necessity of the time, in accordance with what the Lord promises to two who consent together, saying: “If two of you agree on earth about any matter they ask, it shall be done for them by my Father” \[Matthew 18:19\]. Hence, with necessity compelling, their present or past conduct is not scrutinized, but sole regard is given to the ministry and preaching of the Christian faith in such persons. Their conduct even after promotion is sometimes tolerated by the Church — unless by chance they deviate from the faith.
