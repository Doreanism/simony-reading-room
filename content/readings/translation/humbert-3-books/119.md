And even if health follows, the scar is nonetheless never entirely eliminated, for after penance no one is to be admitted to ecclesiastical honor. It is therefore clear by a double argument that those who are shown to have been ordained by heretics are to be rejected: both because they became partakers of them, and because they are now penitents. Let them therefore consider what great ruin they have incurred, since even the medicine itself — that is, penance — stands in their way, preventing them from aspiring to ecclesiastical honor. But lest those who cling to the words alone and neglect the meaning suppose they have some ground in what is read in the aforementioned passage — namely, that he who has lost honor cannot give honor, and that one who has become a partaker of the condemned man — as though it were said of one who once had honor and later lost it, or who was once regularly ordained and later condemned — let them know it is not to be understood in this way from other divine scriptures; rather, he lost honor at the very moment when he sought to receive honor in a disordered way, and was condemned there where he did not fear to become a heretic. For it is as though in the very act of receiving he lost it — or certainly he was condemned in that he deserved to be barred from receiving ecclesiastical honor henceforth, as the Psalmist says: “You cast them down as they were being raised up.” For he does not say “You cast them down after they had been raised up,” but “as they were being raised up.”

## CHAPTER XIII.

The blessed Gregory also speaks in the *Moralia* concerning the ostrich, whom God had deprived of wisdom and had not granted understanding, saying: Although to deprive and to withhold are different things, he repeated by adding “did not give” what he had first said “deprived.” As if to say: what I said by “deprived” means not that he unjustly took wisdom away, but that he justly “did not give” it. Hence also the Lord is described as having hardened Pharaoh’s heart, not because he himself imparted hardness, but because, in accordance with his deserts, he did not soften it from above with any perceptible touch of infused fear. From the same blessed Gregory’s words, let it now be shown whether a simoniac ought to be considered or called a heretic.

### From his letter to Brunhild, queen of the Franks.

Nor do we pass over this matter, but utterly execrate and condemn it as most grave: that sacred orders there are conferred through the simoniac heresy, which was the first to arise against the Church and was condemned with a strict curse. Hence it comes about that the dignity of the priesthood falls into contempt and the holy office becomes a matter of crime. Reverence perishes, discipline is destroyed, because he who ought to have corrected faults instead commits them, and the censure of the honorable priesthood is brought to ruin by wicked ambition. For who will continue to venerate what is sold, or who will not think vile what is bought? Hence I grieve deeply and lament for that land, because, since they disdain to receive as a divine gift the Holy Spirit whom Almighty God deigns to bestow on men through the laying on of hands, but obtain him by means of payment, I do not think the priesthood there can long endure. For where the gifts of heavenly grace are deemed for sale, not life but money is sought for the service of God, and what is venerated is not God but riches.

It is reported that the simoniac heresy — which crept in first against God’s Church by diabolical planting, and was struck down and condemned at its very origin by the zeal of apostolic vengeance — holds sway within the borders of your kingdom.
