He mocks the brandished spear and pays no heed to the blare of the trumpet, for he holds in contempt the spiritual threat or sentence — which, like a spear, strikes from a distance — and the carnal condemnation that strikes close at hand. He equally disdains the anathema of any priest, and the laws of any earthly ruler. On their account, even the most base individual scorns to restore to holy places even a single foot’s length of all that the heretic \[i.e., the simoniac\] confirmed by his own hand. Thus what was first theft, and step by step became robbery, has now grown to such tyranny that whoever seeks to rule over churches or cities cannot obtain this unless he first confirms by oath and in writing, even to the common mob, that he will observe and defend the libelli of those heretics and sacrilegists. If such an oath is exacted from lesser authorities, it is exacted all the more from the highest emperors themselves. Nor are they permitted to receive the insignia of empire before they have sworn not only that they will not annul those writings, but that they will defend them. O liberty and piety of the Roman commonwealth! O generosity and power of the imperial majesty! The supreme ruler is compelled to swear that he should not observe the laws of the devout rulers who preceded him, but rather nullify them. He wishes to receive back what belongs to Caesar from those who refuse to render to God what belongs to God. Let him consider, I beg, what kind of empire this is that suddenly turns him from a Christian into a pagan — indeed, worse than a pagan, for he apostatizes from God. With the perverse he becomes perverse, with the sacrilegious, sacrilegious, and therefore worthy of death, because he consents to those who do such things. Indeed, he can no longer be said merely to consent to the perverse, but truly, so that they may do worse things, to command them — those to whom by his oath he supplies the license to retain with impunity what they had seized, and the audacity to seize with impunity whatever remains. For if, as the philosopher argues, one who does not forbid sin when he is able in effect commands it, how much more so one who not only does not forbid it when he could, but furthermore swears he will never forbid it — who nonetheless swears to God and the holy apostles and his priests that he will forbid such things and defend ecclesiastical goods.

## CAPUT XXXVII.

### On the incalculable malice of this heresy, and to what beast in the Apocalypse it is compared.

Thus blinded by the wickedness of the people and their own cowardice, the supreme rulers neglect to see how terrible are the works of God in his counsels over the sons of men, and therefore they fall — together with those whom they ought to have ruled and beneath those to whom they ought to have been superior — into the pit of eternal death. Compared to these rulers, the princes of the pagans are so much freer and more excellent, even in their very torments, as they were more generous and ready to preserve and restore to each his right in worldly affairs. For those rulers bestowed privileges with lavish generosity on the pontiffs of their religion (as they believed it to be) and on their temples, defended them with a strong hand in their own time, and provided for their protection by their laws against any diminution or alienation in times to come. The great Constantine turned their industry from superstition to religion, and the privileges that those men had granted to their priests and shrines, he granted to the churches of Christ and their ministers — and what his successors, all who were orthodox, strove to do likewise, down to these perilous times. O how much more tolerable for the churches of Christ were the Arian, Eunomian, Eutychian, or any other heresy! For though they tear and scatter the sheep of God with the savagery of wolves, they still do not bother to take away their sheepfolds and pastures, but leave them behind for the remaining and future sheep to dwell in and live from.
