Christ, the strongest of all and a jealous God, who would not endure the wife whom he had taken from the gentiles being given to any other, seized those who were wise to do evil but knew not how to do good in order to avenge so great an injury to himself — catching them in their cunning, as it were foxes. These three hundred represent only those heresies that applaud themselves in the name of Christian, such as the Simonian, the Sabellian, the Arian, and the rest, excepting the heresies of the Jews and pagans, for whom Christ crucified is either a stumbling block or folly.

## CHAPTER XXIII.

### The allegory of the foxes caught by Samson.

That the number three hundred designates the universality of Christians signed with the cross of Christ is shown by the letter Tau, which, formed in the shape of a cross, expresses that very number. Of this letter it is said in Ezekiel to the man clothed in linen: “Mark a Tau upon the foreheads of the men who groan and lament.” Lest the slayers touch them, it is added: “Upon whomsoever you see the Tau, do not kill.” This sign was also present to Gideon, when, instructed by a divine oracle, he overthrew the Midianites with three hundred men — as a figure of our Gideon, who, circling the vault of heaven, remained wholly within the womb of the virgin. He conquered and continues to conquer the world with three hundred armed men: with the trumpets of spiritual preaching, the jars of bodily frailty, and the lamps of divine miracles. These are the men he taught to deny themselves, take up their cross, and follow after him. Their sun — that is, Samson — afterward, since there is none who can hide from his heat and splendor, hunted out the deceitful and unclean foxes of the heresies from the sheepfold of his Church, which he flooded with the rays of righteousness, and separated them from it: three hundred in the semblance of piety but not in its power. He bound them tail to tail together, teaching that, however much they may turn their faces and mouths in different directions, they nevertheless conceal and hold at their hinder parts one and the same end of errors, as though from the knot of a single faithlessness. For although the individual heresies, seeking to raise their pride like a tower of Babylon against God and thrown into confusion by the diversity of tongues divinely wrought, may hold and profess different views about the Christian religion and openly contend against one another, yet in the end they are joined by the knot of a single impiety and secretly agree whenever they are found doing nothing other than striving to subvert faith with treachery and to seize Christ’s flock for themselves. In the midst of these — that is, at the joining of their tails — our Samson set the torches, where he stirred up their malicious harmony, joined at the end with mutual and intestine hatreds and the zeal of envy, against one another. Hence it is that, while each strives to drag the other after itself or to break free from it, all rage against one another with stubborn spirits. “There is no peace for the wicked,” says God. He lit these torches when, by an irrevocable anathema, as it were by the fire of his fury that would burn to the uttermost depths of hell, he openly inflamed their hatreds to their own ruin and to the ruin of many by the sentence of his Catholic \[Church\]. Thus, set loose, they wander as vagabonds and fugitives hither and thither, because when the wrath of God quickly flared up they perished from the right way. Finally, entering the fields of the Philistines, they burned up all the provisions of that adulterous and alien people together with themselves, because beyond all doubt those who consent to the adultery of Christ’s bride lose whatever good they had, through the fire of his just judgment, which the heresies carry as it were in their tails at their end. Hence the Philistines are interpreted as “falling by their mouth or cup,” with “their own” or “another’s” being understood. For whether aliens — that is, pagans — or their senseless and evilly-living Christian neighbors fall by another’s mouth when they believe the teachings of heretics (which are assuredly the teachings of demons), as did the wretched Goths, some of whom passed from paganism and others from the Catholic faith to the Arian heresy through the emperor Valens and Eudoxius of Constantinople — they also fall by their own mouth when they protect and exalt heretics. Nor without reason are their harvests, vineyards, and olive groves specifically said to have been consumed, since many other things also perished along with these.
