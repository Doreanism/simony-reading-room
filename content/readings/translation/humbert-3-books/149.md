Let simoniacs, around whom nearly this entire tragedy of ours revolves, come to their senses, and let them at least when admonished acknowledge what great fraud they perpetrate. If Ananias was struck with sudden death for fraud alone — marked by theft, sacrilege, lying, conspiracy, and temptation of the Spirit of God all at once — and that merely because he kept a little of the price of his small field out of anxiety for the morrow: let these men, if they are capable of it, reckon what theft, sacrilege, lying, conspiracy, and temptation of the Spirit of God they are liable for, and what punishment they deserve — men who, intending to defraud the Holy Spirit in concert, seek out that same \[Spirit\] as their intermediary and as the occasion for the price, so that by pursuing what belongs to another and another’s money they may fulfill their conspiracy whereby they have agreed to tempt the Holy Spirit himself. Since, however, he is not found by those who tempt him, all their thought, speech, and action is without doubt reckoned as lying to the Holy Spirit, even if it appears to men to show some mark of truth.

## CAPUT IX.

### That the Holy Spirit appears to the faithful but is hidden from the unfaithful, who do not seek him.

Moreover, that such lying, condemned by the Holy Spirit, brings damnation upon itself and upon its accomplices, while it brings sanctification to none — this the death of Ananias and Sapphira proclaims. Indeed, if God appears to those who have faith in him, it is plain that the Holy Spirit does not appear to simoniacs, who assail him with faithlessness and lying. For where there is lying, there too is faithlessness. Hence the Lord, who appears to the faithful, hides himself from the faithless. He appears to the Catholic faith, since it is in her and through her that he dwells in our hearts; but to faithless heresy — which he utterly abhors — he is never present except in anger and as avenger. Hence it follows that he is not found by them, since by defrauding and lying they tempt him — as men who have no faith in him — in accordance with what the apostle, warning against the unbelief of the Jews, says: “Neither let us tempt Christ, as some of them tempted, and were destroyed by serpents” \[1 Corinthians 10:9\]. Of these the Psalmist also says: “They tempted God in their hearts, asking meat for their souls,” as the narrative records, saying: “Is God among us, or not?” There is also the word of Wisdom: “The wicked shall seek me and shall not find me.” If “in this age” is understood, it must be taken in light of what precedes, as if to say: The wicked will try me, but they will not find me willing to favor them as they wish. For otherwise, who has sought the Lord and not found him? Since “his mercy endures forever,” and “he who seeks finds.” But if “in the age to come” is understood, then he will be sought and not found, because while he can be found, he is not now sought. Hence the prophet exhorting to truly seek the Lord says: “If you seek, seek.” For one who seeks God insincerely will never find him. “Seek,” says the Psalmist, “the Lord and be confirmed; seek his face always” \[Psalm 105:4\]. And yet, since it is altogether true that “he who seeks, finds,” let us grant that even simoniacs seek — not insincerely but greedily — and therefore find without doubt what they seek. But what is sought? The Psalm shows this reproachfully, saying: “O children of men, how long will you be dull of heart? Why do you love vanity and seek after lying?” \[Psalm 4:3\]. And what do they find? The father of lying, namely the devil, who, as the Lord attests, “stood not in the truth, because truth is not in him. When he speaks a lie, he speaks of his own, for he is a liar and the father of it” \[John 8:44\]. Hence too the wise man says: “The wicked man always seeks quarrels; but a cruel angel shall be sent against him” — the author, that is, of discord. If then one finds what he seeks, and if one who seeks lying or quarreling finds the father of lying or the author of discord — what do simoniac buyers and sellers seek? Earthly gain, without question, and to say it plainly and in sum: money alone. But what do they find? The temptation and snare of the devil, for in assailing the grace of God they wish to grow rich. And which devil? Mammon, to be sure, who presides over earthly gain and money. And to what end do they find him? That he may be their lord and they his servants, according to what is said: “The lover of money is the slave of mammon.”
