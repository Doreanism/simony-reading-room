And we take care neither out of any fear nor out of love of our Lord to avoid incurring his offense, nor do we recall that, like a harmful dog, we are to be cast headlong into a rapacious whirlpool and overwhelmed with the ultimate punishments. But what is still more to be lamented, we become fearsome to the Lord’s household and gentle to its plunderers. Moreover, such a stupor overcomes us that we believe what is ours belongs to the laity. Hence it is that certain among us, driven by blind ambition — which is the greatest evil that afflicts us within Italy — setting aside the ecclesiastical rectors in whose concern it is solely, rush unbidden and uncalled into palaces, shamelessly approach secular powers, and exhaust the income of their own patrimonies and resources by attaching themselves to and serving princes and their dependents, so that they may perhaps eventually hunt down some ecclesiastical dignity after prolonged, great, and continual distress, which they endure with utter insanity by day and by night, suffering exile, hunger, cold, and sleeplessness beyond measure. In this they are surely to be judged more detestable than the ambitious Catiline, who did not shrink from becoming a parricide of his country in his ambition for the consulship. For if he was called and held to be a parricide for disturbing and confounding an earthly and pagan kingdom through his desire for earthly dignity — because he attempted to rule even at the cost of the destruction of the city from which he had sprung, when he perceived the conscript fathers resisting his ambition — what, we ask, are those to be called and held who by their ambition confound and destroy the heavenly and Christian kingdom, that is to say, Holy Mother Church, with Christ the King of kings himself opposing them and with the council of all venerable fathers past and present? Who will doubt that these men are simoniacs beyond measure, who spend not only money but themselves besides in such dealings? For more tolerable are those who purchase what they seek all at once and at one time and in their own place, than those who pursue it piecemeal and many times, wandering and running from place to place throughout the days of their life. Such men, at length having obtained dignity from seculars not by any merit of their own but by servile compliance or human favor — a dignity which they ought to have obtained through ecclesiastical channels had they served well in their own churches — enter tyrannically and in fury into the labors of those others. Against such men the Church — that is, the clergy and people of whatever city — may complain and lament with the Lord saying: “They have reigned, but not from me; they have been made princes, but I knew it not.” Nevertheless, in saying this we do not accuse those who, on account of their distinguished character and public usefulness, at the invitation and request of princes and with the permission or command of their own rectors, live in and serve the secular court, and who sometimes, having been sought out from churches deprived of a rector and having none among their own who could succeed them profitably, take on their governance; such men are not to be said to have invaded the labors of others, but to have come to the aid of brothers who were failing in the work. And what is more praiseworthy, they devote to imperiled churches the advancement that was owed to their own church in whose pay and in which they served, and do not seek the dignity that was owed them as veterans in their own church or homeland. Such men are indeed to be revered all the more because they seek not their own things but those of Christ; and those who importune themselves shamelessly on this account do injury to these good men and to all good people — nay, they resist the will of the Almighty, in whose power all things are placed. So undisciplined and wandering are they, and so great is their number — not only among the noble and learned, but equally among the base and unlettered — that with churches and cloisters emptied and standing vacant, the palaces and houses of seculars can now scarcely contain the swarms of clerics. Among these are found some who hunt ecclesiastical honors not so much by clerical office as by medical, buffoon-like, or sycophantic \[arts\]. I lie if Italy has not produced more of this type than most, men promoted by no grace of character or learning but by buffoonery or deceitful flattery, or — what is thought the more excusable — by medicine alone. With such men no Christian should communicate, since they are truly headless and wander without letters or permission from their own rectors; yet in addition, ecclesiastical governance is entrusted to them, and having obtained it they not only confound and scatter it like the undisciplined and foolish, but also defile it with manifold fornication and foulness like the dissolute.
