But Uzziah, king of Judah, when he attempted the same thing — though burning with an indiscrete zeal for God — was struck with leprosy, even before he had approached the incense altar with the censer, which he had merely seized. As the blessed Jerome asserts in accordance with the historical account, Uzziah was a just man who did what was right before the Lord: he built the temple, constructed an aqueduct, offered vessels, and on account of this merit overcame his adversaries — which is the greatest sign of piety — and had many prophets in his realm. So long as the priest Zechariah, surnamed “the Understanding,” was alive, he pleased God and entered his sanctuary with all reverence. But after Zechariah died, Uzziah wished to offer gifts by his own hand, and he invaded the priestly order not so much with piety as with boldness. When the Levites and the other priests cried out, “Are you not Uzziah the king, and not a priest?” he refused to listen, and immediately leprosy broke out on his forehead — the very part of the body that a priest protected with a golden plate. On account of this, an earthquake is known to have arisen in the temple of God, which both the prophet Zechariah and the title of Amos also mention. He was therefore leprous until the day of his death, and lived in a separate house apart, while his son held the kingship. With this leper alive and, as far as it lay with him, devastating the priesthood, Isaiah was unable to see a vision of God. As long as that man held the kingdom in Judah, the prophet did not lift his eyes to heaven; the heavenly things were not opened to him; the Lord Sabaoth did not appear to him; nor was the thrice-holy name heard in the mystery of the faith. But when that man died, all these things revealed themselves in open light. And after a few words more: “While Uzziah lives, you do not understand, Isaiah, that you are wretched; you are not pricked to the heart, you are not moved; but when he died, then you perceived that you had unclean lips, then you acknowledged yourself unworthy of the vision of God.” Isaiah had sinned only in word, as a just man, and therefore had only unclean lips; and in my estimation, this was because he had not rebuked Uzziah when he rushed into the temple, nor had he, after the example of Elijah, openly denounced the impious man with a free voice.

## CAPUT XIIII.

### On the presumption of Saul and his punishment.

Saul too — who, as we read, was a chosen and good man, with none among the sons of Israel better than he, and humble in his own eyes — was he not repudiated on account of his usurpation of the priesthood, and did he not lose forever the kingdom that the Lord had prepared for him, and destroy himself by his own hand? Yet with what great necessity he usurped it on this occasion, we have not been reluctant to recall here according to the historical account: “The Philistines,” it says, “gathered to fight against Israel, thirty thousand horsemen, and six thousand chariots, and the rest of the multitude like sand on the seashore. And they came up and encamped in Michmash to the east of Beth-aven. When the men of Israel saw that they were in dire straits — for the people were hard pressed — they hid themselves in caves and thickets, in rocks and tombs and cisterns. And the Hebrews crossed the Jordan into the land of Gad and Gilead. While Saul was still in Gilgal, all the people following him trembled with fear. He waited seven days according to the appointment of Samuel, but Samuel did not come to Gilgal, and the people scattered from him. Saul therefore said: ‘Bring me the burnt offering and the peace offerings.’ And he offered the burnt offering; and just as he had finished offering the burnt offering, behold, Samuel arrived, and Saul went out to meet him to greet him.
