But Peter said to him: ‘Ananias, why has Satan tempted your heart to lie to the Holy Spirit and to defraud from the price of the land? While it remained, did it not remain yours, and when sold, was it not in your power? Why have you conceived this thing in your heart? You have not lied to men, but to God.’” Ananias, hearing these words, fell down and expired. And great fear came upon all who heard it. And the young men rose and wrapped him up, and carrying him out, buried him. Now after an interval of about three hours, his wife came in, not knowing what had happened. And Peter answered her: “Tell me, did you sell the land for so much?” And she said: “Yes, for so much.” Then Peter said to her: “Why have you agreed together to tempt the Spirit of the Lord? Behold, the feet of those who have buried your husband are at the door, and they will carry you out.” And immediately she fell down at his feet and expired. And the young men coming in found her dead and carried her out and buried her beside her husband. And great fear came upon the whole church and upon all who heard these things. This divine reading bears unmistakable witness that a defrauder — who has lied not to men but to God, that is, to the Holy Spirit — is convicted of putting the very Spirit of God to the test. As punishment, sudden death is brought upon the wretch; and his wife Sapphira incurs the same death a little while later — she who was at that moment ignorant of the killing but had previously been aware of the fraud. In them, too, the sentence is fulfilled to the letter which says: “Those who commit the act and those who consent to it are punished with the same penalty.” “And those who do such things are worthy of death — not only those who do them, but also those who consent to those who do them.” Blessed Peter argues that this consent was indeed a conspiracy, saying: “Why have you agreed together to tempt the Spirit of the Lord?” For since the Holy Spirit had inspired this charity in the primitive church by the outpouring of his grace — so that the multitude of believers was one heart and one soul, and no one said that anything he possessed was his own, but all things were held in common among them — no one who had once put his hand to this plow was permitted to look back; otherwise he is not fit for the kingdom of God, for by voluntarily becoming a defrauder of his former resolution he apostatizes entirely. Hence, according to the saying of the wise man: “He who transgresses from righteousness to sin — God has prepared him for the sword” — namely the sword by which, barred from his paradise, he is left to be associated with the devil and his angels. Furthermore, even if someone withdrawing from a higher resolution betakes himself to a good but lower one, that very good is no longer good for him but becomes sin. For example, if women who have professed virginal or widowed continence turn to marriage — which is certainly a good thing — they have damnation according to the Apostle, because they have made their first faith void. Hence also Moses says in Deuteronomy: “When you have made a vow to the Lord your God, you shall not delay to perform it, because the Lord your God will require it of you, and if you delay it will be imputed to you as a sin. If you do not wish to make a promise, you shall be without sin; but what has once gone forth from your lips, you shall observe and perform, as you promised the Lord your God, speaking of your own will and with your own mouth.” Therefore, the Holy Spirit, not bearing fraud to be done to his institution — that is, to the common life — even for a moment, as soon as the unfortunate Ananias entered and laid part of the price at the apostles’ feet, seized him and he expired, not permitted to remain in communion with the saints whom he had defrauded, even for a single moment — neither in life nor in death — nor was his wife, who had been privy to the fraud.
