Grammarians of old taught the force of this word \[catachresis\] as follows: catachresis is the appropriation of a foreign name, as in “fishpond” \[*piscina*\], even when it contains no fish. This trope allows a thing that lacks its own proper name to borrow one from another. It is therefore established that in this manner our holy fathers rightly borrowed foreign names from some resemblance between things, whenever they found that proper names — which are few and sometimes ill-suited to narrative — were unavailable. Thus many authors, and among them the blessed Pope Innocent, speak of certain persons as having been ordained by heretics, while immediately asserting that they received nothing from that ordination but damnation. Had he spoken properly, saying that those persons were condemned by heretics, he would be thought to have said that certain Catholics had been deposed or excommunicated by heretics. Furthermore, the blessed Gregory himself shows, at the end of his account, what intention lay behind his saying that sacred orders are conferred by such men, or that spiritual grace is sold or bought, when he declares that their blessing is turned into a curse and that they are promoted only to become heretics. Whoever, reflecting on his own office, did not burn with zeal to correct that crime would know without hesitation that he would have a portion with the author of this heresy. In much this same way it is said almost daily that someone sells God when he sells justice, yet it is certain that neither God nor justice can truly be sold — only offended by a price. For the seller grants nothing but falsehood, and the buyer receives nothing but the same, on account of which both incur equal damnation. And yet, though both are truly guilty and both condemned by the witness of their own conscience, one of them is abusively called a judge and the other acquitted, when in fact the latter is rather bound. So too among heretics there are said to be bishops, priests, and clerics of the remaining orders, churches, ordinations, consecrations, sacrifices, and all the other sacraments — but in name and appearance only, with none of the power and truth of those realities. Nor is this surprising, since among the pagans nearly all the names of our sacraments can be found, and yet no one but a madman would suppose that any sanctity or truth inheres in them rather than mere sacrilege; for whatever is without faith is sin. Let no one, then, misled by borrowed and improper language, stray from the proper and inward truth of the sacraments so as to believe or to preach that he or anyone else can receive — freely or otherwise — from someone who neither received nor possesses what is sought. For however earnestly you devote yourself to a fishpond \[*piscina*\] in hope of catching fish from it — fish it does not have, because it never received them — the name or likeness of the thing affords you nothing but vain exhaustion, whether you seek it freely or at a price. It remains, therefore, that simoniac priests, alongside other heretics in comparison with priests who are what they are by the grace of God, may fittingly be likened to little statues or images of gold and silver, of which the psalmist sings: “They have mouths and will not speak; they have eyes and will not see; they have ears and will not hear; they have noses and will not smell; there is no breath in their mouths. They have hands and will not touch; they have feet and will not walk; they will not cry out with their throat” \[Psalm 135:16–17\]. For it was not faith but faithlessness that invented them. Hence, while they carry the same names and bear to the ear or eye some likeness to true members, they retain nothing of the truth within themselves. For their mouth presents nothing but the empty name and some slight, yet vain, resemblance to a true mouth, since in its material, workmanship, and function it is utterly unlike — lacking bones, joints, sinews, veins, blood, flesh, skin, life, and motion, and the capacity for tasting, speaking, breathing, spitting, or coughing; and the same must be said of the other members, which render statues incapable of benefiting themselves or anyone else or accomplishing anything. Simoniac priests, therefore, like statues, usurp nothing but the empty name of Catholic priests and the outward resemblance of their worship; because they were made of gold and silver through the mediation of avarice — which is the service of idols — they have received from idolatry what amounts to golden or silver statues. And since everything about them is counterfeit, it is entirely clear that what are called sacraments among them are to be esteemed of the least account.
