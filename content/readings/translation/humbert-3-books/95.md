# THREE BOOKS OF CARDINAL HUMBERT AGAINST THE SIMONIACS.

EDITED BY

FRIEDRICH THANER.

Humbert, whom Berengar calls a Burgundian, was offered as a child to the monastery of Moyenmoutier \[Medianus\] in Lorraine, where the new observances of the Cluniac congregation had lately been received. There he applied himself so diligently to literary studies that he came to be regarded as the ornament of that house at Toul, and became most dear to Bruno, the diocesan bishop.

When Bruno was designated supreme pontiff by Emperor Henry III in 1048, intending to raise Humbert to high ecclesiastical honors, he immediately brought him to Rome in his retinue.

Shortly after being ordained archbishop of Sicily, Humbert was promoted in 1051 to cardinal bishop of Silva Candida and was entrusted with the weighty office of legate. From that time onward Humbert was most constantly in the pope’s company, so that his contemporary Othloh could rightly say: “He was the constant companion and most trusted counselor of the recently deceased Pope Leo.” Through Humbert and Frederick, then chancellor, Desiderius the monk — who later became Pope Victor III and was already very closely bound in friendship with Humbert — was introduced to and became a familiar of Leo IX.
