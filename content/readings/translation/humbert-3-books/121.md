The enemy of souls therefore often, when he cannot slip in what is wicked under an open face, cunningly injects a kind of specious appearance of piety and strives to supplant \[his victims\], perhaps suggesting that \[payment\] ought to be received from those who have means, so that there might be something to distribute to those who have none — provided that he might in this way pour his deadly poisons hidden under the shadow of almsgiving. For neither does the hunter ensnare the beast, nor the fowler the bird, nor the fisherman catch the fish, if the one were to set the snare openly or the other did not have the hook hidden in the bait. The craftiness of the enemy is therefore entirely to be feared and guarded against, lest he be able to slay with a hidden dart those whom he cannot overthrow by open temptation. Nor is it to be counted as almsgiving if what is received from illicit sources is dispensed to the poor, because he who with this intention receives evilly in order to distribute well is burdened rather than helped. That almsgiving is pleasing to our Redeemer which is gathered not from illicit means and iniquity, but is spent from permitted and honestly acquired goods. Hence also this is certain: that even if monasteries or hospices or anything else are built \[with such funds\], it profits nothing toward reward, since the perverse buyer of honor, entering the place of the seller, and establishing others in his own likeness by means of payments, destroys more by ordaining badly than the one who received money from him for ordination can build. So that we do not strive to receive anything with sin under the pretext of almsgiving, holy scripture openly forbids us, saying: “The sacrifices of the wicked are an abomination to the Lord, those offered out of crime.” For whatever is offered in God’s sacrifice out of crime does not appease the wrath of Almighty God but provokes it. Hence it is written again: “Honor the Lord from your just labors”; therefore he who takes wickedly in order to give well, it is established without doubt that he does not honor the Lord. Hence also it is said through Solomon: “He who offers sacrifice from the substance of the poor is as one who slaughters a son before his father’s eyes.” Let us consider how great a father’s grief would be if a son were slaughtered before his eyes, and from this we will easily understand with how great grief God Almighty is provoked when a sacrifice out of plunder is offered to him. It is therefore greatly to be avoided, dearest brother, to commit the sins of the simoniac heresy under the pretext of almsgiving. For it is one thing to give alms for one’s sins, another to commit sins for the sake of almsgiving.

And afterward the same \[Gregory\] to the same \[Syagrius\], on the same subject:

Lest anyone still wish to shield himself by invoking the excuse of evil custom, let the discretion of your brotherhood restrain them with the bridle of reason and not permit them to slip into unlawful acts, because whatever is deserving of punishment ought to be adduced not as an example for imitation but rather as an example for correction.

And shortly after:

Concerning these matters which have been spoken of above, we have wished, with God as our guide, to convene your brotherhood in synod, and in it, with our most reverend brother Bishop Aregius and our most beloved son Abbot Cyriacus as intermediaries, to condemn strictly under the interposition of anathema all things that are contrary to the holy canons — as we have said — namely: that no one shall presume to give any payment for obtaining sacred orders, nor receive \[payment\] for what has been given; nor shall anyone from lay status dare suddenly to attain to a place of sacred governance; nor shall women other than those permitted by the sacred canons live with priests.

Likewise, the same \[Gregory\] to Bishop Victor.

Whoever does not burn vehemently with the consideration of his office for the correction of this crime — namely the heresy of simoniacs and neophytes — let him not doubt that he has a share with him from whom this peculiar wickedness first took its beginning.
