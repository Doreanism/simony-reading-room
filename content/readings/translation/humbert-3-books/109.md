## Chapter VI.

Lest anyone perversely contend that pseudo-bishops of this kind cannot at any time make ordinations valid — just as neither can simoniacs or any other heretics — or conversely that simoniacs or any other heretics can make ordinations valid just as these can, and that these and those are to be treated in one and the same manner, on the grounds that these are called pseudo-bishops just as those are, that is, false bishops: let whoever he is take note that “false” is not always said in a uniform sense, but sometimes in part, sometimes entirely — as is most easily proved by the everyday example of a counterfeit coin. A coin may indeed be called counterfeit, yet it presents itself differently when viewed by the wakeful than when viewed by the sleeping. For the one deceives either in the stamp or in the material or certainly only in the weight, while the other deceives in every respect, because it neither existed nor exists nor will exist — it is entirely false and nothing. The one, even if human deception defrauds it to the highest or lowest degree of its quality, still gives it something, however little or great. The other, diabolical illusion fashions as void of all worth, attributing to it nothing but bare deception. By right, therefore, the pseudo-bishop from among Catholics is compared to a counterfeit coin — but one which human craft, even if imperfect in some respects, has nonetheless formed to be genuine in others. But the pseudo-bishop from among heretics is likewise by right likened to a counterfeit coin — but one which diabolical craft has fashioned as nothing in itself in every respect, yet perfect in illusion alone. Now our \[Catholic\] coin, however false, will still have some value, whatever its degree of falsity. For if it is rejected on account of weight, it will be approved on account of material and stamp; or if not on account of both together, it will at least be approved by one of these three in some manner. So too our pseudo-bishop, however little justice he may have in himself, is nonetheless proved worthy before God and those of sound mind, either by the sincerity of his faith or his life, or by his consecration alone, even though he may be rejected on account of the election. But the counterfeit coin of the sleeping — however perfect in appearance it may seem — is nothing, and of no value or weight in any part of itself; and to believe it to be, to have been, or to be likely to be a coin is a pernicious lie. So too the pseudo-bishop of heretics, however great his seeming continence or sanctity — if indeed it can be called that — is counted as nothing before God and before those of vigilant heart, because whatever is without faith is sin. To believe him in any way a bishop is not only sinful but an enormous sacrilege. Hence, for the confirmation of our assertion, it pleases us to insert in this little work the commemoration of certain persons who were neither elected by the clergy nor sought by the peoples nor consecrated by the co-provincial bishops with the judgment of the metropolitan, and yet were found, by their consecration alone, to be of such great authority, holiness, and usefulness in the Church of Christ that no one but a sacrilegious person would presume to call them pseudo-bishops in any respect. And first indeed we would commemorate the most blessed apostles, founders and princes of the universal Church, in this connection, were it not that we judge it most just that laws and natural principles must necessarily fall silent where the Lawgiver and Lord of nature has given blessing, grace, and glory.
