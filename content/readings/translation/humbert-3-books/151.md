## CAPUT XI.

That Heretics Are to Be Avoided by the Example of Vipers and the Evil Tree.

But lest anyone suppose himself free from guilt if he neglected to beware such men on the grounds that he could not penetrate their hearts, the Lord himself, warning in the Gospel to beware of false prophets, immediately added a mark by which they may be known, saying: “By their fruits you shall know them” \[Matthew 7:16\]. Hence those whose avarice is seen to rage so openly that they do not hesitate to offer the Holy Spirit for sale are clearly false prophets, for just as the hidden nature of a corrupt tree’s seed is revealed by the taste of its poisonous fruit, so what comes forth from the mouth proceeds from the heart; and therefore if there is evil in the heart, there cannot be good in the mouth, even if it appears to be so. These are the things that defile a man; and in one whose blessing with the mouth does not resound for God but for coin, that blessing counts as great malediction and no blessing at all. For, as the apostle says, such men’s “pleasing words and blessings seduce the hearts of the innocent” \[Romans 16:18\]. See what sort of blessings these are, that seduce the innocent. And what precisely does “seduce” mean? It leads aside. Whence and whither? From God to the devil. For if it led from the devil to God, the seduction would be good. Truly, “the evil man from the evil treasure of his heart brings forth evil” \[Luke 6:45\]. For what else can he bring forth from there but what he has stored up? Against such men the Lord cries out: “Brood of vipers, how can you speak good things, when you are evil? Or make the tree evil and its fruit evil?” \[Matthew 12:34; 12:33\]. By these words of truth they are shown to be worse than evil trees, for those trees, whatever they are at the root, show themselves to men the same in their taste; but these men, though evil at heart, presume to put on goodness verbally in their mouths. And how fittingly are pagans — who display in the mouth the faithlessness of idolatry that they hold in the heart — represented by evil trees, which are alive but do not perceive; while unbelieving and perverse heretics are represented by broods of vipers, which are alive and do perceive yet do not discern, and who maintain their faithlessness in both heart and deed, yet sometimes show in their mouths that they too sense something correct? Because they do not discern this, they are more harmful and more pernicious to themselves and to many than pagans — who sense nothing of Christian faith — just as vipers are more harmful than the evil tree, inasmuch as poisons differ from bitter or noxious fruit. Vipers, if they are not attacked or harmed, will still of their own accord attack other creatures and naturally harm them. But an evil tree neither attacks anyone with its fruit nor, if it is not attacked, harms anyone. Since therefore vipers, said to prefigure heretics, and evil trees, said to prefigure pagans, differ so greatly, who doubts that pagans are more to be tolerated by Catholics than heretics? For pagans, unless you provoke them, do not provoke you; and if provoked and they prevail, they inflict losses upon your outward substance and insults, and very rarely do they ever bring death even to the body. But heretics, arrogating to themselves the guise of Christian understanding and pressing forward, provoke you of their own accord even if you are quiet and silent; and, stubbornly seeking only the death of the soul, they think nothing of inflicting outward losses and the death of the body, though they also sometimes inflict these consistently on those who firmly resist them when they prevail. In this they are in every way like the devil, who — as blessed Gregory teaches — thirsts only for the death of souls and does not care to take away earthly things, but strives to strike down charity within us, of which faith is the foundation. Pagans, however, when provoked in their savagery, are like wild beasts that tear the garments of those who provoke them and sometimes rage against them even unto the death of the flesh.
