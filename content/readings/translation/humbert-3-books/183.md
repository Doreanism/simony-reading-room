Who would dare even in a dream to believe these men trust in the Holy Spirit, whom they attack to sell more than a thousand times over? And can it be believed of even the most worthless little servant, that he would suffer being sold so many times with equanimity and would not rather choose to be free from such contemptible lordship and such ridicule at whatever risk? But — oh the unspeakable crime! — the spirit of freedom is believed to be subject to so shameful a bondage and injury that not even the vilest of slaves could tolerate it, and to obey at the beck of those who sell it — those whose thorns in their sprouting put on the softness of grass, but long since grown to maturity are found to be brambles. For was not the attempt of faithless Simon like soft grass compared to the actions of his followers in our own time? Since he, though he wished to buy the Holy Spirit in order to sell it at a higher price, yet offered his own money and not another’s for this purpose, as blessed Petrus also showed in his reply to him, saying: “Your money perish with you \[Acts 8:20\].” His closer followers are known to have done the same, when they sought only to purchase ecclesiastical orders from their own estate or from the resources or possessions of their fathers or friends, chiefly for this reason: that those who had previously been powerful and noble from secular wealth might be even more powerful and more noble than others by virtue of ecclesiastical dignity. For there is nothing that every rational soul, whether sound or unsound, desires more for itself than power; which power the sound soul seeks to attain by cleaving to the Lord God and placing her hope in him, after the example of the holy angels who stood firm; but the unsound soul, by removing herself from God and trusting in herself or presuming upon herself, after the example of the fugitive spirits who fell from the beginning, whose prince, as the prophet declares, before he fell said in his heart: “I will ascend into heaven,

### On the birth and growth of the simoniac heresy.

But this aspiration toward ecclesiastical dignity, whitened over with chalk, was deterred from appearing by the severity of persecutions from the time of the apostles until the age of Christian princes. For he who was seen as first ruler and more powerful than others was the first of all to be subjected to the severest torments. But as soon as peace was restored to Christians — to such a degree that even the emperors bowed their august heads and all the trappings of earthly rule at the feet of Christ’s priests — that ancient pestilence returned and stood aghast at such power and at the glorious kingdom and priesthood in which the saints were already reigning with Christ; and since it was accustomed to dominate and rule, and could not seize it by truth, of which it has no share, it preferred to snatch it for itself by deceit. Hence what the catholic Church had merited by God’s grace, simoniac ambition sought to obtain by money — not in order, as a son of adoption in the catholic Church, to be subject to God’s grace and to serve it, but in order, as a tyrant of presumption, to be over it and to command it. And this they at first sought to achieve — as we have said — by their own or their associates’ money alone, so as to rule even the smallest part of the Church; at times they would also add their own great estates to ecclesiastical possessions and under cover of such advantage conducted the business of their ambition, so that it might be supposed by the unlearned that the rule of such men was not only not harmful but even very profitable for the Church of God, which — to obtain the privilege of presiding over it in this manner — they enriched from their private means. But because, as goodness advances by its own degrees, so too does malice by its own, this charade — such as it was, somewhat excusable trafficking in the Church — persisted as long as some degree of Christian decency or honorableness remained embedded in our forebears. But when, with sins prevailing, they lapsed entirely into ignorance of God, they cast off every coloring of excuse or of any honorableness, and no longer troubled to cloak their avarice, but allowed it, rolling up its sleeves, to rage openly; and it, having at last attained its most wicked freedom, drove those greedy for ecclesiastical dignities to the point of now not deducting or subtracting anything at all from their own purses or those of their associates or from their resources, but scraping together the entire price — paid or promised — from the revenues or possessions of the churches over which they are placed.
