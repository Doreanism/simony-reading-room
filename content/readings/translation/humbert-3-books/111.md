But one found guilty of some lesser transgression has a satisfaction of private penance enjoined upon him, yet in such a way that he is not deposed but his order, which he holds, is entirely preserved to him. Just as blessed Gregory himself is known to have intended at the end of the present chapter — which that crafty and impure person is proven to have suppressed because he clearly perceived it to obstruct his cavillings — saying: “If someone has incurred \[suspension\] from the priesthood on account of a slight fault, penance ought to be imposed and yet the order preserved.” For one justly deposed for criminal offenses is rarely or never found to be licitably restored. And if one unjustly deposed is restored — as is stated above from the Council of Toledo — one justly deposed is nowhere and never licitably restored; and one found in lesser transgressions is not indeed deposed, but suspended for a time from his own order while that order is reserved to him: who then is accused of being consecrated anew by Catholics? Certainly, setting aside those of irreproachable life, no more than these three kinds described above are found among Catholics. For they will either be those unjustly deposed, or justly deposed, or certainly not deposed; and if they are found in lighter faults, whoever is more than these is without doubt outside the Catholic Church. For they are heretics, whether called bishops, presbyters, deacons, or clerics of any other rank whatsoever. Hence, even unwillingly, that thorny one \[*Spinosulus*\] is now known through the progress of the disputation to reveal the beginning, progress, and end of his perverse intention, and like an entangled hedgehog he is compelled, as water approaches, to unfold his head, his feet, and his tail. For it is established that he persistently defends the position that those ordained by heretics and especially by simoniacs \[should remain in Catholic orders without reordination\], because in the Catholic Church, as is shown abundantly in what precedes, an order once received is not repeated. He also labors with various but futile arguments to establish that those ordained, as they say, by simoniacs ought to be held in the Catholic clergy without penance and without the laying on of hands, and to remain in it — as if nothing were lacking to them in grace in any respect. But we have proven above with sufficient testimonies of the holy fathers that simoniacs are heretics worse than the worst Arians. And if it is still doubted whether simoniacs are to be held as heretics, let the books of the Catholic fathers on various heresies be consulted — namely those of Epiphanius, Philaster, Augustine, and the others who have attempted to write on the subject — and in all of them the Simonians, who are also the simoniacs, are found in the catalogue of heresies as the first, whose founder Simon was justly condemned as a manifest heretic because he estimated and thought that the Holy Spirit could be purchased for money by him and bestowed on whomever he wished. Let those men see for themselves what they deserve, since they most certainly believe that they could become or have been able to become bishops through the mediation of money, and that they could impart ecclesiastical consecrations or ordinations to anyone. For unless they believed they could do this, they would restrain themselves from such great presumption. From this their estimation, if that Simon himself is manifestly declared to be worse than the worst Arians — because he sought to subject the Holy Spirit to himself — what will those Simonians of his deserve, since they altogether believe so great an abomination, and in comparison to whom they will be all the worse? For they are not recognized as worse than Arians in the entire catalogue of heresies. It is therefore manifest that simoniacs are prior to the remaining heretics both in time and in error. But lest they complain that we are burdening them excessively, we at last concede them to be equal to the Arians — although we have shown above their founder Simon to be more perverse than the Arians, by which standard these disciples of his are proven more perverse. Once this equality is conceded, it will be necessary for the Simonians to expect from Catholics an equally adverse judgment against them as that brought against the Arians who are their equals. Therefore now let them be assailed not by our own weapons but by those of the most ancient, entirely Catholic, and orthodox fathers, and overwhelmed by their sentences, so that, according to the saying of Solomon, those who make themselves out to be rich may at last — if belatedly — blush that they have nothing but extreme poverty, or, according to the testimony of his father who spoke of God, when all the men of riches awake from their sleep they find nothing in their hands — that is, in their ordinations.  But first let the sum of their calumny be here affixed, and then let it be pierced by the evident thunderbolts of the fathers.
