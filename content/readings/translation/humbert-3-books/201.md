Behold: the steward sought to dispense the properties of the church through purchase, yet he was not seeking to be consecrated; for a steward is not consecrated. And yet, if he is promoted for a price, he incurs the same condemnation as a bishop or presbyter promoted for a price. Why is this, if not because the Holy Spirit operates not only in the greatest but also in the least offices of ecclesiastical administration? This the faithful dispenser knew from experience, he who said: *Let a man regard us in this manner: as servants of Christ and stewards of the mysteries of God. Moreover it is required of stewards that one be found faithful* \[1 Corinthians 4:1–2\]. Then, wishing the Corinthians not to be ignorant concerning spiritual gifts, he said: *There are varieties of gifts, but the same Spirit; and there are varieties of ministries, but the same Lord; and there are varieties of operations, but the same God, who works all things in all* \[1 Corinthians 12:4–6\]. Shortly afterward, following the pattern of the nine orders of celestial hosts which he had beheld when caught up into the third heaven, he enumerated also nine distributions of diverse graces in this our kingdom of heaven — that is, in the present Church — and added: *All these things are worked by one and the same Spirit, distributing to each one individually as he wills* \[1 Corinthians 12:11\]. And note that the apostle, who had first comprehended the spiritual graces under the number three, immediately expands them to nine, signifying that — after the pattern of the blessed Trinity — only three distinctions of persons are found in the Church of this age: namely, teachers, those living in continence, and those in the married state. Yet if these three, considered as it were personally in accordance with the diversity of their offices, are examined, nine distinct orders of persons are without doubt found in this Church, after the pattern of the celestial army, as the venerable father Dionysius the Areopagite attests subtly and sufficiently in his writings on the celestial hierarchy.

## CHAPTER IV.

### On the sanctification that sanctifies ecclesiastical things, with the testimonies of the holy fathers appended thereon.

Hence it is no wonder that a dispenser of any ecclesiastical matter whatsoever, however small, who is promoted through money — even if he is not consecrated — is condemned by ecclesiastical rules along with those who authorized him and those who brokered the transaction, just as if he had been consecrated: because whatever serves and is owed to the uses of the sanctuary and the altar must of necessity be holy. And if these things are holy — as they truly are — they must of necessity have within them the holiness and sanctification by which they are holy. But holiness or sanctification neither is nor can be without the Holy Spirit; rather, it is the Holy Spirit himself — just as whoever is wise or chaste must necessarily have within himself the wisdom or chastity by which he is wise or chaste. It is therefore established that sanctification or holiness — that is, the Holy Spirit — is present in sanctified things or persons. Accordingly, whoever sells or buys an ecclesiastical stipend or benefice beyond doubt sells or buys the Holy Spirit — not in the sense that the Holy Spirit himself is held as part of or together with the thing sold or bought, but in the sense that, offended, he flees from it, from the thing which he used to sanctify, and that the thing is invaded and defiled by an unclean spirit, in accordance with what is said through Malachi: *Judah has profaned the sanctification of the Lord which he loved* \[Malachi 2:11\]. For the sanctification itself cannot be defiled, but by metonymy \[*metonomicos*\] — using what contains for what is contained — the thing which the sanctification had hitherto sanctified is said to be defiled, in the manner described above.
