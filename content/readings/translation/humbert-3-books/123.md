By it, assuredly, all priestly reverence and conduct is corrupted both inwardly and outwardly, and the holy office is held to be a matter of crime. For no one thinks venerable what is sold; indeed, what is bought is considered altogether vile. Moreover, where the gifts of heavenly grace are deemed for sale, money is venerated against God, so that in the wretched man not the true and ancient God but a false and recent one now rules — namely the most wicked Mammon, who presides over such riches. And there is no doubt that the innocence of the poor gives offense where payment is proven to commend wrongdoing, for where gold finds favor, so does vice. Hence not only is a deadly wound inflicted on the souls of the ordainer and the ordained, the doctor declares, but also those who ought to have been relieved by the intercessions of the bishops are burdened by their fault. The same doctor also maintains that if he is judged worthy of the priesthood whose claim rests on abundance of money, the consequence is that no uprightness defends itself in ecclesiastical honor, but the profane love of gold obtains everything, so that vices deserving of punishment are rewarded with honor, while he who ought to have been corrected usurps the place of the corrector. Such priests, he affirms, do not advance but rather perish, their root infected with a grievous plague. Likewise he maintains that greater calamity is to be feared in those places to which such intercessors are given for governance, who provoke God’s wrath against themselves rather than placating him before the people. Then he testifies to being afflicted with deep sorrow if money has any place in ecclesiastical offices, since what is sacred straightaway becomes secular. For he asserts that everyone who strives to obtain what is sacred by price is not a priest but only wants vainly to be called one. Finally, after much by which he condemns them, he asserts that blessing is turned into curse for them, and that they are promoted for this sole end: that from being Catholics they become heretics, all of whose actions, even if they seem to be of some moment, are nevertheless reckoned as nothing before God.

But because from these and certain other sayings of the blessed Gregory some persons presume to find material with which shamelessly and stubbornly to defend the error of their opinion — to the point of dogmatizing that the Holy Spirit is both sold and purchased — one must insist that they at some point repent of having thought or said this. For they say: “Behold what the blessed Gregory thought on this matter — the tenor of his letters to the Frankish rulers shows it openly. ‘We utterly execrate and condemn as most grave,’ he says, ‘that sacred orders in your regions are conferred through the simoniac heresy.’ And a little later: ‘Hence I grieve deeply and lament for that land, because since they disdain to have the Holy Spirit, whom Almighty God deigns to bestow on all men through the laying on of hands, as a divine gift, they obtain him by means of payments.’” From this phrasing of so great a pontiff’s words, the senseless conceive for themselves grounds of faithless argument, and — as the sage says of the face of a woman in labor — they say: “Behold! Here the blessed Gregory plainly attests that the Holy Spirit is sold and conferred by money, and that through the laying on of hands redeemed at a price the Holy Spirit is nonetheless received and bestowed. For he declares openly that sacred orders are conferred through the simoniac heresy, and that men obtain the Holy Spirit by means of payments.” Alas, wretched men and day by day more wretched, who do not fear to lay violent hands on the seamless tunic of him who is God’s virtue and God’s wisdom — more wickedly than the soldiers who crucified him — and to cut off pieces for themselves, and what is still worse, imagine that the whole tunic has been ceded to them.
