### Also from the decrees of Pope Gaius to Bishop Felix.

Know, first of all, that pagans and heretics cannot accuse Christians or bring a charge of infamy.

By these and similar decrees and judgments of the Catholic fathers it is plainly recognized that heretics are forbidden entry into the house of God and are to be removed from all communion with Catholics — namely from prayer, meals, marriages, and donations of property or patrimony — and that the right to accuse Catholics or to testify against them is not granted to them; and Catholics are altogether forbidden to enter their cemeteries for the purpose of prayer, or to receive eulogies or festal gifts from them. Hence no one doubts that heretics are to be repudiated by Catholics in every respect, as much as — or certainly more than — Jews or pagans, because in the latter there is still hope of faith, as was said above, whereas in the former there is already the warfare of discord.

## CAPUT XII.

But now let us attend to what the holy fathers thought about the orders or rather the ordinations of heretics.

### From the letter of the blessed Pope Innocentius to the bishops of Macedonia.

We have come to the third question, which by reason of its difficulty demands a rather lengthy treatment — when we say that those ordained by heretics have their head wounded by that laying on of hands, and where a wound has been inflicted, medicine must be applied so that it can recover health. But the health that follows a wound cannot be without a scar, and where the remedy of penance is necessary, the honor of ordination can have no place. For as it is written: “Whatever the unclean touches shall be unclean” \[Leviticus 11:28\] — how shall that be conferred upon him which cleanliness and purity is accustomed to receive? But on the other hand it is asserted that one who has lost honor cannot give honor, and that the other received nothing because there was nothing in the giver that he could receive. And it is certainly true that he could not give what he did not have. The damnation that he had he certainly gave through the corrupt laying on of hands. And how one who has become a partaker of the condemned can receive honor — I cannot find.

From this let those now at last wake up who have been ordained by simoniacs, suddenly disturbed and confounded by such great thunder and such great lightning from so distinguished a bishop among the distinguished, and let them recognize at last — even if belatedly — what they received from those who ordained them; or let them teach us reasonably, if they can or dare, that simoniacs neither are nor ought to be called heretics.

Indeed, those ordained by heretics are taught to have their head wounded through that laying on of hands, because “whatever the unclean touches shall be unclean.” It is established, therefore, that every heretic is unclean. Whatever he touches is without doubt made unclean according to the word of the Lord in the law of Moses.

Nor could it have been, nor will it ever be possible, that what cleanliness and purity is accustomed to receive should be conferred by an unclean heretic, whose heart is established not to have been cleansed by sincere faith. To one upon whom nothing of cleanliness and purity could therefore be conferred, what else can he confer upon whomever he touches, but the uncleanness which alone he possesses? It follows, then, that whoever is touched by the unclean is nothing other than unclean, because the condemned man gives the damnation he has through the corrupt laying on of his hands. For one who does not have honor cannot give honor. And where there is nothing in the giver, there is nothing which anyone, even if he desires it in every way, is able to receive. And so one receives the damnation which that person has, who performs upon him a corrupt and not a correct laying on of hands.
