as the distinguished Prudentius says in refutation of the Marcionites, who fabricated for themselves two gods, namely a good and an evil one:

No one has seen two suns except under glaucoma.

Wherefore, if they are able, let them consider how far they stray from God who labor to divide what is indivisible. For God himself commends his own indivisible trinity by its unity, and he imparts and preserves this pattern of his own immutable and invisible nature not only to his invisible and mutable creatures, but also to his visible ones. For everything that God made in his one wisdom he has marked with the trinity by which all things subsist, giving to all things measure, number, and weight. These three are present in all creatures simultaneously and inseparably, and no one of them can be found prior or posterior to the other two, nor can it be sensibly separated from them. Whether you consider measure, number, or weight in any bodily natures whatsoever, you will perceive through the bodily senses alike and inseparably that in the same three things they are born, exist, grow, diminish, and perish. These things you will likewise understand, if you observe them, in incorporeal creatures. For they have measure by which they are bounded, the weight of their merits, and the number of their diverse effects. As the diversity of members or parts gives bodies their number, so the diversity of effects \[gives number\] to created spirits. Thus, as the venerable Prosper says:

So the character of his works proclaims the maker,
Who fashions for things their forms, places, times, and motions
With their proper measures, numbers, and weights.

Although these three are present in all creatures equally, they nevertheless produce a single effect and are bound together by unity — so that according to their mode each is expanded through its trinity in such a way that each is also constrained by its unity; and thus they have number and yet do not have it, after the manner of their author, who is said to be both three in the highest sense and one. Moreover, just as the blessed and incorruptible Trinity is rightly called and held to be one God, so also that corruptible trinity in each individual creature is called and held to be one spirit or one body. From all of which we are taught in many ways that God, both in himself and in all his works, commends and above all loves the monad, and supremely detests and hates anything that cuts itself off from him as a dyad. For the monad, being indivisible by the gift of its own nature, restores even what is divided to indivisible unity and connects it indivisibly. But the dyad, being divisible by the evil of its own vice, seeks to divide even what is indivisible. The monad, as if inviolable and peaceable, knows nothing but how to draw back from things that are divided, diverse, and private, and to unite them all in its own common and present indivisible \[principle\]. The dyad, on the contrary, as if violated, violating, and schismatic, attempts nothing but to tear away and cut off from what is indivisible, from what is similar, and from the common good. This \[the dyad\], without doubt, departing of its own accord from that immutable and indivisible monad, first explored a place for its private and solitary seat in the sides of the north, and then divided man from God. This too, in its own manner, spoke through the harlot: “Let it be neither mine nor yours, but let it be divided.” \[3 Kings 3:26\] This too in the gospel seeks to persuade the eternal monad to division through another, saying: “Speak to my brother, that he divide the inheritance with me” \[Luke 12:13\]; to which the blessed monad, proclaiming the impassibility of its own nature, replied: “Man, who made me a judge or divider over you?” \[Luke 12:14\] And if you consider all the works of the divinity, you will find that they subsist altogether by the monad, but perish by the dyad. As the monad is the principle of subsistence for these, so the dyad is the beginning of dissolution. For as long as the diversity and multiplicity of members or parts is bound together in unity or connected in its whole, it subsists; but when it approaches the dyad, it begins to dissolve and perish. Moses also hints at this when, having recorded that God saw the works of each individual day that they were good, he nevertheless kept silent on this point when speaking of the second day, signifying that it does not appear good to God when anything, departing from his immutable unity, begins and falls into the mutability of plurality.
