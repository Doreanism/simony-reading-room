“Everyone who believes that Jesus is the Christ is born of God.” Who is it that does not believe that Jesus is the Christ? He who does not live as Christ commanded. For many say: “I believe” — but faith without works does not save. The work of faith is love itself, as the apostle Paul says: “Faith, which works through love.” Your past works, before you believed, were either nothing at all, or, if they seemed good, were empty. For if they were nothing, you were like a man without feet or unable to walk because his feet are impaired. But if they seemed good before you believed, you were indeed running, but by running off the path you were going astray rather than arriving. We must therefore both run and run on the path. He who runs off the path runs in vain — indeed, he runs toward toil; the more he runs, the more he goes astray, because he runs off the path. What is the path on which we run? Christ, who said: “I am the way.” What is the homeland to which we run? Christ, who said: “I am the truth.” Through him we run; to him we run; in him we rest. But that we might run through him, he stretched himself out to us. And so on. From the same author as above: What more could the demons have believed than to say: “We know who you are: the Son of God, the Holy One of God”? What the demons said, Peter also said. The same words, but not the same spirit. And how is it evident that Peter said this with love? Because the faith of a christian is with love of God, while that of a demon is without love. How without love? The demons were saying this so that Christ would withdraw from them. For before they said “We know who you are, the Son of God,” they had said: “What have you to do with us?” — “Have you come before the time to destroy us?” So it is one thing to confess Christ in order to hold fast to Christ; it is another thing to confess Christ in order to drive Christ away from you. You see, then, how the phrase “he who believes” here \[denotes\] a certain proper faith, not one shared in some general way with many. Let none of the heretics say to you: “We also believe.” For the very reason I proposed the example of demons is that you should not rejoice at the words of those who say they believe, but should examine the deeds of those who live. What does it mean to believe in him? “And everyone who loves the Father loves him who is born of the Father.” Immediately he joined love to faith, because without love faith is empty. With love it is the faith of a christian; without love it is the faith of a demon. Those who do not believe at all are worse than demons and more sluggish. Everyone who loves the Father loves the Son. “By this we know that we love the children of God.” He said “children of God,” he who had a little earlier said “Son of God,” because the children of God are the body of the only-begotten Son of God. And since he is the head and we are the members, there is one Son of God. Therefore, whoever loves the children of God loves the Son of God, and whoever loves the Son of God loves the Father; nor can anyone love the Father unless he loves the Son. And whoever loves the Son loves the children of God. Which children of God? The members of the Son of God. And by loving he himself becomes a member and is incorporated by love into the body of Christ, and there will be one Christ loving himself. For when the members love one another, the body loves itself: “and if one member suffers, all the members suffer together; and if one member is glorified, all the members rejoice together.” And what does the following say? “You, however, are the body of Christ and members of a member.” “For whoever does not love his brother, whom he sees, how can he love God, whom he does not see?” Extend love through the whole world, if you wish to love Christ — because the members of Christ lie throughout the world. Where must you be in order to be under the head? If you are not in the body, you are not under the head. What does it profit that you believe and yet blaspheme? You worship him in the head and blaspheme him in the body. He loves his own body. If you have cut yourself off from his body, the head has not cut itself off from its body. “You honor me in vain,” he cries out to you from above; “you honor me in vain.” It is as if someone wanted to kiss your head while treading on your feet — and were crushing your feet with hobnailed boots, wanting to hold your head and kiss it: would you not, in the very midst of words of honor, cry out and say: “What are you doing, man?
