## CAPUT XXI.

### On the Chief Evil of Italy, and on a Certain Vain Bishop, and on the Office of Priests, Princes, and Peoples

With such ordinations defiling certain provinces, it is above all Italy that is thus afflicted, into which the dregs of this sort habitually flow — dregs that not only by their stench compel those desirous of sailing toward the kingdom of heaven to fall ill, but also by their weight drag them under. Men of this kind are slowest to hear good things and slower still to live well; yet being most prompt to speak and most practiced in flattery, they deceive with cunning fraud the simple ears of princes who by their own nature judge others by themselves, and they pervert their right intentions. For the wisdom of the flesh, which as its enemy cannot be subjected to the law of God, inflated \[with pride\] and now skilled in simulating and then in dissimulating whatever they please, they abuse plausible arguments and garbled sound sentences of Christ and the holy fathers for purposes of persuasion; and so, now merry with mimic levity, now severe with philosophical gravity, now witty with secular urbaneness, now pressing with monetary generosity, like another wicked and many-formed Proteus, themselves also shape-shifters, violent at their own pleasure, and — what is more grave — blasphemers, they twist around whatever had stood firm before God and men as useful and honorable to Holy Church. For flattering persons for the sake of gain, among their other ravings and deceptions by which they ensnare the incautious as if with some new fowling, they magnify to the wide and greedy ears of princes worldly power — above all imperial and royal power — beyond all measure, and diminish ecclesiastical dignity. And because there is an alternation of all things under the sun, such that they sometimes advance and sometimes decline, they assess the merit and power of sacerdotal dignity according to the outward advance or decline of ecclesiastical affairs, so that at one moment they place secular powers above it as the sun \[is placed\] above the moon, at another they set beside it a second sun as if \[equal to\] the sun, and at yet another — which nonetheless happens most rarely — in the name of sonship alone they set it below \[priestly dignity\] as a son below a father. This fiction of impure and shameless flattery has so prevailed in our days that a certain sycophant of this sort, calling himself a bishop, was accustomed to curry popular favor in this way to the confusion of Holy Church and his own damnation: “The Roman Pope,” he said, “is the Father, the emperor the Son, and I, who run between the two of them, am the Holy Spirit.” What Catholic can bear to hear this blasphemy, let alone utter it? But the blind flatterer, pleasing himself beyond measure because of the effect of his flattery, while attempting to be esteemed by men above what he was or seemed to be, is convicted of having fallen into the blasphemies of two heresies. In his presumption he established grades within the Holy Trinity like Arius, and like Manes he professed himself to be the Holy Spirit. By this sacrilege and many other deeds, hated by God and by God’s faithful, like another Jason he perished in a foreign land as a wanderer and fugitive. Therefore, whatever prince seeks to secure his happiness here and blessedness hereafter should beware of acquiescing to such men. For a king who willingly hears words of falsehood will have all his ministers be impious. And let the priests of Christ and what pertains to them have no other place than what the great Constantine and his orthodox successors in the empire gave them, as is shown in part somewhat earlier in this little work. Hence he who wishes to confer the priestly and royal dignity irreproachably and usefully should say that the priesthood in the present Church is likened to the soul, while the kingdom is likened to the body, because they love one another in turn and each needs the other and each in turn demands and renders its service to the other. Of these two, as the soul excels and commands, so does sacerdotal dignity over the royal, as the heavenly over the earthly. So that all things may be not disordered but orderly, let the priesthood as soul give prior warning of what is to be done; then let the kingdom, as the head of its body, preside over all its members and lead them where it is expedient.
