From this it can be inferred that heretics and pagans are inferior even to demons in unbelief, since demons at least believe that God is one and tremble, while those others do not even that much. And yet they are superior to demons in this: that pagans can be saved through baptism and heretics perhaps through penance. Although, if the definition of faith is carefully considered, it is said that faith or belief inheres in demons not properly but only in an improper sense \[i.e., abusively\]. For if faith, as the apostle defines it, is “the substance of things hoped for, the evidence of things not seen” (Hebrews 11), it is clear that demons neither have faith nor believe in God — whom they knew, having been created by him in angelic dignity before their fall, and whom they were accustomed to behold face to face. And so they do not believe that God exists, nor can they say what the fool said in his heart: “There is no God” (Psalm 14), because in what they previously saw they know him to be. “For what does one hope for when he sees it?” (Romans 8). From this it is therefore established that not even demons have faith. Otherwise, if they truly had faith, they would not lack hope and charity either, and would therefore no longer be demons. Accordingly, even that hope of the devil of which the Lord speaks to blessed Job — “His hope will deceive him” (Job 40) — is not true hope but a kind of false security by which he and his kind flatter themselves vainly about God’s mercy, while they in no way fear the strictness of his justice. They would certainly fear this if their hope rested on faith, because faith believes in God both insofar as he is to be feared and insofar as he is to be loved. Thus faith, retaining for itself the fear of the most just judge, imparts to hope the love of the most merciful Father, so that hope loves what faith also believes to be lovable. So neither faith nor hope inheres in the devil or the impious, though they are found to be said to inhere in them in an improper sense — as blessed Gregory asserts that unbelievers have faith when they believe they were born of those they call their parents, though they did not see their own conception and birth, and many other matters of human life and conduct. Of these the same most learned father says: “Unbelievers have faith — but would that it were in God!”

## CAPUT XVII.

### On the difference between secular merchants and those who are called simoniacs.

But with these not entirely fruitless preliminary observations, let it be examined in what manner ecclesiastical men who do not freely give what is to be given freely in ecclesiastical sacraments can be said to lose faith entirely — and \[to lose it\] more completely than any Christian clergy or laypersons who, out of desire for earthly gain, defraud brothers in business or sell too dearly what they received cheaply or indeed freely. And let us first consider what common intention or motive each type of merchant perpetually holds, and then what is proper to each. Therefore, to put it briefly, the common intention of both parties in their trade seems to us to be this: each seeks to become temporarily rich through temporal goods. The result is that both alike — though unequally due to the difference in their offices — are struck by the wise man’s verdict: “He who seeks to become rich will not be innocent” (Proverbs 28). For according to the common intention they incur a common injury to their innocence, though by reason of their different orders one incurs it more and the other less. Moreover it is common to both to offer goods for the sake of profit, to set a price, to sell, and to desire a buyer. In these acts, if nothing else were involved, there would be no fault or only minimal fault. For it cannot be charged as a fault if goods are offered, priced, sold, and bought for the sake of common necessity; since what is licitly bought is not illicitly sold. It is also sometimes common to both to defraud a brother, because one of them, whenever he trades, both defrauds and is defrauded; while the other does so sometimes, but not always. For this fraud, though the Lord takes vengeance on both, he does not take vengeance more gravely on one than the other, proportionate to the nature of the fraud and its cause. Likewise, selling cheaply what was bought dearly is sometimes common to both, because one of them, whenever he sells what he bought at a high price, sells it cheaply, while the other does so sometimes but not habitually.
