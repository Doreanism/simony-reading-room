XXIV. On the exemplary pattern of the indivisible Trinity in all creatures.

XXV. That the sanctification is inseparable from the things it sanctifies, and how they are sanctified.

XXVI. On the punishment of those who divide, and that none but the followers of the apostolic faith receive the Holy Spirit.

XXVII. On the grace of the Holy Spirit poured out upon the disciples after the resurrection, and the punishment of Ananias.

XXVIII. How great are the works that the same Spirit has wrought or continues to work in the Church until the end of the age.

XXIX. The opinion of Saint Augustine on the power of the soul, a retractation drawn from it, and what sort of men are promoted by laymen and what sort are their metropolitans.

XXX. Recapitulation on the deposition of heretics and the judgment of Ezekiel against them.

XXXI. On improper expressions in the aforementioned words of the prophet and of Blessed Gregory subjoined thereto.

XXXII. On criminal Catholics who have been promoted or who have lapsed afterward, and on their ministry and their deposition.

XXXIII. On persons of whatever kind promoted by simoniacs, and the refutation of the objection of the adversaries.

XXXIV. Arguments drawn from secular laws against those who promote or are promoted as simoniacs.

XXXV. A restatement of the Lord’s judgment on good shepherds and hired hands.

XXXVI. On the wages of hired hands and their promotion.

XXXVII. On the fact that Catholics promote no one on account of crime, whereas simoniacs promote everyone solely on account of crime.

XXXVIII. In what matters hired hands are to be heeded, and in what matters they are not to be heeded.

XXXIX. On those promoted in evil, whether knowingly or unknowingly, and on their ministry: the judgment of Paschasius.

XL. On simoniac thieves, and how they enter the sheepfold.

XLI. How simoniacs imitate the thieves of the world, and what is their voice, which the sheep do not hear.

XLII. With what great vigilance the sheep ought to resist thieves who have broken into the sheepfold.

XLIII. On the difference between hired hands and thieves, and that heretics are not to be tolerated by Catholics as evil-living Catholics are.

XLIV. It is shown by proposed examples that heretics were not tolerated even for an hour by the Catholics of old, whereas hired hands were tolerated.

XLV. On the fact that Saint Gregory, while still a deacon, shunned Eutychius, and that Saint Jerome asserts that public judgment in their case need not be awaited.

XLVI. On synods that determine how heretics are to be reconciled, and on some excuse for the Quartodecimans.

XLVII. On libels of protestation and their authority.

XLVIII. On the steadfastness of certain Catholics in refusing to yield or give assent to heretics.

XLIX. On Blessed John the bishop, who was tempted but preserved from selling grace, and on his admonition against heretics.

L. On the prudence of Blessed Anthony and his vision, and on the vigilance of Blessed Jerome.
