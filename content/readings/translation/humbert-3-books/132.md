It is therefore clear that one who does not obtain priesthood through the laying on of hands is not a priest. It follows, then, that it is not necessary for him to be deposed from an order he never held. Consequently, the canonical statutes issued by the holy fathers decreeing that such persons are to be deposed are cited in vain.

### Refutation.

This perversity, unafraid to resist the Holy Spirit openly with so sophistical and tangled a syllogism, because it perversely believes the same Spirit to be sold and bought wherever the names of seller and buyer are heard, ironically concludes that it is not necessary to depose from an order one who never obtained it — or, if he did obtain it, that he ought not to be deposed — and thereby that the chapter issued by the holy fathers against simoniacs is to be annulled. For that phantastical syllogism, threatening with its horns, presses you from either side whichever way you turn. If you concede that they are to be deposed, you are also forced to concede that they have obtained the dignity from which they are deposed. If you concede that they obtained the dignity, it will be established that the Holy Spirit was also sold and bought by them. And if that is established, it will necessarily follow that the Holy Spirit is possessed, given, and received by them; and hence you will be compelled to confess that ordinations or consecrations performed by simoniacs — who are assuredly heretics — whether for payment or not, are in every respect valid and in no way inferior to those performed by Catholics. On the other hand, if you deny that they obtained the dignity, you are obliged to declare that they need not be deposed from a dignity they never held. But if you concede that they need not be deposed, you are compelled to judge that the decrees of the holy fathers, which ordain that sellers and buyers of grace are to be deposed, were issued in vain. It remains, therefore, that either, with the deposition of simoniacs granted, all those other things that seem wholly absurd — as stated above — must also be granted, and so those chapters of the holy fathers will be approved; or, with their deposition denied, they will be repudiated. Yet although it has been shown at length above that the Holy Spirit can be neither sold nor bought — without whom no priesthood can be acquired or held, nor any consecration performed — and that therefore such presumptuous persons must be deposed from that counterfeit form or semblance of office which, as servants of justice, they unjustly usurp for themselves; nevertheless let it be granted that between a seller and a buyer something is necessarily the object of the transaction, since neither can rightly be called a seller or buyer otherwise. But that thing cannot be the Holy Spirit, since the Holy Spirit is both called and is grace. Grace, however, unless it is received and given freely, is neither grace nor truly so called. For if grace is sold and bought, the conclusion rightly follows that it is no longer grace. It is therefore established that the Holy Spirit — or certainly grace — is not the object of the selling and buying. Accordingly, another object must be sought; and lest we grow weary or go astray by searching for it too long and too widely, let us attend to blessed Ambrose, who demonstrates it to us. “What he gave,” he says, “when a bishop was ordained, was gold — and what he lost was his soul. When he ordained another, what he received was money — and what he gave was leprosy.” From the statement of so eminent a man we are taught without ambiguity that the object of this vile commerce is rightly called and truly consists only in the ruin and damnation of souls and in leprosy. Ruin, indeed, in the sense also affirmed by the prince of the apostles; damnation in the sense of blessed Innocentius; and leprosy in the sense of the same Ambrose. Hence, wherever ruin or damnation is charged, the devil — the author of ruin and damnation — necessarily cooperates and holds dominion in a transaction of this kind, just as that same unclean spirit likewise presides where leprosy is given. For he cannot fail to hold dominion and lordship over that whose author he is. And from those over whom the spirit of ruin, damnation, and uncleanness holds dominion, the saving Spirit of our Lord Jesus Christ — who is the author of all purity — is kept far away. For what fellowship has Christ with Belial?
