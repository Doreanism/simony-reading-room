Yet they build an altar above the Jordan itself as a testimony that the Lord himself is God, lest afterwards the children of the other tribes should be able to say to their children: “You have no part in the Lord, because he has put the Jordan as a boundary between us and you.” So too all secular persons who are nonetheless faithful in the holy church — whenever the battle of faith is declared against catholics who preserve baptism in faith and works, by heretics or pagans — immediately, armed with the sentences of the holy fathers and sometimes supported by secular power, they hasten to the aid of their brothers who are about to fight for the faith. Nor do they desist until, with the enemy vigorously defeated, they have secured the desired peace for them. But they return as quickly as possible to their own territory across the Jordan, because though they assist the catholics for a time out of zeal for the catholic faith they profess, nevertheless after the crossing of the Jordan — that is, the sacrament of baptism — they transgress the faith of baptism as regards works, and in peace pass by what they bravely defended in war. For, greatly desiring only fleeting and transitory things, they do not care that any other inheritance be provided for them along with their brothers, except the profession and defense of the same faith. This too they confirm, as if by the testimony of an altar erected by them above the Jordan in the land of promise, whenever in response to catholic and spiritual men who rebuke and detest their carnal way of life to the point of denying them a part in God, they have recourse — for the confirmation of their communion with the saints — to the confession of the catholic faith, which they and their fathers professed above the font of sacred baptism as though an altar above the Jordan; and by this they claim for themselves a part in God, in that they faithfully retain the creed in heart and mouth, and whenever occasion demands, when admonished, they defend it eagerly with all their strength. The result is that the altar, which was previously considered and called sacrilegious — as if it had been built for burnt offerings and sacrifices against the divine commandments — is afterwards approved by its reasonable purpose and called a testimony of faith and Christian fellowship between the carnal and the spiritual. He who is Lord of the spiritual through faith and works is also God of the carnal through faith alone. For the examination of this case not without reason is Phinehas sent as legate — to whom the Lord established a covenant of eternal priesthood (Numbers 25) — because it is the priest’s duty to inquire diligently lest perhaps anyone, having forsaken God and despising his one unique faith as one unique altar, presumes upon an unwarranted priesthood and chooses another altar for the sacrifices of his perfidy and the worship of demons. This is precisely what those do who stray from the faith and usurp the outward appearance of ecclesiastical sacraments — just as many years later the children of Israel, having forsaken the Lord through the impious Jeroboam, erected altars with golden calves and appointed priests of a demon in opposition to the altar of the Lord and his priesthood. But of these things fuller treatment will be given in another place. For now let only this be observed: that as long as the children of Israel did not presume for themselves an altar beyond the one, and a separate priesthood, they maintained peace among themselves under the worship of one God, that is, the faith. But as soon as they did presume, they immediately attacked one another in continuous war, nor could Judah make peace or dare to form alliances with Israel, since the Lord forbade it and rebuked King Jehoshaphat for this venture, saying: “You have joined in friendship with those who hate the Lord and lend aid to the impious — and therefore you were deserving of the wrath of the Lord,” etc. (2 Chronicles 19). Indeed it is shown without doubt from the Moralia of blessed Gregory that even those who live evilly within the church are and are rightly called faithful. For he says in Book XXXIII: “Behemoth devours some who are placed under the name of Christianity by supplanting them in the very error of faith; others he does not at all divert from the rectitude of faith, but bends them toward the practice of depraved conduct. Others he does not wish to incline to the work of impurity, but turns them inwardly within themselves in the bent of their intention, so that while they divide their mind from charity, whatever they outwardly perform is not right.
