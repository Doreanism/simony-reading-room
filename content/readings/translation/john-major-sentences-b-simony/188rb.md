In conclusion, I say that spiritual things are to be exchanged for spiritual things, and a spiritual condition may be added to a profane thing, provided the condition is reasonable — the rationale for which is gathered from the written law and from what follows from it, or from the light of nature and the judgment of prudent men. Certain conditions, however, would be dishonest and contrary to reason — for instance, if a benefice were always to be given to one of one’s kinsmen, since the collator is bound to confer it upon the most suitable person. To attach a condition concerning the celebration of an anniversary each year is in effect the same thing as giving that thing with its attendant obligation to the church and annexing the condition at the time of the donation — which is proved as follows. Either the condition was licit from the beginning, or it was illicit. Not the second, as is well known according to these authorities. If the first: then it can be added at the transfer of the thing just as it could before the transfer. Nor is it necessary to say, “We leave to you such-and-such revenues to be collected each year from such-and-such lands, and we ask you for the love of God to celebrate an anniversary for us each year on such-and-such a day.” I have said that this is not necessary, for that manner of speaking is good, and from the custom of men an obligation could arise from it; but I mean to say that the condition may be attached — and quite distinctly so — as when a Mass is instituted for the feast of Corpus Christi, or when one is to sing the Salve Regina every Saturday before Our Lady, with the condition attached that if they do not fulfill the duty they shall not collect the revenues. For in that case a spur to doing good is given to them, so that the aid of the Christ-bearing Virgin may be implored — just as children are enticed to study or to holy conduct by small gifts. In no different fashion were the nocturnal and diurnal distributions in the church instituted. It is therefore astonishing that there are men who call this into doubt. And it is equally astonishing that others have devised various precautions (as he says) for avoiding simony, when the meaning is the same — as is evident from human custom, and this would be declared so in a court of law. If, however, the heirs of the deceased wished to recover the revenues, or if the church refused to perform the appointed office, people should always understand that the spiritual office is not given for those temporal goods as an ultimate end and as an adequate price. Nor should simple folk be condemned no matter what manner of speaking they employ, since they conform their faith and manner of assent, formally or virtually, to the Church. For if an objection were raised against them that they wrongly judged there to be a purchase or something of the kind, they would say that they thought they were getting the better of the exchange — namely, receiving the spiritual for the temporal — and that they were doing these things for the glory of God and the salvation of their own souls and those of their parents, and that they would wish to be shown how one ought to speak in this matter. Indeed, some people establish numerous obits, assigning revenues to the Friars Minor, who sin by accepting those revenues since it is contrary to their profession. Far be it that those who piously endow revenues should sin, unless they had been instructed on this point — just as the common people who give them money do not sin, because they do not know the friars’ rule and are not bound to know it. It is different for a learned person, who sins because he knowingly cooperates in the sin of those who receive, or does so through vincible ignorance. For what is vincible ignorance for one person is invincible ignorance for another.
