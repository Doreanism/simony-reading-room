### A second argument is made to the same effect.

It may be that this manner of paying was introduced through exaction and with the sin of avarice. Therefore it is not a praiseworthy custom but a corruption, which does not bind at all. And this is clear from the chapter *Suam nobis* [X 5.3.14], *De simonia*. To the first argument it is conceded that the people originally gave freely and of their own accord — for instance, some devout persons at the beginning, seeing a curate wholly worthy, by whose example neighbors were moved to give similarly and freely, so that such giving continued for some time until many came to think it was obligatory; and afterward the greater part [concurred], and thus there was a virtual consent of the people binding themselves to this — the people being unable to discern whether such a sum of money was owed to the prelate by right or not, since the fruits of his labors depended on customs. An analogous example: only for the feast of the Assumption of the Blessed Virgin is fasting a precept of the Church, and yet by custom the people fast on all the vigils of feasts of Our Lady; those who omit them judge themselves evildoers. And so the custom is binding upon them, yet it had its origin only in counsel voluntarily taken up — because the people erroneously believed it to be a written law obligating them, or because they believed the same about the vigil of the Nativity of the Christ-bearing Virgin as about the Assumption into heaven. Thus through some plausible rationale a preceptive custom is introduced. And the same would apply if a farmer holding lands from a lord were to give of his own accord from the beginning certain capons: by continuing the free gift he can convert it into an obligation when he cannot prove its origin — because it is not presumed that he would continue so freely. And if the farmer already owes certain rents to the lord, the presumption is even stronger. But the first case alone suffices. You see, then, that the analogy is not apt if I give someone a scudo on the first day of the year, where I can prove the free origin and the voluntary continuation. From this it is clear that this argument is of no weight: I can prove that this payment of money after funerary rites was originally free, therefore I am not now bound to it. For the priest ought to take this money not by reason of the spiritual services rendered to the people — because those he renders freely. Just so, Christ who commanded that these things be rendered freely also commanded that those to whom they are rendered should give the ministers what is necessary for their sustenance, as is clear from Matthew 10, where those two things are commanded together in a single continuous passage. Let the priest therefore say that he takes it by reason of sustenance, and his title to this is grounded in custom — because otherwise he cannot prove in a particular case that anything is owed to him.

### But against this the following argument is made.

Innocentius III in the aforesaid chapter *Suam* [X 5.3.14] states the opposite, saying thus: “Their parishioners from Villafranca have sent us their complaint that their chaplain unlawfully demands and extorts money from them for the burial of the dead and the blessing of those marrying.” And further on: “We command that you admonish the said chaplain to desist from such presumption, and also to make fitting satisfaction for his excess; and if he refuses to obey, inflict canonical punishment upon him.” And in the chapter *Cum in ecclesia* [X 5.3.15], after reciting the same spiritual services, the pontiff says: “Many people think this is permissible because they consider that a law of death has prevailed through long usage, not realizing that crimes are all the graver the longer they hold an unhappy soul in bondage.” And afterward it continues: “Lest therefore these things be done henceforth — whether for ecclesiastical persons being installed in their seats, or for priests being instituted, or for burying the dead, or for blessing those marrying, or for conferring or having conferred other sacraments — we most strictly prohibit that anything be demanded.
