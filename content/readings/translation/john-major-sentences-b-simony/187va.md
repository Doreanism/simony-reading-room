And so a person who holds one spiritual right of uncertain title but greater revenue may exchange it for another spiritual right of lesser importance — it does not matter that it is not called a benefice; names signify by convention. For example, when two persons are in litigation and each has an apparent title: if Sortes holds a small benefice, he may concede it to Plato, the other litigant, on condition that Plato cede his right; and neither party commits a sin. Therefore, if neither of them held a benefice, one takes the title of the benefice and is called curate or rector in Britain, assigning to the other a pension for life or until provision is made for him of a richer living — provided this is done on the counsel of prudent men, all of this is licit. But if one of them has no right at all and knows this, the one who does have the right may redeem the vexation by assigning the other a portion, or by giving money so that he will withdraw, without sin — because he acquires no more right than he had before. This is clear: he gives money so as not to be harassed, while the other party takes it unjustly and is bound to make restitution. Against this: when two persons are litigating with probable claims, it may happen that at the end of the proceedings it is discovered that Plato has no right but had only a sophistical claim. It follows that, although Sortes may remit to Plato the expenses incurred, Plato cannot accept them with a clear conscience. I reply: this does not follow, because even though Plato has less right, he will appeal; and now the right appears to grow in favor of one party, now of the other — and without that [certain right], the probable basis for his litigation and proceedings will, for the time being, excuse him. From what has been said you can discern whether one who has an acquired right to a benefice, even though he is in fact being harassed, may exchange that right for another actual benefice — and it is clear that he may. Furthermore, you can discern whether for a probable right one may accept a less certain benefice.

### In summary, we say that the Roman Pontiff acquires a true papacy

even if he is elected simoniacally and by corrupt agreement — because there should be no difficulty regarding the episcopate, since it is an order, and also with respect to the jurisdiction inherent in it. And this is reasonable, lest there be confusion and uncertainty in the head of the Church. It is, however, a sign of great ambition and of a man who does not fear God to ascend to the papacy in this manner. From this it is clear that, although such an ascent is forbidden by divine and natural law, the papacy itself is not thereby denied to him. Furthermore, it is clear that a person may redeem a vexation in temporal matters, both for a right already acquired and for one being acquired. In spiritual matters, one may redeem a vexation for a right already acquired — whether or not one is in possession — since such a person buys no right but merely redeems a vexation. Concerning transactions and the exchange of benefices, we have said that if they are carried out on the counsel of prudent men, neither simony nor the appearance of simony is committed. Furthermore, we have said that individuals may confer privately about the exchange of benefices when they see that it would benefit the Church or serve them spiritually, or that it is harmful.

## Distinction Twenty-Five, Question Six.

The sixth question is: whether there can be a pact in spiritual matters. The answer is given through two conclusions, of which the first is: A spiritual thing may be contracted for in exchange for another spiritual thing. This is clear from the exchange of churches — as in “I give you the title to my church, and I take the title to your church,” from the preceding question.
