And the reason is that he is bound to do the opposite. So it is in the case at hand. The cardinals are bound to elect the Roman Pontiff gratuitously, if they are to elect at all, and they are forbidden to accept such a gift — not only by positive human law. Therefore they do not acquire ownership of that money, but are obligated to restore it. You object: a woman receives money for the shameful hire of her body, and she is bound to do the opposite; nevertheless, she does acquire ownership of that money. The reply is: a woman is not bound to communicate gratuitously the dominion of her body to another, because she is in no way bound to communicate the use of her body to another. Rather, although such use is forbidden to her, the acceptance of money is not forbidden to her, nor is she bound to make restitution. Nor can the Pope himself, once he is pontiff, remit that money to them — that is, so that they are safe in conscience however they spend that money. That was ecclesiastical money, as is supposed, of which the pontiff is not the owner but only a faithful steward. But against this it is argued from the statement of Pope Nicholas that is recited in Distinction 79, chapter *Si quis pecunia*, where it says: “If anyone has been enthroned in the Apostolic See by money or by human favor or by popular or military tumult, without a concordant and canonical election by the cardinals and the other religious clergy, let him be held not as apostolic but as apostate, and let it be lawful for the cardinals and other God-fearing clerics and laypeople to anathematize him and to drive him from the Apostolic See with human assistance.” The Gloss there responds that the text speaks of one who was elected through simony by those who did not have the power of election — not by the cardinals. This is the Orleans gloss. And this is evident from the words of the text just cited. But I say that was the old law, and it is revoked in the chapter *Licet*. Nor does the law cited at the Council of Constance in the proof of the conclusion revoke this law, because that law in the chapter *Licet* is reasonable, considering the times in which we live.

### Second, it is argued.
If Sortes has a true right to benefice A and is impeded by someone, he can redeem the vexation with money so as to have his benefice. Therefore the conclusion is false. The antecedent is evident from the chapter *Dilectus filius*, where a provost recovered money by mandate of the supreme pontiff from the Archdeacon of York, who had not kept his promise to cease from vexation. The reply is: simony is not committed here, because a temporal thing is not given for a spiritual thing, but a temporal thing is given for something that has an estimable monetary value. For the cessation of unjust vexation is a temporal thing with an estimable monetary value. Therefore someone may lawfully redeem that unjust vexation, even though the one who unjustly vexes him sins and is bound to restore what he accepted, because the one redeeming the vexation has a just cause for redeeming it, and he does not cooperate in the sin of the other, because he does not consent to the active vexation of the other, nor is he pleased by the reception of money that the other accepts; rather, what pleases him is the cessation of the unjust vexation and the payment of the price for it, which is not evil — as we said in the fifteenth distinction, concerning usury. But when a person has a fully acquired right, whether in spiritual or temporal matters, it is lawful to redeem vexation, according to the saying of the Apostle: “Redeeming the time, because the days are evil” [Ephesians 5:16] — whether he has been placed in possession or not, but is unjustly impeded from possession.
