Therefore in the same way one may give money to the bishop for obtaining a benefice. The antecedent is clear: a person licitly gives money to a doorkeeper so as to gain access to the bishop — at least without the vice of simony. This is evident: if the petitioner were to open the lock with a latch or with a key hidden under the door, or were to break down the doors, even though he would sin in doing so by removing the obstacle that prevents him from gaining access to the bishop, he would not commit simony.

The reply is that it is denied that a person may give money to another to intercede on his behalf. And I grant the point that is assumed about the doorkeeper, provided the doorkeeper says nothing further — especially if the petitioner is suitable — because he is merely removing an obstacle that prevents him from gaining access to the collator. This is without the vice of simony, as the argument has sufficiently made clear.

### Thirdly, it is argued: Service is a less principal cause,

or a cause without which the conferral would not have occurred [causa sine qua non]. Therefore money may serve in the same way. The reply: it is not the same thing to say “it is a less principal final cause” and “a cause without which not.” For knowledge, according to many, is a cause without which volition does not occur, yet it is not a final cause. Service in this case is a cause without which not, because through it the man’s faithful diligence and industry become known. Nothing comparable applies to money.

### Fourthly, it is argued.

The Roman pontiff licitly sells benefices, and in his case it is not simoniacal. Therefore it is simoniacal in no one’s case. You say, moreover, that the Lex Julia on electoral bribery [ambitus] does not apply in the city of Rome, because there the prince creates the magistrates [Digest, *Ad legem Juliam de ambitu*, lex unica]. And thus the turpitude of trafficking in benefices ceases when a benefice is purchased from the supreme spiritual prince. He also appears to dispense with the one to whom he sells the benefice, so that that person may hold it.

The reply: some things are simoniacal because they are prohibited [mala prohibita], and in these matters the supreme pontiff does not commit simony. An example: as in the blessing of chalices, priestly vestments, and the like — things instituted solely by the Church. These are not simoniacal by natural or divine law. But other things are prohibited because they are simoniacal [mala in se] — namely, those that contravene natural and divine law. In such matters the pontiff commits simony, just as any other person is subject to some law — though he does not incur the positive human penalties that an inferior would incur. Taking anything temporal for the conferral of a benefice that one is bound to confer freely contravenes divine and natural law. The text is clear in the chapter *Ex diligenti*, *De simonia* [X 5.3]. And this is the position of Panormitanus on *De simonia*, chapter 1. Durandus and Petrus Paludanus prove this sufficiently, because since it is simoniacal to take anything for the license to teach (as was established above), a fortiori it is simoniacal to take anything for the right to elect a bishop and to officiate in divine services. Blessed Thomas, in the *Secunda Secundae*, question 100, says it would be heretical to hold that the pope cannot commit simony. To the last part of the argument — whether he dispenses with the one to whom he sells the benefice — Joannes Andreae says in the *Mercuriales* [commentary on the rule *Cui licet*]: If a bishop establishes a penalty of excommunication, applicable equally to all who gamble at dice [hasardum], he is not thereby released if someone, induced by the bishop, plays dice with him. On the contrary, the other party incurs the excommunication, and the bishop, playing with him again, incurs a greater excommunication — by communicating with a person who is criminal in a crime for which he has been condemned. For the first offense, the induced party incurs only the lesser excommunication if the bishop induced him, because the bishop is then considered the cause of the other’s playing, and thus as the principal agent he incurs the greater excommunication — just as if by his command or counsel a cleric were struck [cf. the chapter *Mulieres*, *De sententia excommunicationis*]. Just as one who knowingly participates with an excommunicated person by word or kiss is not thereby deemed to have absolved that person — unless he expressly states his will that the person be regarded as absolved [cf. *De sententia excommunicationis*, chapter *Si summus pontifex*, in the Clementines] — so it seems entirely that in our case the pope does not dispense with the buyer unless he makes this clear by some other means.
