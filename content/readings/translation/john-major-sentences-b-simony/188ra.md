Here blessed Ambrosius says that the wealth of the Church should be given not only for the redemption of captives, but also for the nourishment of the poor and for burial — concluding that in these three categories it is lawful to break, melt down, and sell the vessels of the Church, even those that have been consecrated. And he means not only these cases but all similar pious causes — against the more recent prelates of the Church and against certain men of his own time. Now, as I was saying, it is a greater thing to free a soul from purgatory than a body from captivity. Is not the obligation of teaching attached to a prebend? There is an express text in c. penult., de magistris. Nor is the safeguard of the Gloss even necessary. I see no reason why a spiritual and pious condition may not be attached in the giving of a temporal and profane thing. And for the question of simony, it makes no difference whether the spiritual obligation is attached before or at the time of the giving. The same point is evident from the dispute between a certain abbot of ours at Melrose and a certain nobleman of the forest named Alanus, where the Pontiff looked to the intention of Alanus’s father regarding the condition that had been attached.

### Against these conclusions the argument is raised again.

From the conclusions and their proofs it follows that if someone gives goods to the Church with a condition attached, then if the Church does not fulfill its promise, the heirs — even by their own authority or that of successors — may revoke those goods. This is an absurd consequence. And that it follows is clear from the proofs for the first conclusion. I reply: a donor may give something to the Church in many ways. In one way, thus: “I give goods to the Church” — even though he intends that the minister live well and spend the surplus on pious uses. In a second way, thus: “I give such-and-such a possession to the Church so that on such-and-such a day each year an anniversary should be celebrated,” adding: “If this is not observed, I and my heirs may revoke the gift.” In a third way: “We may revoke it without the authority of a superior — indeed, by our own authority.” In the first way, the gift is valid and cannot be revoked even if the condition is not observed. In this way churches have been endowed with benefices, for those who endowed a church intended that the revenues be spent for Christ and his ministers. In the second way, the goods may be revoked if the condition is not fulfilled. To this effect there is an express text in c. Verum, de conditionibus appositis in desponsatione, and in other contracts, where the Roman Pontiff says: “Indeed, when any possession is donated to a church under whatever condition, the gift cannot afterward be revoked by the donor, unless perhaps it was conferred under such a condition that upon its cessation the gift ought to be revoked.” And in this way stipends have been granted for the celebration of anniversaries, because this is how common custom regards the matter, unless there is a reasonable impediment. Whence Augustinus says to Casselanus: “Concerning those matters about which divine Scripture has determined nothing certain, the custom of the people of God and the practices of our ancestors are to be regarded as law.” If you say that things once dedicated to churches ought not to be recalled to profane uses, this is true if they were given unconditionally. Concerning the third case, there would be a difficulty. But I hold the position that a person cannot revoke by his own authority but only by the authority of a judge — otherwise it would be sacrilege [c. Quicunque militum; c. Nulli, C.12 q.2]. And the reason is this: on account of the many wicked persons who are excessively attached to earthly goods, who would fabricate a cause where none exists. If you ask how this arrangement can bind successors to celebrate the anniversary, I reply: the thing passes with its obligation, and a benefice is given on account of the office [c. finali, de rescriptis, libro ut] — so that on account of the stipend, which is the benefice, they are bound to perform this anniversary office.
