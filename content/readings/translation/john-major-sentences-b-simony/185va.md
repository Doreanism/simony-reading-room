And he argues a posteriori thus: for indeed, if the third conclusion were true, you would say that all bishops act rightly who by fair means and foul heap up the revenues of their bishoprics, both in the conferral of benefices and of sacraments — bishops who abound in goods to the point of nausea, while they take from the poor on whom they ought to have pity, to the great disfigurement of the clergy, from which the sacred canons have commanded us to guard as from a serpent. Furthermore, the same point is argued: if current custom excused them in this way, for the conferral of a single order, in the course of time a way would be opened so that it would be permissible to take four or five scudi — not by reason of the sacrament (they will say) but under the veil of custom. This would open the way to sinners. Moreover, since this is contrary to the sacred canons — canons founded not merely in positive law but in natural equity — no custom can remove these canons from their force. I believe that Aristotle and Plato, walking in the darkness of paganism, would freely confess that it is contrary to reason for one member of the clerical estate — even though he is the head of the diocese — to suck nourishment from the lowly members to their detriment, especially when the head is monstrously too large, for whom an ample and honorable reduction would be most expedient — at least by refraining from extorting from the poor members, if not by giving up what he has. Again: if you wish to excuse all of them on the basis of custom — or rather corruption — this will be because, under this third conclusion, a great many bishops are living in a perilous state. But this does not overturn the conclusion, as is evident from the many who live wickedly, both in the clergy and among the people. Some bishops, however, are good and immune from all these things. I therefore hold the third conclusion and would not dare in conscience to excuse them in the manner of that [canonist].

### Against the second conclusion the following argument is made.

To take this money introduced by custom is to collect beyond the revenues owed to the Church, as is clear from the revenues the pastor had before the custom was introduced. Therefore he unjustly compels the people to observe the custom. The response is: pastors often sin in this matter too, and are excessively solicitous, as is clear from the chapter *Suam* [X 5.3.14] of this title. But in kingdoms where every village regularly has a parish church, the livings are commonly meager. It is natural that when clerics labor in spiritual things for the people, they should be sustained by them. And they do not have tithes in respect of their quota in a great many places. The ordinary revenues before this payment of moneys did not suffice. Therefore, by a free gift, the people of their own accord, after spiritual services had been rendered, piously gave the clergy something. And although some livings are rich, as we see in Paris, those are few in comparison with the entire multitude. In such places too, pastors can compel their subjects to observe customs, even though the rationale for the law is not the same — especially when the curate is not very suitable. But when a law or custom is general, it must be observed even if the same force of the law does not apply in every individual case. Regarding the preservation of this custom, the Roman pontiff in the chapter *Ad apostolicam* says that the truth is to be ascertained by the bishop of the place — as if to say: if the parish priests demand exorbitantly, they are to be restrained. And although at first glance it might seem that in a meager living the pastor should compel his subjects to observe the custom but not in a rich one, this does not hold — because it would be to enervate the custom of the diocese and consequently to abolish the force of custom to the detriment even of a very suitable man who is worth more than two others combined.
