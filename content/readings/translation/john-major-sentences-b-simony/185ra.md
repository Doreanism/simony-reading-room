The major premise is clear from the case of Saul (1 Samuel 9), who offered money to Samuel so that he might reveal the whereabouts of his father’s donkeys. The text reads: “Again the servant answered Saul and said: Behold, I have found in my hand a quarter of a shekel of silver; let us give it to the man of God, that he may tell us our way.” The same is evident from 2 Kings 14 [1 Kings 14], concerning the wife of Jeroboam, who brought gifts to Ahijah the prophet. I grant that it is simoniacal to accept money by prophetic contract; nor did the prophets do this. Or as Hieronymus says commenting on Micah: the Jews, accustomed to true prophets, offered the most trifling gifts for the prophet’s sustenance. The wife of Jeroboam, queen of the kingdom of Israel, brought only ten loaves, a cake, and a jar of honey; she judged that a man of God, content with very little, would be vexed by a lavish royal offering. And indeed we do not read that the prophets accepted such things, though it was lawful for them to do so — not, however, by accepting them under a prophetic contract. Rather, it was the idolatrous pagans, deluded by false prophets, who were accustomed to trade immense gifts [for prophecy]. As we read in 3 Kings [1 Kings] concerning Naaman the Syrian. And in the eighth chapter of that book it is written: “Elisha also came to Damascus; Ben-hadad king of Syria was sick, and they told him, saying...” And shortly afterward it continues: “So Hazael went to meet him, bringing with him gifts and all the good things of Damascus, the loads of forty camels.” But nothing is read to the effect that Elisha accepted them; indeed, that he did not accept is proven by what Naaman the Syrian said to him: “I beseech thee, take a blessing from thy servant.” And he answered: “As the Lord liveth, before whom I stand, I will receive none.” And when Naaman pressed him, he utterly refused. And it is plausible that in a similar case he would have done the same. Behold, then, how in the threefold gift — namely of the tongue, of entreaty, and of service — and in other things that draw persons improperly toward the conferral of benefices, simony is committed; and how certain kinds of service and entreaties induce simony, while others do not. And that someone may acquire a benefice simoniacally and wrongfully, yet not unjustly against distributive justice — that is, he does not lack what is essential for the conferral of the benefice, even though something is added that vitiates the moral goodness of the act in the one conferring, or in the one receiving, or in both. Such a person is not bound to relinquish the benefice; rather, it suffices to repent of the sin, serving well since he is suitable.

## Question Four of Distinction Twenty-Five.

The fourth inquiry concerning the matter of simony is this: Is it permissible to receive something by reason of custom and to compel the unwilling to give according to the lawful custom of the place in the conferral of sacraments and benefices? This question is difficult and touches upon matters of conscience. For this reason I shall dwell upon it a little; yet ever mindful of prolixity, lest I weary my readers. First I shall set out conclusions stating the manner of speaking of others; afterward I shall state what seems to me the right thing to say.

### Let this be the first conclusion of all that is to be said.

It is permissible after the fact to accept something as a gratuitous gift. Proof: no one sins by accepting a gratuitous gift; and when money is given after the fact, it is the clearest possible sign of a gratuitous gift. Therefore, one who confers a benefice may accept something after the conferral of a benefice or sacrament in the nature of a gratuitous gift. For if the opposite were maintained — that he cannot accept — this consequence would follow: that the benefactor of someone would be less capable of receiving a free gift than a highway robber of Jericho.
