And Martin V, in another extravagant issued in the second year of his pontificate at Mantua, which begins *Damnabilem*, confirms all excommunications, suspensions, deprivations, interdicts, and other censures issued by the supreme pontiffs against simoniacs. He wills that every simoniac, whether secret or manifest, wherever he may have committed simony, of whatever status — even if he holds the dignity of a bishopric or cardinalate, or any other ecclesiastical or temporal dignity — shall incur these penalties by the very fact of the act, reserving to himself the absolution from the said excommunications, suspensions, and penalties except at the point of death. Nor does he will that by virtue of any grant, indult, or any faculty of absolving, whether already granted or to be granted by the Apostolic See, such persons may be absolved, unless the authorization specifically includes the power to absolve from the sin of simony. Eugenius [IV] willed the same in the extravagant beginning *Cum detestabile*. So too Paul II in the decree he issued on Holy Thursday, and Sixtus IV. The second penalty concerns restitution, which is more burdensome in the forum of conscience. It is said that the pontiff can dispense from all penalties imposed by canon law — suspension, irregularity, infamy, and excommunication. Let this stand as a first proposition or conclusion, the rationale for which is as follows: there is no purely papal statute that can bind him, for just as an equal has no authority over an equal, so neither does the same person have authority over himself.

### Second conclusion.

He cannot dispense so as to make simony committed against the divine law not a sin. Proof: an inferior cannot revoke the law of a superior.

### Third proposition.

He can, however, bring it about that an act which would be simoniacal on account of his own prohibition in the case of an inferior is not simoniacal. This follows from the proof of the first proposition. He cannot, however, bring it about that what was a sin in the past was not a sin. Concerning the past, he cannot make the past not to have been past — just as not even God can, as stated in Book 6 of the *Ethics* [of Aristotle].

### Fourth proposition.

He can dispense with a pactional simoniac, when money has been given, so that such a person may licitly hold a benefice. Proof: he can give him a new collation of that benefice, and neither natural law nor divine law stands in the way of such a person being capable of holding that benefice; therefore he can confer it upon him. The taint is sufficiently purged when the matter comes into the hands of the lord [*Digest*, *De usucapionibus*, lex *Si fur*, and the passage beginning *quidam*, section *si forte*; *Causa* 1, *quaestio* 1] — that is, just as if I were to hold your horse, obtained by theft, with the horse remaining in my possession, you could transfer ownership of the horse to me, and it would remain mine. Now the pontiff can confer a benefice and can act in advance of ordinary collators, and this is reasonable in the case at hand, because otherwise men would not resign these benefices against their own consciences — although there can be some occasion for purchasing benefices. But concerning the gift of service when there is no pact: if a man is suitable, for whatever purpose he may serve, I do not believe he is bound to restore the benefice. In the same way, if someone acquired a benefice through entreaty or kinship, provided he is suitable, it suffices to repent of the sin, whether or not his principal intention was to obtain the benefice through service or entreaties. This can be shown from the final chapter *De simoniis* [in the *Decretals*].

But concerning mental simony, among the doctors there is no small difficulty, on account of the final chapter under this rubric — namely, whether a man who has acquired a benefice through mental simony is bound to resign it if the Roman pontiff does not dispense with him. For understanding this, it should be considered that there is one kind of mental simony without any external act, another kind of mental simony with a subsequent external act but without a pact, and another with a subsequent pactional external act. And I understand “pact” here as referring to a gift already given or one promised in expectation. I offer examples. First: I give to a collator a sum of money so that I may have, by real agreement, a benefice, and he accepts on that basis. Concerning this pactional simony, no one calls into doubt that the man is bound to resign the benefice.
