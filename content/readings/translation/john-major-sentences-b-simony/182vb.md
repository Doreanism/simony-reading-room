## Question Two of Distinction Twenty-Five

Secondly, concerning this distinction, it is asked: whether simony is a grave sin. The answer is given through conclusions. The first conclusion is affirmative to the question, and it is proved as follows. Every act opposed to natural, divine, and human law is evil; simony is of this kind; therefore. Proof of the minor premise: it is contrary to natural equity for someone to sell the gifts of the Holy Spirit, which are not within his power — indeed, he hands over the Holy Spirit himself as though he were his own slave, as is evident from the letter of Tharasius, Bishop of Constantinople, sent to Pope Adrianus. And this is recited in the first question, chapter one, *Eos qui*, where it is written: “More tolerable is the impious heresy of those Macedonians who are assailants of the Holy Spirit himself. For they, in their madness, declare the Holy Spirit to be a creature and servant of God the Father and the Son; but these men make the Holy Spirit their own servant. For every master sells what he has if he wishes — whether a slave or anything else among his possessions.”

Moreover, it is contrary to natural law for anyone to take money for the transfer of a thing that is not within his power. Third, even granting that it were within his power, he sells a thing that is beyond temporal estimation, because it is something spiritual exchanged for money. Wisdom 7 states that wisdom is more precious than all riches, and desirable things cannot be compared to it.

Perhaps you say that one need not look to the value of a thing in itself, but that things are exchanged according to human need, in accordance with what Augustinus says in *City of God* XI, chapter 18: “For who at home would not rather have bread than lowly mice and fleas?” A slave will be sold at a lower price than a gemstone. Although you might refute the argument plausibly in the case of created knowledge, you cannot do so for the spiritual goods of which we speak, since they surpass human goods in the estimation of human need — that is, speaking of market value and not of the intrinsic value of the thing. And for this reason all people rightly censure the traitor Judas for having sold the Redeemer of the world, who is God, for so paltry a sum.

And although simony is contrary to natural law, it is still possible to have doubt about many cases — whether simony is present in them. This is evident from the cases of usury and simple fornication, which the pagans did not consider to be contrary to natural law. That simony is contrary to divine law is evident from Acts 8 and Matthew 10: *Freely you have received; freely give*, where the Gloss says: “Here simony is prohibited.” For there the discourse concerned performing miracles and astounding prodigies, because immediately before those words it says: *Heal the sick, raise the dead, cleanse lepers, cast out demons. Freely you have received; freely give*, where the Interlinear Gloss says: “Just as I give you such power without price, so you too should give freely.” Nevertheless, it does not follow from this that one who possesses acquired knowledge freely must communicate it freely to others.
