### Sixth conclusion.

One who gives a benefice to his servant in relief of wages owed to that servant commits simony. Proof: he virtually takes money for a spiritual thing that is annexed. Therefore he commits simony. For it is the same thing to remit to the collator a hundred crowns that he owes the servant, as to receive the money and then give it to the collator. For this reason the dictum of Hostiensis is worthless when he says: if a bishop takes a cleric into his service, promising him an annual salary until he provides him with a suitable benefice, and after conferring the benefice remits the salary — this is not simony. On the contrary, it is simony, because the understanding between them is that the cleric will serve without pay once the benefice has been obtained. Therefore the bishop virtually takes the money he ought to give for the service on account of the benefice. Therefore it is true simony. This is clear from the common dictum concerning the threefold gift — namely, the gift from the hand, from the tongue, and from service — any of which suffices to incur simony.

### Seventh conclusion.

In truth, it does not matter that the collator takes only a small amount as the cause for conferring a benefice, even though it is not presumed that a bishop is corruptly moved by a trifle. Proof: to give a benefice on any consideration other than suitability and public utility is unjust and amounts to respect of persons, as we showed in the preceding distinction; and this is simoniacal, as will be clear from what follows. Furthermore, a petty theft — petty on the part of the object that moves the thief to steal — is nonetheless theft and sin, just as a large theft is, even if not as grave. Therefore a small amount received that moves the collator to confer corruptly is a sin just as a large amount is. Another person’s small gift moves the complaisant and imprudent man to act corruptly just as a large gift moves the generous and prudent man. Therefore one must look not to the magnitude of the object but to the disposition of the one who is corruptly swayed. For it happens that a man is moved as much by a gilded image scarcely worth two crowns as another man is by a first-rate riding horse.

### Against the foregoing, an argument is raised concerning the threefold gift.

If these conclusions were true, the following conclusion would follow: that a collator who confers a benefice on a relative on account of kinship would be simoniacal, just as the cantor of Paris maintained. This position Altissiodorensis, Blessed Thomas, and all subsequent authorities reject. And that it follows is shown thus: the collator gives in return for a gift of blood [munus a sanguine]. But you will say — as these doctors do — that the bishop here receives nothing, and thus, although he sins in building Sion with blood [cf. Micah 3:10], he does not commit simony. I prove that these authorities contradict themselves in saying this: for neither does the collator take money when he confers a benefice on an unsuitable candidate, or on a suitable one principally on account of prayers. Therefore there is a contradiction in granting the one case and denying the other on the same grounds. The Lord Innocentius, in the chapter *Tuam* [*De aetate et qualitate ordinandorum*], says that prayers, considered merely as entreaties, do not cause simony, but they do if they are directed toward money. And in the same chapter the Cardinal follows suit.

### Against this.

These doctors and the common opinion cannot consistently say this, because on their account this distinction [between types of gifts] would be meaningless. The point is also argued on rational grounds: the prayers of a particular person move a collator to confer a benefice corruptly as much as two hundred or three hundred ducats, because the prayers are equivalent to that amount in the estimation of the one conferring. But the one suffices for simony; therefore so does the other. Moreover, Seneca rightly says in the second book of *De beneficiis*: “He did not receive it freely who received it after having asked.” Furthermore, nothing costs more dearly than what is purchased by prayers. As is clear in the chapter *Sunt nonnulli* [C.1 q.1]: “There are some” (says Blessed Gregory) “who take absolutely no payment for ordination, and yet they bestow sacred orders for the sake of human favor.
