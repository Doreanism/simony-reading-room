### Gathering together the points made in passing.

We have said that a parish priest may lawfully compel his subjects to observe a praiseworthy custom — but only after the fact, when the custom is fully established by prescription. Before the fact, or at the time of administering the sacrament, he cannot do so, even though he takes payment not by reason of the sacrament but by reason of the custom, because the custom does not yet exist, and there would be an appearance of selling sacraments — and the appearance of evil is to be avoided. He may, however, accept what is freely offered. Nor does the chapter *Emendari* [C.1 q.1] stand in the way, where it is forbidden to place coins in the basin lest the priest appear to be selling baptism — this is on account of the appearance of evil. But in the following chapter it is said that he may accept what is freely offered. Bishops, however, cannot do these things, since they regularly have sufficient revenues. Whence it follows that less pardon should be given to a wealthy bishop who takes such payments than to a poor one. But you say: if a poor priest were to accept something secretly for his sustenance, he would not commit simony; therefore neither does a rich one commit simony. I reply: to one who understands, the consequence has no plausibility. Where there is no custom compelling people to pay, he would commit simony; but not where there is a custom among parish priests — just as a merchant takes profit beyond the principal without usury, whereas if one who is not a merchant were to take similar profit, he would commit usury. That person commits simony who, in the administration of spiritual things, takes a temporal payment without justification, even though he says he takes it for his sustenance. And this concludes the matter of custom as it pertains to both bishops and their inferiors. Regarding notaries and similar officials, one must consider whether they have an assigned sustenance so that they may carry out their office without charge, or not. And accordingly one must judge whether they are simoniacal or not.

## Question Five of the Twenty-Fifth Distinction.

It is asked, fifth, whether one who has been simoniacally promoted by a gift from the hand [*munus a manu*] acquires a just title to a benefice. The answer is in the negative, as is evident from the extravagant decree of Martin V issued at the Council of Constance, which begins *Culte*. It states as follows: “With the approval of this sacred council, we declare that those ordained simoniacally are by that very fact [*ipso iure*] suspended from the exercise of their orders. Moreover, elections, postulations, confirmations, and any simoniacal provisions whatsoever to churches, monasteries, dignities, parsonages, offices, and benefices of any kind that are hereafter made shall be null by that very fact, and no right shall be acquired by anyone through them.”
