### Third argument.

It is permissible to sell the right of patronage, and yet the right of patronage is something antecedently annexed to a spiritual thing, since it is ordered toward presenting a cleric for an ecclesiastical benefice; therefore. Proof of the major: one who sells an estate in which there is a right of patronage transfers the right of patronage to the buyer, unless there has been a special mention of its retention — *de iure patronatus*, chapter *Ex literis*. Response: I deny that he sells the right of patronage; rather, he transfers it with the attachment of the estate to which it is annexed. And if the estate is worth a thousand pounds sterling without the right of patronage, he may not take more when selling the estate on account of the patronage.

### But against this it is argued.

Every right that the seller has, he transfers to the buyer by title of purchase and sale. But sometimes you will deny this proposition without qualification; in that case the question arises: by what title does he transfer the right of patronage to another? Response: by title of attachment — the right of patronage can incidentally be separated from the estate, but only in a special case. Just as one who sells a person alienates the good angel and the bad angel from his household, but not by title of sale except incidentally. I grant, however, that the sale is a *sine qua non* cause of the accession of the right of patronage.

### Fourth argument.

A person can buy all the revenues of a benefice, such as tithes, and yet tithes are annexed to a spiritual thing; therefore. That it is permissible is clear, since tithes consist of grain, lambs, onions, and things of that kind. Perhaps you say that it is permissible to buy those tithes and to sell them, but it is not permissible to buy the right of collecting tithes. For this right belongs to the churchman who holds the title on which the right of collection is founded. Against this I argue: the right of collecting tithes is something; therefore it is either really the tithe-goods themselves, or the person collecting the tithes. If the first, then I argue as follows: it is permissible for Sortes, a layman, to buy these tithes; these tithes are the right of collecting tithes; therefore it is permissible for Sortes to buy the right of collecting tithes. And by the same reasoning I shall prove that it is permissible for another person to sell it. Furthermore, a layman who takes the revenues of a church for a three-year period from a prelate has the right of collecting tithes; otherwise he would collect them unjustly. If you say that the right of collecting is the priest himself: Sortes has the right of collecting just as he has a degree. I ask you: what thing is a degree? It is nothing other than a graduated person. But suppose that he is sold, as Paulinus, Bishop of Nola, sold himself for the liberation of the son of a widow. Then: the prelate buys this priest; this priest is the right of collecting tithes; therefore. This is laughed off by denying the consequence. But he who takes a benefice for three years and justly collects does buy the right of collecting tithes, and he has the right of collecting — but not by reason of office. A beneficed cleric, however, is the right of collecting by reason of office; it is otherwise for one who takes from him. And even granting, for the sake of argument, that a beneficed priest were a slave (though this is against reason), his master would not have dominion over the goods of the church, because they are not his, nor would it be permissible to dispose of them in that manner — let them collect the tithe-revenues. I likewise concede that he who buys from a prelate does buy the right of collecting tithes, but he does not buy the right of collecting tithes by reason of office. For “to buy” implies an act of the will, and thus qualifies the appellation of reason. This complex expression — “the right of collecting tithes by reason of office” — stands for the prelate while connoting that it pertains to his office to collect these revenues, or for the tithes themselves while connoting that they can be collected by such a person by reason of office.
