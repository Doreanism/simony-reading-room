Proof: otherwise it would not be lawful for a craftsman to accept money for his craft, the opposite of which everyone asserts. Nor is it sufficient to say that he accepts payment for labor, because then the person who labors more should always receive more — which is false. Likewise, a jurist can sell his counsel and an advocate his services.

But against this position the argument of Altisiodorus is raised: the smith’s art cannot be sold; therefore no art can be. The antecedent is clear: it is a gift of God, as the interlinear gloss on Exodus 31 says concerning the word “Beseleel”: “I have filled him with the Spirit of God, with wisdom and understanding and knowledge in every craft, to devise whatever can be made by a smith” [Exodus 31:3-4].

The response is this: although every thing in the world is a gift of God, and every art that illumines the intellect is likewise such a gift, this does not suffice to make it spiritual in the present sense, as is clear from what has been said. Second, the argument is made that to be able to sell knowledge without simony is contrary to a determination of the Roman Pontiff. This is clear from c. *De magistris*, chapters one and two [Extra, de magistris]. In the first chapter, concerning the license to teach, it is written: “For the license to teach, let no one exact a fee, nor under the pretext of any custom seek anything from those whom he teaches.” And in the following chapter it is written: “You shall prohibit anyone in your parish from exacting or even having promised anything for a license to teach. If anything should afterward have been paid or promised, you shall cause what was paid to be returned and what was promised to be released.”

The response is to deny that what we have said is contrary to the Pontiff’s determination. For in those passages what is prohibited is not accepting something for teaching — indeed it is lawful both to accept and to ask for payment, both for the teaching itself and for the labor involved in teaching, if one is not bound to this by stipends. But what the Pontiff prohibits there is accepting money for granting someone the faculty to teach, because in that way unqualified persons would be admitted. And therefore in the first chapter of *De magistris* cited above, the Pontiff forbids denying a qualified person the license to teach, saying: “Let them not forbid anyone who is qualified from teaching, once a license has been sought.” “But whoever shall presume to act against this shall be removed from his ecclesiastical benefice. For it seems fitting that in the Church he should not enjoy the fruit of his labor who, through cupidity of spirit, while selling the license to teach, strives to impede ecclesiastical progress.” And for this reason the Cardinal of Cambrai wrote a certain treatise against the Chancellor of Paris, laboring to show that it is simoniacal to accept anything for the conferral of a license. And this is clear from the two chapters cited.

Thus far we have discussed the definition of simony, of “spiritual” and “annexed to a spiritual thing,” and established that in the administration of spiritual things — such as the sacraments or any other such things — it is lawful to accept something temporal, not only for sustenance but also for labor itself. Also, anyone may sell any created quality, just as a painter accepts payment for the training in his art; and similarly a harpist accepts something — and not for labor alone. Indeed, a man trained and skilled in an art has less labor than one untrained and unskilled, and yet he receives more in payment. And so it is finally with all craftsmen. Not, however, because a craftsman produces the art inhering in himself in another person, for natural philosophy does not allow an accident to migrate from one subject to another. But a light — a spiritual light — produces its effect in a susceptible patient, just as one luminous body is illuminated by another. But in the production of spiritual light, the patient not only receives but also acts — which does not occur in the case of corporeal light.

Furthermore, consider carefully: whether you say that labor is saleable, or that created knowledge is or is not, the scope of simony is no more broadened than by saying the opposite.
