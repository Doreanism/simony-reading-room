You see that these chapters are apparently against the second conclusion and seem to wage war with the chapter *Ad apostolicam*. But we say that these chapters are to be understood of cases where the custom has not been sufficiently established — because at least ten years are required while the people do not resist, since it is well established that a custom against laypeople alone is strengthened by a decade, as is customarily noted on the law *De quibus* [Dig. 1.3.32], *De lege*, and through the gloss on the final chapter *De consuetudine* in the Liber Sextus. To the second argument I say two things. First: even if it were so [i.e., introduced with sin], it does not follow that the custom is not now good. This is clear from the first Gauls, who did not fast on the vigils of all nine Apostles’ feasts. And from all those who introduce a custom contrary to written law while sinning — yet their successors do not sin, because the law has now been abrogated by custom. Moreover, to establish a custom it is not necessary to prove that its origin was good, because some custom is now reasonable that could have had its origin in sin, and some without sin. Secondly, I say that the more favorable interpretation is to be presumed where the opposite is not demonstrated. From the entire solution it is clear that where there is no custom of accepting money for the things named, the prelate cannot compel the people to give. It is clear that where the custom has been introduced in some fashion but not sufficiently established, he cannot compel — as is evident from the chapter *Cum in ecclesia* — and therefore all the more so he cannot compel where nothing of custom has been introduced. Likewise the question arises: by what title does he demand such money? It does not suffice to cite other places — for in that way I could eat the inner parts of animals in France on every Saturday outside of Lent, because the Castilians do so. And on those days between Christmas and Candlemas inclusive, it would be permissible to eat meat in Brittany after the custom of the Parisians — which is false. But against this it is argued: it happens that the parish priest does not have the necessary means of support; therefore he can then compel them to adopt the custom of paying that prevails in neighboring places. The response is: he still cannot exact anything. Rather, let him teach children, let him write, let him seek his livelihood by his hands or by his industry, as is stated in Distinction 91, the chapter *Clericus victum* — just as the Apostle did, as in the chapter *Cum apostolus* [D.91 c.4]. And if he does not know how to seek a livelihood by other means, then the people will provide for him and will be compelled to do so.

### Against the third conclusion the following argument is made.

The second conclusion entails the contradictory of the third; therefore these two conclusions are in conflict — because if it is permissible for the inferior, it will be permissible for the superior, since otherwise the inferior in jurisdiction would be proportionally greater than the superior. The response is to deny that these conclusions are repugnant to one another. For a poor parish priest who has great labor among his people, even with all his customary dues, sometimes barely collects sixty ducats, and sometimes scarcely thirty or less. In my estimation you will find many who do not collect a hundred ducats — indeed, not even sixty — and you will find some collecting beyond that sum even when all customary dues are counted in their total. Bishops, however, regularly have sufficient revenues. Are two or three thousand ducats not enough for a bishop’s sustenance — even if he were the most profound theologian, with as many eyes in his pastoral care as Argus, and noble besides? What need is there, then, when he has so many revenues for supporting himself and his household, to make so many exactions? Add further: if need be, the bishop can have ecclesiastical men in his service by giving them small amounts, for which he can provide according to place and time — not by reason of service, but by reason of recognized suitability in their service.
