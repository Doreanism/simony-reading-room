Moreover, a bishop confers upon someone a parish church on the condition that he resign his first — and the conferral is valid, for it is tacitly implied that by accepting a second parish church the first becomes vacant [capitulo De multa, de praebendis]. ### Second conclusion.

A temporal thing belonging to the Church may licitly be applied with the addition of a spiritual obligation, and conversely a spiritual thing may be promised in exchange for a temporal one — because in the handing over of one’s property, anyone may set whatever condition he wishes [lege In traditionibus, ff. de pactis]. Even if the transfer is made to a church, there is a text to this effect [in capitulo Verum, de conditionibus appositis in desponsatione]. In this way many monasteries and many churches have been endowed: receiving temporal goods with the obligation that the religious or priests, both present and future, should pray for the benefactors and their posterity. Just as gold, a profane thing, is given to the church so that a chalice may be made for use in the divine office, so it is with money left for anniversaries and in many other similar cases. Again, we see upright parish priests who have reasonable cause compelling their absence from the church appointing good vicars and agreeing with them on a fixed sum for service in the church. Moreover, a priest may celebrate Mass for six small coins, as established in the second question of this distinction; therefore, this may be made the subject of a condition. But you rightly object: the inference is invalid, because the one who confers a benefice may accept a palfrey after the conferral as a free gift, and yet he may not make this a condition. For this reason, the argument is made differently: Everything that is licit without a condition — and no illicitness or impropriety arises from the condition itself — may be made a condition. But this is the case in the matter at hand, as is clear from what was cited for the first conclusion. Furthermore, I may licitly make a condition of that which is tacitly implied — and when I have someone perform spiritual labor on my behalf, it is tacitly implied that sustenance should be provided to him in part or in full, as is clear from 1 Corinthians 9. Therefore I may make such an agreement: “If you celebrate for me, whenever you are disposed to do so, I will give you food and clothing.” From the Council of Agde it is written, as is found in capitulo Clerici, C.1 q.2: “Let all clerics who faithfully and vigilantly serve the church receive the stipends due to their holy labors according to the merit of their service, by the ordinance of the canons, from the priests.” And since it is licit to receive a stipend for holy labors, and no impropriety appears to be involved in making a pact to that effect, this is licit.

### Against these conclusions the argument is raised, especially against the second conclusion.

It is simony to attach a spiritual obligation to a profane thing. I reply by denying that this is simony, as is clear from what was said in the second question. And on this point, the Gloss on capitulo Signatum, de praebendis, provides a safeguard for avoiding simony. It is this: that the obligations of divine services to be celebrated should be attached to the thing itself, and afterward the thing should be transferred unconditionally — then the thing passes with those obligations attached. Innocentius says that a temporal obligation may be attached, but not a spiritual one, except for the redemption of captives [C.10 q.1, Hoc ius; C.12 q.1, Aurum]. Panormitanus follows Innocentius. This does not hold. If that pious cause can be attached — namely, redeeming captives — then a fortiori there can be attached the cause of redeeming souls from purgatory and promoting the glory of God.
