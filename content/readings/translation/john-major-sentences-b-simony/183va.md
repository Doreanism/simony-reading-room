### Regarding the knowledge of ends,

certain rules are set down. The first rule is: God is the final cause of all things, just as he is the efficient cause. Apocalypse [Revelation] 1: *I am the Alpha and the Omega, the beginning and the end* — to whom all good works are to be ordered virtually and finally, though not always actually. The second rule: among intermediate ends, that one is more principal which is more perfect than another and tends more immediately toward the ultimate end than the other. Thus it is to preach, communicating the word of Christ to the people, which is something highly excellent spiritually. But eating concerns the body, without which in this mortal life we cannot live. For however high an eagle may fly through the air, it must descend to earth for the sake of bodily food. The third proposition: nevertheless, the order of ends is not perverted when one mediate and more principal end is ordered toward another, less principal end in some good sense — as, for instance, when the principal end cannot be had without the less principal end. For example, one who has no provision from elsewhere cannot preach the Gospel without sustenance. Therefore he can preach in order to have sustenance. And in turn the prudent person takes that sustenance in order to preach; he preaches in order to please God and to attain blessedness; and thus there is a reciprocation of ends. And in this way there is in a certain sense only one end, according to the Philosopher in *Topics* III: “Where one thing exists on account of another, there is only one thing.” And thus one who preaches the Gospel receives a double reward: one temporal, with respect to sustenance from the people; another eternal, which he awaits from God. Hence, on the passage in 1 Timothy 5, *Let the elders who rule well* etc., the Gloss of Augustinus says: “Receiving the sustenance of necessity from the people, the reward of dispensation from the Lord.” From this it is evident that a person can enter religious life in order to have sustenance therein. Likewise, one can wish to be a priest in order to obtain a benefice — though not as an ultimate end.

### But against what has been said you may perhaps object.

From this sixfold distinction offered in the solution to the argument, a cloak is given to simony. For one selling a benefice will say: “I take a thousand crowns in the bestowal of this benefice — not as an adequate price, nor as an ultimate end, but for sustenance, labor, or the like.” Response: to take a thousand crowns in the bestowal of a benefice is not a means subordinated to that purpose. And it is just as if you were to say: “I wish to kill this man for the glory of God.” Some things can be ordered toward an ultimate end; others cannot. And even if the crafty conferrer says, “I take a thousand crowns freely, and I give the benefice freely,” God searches hearts and minds, and the presumption marks both the one who takes and the one who gives with the taint of simony. And even granted that it were not simony (which is false), he would sin by scandalizing others and culpably neglecting his own reputation. And for this reason the Apostle commands in 1 Thessalonians 5 [at the end]: *Abstain from every form of evil.* For it can happen that something is not intrinsically evil, yet is evil because of the appearance of malice.

### But against this it is argued.

When some rich prebend falls vacant and is to be conferred, a bishop can reserve for a time, for some pious cause, a certain portion of that prebend for two or three years according to the needs that arise. Let us suppose that the revenues to be collected amount to a thousand francs. Given this supposition, the argument proceeds as follows: the bishop can say to the one he is beneficing: “Give me a thousand francs on the spot and I will freely give you the benefice with all its revenues; I will apply your thousand francs to the fabric of the church, or to repairing or furnishing such-and-such a hospital.” If he can collect these revenues successively, why can he not take them at once?
