I do not acquire a new right, but I remove an impediment to holding the right I already have. This is evident from another angle: I can give money to a tyrant so that he ceases from a greater sin, if I cannot entirely dissuade him from sinning — and so, lest he kill me in the forest, I give him money, in the acceptance of which he sins less than he would by killing me. But it is a lesser sin to take a small amount of money than to hold a benefice continually [without right]. This is evident from the cited chapter *Dilectus*, where it is held that the giving of money for the preservation of an acquired spiritual right against undue molestation does not constitute simony or turpitude. Otherwise the one vexed could not have sought recovery of the money given, because when turpitude exists on both sides, there is no ground for recovery [C.1 q.3 *Quantum*]. But if Sortes is a suitable person who has been elected, whether he can give money to the one confirming him so that the latter will confirm him — when the confirming authority wrongfully denies confirmation to a suitable person who has been elected — is a matter of greater difficulty. There is a colorable argument for the affirmative. For since he is a suitable person who has been elected, he has a right to seek confirmation; the superior is bound to confirm the election unless he has a legitimate cause against the elect. Where he knows for certain that there is no cause for not confirming except that money is not given, it is perhaps not implausible that in the forum of conscience he can give money to remove the vexation. In spiritual matters, however, in order to avoid the taint of simony, it is not lawful to give money for the acquisition of a right. For in that case there is no ground for redeeming unjust vexation. But if the objection to his right is made on legal grounds, the taint of simony in giving money is manifest — as is evident in the chapter *Matther* and the chapter *De hoc*. I can, however, give money so that the electors do not elect an unsuitable candidate and so that they elect suitable ones. To this I have an acquired right, and it falls under the second category — but not so that they elect me as a suitable candidate, for I ought not to presume that I alone am suitable, nor that Sortes alone is. In this way, Jacobus did not buy the right of primogeniture that God had actually given him from his brother, but redeemed his vexation in the manner of a purchase — as if money were given to those opposing in fact but not in law. It is safer, however, to abstain from giving money to those opposing in fact and for confirmation, on account of the appearance of evil, and where the right in confirmation is fully acquired and those opposing in fact may perhaps have the appearance of opposing in law. In the same way, if someone has been unjustly excommunicated, he can give money for the purpose of redeeming the vexation, because perhaps he is impeded from electing or being elected, or incurs some other loss. I am speaking of an unjust excommunication that is null. The reason is: such an excommunicated person already has an acquired right to this. In the same way, if I have a small child near death, and no one present will baptize him except a priest who is alone and who will not baptize without payment, and I cannot persuade him otherwise, I shall give money, redeeming my unjust vexation. Whatever he may specify — whether for sustenance or for the sacrament — it suffices for me that I do not give with that intention. In that case only a temporal thing is given for a temporal thing, and I have an acquired right.

### Third, it is argued.

Something annexed to a spiritual thing can be exchanged for money. This is evident in the exchange of two benefices, one of which is richer than the other, with money refunded until equality of revenues is reached — as is evident in the chapter *Ad questiones*, under the title *De rerum permutatione*. And yet there is simony in this according to what has been said. Therefore. The reply is: the chapter explains itself. Possessions can be exchanged among themselves, and churches among themselves, but these things ought not to be mixed together — namely, a possession with a church, as the text says.
