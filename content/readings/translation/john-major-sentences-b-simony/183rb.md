### Response.

A person can celebrate for six small *albi* in seven different ways. First, as for a principal and ultimate end. Second, as for an adequate price, as if it were commutative justice and the value of one thing against another, as in the purchase of an ox at market. Third, for six small *albi* as for a less principal end, subordinated or subordinable to another, further end. Fourth, he may wish to celebrate for six small *albi* and to have the money for his sustenance. Fifth, for his labor, which he undergoes in celebrating. Sixth, for the distraction and impediment by which he is prevented in celebrating from attending to other business — and there can be a mixture of these types. The first two intentions are simoniacal by intrinsic denotation, and one who has such acts is a simoniac. The other simple intentions are licit, and in one who possesses grace, meritorious. But if one of the first two modes is mixed conjunctively with another, the mixture is illicit. Thus there is an amphiboly here: “Sortes celebrates for six small *albi*,” insofar as the word “for” can denote a final circumstance — and this in two ways: either the circumstance of an ultimate end, or of a non-ultimate end. The other senses are clear.

### From what has been said it is evident

that a canon rises at night in order to receive his distributions and for the sake of God, and yet he does not thereby commit simony. Likewise, many come to the service at the hour when distributions are given, who otherwise would not come, without the taint of simony. And even if an unlearned priest does not know how to distinguish so precisely in what sense he celebrates for six small *albi*, he is excused, because he sees that such a custom of celebrating is accepted and not condemned by learned men; therefore he is to be pardoned, even if he errs somewhat in his end — for it can happen that he actually refers his action to no end beyond the six *albi*. And although simony is contrary to natural law in the extended sense of the term that the wise discern, it is not, however, contrary to natural law in the strictest sense.

### But against that I argue.

A priest will not celebrate unless he receives six small *albi*. Likewise, a canon will not come to the canonical hours if he is not going to receive distributions. Therefore money is the principal cause, since when it is present the attendance is present and when it is absent the attendance is absent; therefore it is the principal cause. I reply: not conceding the antecedent, I deny the consequence, because when a less principal cause is present together with a principal cause, the effect is produced, yet neither of them suffices by itself to produce the effect, but the effect is removed if either of them is removed. Just as someone goes to the Sorbonne both to hear the disputants and to drink the wine of the bachelors, and yet would not go for one of those reasons alone, but for both together — for if one of them were removed, as on a fast day, he would not go. Nevertheless, it does not follow that the six small *albi* are the principal cause. For that cause is more principal which moves the agent to a greater degree in his estimation, or to which the other is reduced as an end under an end. But against this: by celebrating in order to have six small *albi*, those coins are the end of the celebration in one of the illicit senses, and therefore the six small *albi* are the principal cause. This is evident from a parallel: we do not preach the Gospel in order to eat, but we eat in order to preach. Likewise, we do not live in order to eat, but we eat in order to live. Response: although it is the opinion of some that the order of ends is perverted when the principal end is ordered toward a less principal end, this is not necessarily the case, as is evident from the solution to the argument, where the celebration is ordered toward sustenance.
