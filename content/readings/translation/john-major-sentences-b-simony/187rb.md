### Fourth, it is argued.

When two persons contend for a benefice, they can settle between themselves, as we see every day: a perpetual pension is assigned to one, and the benefice to the other. And the case is expressly stated in law, in the chapter *Nisi*, under the title *De praebendis*, where one person received the benefice and the other a pension of a certain number of pounds annually, and sold his right to the benefice for the price of a certain number of pounds. Therefore something annexed to a spiritual thing [is exchanged] for a temporal thing. The essence of a settlement is that a non-gratuitous agreement is made in which something is given or retained so that the parties withdraw from the lawsuit. For it is by this feature that it differs from an amicable composition properly so called.

### The reply is:

When both have a probable title, they can settle by the authority of prudent men; nor is a settlement and exchange of benefices simoniacal in itself, but on account of the danger of simony and fraud the laws forbid it. Therefore the Pontiff says in the said chapter *Nisi* that he would hold that composition suspect if it were not for the respectability of those persons — there is nothing to fear. Those who weigh the words of the law and not their meaning are not to be believed when they say that it is not lawful for those exchanging benefices to have discussions with one another, but that one must go to the bishop, informing him that he would do better in another place, making mention of the other’s benefice, and then the bishop will bring both together. This is not necessary: they can speak with one another about this and explain their intention to each other, and say that they are content if it pleases the superior. But on account of the law invalidating the contract, they cannot effectively exchange without the permission of the bishop. For example: if I am disliked and less welcome in this place, but can serve my subjects well elsewhere, and another would be more welcome to my flock — why could I not desire to go to another place? Since I cannot in this mortal state speak in the manner of angels, why should I not make my mind known to another? The advantage of the churches or of the church, or the advancement of the persons without detriment to the church, is a sufficient reason for exchange. If there were no law enacted that an exchange should not take place without the consent of the bishop or bishops (when they are from different dioceses), two prudent men could exchange among themselves, and it would in no way be simony. But on account of the danger of simony or avarice, this is forbidden; and so an exchange made without the authority of the bishop can be tainted with the vice of simony in name, because it is prohibited. I say further: if Sortes has benefice A and simoniacally exchanges it for benefice B of Plato who acts justly, Plato thereafter has a just title to benefice A and Sortes to benefice B. The reason is that they resigned their benefices into the hands of the superior, who conferred these benefices anew. Each is capable of holding the benefice, unless some positive human law prejudices Sortes. Benefice B is not given as the price corresponding to benefice A, but is conferred anew. It is otherwise with fields purchased through usury: the defendant who takes the place of the usurious increment succeeds [to the obligation of restitution].

### To the form of the argument:

I deny that a pension is something purely temporal. “Benefice” sometimes stands in the nominative for the right of collecting fruits by reason of the divine office, which is commonly called a canonry, to which a person is obligated; and sometimes it stands for the fruits themselves, and then it is something temporal — as in chapter 1, under the title *De usuris*. So a pension is understood as the right of levying such fruits by reason of divine worship when a person is bound [to the office]: the thing is virtually a benefice and a temporary apportionment.
