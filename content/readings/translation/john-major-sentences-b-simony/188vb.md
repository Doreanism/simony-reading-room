Concerning mental [simony] without an external act, there is no obligation to make restitution — this is certain, for there nothing is given, as was shown in one corollary of the first question. Concerning the second member — that is, when I give money to a prelate so that I may have a benefice at the time it falls vacant, or beforehand, and he knows my intention in giving and accepts with that same intention, yet nothing is said openly between us — there are various positions. Johannes Andreas says that intention alone never gives rise to anything other than mental simony. Panormitanus approves this in c.Tua nos. Against this it is argued: a person acquiring a benefice in this case is bound to restore it, nor does he acquire title to the benefice, as is clear from the authorities cited — for otherwise it would be a disguise for simony, and men would tacitly purchase benefices without any formal pact. And the pontiffs, wishing to remove the stain of simony from among men, speak against this kind most especially, as against a little fox. Moreover, if someone, without the knowledge of the chapter and even of the bishop, committed simony alongside a chapter member, the chapter is bound to resign the benefice — i.q.i. *Presentium* — therefore *a fortiori* in our case. And it is clear that the pontiff can dispense with regard to irregularity, infamy, and excommunication. You may say: usurious money must be returned to those who received it on interest, money which the pontiff cannot remit; for simoniacal restitution must be made to the church, which the church can remit if it wishes. Against this: one church cannot remit money belonging to another church. The response given has no force. After this, you may say that usury is prohibited by divine law, whereas simony in respect of a benefice is prohibited by human law alone — but this runs counter to what was said in the second question of this distinction. Johannes Andreas gives some trivial arguments, saying that a man is more strictly bound in respect of a loan than of a benefice; that in simony the mind of both parties can be corrupted, and therefore the condition of the possessor is stronger; whereas in a loan only the lender’s mind is corrupted. The first argument is false: for who is bound to give a benefice to another? In a loan, no such obligation holds, except in a case of necessity. It also happens that in simony only one party’s mind is corrupted, and in usury sometimes both parties’ minds are. Panormitanus, however, in c.fi. departs from his earlier opinion in c.Tua nos, setting out the member under discussion more fully: Sortes gives three hundred ducats to a prelate at the time a prebend is vacant and says nothing; the bishop takes these three hundred and, on account of them, gives the prebend without any pact. This is true simony, and I believe the bishop is bound to restore the money, and the other party acquires no right to the benefice. And it is similar if I were to deposit one thousand [ducats] so that the bishop would not give a vacant benefice to my brother, believing that by this means the avaricious bishop would give the benefice to my brother, and he in fact does so: then I say that the bishop is bound to restore this money, and my brother acquires no right to the benefice. And in both cases alike, when the effect of mental simony is the motivating cause of obtaining a benefice. There is another kind of mental simony which is not the cause of obtaining a benefice; a benefice or money acquired in such a case need not be restored — as if some suitable person gives a bishop a palfrey with the intention that he grant him a prebend, signifying nothing of this to the bishop: he is already a simoniac in that regard. A year or two later the bishop, remembering both the gift received and the man’s suitability, gives him a benefice for which he is suitable — perhaps the horse he rides on is what prompted the memory. Neither is the bishop bound to restore the grade to him, nor the cleric to restore the prebend, even though mental simony preceded.
