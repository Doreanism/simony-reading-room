Fourth, it follows that the Roman Pontiff can commit simony just as others can. This is clear: the entire definition can apply to him. And for this reason Petrus shrank from it, lest he accept money from Simon Magus. Fifth, it follows that there is a conditional simony — for example, “I would wish to buy a benefice if that man were willing to sell it” — and another that is absolute and categorical. Sixth, it follows that not every simoniac is bound to make restitution. This is clear: someone may be a simoniac who has obtained nothing by simony. Seventh, it follows that someone may be a simoniac in whom no act of simony is present. This is clear in the case of one who is sleeping or not performing any act, who has bought a benefice and has not yet sufficiently repented. For a habit alone does not suffice to designate someone a simoniac, as is evident when the habit remains after the act of simony has been purged. Eighth, it follows that this pestilence existed in the Church before it was called simony. This is clear from what has been said. Ninth, it follows that sometimes there is simony on the part of the seller only, or of the buyer only, and sometimes on the part of both. Tenth, it follows that someone may be simoniacally promoted and yet not be a simoniac. This is clear if the parents of Titius, acting in complete ignorance, buy a benefice for him: he acquires the benefice by a simoniacal title, and since he is in invincible ignorance, he is not a simoniac. Likewise, someone may be a simoniac and yet not simoniacally promoted — as when a bishop gives someone a just title to a benefice and the other mentally wished to buy the benefice, but the wish never issued in action. A third case: someone is both a simoniac and simoniacally promoted. That someone may be neither a simoniac nor simoniacally promoted falls outside any doubt.

## Now I shall raise a few objections against the definition.

For an understanding of the terms “spiritual” and “annexed to a spiritual thing,” the following argument is made. It is simoniacal to sell the sacraments, and yet they are purely corporeal, as we have customarily explained at the beginning of each sacrament’s treatment. Perhaps you say: they are annexed to spiritual things. Against this: so too are the fruits of benefices, books, and chalices, and yet these may be sold. The response is that one can take “annexed to a spiritual thing” in such a way that it includes spiritual things and vice versa, but that is not the intention of the definition. “Spiritual,” as a concrete term derived from “spirit,” in one sense does not apply to a body — as Christ said: “A spirit does not have flesh and bones,” Luke 24 [Luke 24:39]. Yet we call a devout man “spiritual,” and we say that sight is more spiritual than hearing, because words must be understood according to their subject matter. It is commonly said that something is spiritual in three ways: first, by essence, as grace and the virtues; second, by causality, as the sacraments, which are the cause of grace, of a character, or of an adornment in the soul; third, things are spiritual by effect. Against this last category the following argument is made: a meritorious act would be spiritual in the second sense, and so would a man who preaches or teaches; likewise acquired knowledge, which can be sold — not that it passes from one subject to another, but incidentally, in the sale of knowledge the knowledgeable person produces knowledge in another, and for this reason he may receive a temporal payment, as will be said below. I say that “spiritual” in the present context is that which is ordered to a work that is miraculously and supernaturally hoped for from the Holy Spirit. Examples of such things: the sacraments are ordered to the infusion of grace by the power of the work performed [*ex opere operato*]; to bestow virtue, to cure the sick miraculously, to speak in various tongues, to prophesy, and other things of this kind. For this reason Heliseus refused to accept anything for the miraculous cure of Naaman the Syrian [2 Kings 5].
