### Second Conclusion.

Simony is the worst monster in the Church. Proof: that plague is the worst in the Church through which unfit persons are promoted while the most qualified are passed over — just as it would be a contagious disease that drove people to take rotten stones for building a material temple while setting aside marble. But this happens most especially through simony, by which the wicked ascend to honors, being unable to rise through virtues, adopting the saying of the maddened Juno in Maro: *If I cannot bend the gods above, I shall move the underworld* [Aeneid VII.312]. For gifts placed before them bend the minds even of the prudent, according to the saying of Naso: *Gifts, believe me, win over both men and gods; Jupiter himself is appeased by his own coins.* Furthermore, that is the worst monster in the Church which renders a person filled with iniquity in both hands. But this happens through simony; therefore. The minor is proved by that saying of the Psalmist, Psalm 25 [26]: *In whose hands are iniquities, and their right hand is filled with bribes.* Moreover, that person is a wicked thief who steals the chalice, the books of the church, and things of that sort. But the simoniac steals the entire benefice. John 10: *He who does not enter by the door into the sheepfold of the sheep — he is a thief and a robber.* And in this theft his whole life is caught, for once one impropriety is granted at the beginning, many evils follow, as in *Physics* I. And it is difficult for things that began with a bad beginning to reach a good end, *Causa* I, *quaestio* i, chapter *Principatus*. Furthermore, the gravity of this plague is evident from God’s displeasure, for the fire of sacrifice that was given from heaven in the desert — namely, in the second year of the exodus from Egypt — and which endured until the Babylonian captivity, and which was then hidden by the prophet Jeremias in a deep well, where it remained intact for seventy years, and was recovered again in the time of Neemias, was subsequently extinguished when Antiochus sold the priesthood to Jason, as is evident from 2 Maccabees 1.

Against these conclusions it is argued as follows. Simony concerns the intellect; therefore it is not a sin. The consequence holds: every sin is within the free power of the one who commits it, and therefore concerns the will. The antecedent is evident because simony is heresy, *Causa* I, *quaestio* i, *Quisquis* and chapter *Presbyter*. Response: to assent to the proposition “the gift of the Holy Spirit can be sold for money” is heresy, and this is what Simon Magus believed. Hence Ambrosius, writing against Novatianus in the first book of *De Poenitentia*, says: “When Petrus saw that Simon, corrupted by the practice of the magical art, thought that the grace of Christ through the imposition of hands and the infusion of the Holy Spirit could be purchased with money, he said: *You have no lot or part in this faith, because your heart is not right before God* [Acts 8:20-21].” That is: you err in the Catholic faith, and thus one who pertinaciously asserts this is a heretic. Nevertheless, it is not necessary that any other simoniac now hold heresy, for it can happen that a prelate rightly assents in his intellect that simony is committed in the sale of a benefice and that it is wrongly done, yet notwithstanding this he acts, because the will frequently, by its own freedom, goes against the right judgment of the intellect. Furthermore, the consequence drawn there is invalid, because it would prove that no heretic sins — for an erroneous assent in matters of faith resides in the intellect, is free, and depends on the will, because a person can freely refuse to admit heresy, or having admitted it, can set it aside.
