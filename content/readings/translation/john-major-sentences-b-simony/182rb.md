Therefore the sacraments themselves, and performing those supernatural works, are spiritual things. What is annexed to a spiritual thing is the right that someone has to collect the revenues by reason of a divine office, and that which is needed precisely for maintaining such an office, as will be said at the end of the seventh question. Labor, however, although it is connected here, is something common that is necessary in any office, and therefore it is not annexed to a spiritual thing. Just as acquired knowledge is not spiritual in the present sense, so neither are books nor other necessaries annexed to a spiritual thing as we understand the term here. From this it also follows that not everything spiritual by essence is spiritual as we understand the term here. This is clear from the case of acquired knowledge.

### But some hold that labor expended on spiritual things is not saleable.

So hold Durandus and Paludanus, because to say otherwise would be a cover for simony. Nor is it plausible, they say, that so much money would be given for such little labor, as is evident in the case of a priest celebrating Mass who is offered six small white coins. But those arguments are not conclusive. The reason: a priest celebrating, or anyone performing another divine office, can say that he receives money for his sustenance, which is lawful according to everyone. And if such a pretext ceases, there is as much cover for selling spiritual things by accepting money for sustenance as for labor. Nor does the other argument conclude — that for the small labor a priest has in celebrating he receives six small white coins — because he receives them both for labor and for sustenance. For by celebrating he has completed the work of the entire day, since for vespers and the other hours nothing is customarily given him. Also, one person receives more for a small labor than another who labors much. This is clear from the advocate and the stone-carrier. And that it is lawful to receive payment for labor is clear. In Numbers 18 it is written: “To the sons of Levi I have given all the tithes in Israel for their ministry by which they serve me in the tabernacle of the covenant” [Numbers 18:21]. And shortly afterward: “You shall eat them, you and your households, for it is your payment for your ministry in the tabernacle of the testimony” [Numbers 18:31]. This is held by the gloss on c. *Significatum*, *de praebendis* [Extra, de praebendis]: that it is lawful for a priest to hire out his services for a fixed period and receive payment for his labor, provided he is not already bound to this by reason of a benefice — in conformity with the divine law that renders to each his reward according to his labor [1 Corinthians 3:8]. This is the view of Bonaventura here. Furthermore, there is another matter that weighs heavily: the determination of our Faculty, thirty-four years ago, against Brother Joannes Angeli, who in his second article preached as follows: “A parish curate ought to receive nothing from his parishioners for confession and the administration of the sacraments.” The Faculty defined thus: “This proposition is contrary to the disposition of natural and divine law expressly, and is therefore false and notoriously heretical.” For the priest does not receive payment for the sacraments but for their administration, which involves action and labor.

### But whether one may accept money for teaching, counsel, or testimony is a debatable matter.

Altisiodorus, in the third part, in the penultimate question on simony, says it is simoniacal to accept anything for teaching. Others accept payment for labor, just as a hired priest accepts payment for labor — a position that Alexander Halensis follows in the second part. Another view, being both more common and more probable, holds that spiritual acts proceeding from a created spirit can be sold.
