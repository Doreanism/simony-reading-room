We decree furthermore that those who give and those who receive shall incur the sentence of excommunication by that very fact, even if they hold the honor of the pontifical or cardinalate dignity. In reality, however, one who has been simoniacally promoted does acquire orders, but does not acquire the right to exercise those orders — that is, he cannot perform those things that pertain to the order without the penalty of mortal sin.

### Against this conclusion it is argued.
It is possible for the Roman Pontiff to be simoniacally elected by the cardinals, and yet he acquires a true right to the supreme pontificate. Therefore the conclusion is false. There appears to be no doubt except regarding the minor premise, and so the objection is answered: because if the opposite were granted, it would frequently be uncertain who is the head of the Church, which is unacceptable. The reply is to concede both premises, for he alone is excepted. And this is evident concerning elections in the chapter *Licet* [*Licet de vitanda*, X 1.6.6], where it is written: “Although concerning the avoidance of discord in the election of the Roman Pontiff, sufficiently clear provisions had previously issued from us, nevertheless, because the Church has often thereafter suffered a grave schism from the audacity of wicked ambition, we too have decreed that something should be added to avoid this evil, with the counsel of our brothers and the approval of the sacred council. We therefore decree that if, perchance, the enemy sowing tares, there cannot be full concord among the cardinals regarding the appointment of a supreme pontiff, and two parts being in agreement, the third part should refuse to concur or should presume to nominate another for itself, that person shall without any exception be held by the universal Church as Roman Pontiff who has been elected and received by the two parts.” Note that it says here “without any exception” — this is of positive human law. The Gloss excepts heresy. But I say that exception is void. For a pope does not fall from the papal dignity on account of heresy, nor is it inconsistent that a heretic be elected, because then there would be uncertainty in the pontificate. Therefore, if he were simoniacal or a homicide, after the election by two thirds he will be pontiff. Panormitanus, in the aforesaid chapter, infers that a layman elected by two parts will be pontiff. His proof is from Petrus, who was the first Pope and a layman. But the proof is invalid. For although Christ promised him the pontificate before the Passion, as is evident from Matthew 16 — and by extension, [C.24 q.1] *Quodcunque* — nevertheless he was not supreme pontiff while Christ was living. Rather, that power was conferred upon him after the Resurrection, as we said in the preceding distinction; and yet he had been made a priest at the Last Supper. Therefore Panormitanus assumes a falsehood in his proof. I think the conclusion is true, however. I do not believe it is required by divine law that a person be a cleric in order to hold the papal dignity. You see that just as the Pope has a right separated from other pontiffs, so he has a different mode of election. For other bishops are elected by the greater part of the chapter, and the agreement of two thirds is not required — a plurality suffices, as is evident. From this, in the said chapter *Licet*: other bishops are confirmed and consecrated, which is not required in his case. He is not called “lord elect” in the manner of others, but “Roman Pontiff.” But one who gives money to be elected sins gravely: it is a mark of great ambition and gives the worst example to other prelates. We read that some who doubted their election renounced the papacy, as did Leo IV and Honorius II.
