They seek only the reward of praise from their generosity itself. Assuredly, those who receive freely do not give freely in return, because from the sacred office they have performed they seek the reward of favor. Whence, when he was describing the virtuous prophet, he aptly said: *Blessed is he who shakes his hands from every gift* [Isaiah 33:15]. He did not say merely ‘from a gift,’ but ‘from every gift,’ because one kind is the gift from service, another from the hand, another from the tongue.” So says Gregory.

### I hold, therefore, that a gift of blood — that is, carnal kinship alluring a man to confer a benefice corruptly — does cause simony;

and not blood alone, but fear instilled by a tyrant, and all other things of that kind, whatever they may be. Nor should one be concerned with the threefold distinction [of gifts], for there are many other categories. Perhaps you will say: when a collator confers a benefice on a kinsman or in-law or on another person, some man or woman speaks on behalf of the kinsman, and so it should be considered a gift of the tongue [munus a lingua]. This does not help the adversaries who speak of blood as something separate from the other three categories. And moreover, it remains the case that a collator may give a benefice with no one speaking on the candidate’s behalf, solely out of regard for kinship or affinity. Therefore.

But against this position it is argued: In the preceding distinction I said that an unsuitable candidate who subsequently attains suitability is not bound to restore the benefice conferred on him, and that anyone who holds a benefice obtained simoniacally is bound to resign it. Furthermore, according to what has been said, anyone who sins in conferring a benefice would be committing simony, and so a collator who confers a benefice on a suitable candidate while passing over a more suitable one would commit simony — which is absurd.

To the first objection, I grant the major premise. The minor premise is false. Indeed, in the case of the third conclusion, a benefice is conferred on a suitable candidate simoniacally, and yet that suitable candidate is not bound to restore the benefice, since he is capable of holding it. And the collator does validly confer, even though he acts from a bad motive: this does not vitiate the conferral. Just as if I were to give ten coins generously to a brave man while adding a circumstance — venial or mortal — falling under the formal aspect of the object, the gift holds, and the other party acquires ownership of the gift. We discussed this in the preceding distinction in the case of the hypocrite and the poor man. The argument of the Lord of Armagh, in the tenth book of his work *Ad Armenios*, is not valid when he says that one who enters a benefice through sin does not acquire true title to it, since he does not enter through the door but by another way, and therefore is a thief and a robber (John 10[:1]). “But far be it from a Christian to say” (he writes) “that one who enters through sin enters through the door.” This argument, I say, is not valid, because in many ways a person acquires true title to a thing through sin. This is evident in the case of someone who sells a horse on a prohibited feast day: the seller acquires ownership of the money, and the buyer acquires ownership of the horse — even if the other party is excommunicated and is a cleric for whom such commerce is forbidden, so that he sins on three counts. And yet the transfer of property takes place. Therefore it is not inconsistent that someone enters upon a benefice through sin, as through a door: the act by which he enters is a sin, and he enters through sin — but not by reason of the act being sinful, but by reason of the fact that all the essentials for the conferral of the benefice or for the transfer of property are present. The passage in John 10 is to be understood literally as referring to the Catholic faith, as the interlinear Gloss explains: he who holds right belief about Christ enters the sheepfold through the door; he who climbs in by another way — by holding false belief about Christ — is a thief and a robber.

### To the second objection, the assumption is denied,

because it is established that a man may sin with a sin that is irrelevant to the conferral of a benefice, and yet on that account the conferral will not be simoniacal. But if he sins in the act of conferring itself, I grant that he commits simony. And it is granted that one who confers a benefice on a suitable candidate while passing over a more suitable one commits simony — but the recipient is not bound to make restitution, as we said in the preceding question. When we speak of the penalties for simoniacs, this will become further apparent.

### Secondly, an argument is raised concerning the gift from the hand.

Perhaps one may licitly give money to a prelate so that he will intercede on one’s behalf with the bishop for obtaining a benefice.
