## Question Three of Distinction Twenty-Five.

The third question concerning the matter of simony is whether simony is committed through prayer and service. For the resolution of the question, certain conclusions are set forth, the first of which is: It is not simoniacal to offer prayers on behalf of a suitable candidate, whether for oneself or for another. That a person may petition for a benefice for himself we demonstrated in the preceding distinction. The conclusion is proved thus: every act of simony is a sin; to offer prayers on behalf of a suitable candidate is not a sin; therefore to offer prayers on behalf of a suitable candidate is not simony. The inference holds in Baroco [a valid syllogistic mood]. Proof of the minor premise: a person may licitly petition for himself; therefore, a fortiori, he may petition for another suitable candidate toward whom he is less personally involved. A second proof of the same point: I see that Sortes is highly suited for the duties of a parish cure and would make an excellent pastor for the flock, yet he is in need. Who would say that I act wrongly in asking the collator to confer a benefice upon him?

### Second conclusion.

A bishop who confers a benefice on a person who is suitable — even though prayers are the cause without which he would not have done so — acts rightly. For it may happen that the person offering the prayers is one whose testimony regarding the worthiness of the candidate deserves to be trusted.

### Third conclusion.

Although by a fiction of law a bishop who confers a benefice on a suitable candidate principally on account of prayers, without attending to suitability, does not become simoniacal, nevertheless before God and in truth he is simoniacal. The first part is evident: there is a presumption that he gives the benefice on the basis of suitability. And an ambiguous case, concerning good and evil, is to be interpreted in the more favorable sense, as the rule provides.

### The second part is evident.

For if the candidate were unsuitable, the bishop would confer the benefice on him no less readily. Let us suppose the case of a person who has no concern for suitability: such a man is moved principally by the gift of words [i.e., flattery]. The same part is further evident: one who wishes to kill Titius and strikes a statue that he believes to be Titius is imputably a homicide. The reason is that his intent tends toward the destruction of Titius, with no concern for the statue. In the same way it is in our case.

### Furthermore, the same point is argued.

If the suitability of the one for whom prayers are offered were unknown to the collator, it would be simony; but the collator gives the benefice in consideration of the prayers, whether the candidate is suitable or not, and thus commits simony. For his knowledge of suitability does not in this case move him, and he proceeds just as if the suitability were hidden from the collator. Therefore, if suitability in no way moves him to confer the benefice, it is simony; and even if suitability moves him, but only as a less principal motive, it is simony — though less grave. For a wicked man can pervert the order of ends. The same must be said of service.

### Fourth conclusion.

One who gives a benefice to an unsuitable candidate on account of prayers commits simony. This is clear: he gives precisely on account of prayers. For since there is no suitability in the man, he does not give on account of suitability. And this is no different from taking money from the candidate for the benefice, because prayers can be worth a great deal of money in the estimation of the collator — for instance, because the collator desires the friendship of the petitioner, or his praise, or the like. This is evident from the well-known distinction concerning the threefold gift: namely, the gift from prayer, from the hand, and from service [munus a prece, a manu, ab obsequio].

### Fifth conclusion.

A person reasonably serves the collator of a benefice, believing that the collator will recognize his good and faithful service and that his suitability will thereby become known, so that he will be promoted sooner than another; but the benefice will be conferred on him on the basis of suitability, without any pact. If, however, he serves principally in order to obtain a benefice, he acts unjustly. Nor does it matter what kind of service he renders, provided he is suitable and serves honorably with moral integrity.
