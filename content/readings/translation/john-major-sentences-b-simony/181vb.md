I demonstrate these assertions as follows. Every act of simony is a sin of commission; no will is a sin of commission, since it is the soul; therefore no will is simony. The inference holds in the second mood of the second figure. But you will say: “will” is taken in place of “volition.” Against this: here there is still simony — “I refuse to give a benefice unless I receive money” — and yet it is not a volition [i.e., a positive willing]. Second, “deliberate” is badly added, because by “deliberate” you mean either a repetition of acts or deliberation in the act. Not the first, because if the first act were not simony, neither would any subsequent one be. Not the second, because a semi-deliberate simoniacal movement [*motus secundo primus*], or an act not fully deliberate, is still simony. This is evident inductively in all cases of hatred, anger, and perjury — when indeliberate, they retain the character of the whole. Therefore. Against the position of Hostiensis, the argument runs as follows, proving that there can be simony without a subsequent act, and in this way: This act is a sin — “I will to buy a benefice with money” — and it pertains to no other vice than simony. Therefore. Moreover, the simony that originated from Simon Magus and bears his infamous name did not involve a subsequent act, as is clear from Acts 8. Furthermore, he who wills to commit adultery and does not carry it out in deed commits adultery just as if he had accomplished it, according to the Savior’s pronouncement: “Everyone who looks at a woman with lustful intent has already committed adultery with her in his heart” [Matthew 5:28]. The canons cited, however, are to be understood as referring to canonical penalties not imposed — such as suspension and the like. I therefore define simony differently, as follows: Simony is a volition, in form or in effect, of buying or selling a spiritual thing, or something annexed to a spiritual thing. I say that “buying” and “selling” extend to every non-gratuitous contract. I said “in effect” on account of such an act as this: “I refuse to give a benefice unless I shall have received money,” which in itself gives rise to a volition. All who buy or sell spiritual things are deemed simoniacs. And the same applies to things annexed to them. Hence the Master says in chapter three: “For whoever sells one of these things without which the other cannot be had, leaves neither unsold.” And this is clear from Pope Paschal, C.1 q.3 c. *Si quis obiecerit*: what is the right of one is shared with the other, C.3 q.6 *Hec quippe*, Digest, *De iureiurando*, *Eu qui*, *Si libertus*. Therefore from both reason and common usage it is clear that the definition is sound. And although Balaam wished to sell the gift of prophecy, as is clear from Numbers 22, and Petrus recounts in his second canonical letter, chapter 2: “They have hearts trained in greed, accursed children, forsaking the right way they have gone astray, following the way of Balaam son of Bosor, who loved the wages of unrighteousness, but was rebuked for his own transgression: a dumb donkey, speaking with a human voice, restrained the prophet’s madness” [2 Peter 2:14-16]. And in the Epistle of Jude it is written: “And they abandoned themselves to Balaam’s error for profit” [Jude 1:11]. And Gezi received payment along with leprosy [2 Kings 5]. Yet this pestilence is not called “Balaamia” or “Gezia” but simony. For names signify by convention, and a compelling reason need not be sought, although a fitting one can be given here for why it is called simony — namely, from Simon Magus, because he wished to buy a spiritual thing in order to sell it again, and this under the law of grace. As the poet Baptista Mantuanus says in the first book of his *Alphonsus*: “Simon flying through the air, that infamous magician and wretched author of profit.” From what has been said I draw several propositions. The first is: it amounts to the same thing to say “there is simony” and “a spiritual thing, or something annexed to a spiritual thing, is bought or sold.” Second, it follows that simony is a mental act and consequently largely hidden, although through external signs people sometimes arrive at a perfect or at least a dim knowledge of it. Third, it follows that there is simony without any pact, as is clear in the case between Petrus and Simon Magus.
