### Second conclusion.

Although the one who compels laypeople to give money before spiritual services have been rendered perhaps acts wrongly, nevertheless once those services have been rendered, he can by the authority of his superior compel laypeople subject to him to observe praiseworthy customs — that is, to pay according to the custom that has not been disapproved in that place. This conclusion is proved by the chapter *Ad apostolicam* [X 5.3.4], *De simonia*, where the pontiff writes thus: “It has come to our apostolic hearing by frequent report that clerics demand and extort money for the burial of the dead, the blessing of those marrying, and similar things; and if their cupidity is not satisfied, they fraudulently raise fictitious impediments. On the other hand, certain laypeople, from the leaven of heretical wickedness, endeavor to abolish praiseworthy customs introduced into holy Church by the pious devotion of the faithful, under the pretext of canonical piety. Wherefore we prohibit wrongful exactions from being made in these matters, and we command that pious customs be observed, decreeing that the sacraments of the Church be freely conferred, and that those who maliciously endeavor to change a praiseworthy custom be restrained by the bishop of the place once the truth has been ascertained.” Therefore the pontiff means to say that sacraments and benefices must be given freely, without a prior pact or one made at the time of conferral; but nevertheless those who receive them are to be compelled to observe praiseworthy custom.

### Third conclusion:

This is commonly held by Hostiensis, Panormitanus, and the canonists generally: that no custom is valid by which clerics give anything temporal for spiritual things received or to be received from other clerics. This is clear by argument from the contrary sense in the chapter just cited, in which special mention is made of laypeople. But you will object: an argument from the contrary sense does not hold when an absurdity follows from the opposite, as is the case here — for it is scarcely possible to give a reason why laypeople should be compelled to observe praiseworthy customs and clerics should not. In support of the conclusion: by the chapter *Jacobus* [X 5.3.30], *De simonia*, and the first chapter of the same title, and C.1 q.3, chapter *Si quis episcopus*. Hostiensis, commenting on the chapter *Jacobus*, assigns the distinction between the third and second conclusions — explaining why custom excuses laypeople but not clerics — namely: it serves the public good when clerics receive from laypeople, but it serves only private interest when clerics give to clerics. The Franciscans are exceedingly hostile to clerics [in this regard]. Likewise, one should not be concerned that the Church possesses what does not arise from it, since goods remain with the laypeople. This distinction is of no weight. The force of the distinction rests on this: that clerics can and ought to receive everything from laypeople because laypeople are hostile to clerics. But in the same way, many clerics are hostile to laypeople and live slothfully on the goods conceded by laypeople. And if one should not be concerned about what the Church possesses, then any church should concede its revenues to another — which is false. There is a certain canonist who wrote a booklet on this subject, which I have seen in the past, though I do not recall the name; he excuses everyone indiscriminately. For he goes so far abroad that he is content to lick with an ulcerated finger [i.e., to gloss over festering wounds], while he wishes to excuse indiscriminately all these monstrous plagues of prelates regarding exactions. For this reason a different distinction between clerics and laypeople must be given: namely, that prelates who are collators are bound by their office to administer freely those things that are incumbent upon them, because they have stipends from the revenues of the Church for this purpose. And therefore they commit simony by exacting anything further from ecclesiastics, and they are bound to restitution. And Saint Thomas (Summa theologiae II-II, q.100, a.3) says that the one to whom spiritual power is committed is bound by his office to exercise the power committed to him in the dispensation of spiritual things; and he also has stipends established for his sustenance from ecclesiastical revenues. And therefore, if he should accept anything for the exercise of spiritual power, he would not be understood as hiring out his labors — which he is bound to expend by reason of the office he has undertaken — but rather as selling the very exercise of spiritual grace.
