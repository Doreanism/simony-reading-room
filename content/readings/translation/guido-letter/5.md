# THE LETTER OF GUIDO TO THE ARCHBISHOP OF MILAN.

Whoever fails to rescue his brother from death, when he has the power to do so, incurs the guilt of fraternal homicide. We likewise, who have heard of the grave peril threatening your Excellency, incur the punishment of a most serious offense if we remain silent — for the Lord thunders terribly through the prophet that he who conceals the wickedness of the impious by his silence does in truth shed blood. For we have heard — and are greatly astonished — that sacred orders are being sold among you for money, since all who have attempted any such thing are proven utterly heretical, the Holy Spirit declaring through Gregory that whoever is ordained for money is promoted to the end that he may become a heretic. And to show that the cause of this little letter of ours is not presumption but the fear of God, the same blessed Gregory continues: Whoever does not burn with fierce zeal against the heresy of simony and of neophytes, in the place proper to his office, let him not doubt that he will share the portion of the one who first committed this heinous sin. In these words, since the heretic of the simoniac and neophyte heresy is described as one who must be condemned to eternal death, this is most plainly declared — the divine oracle attesting through Augustine and saying: Hold most firmly and doubt not at all that every heretic and schismatic, however many alms he may give or even if he shed his blood for Christ, is to be handed over to the burning of the eternal fire with the devil and his angels, unless before the end of this life he has been incorporated into and restored to the Catholic Church. Hence it is that the Lord drove out those who sold and bought in the temple; that the earth swallowed up Dathan and Abiron \[see Numbers 16\] as they sought the highest priesthood; that Peter condemned Simon for attempting the same; and that the rock of ecclesiastical censure has condemned all simoniacs with perpetual anathema. And it is deeply shameful that the Church, now confirmed in the fullest vigor, should succumb to so ruinous an enemy — an enemy over whom, in the very beginning of her infancy, she triumphed with such great power.
