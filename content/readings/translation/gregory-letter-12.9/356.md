# GREGORIUS TO VICTOR, BISHOP.

As greatly as it brings us joy to know that our brothers are properly solicitous toward their sons with fatherly love, so greatly do we count it grief when neither the reverence due to fellow brothers nor regard for the priesthood is sufficient to restrain them from unlawful conduct. How grave and harsh is the complaint of his clerics and of those placed in holy orders against our brother Paulinus, bishop of the city of Tegesis, there is no doubt that it is clear to your fraternity, since what has reached us at so great a distance could not have been hidden from you in your proximity. And since it must be carefully guarded against that what they complain he has wrongfully done to them by bodily injury be either lawfully presumed or made worse by dissembling, manifest transgressions must always be restrained by canonical moderation, so that one action may serve both as correction of past offenses and as a rule for the future. Therefore, together with our most beloved common brother Columbo the bishop and with other priests whom you shall deem fitting, it is proper that you examine the case between the aforementioned brother and his clerics with careful investigation. And if the complaint of the petitioners is grounded in truth, correct the matter with such regular amendment that he both recognizes how serious is what he has done and learns henceforth not to overstep the bounds of his office. And do not suffer the order of your rank to be, as they say, set aside by him, lest his contempt for you bring peril to him and guilt to you. For whatever is committed by a lesser party reflects upon the person placed in the higher position, unless it is diligently brought to account.

As for the fact that this same brother Paulinus is reported to have conferred ecclesiastical orders for payment, this must be inquired into by you with all care and strictness. And if — which we do not desire — it shall have become clear that this is so, let your zeal be so inflamed to punish it that both the avarice of the one who ordained may be turned to his punishment, and the unlawful ordination, stripped of its effect, may not obtain the reward of his ambition.
