18   The fifth conclusion concerns the consecration of a bishop, which must always take place on a Sunday. Minor orders conferred outside the time of major orders, however, ought to be conferred on few \[candidates\] at a time — say two or three — and not on many together, as is done on the Ember Saturdays, as is indicated in *extra de co.* The dedication of a church, moreover, can take place on any day, as is clear from custom.   19   As to the fourth point, namely concerning the time for those to be given benefices, there are three conclusions. The first concerns a simple benefice: if the benefice is simple, it suffices that \[the recipient\] be older than seven years, for a ward can be a canon even in a cathedral church, though he may not have a voice in the election \[*de elec.* c. *Ex eo*, lib. 6\]. Nevertheless, by human law such a conferral is not rendered void; yet by divine law it is illicit to give such benefices to such persons, unless no others can be found, because it is contrary to distributive justice if \[a benefice\] is given to one less worthy. Although this boy may not be positively unworthy on account of deficiency of life — since he is innocent — he is nonetheless unworthy in respect of one who has the life and learning that can immediately benefit the church. And therefore this person is unworthy because another is more worthy; hence, when a better, holier, and more just man is available, to give to another is a grave sin, as Augustinus says on James

2.   20   The second conclusion concerns a dignity not having the cure of souls, for which a bishop can dispense in favor of one who has completed his twentieth year \[*de aeta. & quali.* c. 1 lib. 6\]. And thus even an archdeaconate can be given to such a person, since it does not have the cure of souls in the penitential forum — which would require one to be a priest, requiring twenty-five years — but only in the external forum, and \[the holder\] need be a deacon, for which twenty years suffice, so that he is something less than a priest. But although \[such a person\] would otherwise, being under twenty-five, not be fit to be a judge, he can nonetheless be an archdeacon, which by custom amounts to being a judge. Hence it must be said that this is not \[permitted\] by common law but by dispensation of the bishop permitted by the Pope.   21   The third conclusion concerns the cure of souls, for which a man must be twenty-five years of age, but for the episcopate thirty, as has been said. In the first case the bishop cannot dispense; rather, the conferral of a parish upon one who has not reached twenty-five is null by operation of law — and likewise a commendation of a church made to one not of that age \[*de elec.* c. *Licet canon.* §*decernentes*; c. *fe.*\]. The Pope, although he can dispense, does not intend to do so unless he expressly states it; hence one who has obtained a benefice with cure, or without cure, cannot be provided with a benefice with cure unless he was twenty-five at the time of the rescript \[*de rescript.* c. *Si co tempore*\]. It happened that when certain persons had held a benefice with cure for two years by virtue of such a rescript, it was alleged against them that they had not been of the lawful age; and with witnesses produced on both sides, the judge pronounced that he had been of lawful age. My response: in case of doubt, the sentence must stand; and since it is not certain from the contrary, one should presume that the witnesses did not perjure themselves out of affection for him, nor that the judge judged wrongly. But if conscience cannot set aside the fact that \[the holder\] was a minor at the time of the rescript, he is bound to resign, because conscience dictates that no right was acquired for him.   22   As to the fifth point, namely concerning the time within which a curate must be ordained, there are three conclusions. The first: that by modern law a bishop can dispense with curates such that one devoting himself to literary study need not be promoted within the year except to the subdiaconate; but after seven years he must within one year be promoted to the priesthood — so that in the first year he shall have been ordained subdeacon, and within the eighth a priest. If the bishop so dispenses, the church falls vacant by operation of law \[if these conditions are not met\]; but likewise, unless the bishop had dispensed with him, or if he was dispensed yet was not devoting himself to literary study — in which case the bishop has no power to dispense — he must be ordained in full within the year; otherwise he is deprived by operation of law \[*de elec. Cum ex eo* lib. 6\].   23   The second conclusion: although time may pass only with respect to that period and that year in which \[the curate\] cannot become subdeacon or priest, nevertheless the benefice does not fall vacant until the year has elapsed, and he may make \[the revenues\] his own if from the beginning he had the intention
