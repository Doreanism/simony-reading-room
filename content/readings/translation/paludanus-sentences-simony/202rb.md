## On the Exchange of Benefices

The example of the exchange of benefices is deficient \[as a counterargument\], because even if it were never prohibited by law, it still ought not and cannot be done without the authority of a superior. For granted that anyone may resign his own benefice, still one person cannot institute another. And even if it is done with the authority of a superior, if it is done by means of a pact, it is simony — and not merely simony with respect to penalty but also with respect to fault. For this is to exchange a spiritual thing for a spiritual thing, and a temporal thing for a spiritual thing, since canonical right is spiritual. And it is remarkable how an exchange is considered intrinsically less simoniacal than a sale: for the right of electing a bishop and of officiating in a church — which one does not have through conferral — one does obtain through exchange, since in an exchange there is a true conferral, and more, because in an exchange there is a double right, whereas in a conferral there is only one.   16   As to the third \[article\], first the opinion of the Jurists is set forth with its supporting arguments; second, a probable objection \[*impugnatio*\]

## Opinion of the Jurists (as above), according to which the Pope may sell a simple canonry

the first: the Jurists, on the basis of their distinction, hold that the Pope would commit the sin of simony in all matters that are intrinsically simoniacal, but not in the others. To sell a simple canonry is not simoniacal except because it is prohibited, as the same gloss states; hence one who is not bound by any prohibition of positive law does not commit the vice of simony in this case, even with respect to fault. And this position is proved

## Argument \[in support\]

by four arguments, the last of which is stated in the form of an objection.   17   The first argument is as follows: what is not intrinsically simoniacal but only by prohibition of law does not bind the Pope, nor those inferiors with whom the Pope dispenses. But to confer a simple canonry — one to which no cure of souls is attached, nor any sacred order — is not intrinsically simoniacal but only by legal prohibition. Therefore, etc. The major premise is clear, because the statutes of the canons do not bind the Pope, nor one with whom the Pope dispenses, just as the laws do not bind the Emperor. This is proved at ff. de legibus, l. Princeps \[Digest 1.3.31\]; and 9 q. 3 c. Cuncta \[Gratian, Decretum\]. The minor is proved: first, by Hostiensis, who states this explicitly, extra de simonia \[X.5.3\], on the last chapter. It is also proved by reason, because nothing appears to be intrinsically simoniacal except the buying or selling of sacred things, as is clear from Acts 8, from which the root of simony seems to derive. For Simon, from whom simony takes its name, wished to purchase with money the power of conferring the Holy Spirit, which he saw visibly given in the sacred rite of confirmation through the laying on of the apostles’ hands. But in a simple canonry or benefice without cure of souls, and without a sacrament, and without a sacred order, there is no obligation to receive or administer a sacrament. Therefore to sell or buy such a benefice is not intrinsically simoniacal.   18

## Second Argument

The second argument is: when spiritual and temporal things are joined together, it is lawful for a spiritual thing to pass by means of the sale of the temporal, provided the price is not increased on that account — as is clear from the law concerning the right of patronage, which passes with a sold estate, as held in extra de iure patronatus, c. 9, In oculum \[X.3.38.9\]. But in a simple canonry, spiritual and temporal things are joined — namely the office with the prebend. Therefore, since the prebend can be sold, it seems that the canonry may lawfully pass along with it, provided one does not sell the prebend at a higher price on account of the canonry.   19

## Third Argument

The third argument is: things antecedent to sacred things are no less spiritual than things consequent upon them. But things antecedent to sacred things — such as sacred vessels — can lawfully be sold, provided one does not sell them at a higher price on account of their consecration, as will be shown. Therefore any ecclesiastical benefice whatsoever, since it is consequent upon or destined to become consequent upon something sacred, can lawfully be sold, provided one does not sell it at a higher price on account of its spiritual character.
