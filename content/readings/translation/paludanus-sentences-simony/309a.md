But one who has renounced both see and dignity should not ordain to major orders without the permission of the Pope; but minor orders he can confer with the permission of the bishop \[Extra, de ordinatis ab eo qui renunciavit episcopatui, c. 1\]. 9 But against that distinction the new law concerning temporal order seems to be opposed \[Sexti, de temporalibus ordinationibus, c. 1, lib. 6\], where it is stated that, without distinction, one who has been ordained by a foreign bishop without the permission of his own bishop is permanently suspended. But one who has resigned only his see, or both see and dignity, or has been deposed from one or both, has no subject at all. Therefore, it seems that to whomever he confers a minor order without the request of the proper bishop, he confers not the exercise \[executio\] but nevertheless the character, just as is the case with one who is degraded — for since the sacerdotal and episcopal character are one and the same, just as the former is indelible, on account of which a degraded priest can consecrate, so too the latter is indelible, on account of which a degraded and excluded bishop can ordain a true priest and a true bishop. But it is straightforward to harmonize the laws with one another: in one way, though the new law speaks generally, it nonetheless speaks of major orders. 10 But against this is the fact that even under the new law one may not give the sacraments except to a subject, and still less any order. 11 Or it can be said that the new law speaks of a bishop who is simply foreign \[to the ordinand\]; but such a one is not simply foreign to those who were formerly his subjects. 12 But neither does this argument hold: for a bishop translated to another see would err no less if he ordained those formerly subject to him than if he ordained total strangers. Hence it seems better that the earlier rule \[the concession for resignation cases\] is corrected; and similarly as to another case: one who has resigned both \[see and dignity\] should not celebrate in pontifical rites, and therefore should not confer even minor orders at anyone’s request, since orders are not said to be conferred otherwise. 13 To the first argument it must be said that a bishop, however wicked, heretical, excluded, or degraded, is joined and connected in the administration of the sacraments to Christ as the principal agent by reason of the ministerial power which he received at consecration, and by reason of the faith of the Church — provided he intends to do what the Church does — although he does not believe this to be efficacious. 14 To the second argument it must be said that there is no parallel between ordination and absolution from sins: because, as some say, in absolution there is no effect other than the remission of sins, which takes place through grace; and since one who knowingly submits himself to a heretic sins and is not capable of grace, he therefore cannot be absolved by him. In ordination, however, the primary effect is the power of order, and the secondary effect is grace. One knowingly ordained by a heretic or an excluded person therefore does not receive grace on account of the said cause, but he does receive the character or power of order. But what they say about one who knowingly submits himself to a heretic is true, and the same holds even for one who does so in ignorance, from the moment the heretic has been excluded from the Church — indeed from the very fact that he is a heretic by some outward act — since in that case he is excommunicated and consequently lacks the exercise of jurisdiction which is required for absolution, if he is publicly excommunicated. Hence just as a non-priest cannot absolve for lack of order, so neither can an excommunicate, for lack of the jurisdiction by which his material subject — namely, a penitent — is made subject to him. For the Church can take away voluntary jurisdiction, because in a man’s subjecting himself to someone, the authority of a superior is always implicitly excepted, against whose prerogative no one can newly submit himself to another. Since therefore Christ from the beginning subjected all to Peter, saying: “To you I will give the keys,” and “Upon this rock,” and “Feed my sheep,” etc. — against the prohibition of Peter no one can submit himself to another. Since therefore every person submits to another either voluntarily or by the Church’s ordering, the Church can remove either form of submission.
