Therefore, since a judge is bound by his office to render judgment, and a subject is bound — insofar as he is a subject — to give testimony in court, a judge cannot without injustice sell his judgment, nor a witness his testimony. A witness may, however, seek reimbursement for the expenses he incurred in traveling to the place of judgment and returning. From this it follows that, since the seal of a judge is nothing other than an attestation of his judgment, it is not apparent how a judge could charge anything for his seal, except perhaps the costs of the wax, the sealer, and the clerks assigned to that task. When, on the other hand, a person is not bound by office to render such services, it is not unlawful to sell them. Hence a legal expert may lawfully sell his advice, and an advocate his representation. So much for the first point. 15 As for the second point — namely, the inquiry into these matters — the following observations seem relevant. First, it is said that spiritual things by essence can be neither sold nor bought, whether in law or in fact. This is true with respect to the efficacy of transferring and acquiring them, since it is not within human power to transfer or bestow them; it belongs to God to give them and to transfer them miraculously — for God could transfer an accident from one subject to another. But there is an objection concerning the sacrament of the altar, which a Christian sold to a Jew and actually delivered, where a spiritual thing by essence was truly present; and yet this happened incidentally by reason of sensible corporeal matter, and there is no doubt that it was simony. Nevertheless, regarding spiritual things properly so called, it seems that they can in fact be bought and sold insofar as simony is contracted thereby — for Simon Magus sought by simony to purchase that freely given grace by which the apostles through the laying on of hands conferred the Holy Spirit, which grace was the episcopal character and the grace of miracles, whereby the Spirit visibly descended. Similarly, if someone believes that a heretic by giving his “consolation” \[the Cathar ritual blessing\] infuses the Spirit, and purchases this very thing with that erroneous intention, it is simony. Concerning things spiritual by cause — such as the sacraments — it seems that they are also spiritual by essence as regards the Eucharist, and likewise as regards every sacrament in which a spiritual power is present by essence, for the purpose of causing a character or an adornment \[of the soul\]. Hence the second argument touched upon here should be completed as follows: a sale by a steward against the will of the lord is unlawful; but if it is with the will of the lord, it is just as lawful when done by the steward as when done by the lord himself. Concerning judgment and testimony, what is said is true, but the argument is doubtful, because judging and testifying are sometimes free acts. For a judge is not always bound to judge in person — he may delegate, or may from the outset decline to accept the power. Likewise a witness is not always compelled to testify. Yet whether one judges or testifies voluntarily or under compulsion, he is not permitted to receive money for this act, though he may properly claim expenses if he is not an ordinary officer, since he is not required to serve at his own expense — and similarly for the salary of an assessor. There is, however, a prescribed form for this in *de rescripto*, *Statutum*, book 6. As for accepting the role of arbitrator, there seems to be no prohibition. As for the seal, however, the case seems truly to be different; but these matters are not merely unlawful to the degree they should be — they are also simoniacal, if they occur in a spiritual cause and in ecclesiastical jurisdiction, since they are accessories and annexed to things spiritual by a created spirit. But concerning counsel and advocacy, even in a spiritual cause — such as a matrimonial case, or one concerning prebends and tithes — no payment is to be demanded, because \[the advocate\] is not bound to render \[such service\] for free. Yet it also seems that even where he would be obligated, payment could not be demanded. For if a wealthy man were dying of hunger while possessing money, I would be obligated to assist him, but not for free — for it is not necessary to give what one is able to purchase, just as Tobias did not give \[the money\] but lent it to Gabael, because there was hope then that he could repay it \[Tob. 4–5\]. Similarly, a physician is bound by his office to aid a dying man with medicine when it is necessary for life, just as another person is bound \[to offer\] bread that he possesses — but not without charge if the patient is wealthy. And so if someone has no advocate, and for want of an advocate stands to lose the case involving his entire inheritance, the advocate is bound to provide representation and the legal counselor to provide advice — though not for free.
