## Palliation

since this distinction is established in law, and lest it seem to be altogether rejected, the statement of the jurists can be interpreted as follows: that what is not simoniacal by its nature is simoniacal by prohibition — not with respect to fault, because there the sin of simony cannot be present but only that of disobedience, as was said — but with respect to penalty, because one who acts against such a legal prohibition is punished with the same penalty as if he were a true simoniac. It sometimes happens that sins are reckoned under the same name when they are punished with the same penalty. And in this sense something is called simoniacal because it is prohibited — as, for example, the exchange of benefices without the authority of a superior is not simoniacal by its nature. For one could licitly exchange one’s canonry for another’s canonry, if one did so with the intention of serving the Church better in the other church than in the one one holds — were it not for the contrary legal prohibition, on account of which this is judged simoniacal unless it is done with the authority of a superior, whose office it is to judge that no one passes from one church to another out of desire for greater gain rather than out of genuine zeal to advance \[the Church’s good\].   11   As for the third point, by way of inquiry:

## The Doctor Examines Durandus

it could be said, in reply to the first argument against the jurists’ distinction,

## Reply to Durandus’s First Argument

that a prohibition can well constitute a sin other than disobedience: because in every virtue the mean is fixed by reason — and not only from what bears on it directly, but also from what bears on it indirectly. Hence when a man eats forbidden food, there is not only the sin of disobedience but also the sin of gluttony — because, even though he did not exceed the bounds of gluttony as such, he nonetheless exceeded them indirectly. For right reason dictates that one abstain from food not only as much as bodily health and good condition directly requires, but also as much as indirect considerations require. For if someone were to fornicate out of fear of death, whereas he would not otherwise have done so out of love of pleasure, he would sin not only with the sin of timidity opposed to fortitude, but also with the sin of intemperance. And similarly in the present case: one would be very gluttonous indeed who wished to eat where, by eating, he would break a fast

## Reply to the Second Argument

established by precept.   12   Against the second argument it seems that, although a legal prohibition does not change natures in reality, it does change them according to reason: for some things are called spiritual solely by legal or human ordinance — as all things that are called sacred by reason of ecclesiastical consecration, such as chalices and the like. Whatever, however, has no holiness or spirituality except by ecclesiastical statute can be rendered non-holy and non-spiritual by the Pope, and thus can be bought or sold without simony, once that spiritual character has been removed.

## Reply to the Third Argument

13   To the third \[argument\] it must be said: if a man eats twice on a fast day, he sins not only with the sin of disobedience — even though at other times he would eat soberly — but also with the sin of intemperance or gluttony: because sobriety attends to the mean not only with respect to those circumstances that are always present, but also with respect to those that arise in the particular moment when one is to act.   14

## Reply to the Fourth Argument

To the fourth, concerning the trivial lie: it can be said that once a precept is added, the sin is not only one of disobedience but also a sin of the tongue. For sometimes there is a double deformity in a sin, yet one deformity only exists when the other is presupposed — as taking something belonging to another in a church is a double sin, namely of theft and of sacrilege; yet if it were not theft, it would not be sacrilege, because taking one’s own property in a church is no sin at all. So too, where speaking \[in a given way\] would be no sin at all: if it is prohibited, it is not only a sin of disobedience but also a sin of the tongue — though it is not a sin of the tongue except on the supposition of disobedience.   15
