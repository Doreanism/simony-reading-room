But there is an objection: on that basis it would not be lawful for a teacher to sell the labor he expends in teaching, since he cannot sell the knowledge itself — the *opus operatum* — either in law or in fact. 16 Furthermore, just as the buyer is more concerned with purchasing the *opus operatum* than with the worker, so conversely a seller is more willing to sell his labor than anything else. 17 In addition, it seems to some that if in such matters a person sells only his labor, he ought to receive more or less according as he labors more or less — not according to the size of the case or the means of the client. For a thing ought not to be sold for more than it is worth on account of the condition of the buyer or the end for which it is ordered; just as a hammer is not worth more because it is bought for working gold than if it were bought for working bronze or iron. Advocates, therefore, ought not to receive \[payment\] when they do not labor — unless they reckon an old labor \[as still owed\], just as a merchant selling goods considers not only what they originally cost him, but what they cost to transport, etc. But even this does not excuse them, since a seller ought not to sell a thing for more than it is worth in itself, however much more it may have cost him at the outset or thereafter. 18 As for the third point, by way of summary, there are three conclusions. The first is that sacred things ought not to be sold, as has been well demonstrated — and this is true even of things that are spiritual only by cause. Nor does it matter that the argument there proves all sacraments to be spiritual by essence, because even if that power is spiritual in its efficacy, it is nevertheless corporeal by essence, since it subsists in the body in such a way that it is not naturally suited to exist in a spirit. And this is true not only in general but also in particular for each sacrament. Baptism, together with all its sacramental rites, must be conferred without charge. And although in a case of necessity it may be lawful to sell a consecrated chalice, it is not in that same necessity lawful to purchase baptism; hence, given that a priest refuses to baptize a child unless he is paid, on no account should money be given to him. Indeed, in such a case it would be lawful for anyone to baptize the child, or to leave the child in the hands of the Lord, if it cannot otherwise be baptized except for money — because evil must not be done that good may come, as Thomas says here \[cf. Rom. 3:8\]. The same holds for confirmation, chrism, and the like. Nor \[may payment be demanded\] for the confirmation of an election — unless to redeem oneself from harassment; for just as civil law permits one to ransom one’s own blood, so canon law permits one to ransom oneself from vexation, which occurs when a person gives money not to acquire any right for himself but to prevent a right already acquired from being unjustly obstructed. Likewise not for the Eucharist. Voluntary offerings, however, may lawfully be retained, just as in other matters hired priests are excused, because in the sustenance of their livelihood they receive neither the price of the Mass nor of their labor. Similarly a prebend may have annexed to it the duty of celebrating \[Mass\], just as to any office belongs the duty of saying the canonical hours — for this pertains to the sustenance of the ministers. Likewise not for penance: hence priests who refuse to hear confession unless they first receive money are simoniacs. Nor should money be exacted for absolution in the external forum; contumacy, however, may be punished even by a monetary penalty according to the custom of the region — *extra de sent. exco.*, the chapter *Venerabilibus*, otherwise beginning *Caeterum*, §*Denique* — unless one is a legate’s delegate in France, who by doing this would be excommunicated, as in the constitutions of Simon *de offic. dele.*, the last chapter \[Simon of Paris, constitutions on legates\]. Likewise not for marriage, except that insofar as it is \[a natural office\], a dowry is given for the burdens of matrimony; for the blessing, however, nothing is to be demanded, though what is voluntarily offered may be received; and the judge’s office may be invoked for just customary \[dues\], not for the formal conduct of litigation — as is lawful in the case of tithes. Likewise not for holy orders, nor for the blessing of abbots, abbesses, and virgins. Likewise not for extreme unction. 19 The second conclusion is that simony has a place in spiritual acts deriving from the uncreated Spirit, in which nothing may be received even for labor, but only for the sustenance of life.
