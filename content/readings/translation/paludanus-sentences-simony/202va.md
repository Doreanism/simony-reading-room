## Third Argument

This is shown as follows: in a simple canonry there are two things — namely, the office to which someone is assigned, and the temporal benefice which imperfectly fulfills it; for the benefice is given on account of the office, for the sustenance of the minister. Now that benefice is nothing other than a certain temporal emolument given by the founders of churches and other devout persons for \[service at\] a place and office, for the sustenance of the minister who is to officiate at that place. But to institute a minister for such an office and such a place belongs to the prelate, whose function it is to judge the suitability of the minister. The fact that the one instituted receives the benefice, however, does not come from the gift of the prelate — who has no right over the benefice in this regard — but from the gift of the founder, who gave such a benefice to the minister who would serve in that place and in that office. This is sufficiently indicated in the Authentica, de sanctissimis episcopis et clericis, c. Si quis oratorii domum \[Nov. 131.13\], col. 9, where it is expressly stated that the founders of churches are obliged to provide expenses to the clergy, but that the clergy are to be instituted by the bishop, and once instituted they are bound to comply \[*obsecundare*\] — that is, to obey and serve their fellow clerics — and to fulfill the ecclesiastical ministry. From which the argument proceeds thus: to give a spiritual thing for a temporal thing is simoniacal and contrary to natural and divine law, as will be shown. But to sell a canonry is to give a spiritual thing for a temporal thing. Therefore it is intrinsically simoniacal and contrary to natural and divine law, and thus no one can be excused from sin. it can be called simoniacal because prohibited — that is, because it is not prohibited except insofar as it is constituted by legal institution, although once that institution is presupposed, it is prohibited by God. From this it appears: just as the Pope can lawfully sell a consecrated chalice — because one sells it with his permission — and if not on account of the consecration, at least on account of the material; so too a benefice can be sold by the Pope and with the Pope’s permission — and if not on account of the office, which is spiritual, nonetheless on account of the temporal benefice. For just as in a chalice there is the spiritual element \[i.e., its dedication to the altar\] arising from the institution of the consecrating church, and the temporal from the craftsman who made it; so also in a canonry there is the spiritual element — the right of officiating, electing, and the like — which derives from the institution of the church as so ordained; and the temporal — the temporal goods applied by the founders.
