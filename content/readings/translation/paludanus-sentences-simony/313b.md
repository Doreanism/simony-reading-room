Hence in the same chapter *Sacerdos*: a priest who stationed a fighter who slew the defendant with blows is irregular, just as if he had done it himself. But if it was done against his prohibition, he is not irregular. *De homicidio*, chapter 1, *Petrus diaconus*. Likewise, if the end is the only efficient cause — for instance, because a man was killed on account of avenging him, without his having commanded it or ratified it — he is not irregular; and one who does not know of it is called simoniac, that is, said to have been promoted through simony, even when the simony was contracted on his account without his knowledge. Now an efficient cause is also one who does not personally touch \[the victim\] but who accompanies him, ready to defend him if need be: just as Paul, who guarded the garments of those stoning Stephen — yet in his promotion Christ dispensed, who said, “He is a vessel of election to me,” and the Holy Spirit, who said, “Separate for me Paul and Barnabas.” Hence it is said in *De homicidio*, c. *Sicut dignum*, that those are clearly not immune from the guilt of homicide who, even though they do not kill, nonetheless give aid to killers. But if death does not follow, nor mutilation, whatever he may have done he is not irregular, because the law of promotion requires the accomplished act. Hence a judge, a notary, and all such persons, unless death or mutilation follows, whatever they may have said or written in a capital case, are not irregular — just as neither is one who walked about armed and wounded someone and did everything in his power toward death, if death did not in fact follow — although some write the contrary concerning a judge and another who does this ex officio; but it is not plausible that, all else being equal, an innocent person incurs greater irregularity than a guilty one, except where the reason that applies in the one case does not apply in the other: as in bigamy, which arises from a matrimonial act rather than a fornicating one. But a cleric who fought in a duel, even if death or mutilation did not follow, is to be deposed, unless the bishop dispensed with him. Likewise, some say that a cleric who was a minister in a capital case is irregular, but that the bishop can dispense with him unless death or mutilation followed. *Distinctio 236*, last chapter, c. *Saepe*, and the following chapter. But this is not true, because it is not found expressly in writing, nor should irregularity otherwise be judged. Nor does it matter whether one killed a pagan or a Christian, because that man is a man just as this one. Hence it says in *Extra*, *De homicidio*, c. *Petitio*: clerics fighting against Saracens, if they are in doubt about killing someone, shall abstain from the priesthood. *Distinctio 50*, *Miros*. And for this reason those who supply the wood for burning heretics are irregular, if that wood by its burning contributes in any way to the death, even if they receive an indulgence.

15 The third conclusion is drawn from these points: what contracts irregularity on account of homicide. And it must be said that \[it is contracted\] only in two cases. The first is when someone is a homicide by knowledge, that is, by intention, whether lawfully or unlawfully — as is evident in the case of a judge. Hence one who kills a man in lawful self-defense but with intention is irregular. He is not excused because he acted lawfully, as a judge acts lawfully; nor because he acted out of necessity — because the necessity of avoiding mortal sin is greater than \[the necessity of avoiding\] death, and the necessity of avoiding the deaths of many is greater than that of one, and the damage to the commonwealth \[is greater\] than that of one person. A judge, however, cannot avoid mortal sin, nor sometimes the deaths of many, nor harm to the commonwealth, unless he condemns this public murderer to death — and yet he becomes irregular; therefore all the more does this one \[become irregular\] who killed a man to protect his own body, which he was not obliged to do. And this is true with respect to further promotion; but with respect to ministering in \[orders\] already received, unavoidable homicide does not depose from order or benefice — as homicide done ex officio does — because the latter seems to mark out a cleric who deliberately takes up the office of blood, whereas the former does not, since he unexpectedly kills an aggressor in self-defense. *Distinctio 50*, *De his clericis*, and through *Extra*, *De homicidio*, c. *Interfecisti*; *Distinctio 51*, *Aliquotos*. As for the fact that Peter, who cut off the ear of the servant, was \[still\] promoted: Christ dispensed, who afterward said to him, “Feed my sheep”; or else the church’s prohibition did not yet exist.
