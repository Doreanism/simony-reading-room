And Phinehas merited the priesthood by killing, and the Maccabees were warriors and yet were priests. Concerning David, however, it was said on account of a figure, or if it was forbidden at the time, it was a judicial precept and did not bind after the death of Christ, except insofar as the church itself then enacted the same thing, following the Apostle’s word, “Not a striker.” The second case is when, apart from intention, someone is killed or mutilated by chance, yet through one’s own fault — for example, because one was engaged in an unlawful undertaking: as if one going to a brothel with weapons, not intending to fight, and a battle breaking out beyond intention, commits homicide thereby, or something of the sort; or if one who is not a physician meddles with medical treatment and death follows, he is irregular. Likewise, if one was engaged in a lawful undertaking but did not exercise proper diligence — for instance, a physician who omitted something among the precautions, or a pruner who threw a branch from a tree onto a public road and did not call out but killed someone — he is irregular. A physician, however, who amputates a member according to the rules of the art does not appear to be irregular, because this is not mutilating but preserving the body. But if apart from both intention and fault the death or mutilation of another occurs, he does not incur irregularity. It must be noted here that something is a fault for one person that is not a fault for another. Hence a case was put of a boy who, throwing an arrow, struck backward by chance, beyond his intention, the eye of a companion standing behind him and put it out — which was remarkable. And I said that, granted that had he been an adult he would have been irregular on account of that blinding, because it could rarely have happened without his fault, the boy nevertheless was not, because he was not at fault: since he was not required to apply the diligence he did not know how to apply — otherwise he would be held to an impossibility — and therefore he is not irregular. In a case of probable doubt, however, one must abstain just as in a case of certainty. But where the conscience is irrationally scrupulous, it is to be set aside; and if it cannot be set aside, in the meantime one must cease from ministry and promotion. *De tempori. or. ad Aures*; *Distinctio 50*, *De his*. As regards the third \[impediment\], namely the impediment of bodily defect: it must be said that it impedes in three cases. The first is when the defect is such as to cause danger, so that a man cannot perform the acts of orders without peril — for instance, if he cannot make the fraction \[of the host\], or if his hands tremble so that he cannot hold the chalice without danger of spilling the blood. The second case is when scandal is generated — for instance, because he has had his nose cut off or something of that sort. The third is when \[the defect arose\] by his own act and fault. As for the report that Saint Mark cut off his own thumb so that he might be found unfit for the priesthood, and yet was afterward Patriarch of Alexandria: this was by the dispensation of the Holy Spirit or of Peter, who was Pope, and consequently could dispense in every such defect; moreover, so little was amputated that he could still make the fraction. To the argument \[from the hierarchy of beings\] it must be said: nobler beings have less impedibility in themselves, but they have the greatest impedibility in terms of participation in them by others and in approaching them. For those things that are more perfect are immediately close to, and most perfectly participate in \[the highest\], while the rest are remote. Similarly, the sacrament of orders is not impedible in itself, given its proper causes; but those who approach it by receiving or exercising it must be most perfect, and the imperfect are impeded from this in many ways.
