SECONDLY, inquiry is made concerning the time for ordinations, and in particular it is asked whether a curate is bound to be promoted to the priesthood within a year — otherwise the benefice falls vacant by operation of law \[Extra, de electione, c. Licet canon., lib. 6\]. 2 On the other side: \[Extra,\] de plebano, c. tit., c. Statutum. 3 I respond: here five things must be considered. First, concerning the necessity of receiving orders. Second, concerning the time for ordinations. Third, concerning the time of the orders themselves. Fourth, concerning the age of benefice-holders. Fifth, concerning the time within which a curate must be ordained. 4 Regarding the first, there are three conclusions. The first concerns compulsion in fact: here it must be known that by absolute necessity no one can be compelled to receive an order, just as no one can be compelled to baptism. And if someone is ordained entirely against his will, he receives nothing — as with baptism \[c. Maiori\]. But with conditioned will, someone can be compelled; and one who consents under fear does receive the character, because a coerced will is still a will — but not grace, since he acts in bad faith, being unwilling, and accordingly approaches without devotion. Yet he does receive the execution \[of orders\], unless something else impedes, since there is no law suspending him on that account. In neither mode, however, is anyone to be compelled to receive any sacrament in itself — though in the matter of their children, bad Christians or heretics can be compelled by rulers to deliver for baptism the children they are concealing, just as the Maccabees circumcised boys by force of arms, perhaps against the will not only of the pagans but also of the fathers, who had made themselves foreskins \[i.e., submitted to Gentile custom\] in passing over to the Gentile rite.

5 The second conclusion concerns conditioned compulsion through a legal penalty to be inflicted for the sake of the Church’s need. Here it seems that, since greater obedience is owed to the ruler in spiritual matters for the sake of good governance, just as in temporal matters obedience is owed to the ruler in those matters, and since the public good must be preferred to the private — just as one is compelled to accept the guardianship or custody of an orphan, or of an adult who is mad or prodigal, because this is in the public interest — much more so must the office of judging in the commonwealth be made obligatory: for example, that one be a provost or bailiff, if the commonwealth requires it, for whose welfare a good citizen must even expose himself to death, as nature interposes the arm to protect the body. Much more so, therefore, if the Church requires such a person to be a curate or bishop, he can be compelled to this by his superior through precept and excommunication, just as some hermits have been compelled by bishops and are bound to obey — since according to Gregorius, one who refuses to feed the flock of the chief shepherd proves that he does not love him, who says: “Feed my sheep, and feed my lambs.” 6 The third conclusion concerns conditioned and consequent compulsion through deprivation of a benefice by sentence of a human judge — excepting what will be said below concerning the simple curate. 7 It must be known that if someone holds a benefice requiring an order by law or custom, he is to be admonished to have himself promoted as the benefice requires: for instance, if he is an archdeacon, to the diaconate; if a dean, abbot, or the like, or holds a prebend that is sacerdotal, to the presbyterate. If, having been canonically admonished, he has not done so within the due time, with no just impediment ceasing, he is to be deprived of the benefice \[Extra, de electione, Cum in cunctis\], except for what is said there regarding the bishop. If, however, he has an impediment, either temporary or permanent: if temporary — for instance, illness — he is excused for as long as it lasts; when it ceases, the same rule applies as before. If he has a permanent impediment, either it preceded the conferral of the benefice and was of such a kind that no right was acquired — and then, whether he knew it or was ignorant, he is to be deprived in fact, as is the rule in fact: for instance, because he is a homicide or illegitimate and did not know it, or something of the kind. If, however, the impediment was of such a kind that a right was acquired: if he knew and was silent, the benefice must be taken away as from an unworthy holder; if he was ignorant, the same applies as if the impediment had supervened, since one who acquired the benefice in good faith and with just title ought not to be deprived without his own fault.
