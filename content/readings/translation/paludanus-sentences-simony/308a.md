Proof of the consequence: just as priestly consecration is ordered toward the making of the Eucharist, so episcopal \[consecration is ordered\] toward the ordination of ministers of the Church.   3   I answer: three things must be considered here. First, that a bishop by his own right can confer all orders. Second, that a bishop alone \[can do so\] except by commission. Third, what is to be said of one who has resigned the episcopate.   4   As to the first, certain people hold the following position, plausibly and subtly: a bishop can confer all orders. They prove this first as follows: a bishop, by virtue of episcopal ordination, has by office the administration of all sacred things. For among those who hold orders, the higher power can do whatever the lower can, and more besides. Since therefore episcopal consecration is the highest consecration conferred in the Church, the power given through it is supreme with respect to all others; and therefore a bishop can do whatever inferiors can do in administering sacred things, and more besides. The fullness of the administration of the sacraments thus resides with the bishop, from which they argue as follows. If not every bishop can confer orders, this is either because of unworthiness of life — as with an evil man — or because of defect of faith — as with a heretic — or because of a sentence of the Church — as with one who is excommunicated, or suspended, or otherwise cut off — or because of deposition from order — as with one who has been degraded; but none of these things prevents every bishop from being able to confer true orders; therefore, and so on. The minor premise is proved first as to two of its clauses — namely, that neither unworthiness of life nor defect of faith prevents \[a bishop\] from conferring all orders — because that which has its efficacy from the work performed \[*ex opere operato*\] is not frustrated by the unworthiness of the performer, provided that those things which are necessary to the performance are present; but the sacrament of orders has its efficacy from the work performed; therefore wickedness of life and defect of personal faith, which pertains to the unworthiness of the performer, do not prevent a bishop, who by virtue of his consecration is the minister of all orders (as has been declared), from conferring true orders — provided the due form and matter, which are necessary to the sacrament, are observed. Likewise, to elicit some act, a habit is not required — a habit merely adds a mode; a capacity suffices, which anyone can bring into act, as the very word “capacity” \[*potentia*\] implies. But grace and faith, through which works are good and life is good, are certain habits; therefore they are not required in order that a bishop may confer orders, but only in order that he may confer them rightly and lawfully. Second, the same minor premise is evident as to the other two clauses — namely, that an excommunicated or degraded bishop can confer true orders — because if he could not, this would be either because he had been deprived of the power, or of the \[right of\] execution: not only by law, but in fact. It cannot be said that he has been deprived of the power, because certain things remain through consecration as long as the consecrated thing remains; but the power of conferring orders is given to a bishop in his consecration; therefore it always remains and cannot be lost. Likewise, neither is he deprived of factual execution, because factual execution corresponds to power — power being that by which we are able \[to act\]; whereas legal execution, beyond power, requires lawful use. Since therefore through excommunication and degradation a bishop is not deprived of the power of conferring orders, he is not deprived of factual execution so as to be unable to confer true orders — though he is deprived of legal execution, since he confers unlawfully, to his own damnation and to that of one who knowingly receives \[orders from him\]. And this is expressly determined by Augustinus in the text \[of the *Sentences*\], which is found in the *Decretum*, distinction 1, question 1, chapter *Quod quidem* \[Gratian, *Decretum*, C.1 q.1 c.96\].   5   I answer: the first of these arguments seems to have a dubious major premise — namely, that among those who hold orders, the higher power can do whatever the lower can. This does not hold in real and transient things, where whatever the body can do, the spirit cannot necessarily; nor does it hold in hierarchical acts, where whatever the bishop can do, the archbishop cannot necessarily.
