# Question 4

FOURTH, it is asked whether the Pope can commit simony. It seems that he cannot: for he is no less lord over spiritual things than the Emperor is over temporal things — indeed more so, since the Emperor does not depose princes at will as the Pope deposes prelates. But because all things belong to the prince, he cannot commit lay simony, which is punished under the *lex Iulia de ambitu* \[the Julian law on electoral corruption\]. Hence the law says, “the *lex Iulia de ambitu* does not apply in the city” \[Digest, *Ad legem Iuliam ambitus*, l. 1\] — because, that is, the dignities of the city are conferred by the prince — and so on. 2 Against this: the Pope was Peter, who feared incurring simony when he said to Simon: “May your money perish with you” \[Acts 8:20\], and so on. 3 I answer: three things must be considered here. First, the conclusions that all concede. Second, the distinction that the jurists draw among simoniacs. Third, a doubtful conclusion.

4 As to the first, there are three conclusions. The first conclusion is that the Pope can commit the sin of simony in general, just as any other inferior prelate can, with respect to the graver fault. Some explain this as follows, and well. Every sin is a deviation from some natural, divine, or rightly enacted human law — hence Ambrosius says that sin is something said, desired, or done against the law; and every law speaks to those who are under the law, as Romans 3 \[:19\] says. From this one may argue thus: every person who acts against the law to which he is subject sins; but every person who sells a spiritual thing for a temporal thing sins against the natural and divine law, to which all are subject — the Pope as much as others — therefore such a person sins, and not with any sin other than simony. And so on. The major premise is evident from what has been said; the minor will be evident from what will be said. 5 The second conclusion is that the Pope cannot commit simony with respect to the penalties of positive law, to which he is not subject. Hence he does not incur suspension or penalties of that kind under the law — just as, if he strikes a cleric, he is not excommunicated — because the prince is released from the laws \[Digest, *De legibus*, l. *Princeps*\]. 6 The third conclusion is that a person who were to purchase a benefice or an order even from the Pope would not incur the penalties of canon law either: because just as \[the Pope\] could dispense with penalties already incurred so that they would not stand, so he could grant a privilege to someone such that by striking a cleric he would not be excommunicated — though he would not readily do so. But when he gives \[a benefice\] for money, he appears to be dispensing \[that person\] to retain it lawfully, just as when he knowingly grants \[something\] to an irregular person, by that very act he dispenses with him. Hence someone who obtained a benefice through simony in the Roman court, if he is aware that it issued from the awareness of the conferring Pope, need not resign it but may retain it, doing penance for the sin. 7 As to the second: first, the jurists’ familiar distinction will be set out. Second, arguments against it will be raised, as some have raised them. Third, the statements made in its rejection will be examined. 8 As to the first point, it should be known that certain people distinguish that something can be simoniacal in two ways. In one way, by its very nature, and is therefore prohibited in law. In another way, solely by reason of legal prohibition. Things that are simoniacal by their very nature are those whose sale is prohibited in the Old or New Testament, such as sacred things; all other things are simoniacal only because they are prohibited. This distinction is found in the *extra de officio iudicis delegati* \[Decretals, X 1.29\], chapter *Ex parte*, in the ordinary gloss, and Innocentius and Hostiensis agree. 9 As to the second point, some say that the distinction is not true as set out in the second part, though it may be true in some sense. They prove the first claim in four ways. The first argument is this: concerning something that is not evil by its nature but only because it is prohibited, one can only sin with the sin of simple disobedience — because in such cases the entire reason for sin consists in the transgression of the prohibition. If, therefore, the sale of a simple benefice is not simoniacal by its nature, it is impossible that from its prohibition in law there arises anything there but simple disobedience. The second argument is this: simony is the sale of a spiritual thing for a temporal thing; but a legal prohibition does not change the natures of things so as to make a temporal thing spiritual or vice versa.
