But the Pope can confer all orders by his own right — yet this he holds not from his jurisdiction, in which he excels, but from the power of order, in which he is equal \[to a bishop\]; whence he calls them brothers and calls himself a bishop. Therefore, etc. But the argument that the unworthiness of one’s life does not impede \[the conferral of orders\] must be understood as follows: the effect that comes ex opere operato is not impeded on account of the unworthiness of the life of the one performing the act, whether as a first or second effect; but on account of the unworthiness of the life of the recipient the second effect — which is grace — is impeded, not the character or adornment, which is the first effect — provided that the essentials are present. Among the essentials may be counted the intention to receive and to do what the Church does; otherwise what is performed outwardly would bear only an equivocal relation to the sacrament. For the washing is not the matter of baptism, nor the words the form, when someone does not wish to be baptized but merely to bathe and the like — except equivocally. Thus the external acts are essential insofar as they are intended and given or received from that intention; otherwise they are not. 7 Regarding the second point, it must be known that the bishop alone can confer orders by his own right, especially the major orders. For it is no small thing to place a commander over an army and to enroll soldiers; but it belongs to the bishop alone, in the army of the Church militant, to enroll soldiers through the sacrament of confirmation, as was shown above at distinction 7. Therefore, with all the more reason does it belong to him alone to appoint commanders in the Church — which is done through the sacrament of order, especially with respect to the major orders. The Pope, however, can delegate to a simple priest the conferral of confirmation and of minor orders, because Christ determined that the minister of the conferral of the highest orders is the supreme priest, namely the bishop — a minister whom the Pope cannot change. But the minister of confirmation and of minor orders Christ established to be a priest whom the Church would designate, because there was nothing to be confirmed in man until the first confirmers had been confirmed by the Holy Spirit, and the Church did not make use of minor orders in the primitive era. And although this may seem to beg the question, yet from the custom of the Roman Church — which delegates the latter but not the former — it can be established that this was so, and it is certain that it was possible. But unless the Pope has been consecrated as Pope or as bishop (which amounts to the same thing), he cannot make such a delegation, since everything accidental is reduced to what is per se; hence every agent must act either by its own power or by that of another. And since it belongs per se to the bishop to ordain and confirm, if the one ordaining or confirming is not a bishop, this must be done in the power of a true bishop. Hence if one elected as Pope is not a true bishop, he cannot make such a delegation. Thus what was said at distinction 7 — that the consecration of a bishop is not a sacrament but a “sacramental” \[sacramentale\] — is true as regards many of its ceremonies; but as regards that part of the consecration by which the sacerdotal character is extended to the power of conferring \[orders\] and ordaining by one’s own right, and not merely by delegation, it is a sacrament, that is, a part of the holy priesthood. For just as one would say that a priest who had received the power of consecrating the body of Christ but had not yet received the laying on of hands with the power of binding and loosing would not have the power of binding and loosing by his own right — but could have it by the Pope’s delegation, because by reason of his ordination he is capable of it — so here too, a person fully ordained as a simple priest is capable of the power of confirming and ordaining, which he can receive in two ways: in one way, so that he exercises it by another’s right — and this is without a sacrament, by the Pope’s commission; in another way, so that he exercises it by his own right — and this is through the sacrament of the episcopate.
