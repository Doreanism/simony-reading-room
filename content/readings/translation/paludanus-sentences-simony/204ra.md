The major premise is established by C.1 q.1 c. *Eos qui per pecuniam* \[*Decretum* C.1 q.1 c.1\], which says: “Every owner may sell what he has, whether a slave or any other thing he possesses” — and this is applied to the case of simony. The minor premise is proved as follows: a prelate is not appointed by God as owner of spiritual things, but only as minister and steward. First, from 1 Corinthians 4 \[1 Cor. 4:1\]: “Let a man so account of us as of the ministers of Christ, and stewards of the mysteries of God”; and Peter the Apostle, the prince of the apostles and vicar of Christ, says in his first canonical epistle, chapter 4 \[1 Pet. 4:10\]: “As every man has received grace, ministering the same one to another, as good stewards of the manifold grace of God.” They received these things freely, not by purchase, as the Lord said in Matthew 10 \[Matt. 10:8\]: “Freely you have received, freely give.” Therefore it is in no way lawful to sell such things. Third, the same is evident from comparing the thing sold to the nature of the sale: that contract is illicit in which the nature of the thing is repugnant to the nature of the contract. But this is so in the case of the sale and purchase of a spiritual thing; therefore such a sale is simply illicit. The major premise is evident, because the nature of the thing must be compatible with the nature of the contract, and vice versa. The minor is proved as follows: an accessory follows the nature of the principal; but sacred things are accessory to grace, and therefore follow its nature; and the nature of grace is that it be given freely — otherwise it is not grace, as Romans 11 \[Rom. 11:6\] states — whereas the nature of buying and selling is that it is a non-gratuitous contract. Therefore the nature of grace and of the spiritual things annexed to it is repugnant to buying and selling. It is thus clear from all this that the buying and selling of sacred things is intrinsically illicit. The word “simony” derives from Simon \[Magus\], who wished to purchase the grace of the Holy Spirit — the grace of conferring the Holy Spirit — which he saw visibly bestowed in the sacrament of confirmation through the imposition of hands by the apostles, as Acts 8 \[Acts 8:18–19\] records. Those who sell \[such things\] are properly called Gehazites \[*Giezitae*\], after the account of Gehazi in 2 Kings 5 \[2 Kings 5:20–27\], although all who sell or buy can be called simoniacs, since Simon wished to buy that grace in order afterward to sell its exercise. The same must be said of things antecedent to the sacraments, such as sacred vessels and the right of patronage, which is the right of presenting someone to an ecclesiastical benefice. Sacred vessels, by reason of their consecration, cannot in any manner be sold — that is, one cannot demand anything additional by reason of their consecration; however, in necessity of the Church they can be sold for the value of what is purely corporeal in them, namely the material of gold or silver. And if they are sold to an ecclesiastical person, they may be sold whole; but if to others, and not for the use of the church, they must first be broken, lest sacred things be handled by persons other than the ministers of the Church. The right of patronage, for its own part, cannot be sold, nor can it be given in fief; but if the estate to which the right of patronage is annexed is sold or given in fief, the right of patronage passes with the whole — as is clear from *Extra*, *de iure patronatus*, *cum secundum* \[*X* 3.38.2\] — nor should the estate or castle be sold at a higher price on account of the right of patronage. Concomitant things — such as the bodily labor involved in administering the sacraments or in consecrating churches — can in no way be sold, because the labor of the worker never falls under a sale — only the work produced does, even in purely corporeal matters. For who has ever purchased the labor of a worker except for the sake of the product of the work? No one. Therefore it is absurd to say that ministers can sell their labor in cases where they cannot lawfully sell their work. If, then, an ecclesiastical minister who dispenses the sacraments receives anything, he does not receive it as the price of his work or his labor, but as the sustenance of the minister. The same must be said of the offices consequent upon holy orders received — as will be shown. As for those things called spiritual by reason of the created spirit: some are spiritual by essence, such as knowledge and acquired virtues, and these can in no way be sold, even in fact, for the same reason given above regarding grace and things spiritual by essence; others are spiritual by attribution, such as teaching, counseling, judging, and bearing witness.
