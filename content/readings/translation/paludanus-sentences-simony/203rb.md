## Fifth Question.

### On the nature of simony and its species.

THE FIFTH QUESTION ASKED is whether mental simony obliges \[the offender\] to resign a benefice obtained through it. And it seems that it does: because the sin of simony is greater than that of usury; but usury, even without a formal agreement, must be restored by institution \[of divine law\]. Therefore, etc.

### 2   On the Other Side

On simony, the final chapter, where it says: when no agreement has intervened, penitence before God suffices.

### 3   My Response

Here, for clarity regarding simony, three things must be seen in general. First, what it is, how it is punished, and how dispensation is granted in it. Second, in what things simony has its place materially and objectively. Third, by what means or through what intermediaries it is committed, as it were formally.

### 4   As Regards the First

there are three conclusions. The first concerns its nature, for which definitions are given. The first is that of the jurists: that simony is the deliberate will to buy or sell something spiritual, or annexed to the spiritual. “Deliberate will,” because if something that would otherwise pertain to simony occurs without deliberation on the part of those who do not intend it and in no way will it, it is not simony. “Will” as regards God. “To buy” — any non-gratuitous contract, that is, an exchange, is prohibited unless by the authority of a bishop. Likewise an intrinsic or extrinsic transaction \[is prohibited\], but a friendly composition is permitted. “Or to sell” — those who do so are called Gehazites \[cf. 2 Kings 5\], or simoniacs. “Something spiritual by essence, or by its connection to the spiritual” — antecedently, concomitantly, or consequently. Now simony is an ecclesiastical crime, distinguished from a civil or secular one by three things. First, by its subject matter or object: because a crime committed against God is ecclesiastical, as heresy is; whereas crimes against the neighbor are civil, as homicide is. Second, by its jurisdiction: because knowledge of it belongs to the ecclesiastical judge. Third, by its penalty — \[see\] *C. de episcopis et clericis*, *Authent. Clericis*, in the gloss. Hence for every personal debt for which action can be taken to impose an ecclesiastical penalty — because if one does not pay the debt after prior admonition one can be excommunicated — so that action may be brought before an ecclesiastical judge: and similarly for earthly possessions, \[the judge\] will not then pronounce them to be of this kind, but will admonish that restitution be made and will excommunicate the unwilling — for which three reasons simony is an ecclesiastical crime. Simony is moreover called heresy, by reason of similarity of act, or as regards certain disciples of Simon who, together with their master, believed that the gift of God could be obtained with money.

### 5   The Second Definition

is that of Cantor Parisiensis: that simony is the non-gratuitous acceptance or conferral of spiritual things. Here it should be noted that privation is reduced to the genus of the habit; hence just as it is simony when something is given in exchange for electing someone, so also when it is given in exchange for not electing him: thus \[giving\] in exchange for not testifying is simony, just as \[giving\] in exchange for testifying; and just as it is lawful for an advocate to receive payment for pleading, so conversely for keeping silent, as it seems — because if the opposite \[holds\] in the opposite case, so the proposed \[holds\] in the proposed case.

### 6   The Second Conclusion

concerns the penalty, which is threefold. The first, beyond infamy and canonical disability, is suspension, according to the distinction of Thomas: for either the simony occurs in a received order, or in a benefice. If in an order, it admits of no excuse, but the offender is suspended by the law itself, both as regards himself and as regards others, whether the offense is occult or manifest — just as a notorious fornicator is \[suspended\]. *Extra de symonia*, *Tanta*. And this is how he interprets *1.q.1. Reperiunt*. And this is so because \[the order\] bears a stolen character.
