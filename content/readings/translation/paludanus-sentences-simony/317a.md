## 29   On the second \[argument\]

some say, and the case of the right of patronage and the conferral of benefices is not parallel: for the right of patronage passes with the sale of a village or castle, and is not sold directly and in the first instance — because the sale does not fall primarily upon it, but upon the village or castle — nor should \[a property\] be sold or bought at a higher price on account of the right of patronage attached to it, for otherwise it would be simony. But if an ecclesiastical benefice were sold, the sale would fall directly upon what is spiritual, because a prelate has no other way to confer a benefice than by instituting the minister for the office; and therefore whatever he were selling, this he would sell first, and on account of this, whatever other temporal thing is annexed to it — and so the sale would fall directly upon what is spiritual, which could not occur without the vice of simony.

## 30   But

this solution does not seem sound, because a prelate has the right to confer not only what is spiritual but also what is temporal. For the Pope immediately confers the canonry, which is a spiritual right, and confers along with it the first prebend to fall vacant, which is a temporal benefice. Hence one might say: “I confer the canonry upon you freely; but if along with it you wish to have the prebend, you will give me such an amount” — given also that a half-canon with respect to the spiritual right is a full canon. Therefore, if the Pope were to make someone a full canon for money, the sale would fall upon what is purely corporeal. It can therefore perhaps be said otherwise, namely that an accessory follows the principal, and not vice versa. Hence when the principal is of itself corporeal, the sale falls upon it, and the spiritual follows freely — as is the case with the right of patronage. But when the converse holds, as in a benefice in which the spiritual is principal, then just as it must be conferred freely and not sold, so neither may the accessory \[be sold\]; and it is a matter of divine law that one who serves the altar should live by the altar \[1 Cor. 9:13\] — hence it would be against divine law if what he ought to receive for his office were not given to him except for money. If, however, a prelate were in practice to act in this divided manner, saying “I give you the canonry freely and sell you the prebend,” he would act unjustly, because he would be selling what he had already given: for in giving the canonry he gives the prebend by consequence. Nonetheless he would still commit simony, because he is not selling the fruits themselves but the right to receive those fruits; and that right is spiritual by its very essence, because it is the same thing in reality as the canonry — for just as the power of the priest extends to two acts, so the same canonical right is that by which one holds spiritual things, such as a place in choir, and that by which one holds temporal things.

## 31   On the third \[argument\]

some say that the antecedents to a sacred thing, such as sacred vessels, can be licitly sold, provided nothing more is sold on account of the consecration — because the sale falls directly upon what is corporeal. But the office that follows upon a sacred thing is of itself spiritual, and therefore cannot be sold, nor \[can it be sold\] on account of the temporal annexed benefice that follows from it, since \[the benefice\] is not transferred except through the prior transfer of the office — by reason of which the sale would also fall upon the office, which is spiritual. This resolves the subsequent confirmation: for a thing that is partly corporeal and partly spiritual can in some way be sold by reason of what is corporeal, when the seller directly and per se can transfer that corporeal element to another, and not mediately through the spiritual — which is not the case in the present question.

## 32   On the fourth \[argument\]

which was put at the beginning of the question, it must be said that an emperor who sells a magistracy, though he does not sin against the Julian law on electoral corruption \[*lex Iulia ambitus*\], nonetheless sins against the natural law, to which he is subject. Likewise, if the Pope were to sell any ecclesiastical office, though he does not sin against canon law, to which he is not subject, he nonetheless sins against divine law.
