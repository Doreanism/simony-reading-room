But with respect to consecration as bishop it seems the same as for the other orders: that is, that if the parents consented, or if they were not present, and he were ordained by the will of those standing in the parents’ place, he would receive the character. 9 To the first argument of these objectors one can respond to the major premise, which requires that the will of receiving be expressed or interpretive, that it may be denied in the case of a child: for where there were no parent in the world, and only a child and another person, neither father nor mother, one could still baptize the child, and so on the part of the one baptized no intention and no will would concur, and the sole will of the one conferring the sacrament would suffice. Thus also in the present case; but in one who has the use of reason, who has now been entrusted to himself by the Lord, a proper will is required. In a child, however, whom God has not yet released into the hand of his own will, no will is required. One may also say to the minor that the will of the parents is also the will of the child, not only in matters of the necessity of salvation but in others as well: hence by the will of a parent offering him, a child can be confirmed, since Christ also used to lay his hands upon children in blessing; and by the will of the father something can be acquired for the child, just as through a guardian.

10 As to the objection that the boy can afterward lawfully withdraw and dissent: if a father or mother has made a vow on his behalf, this is true. Hence even one who was ordained as a priest while in the cradle, upon coming to the age of discretion, is not bound to observe continence; but, being a true priest, he will be able to contract marriage, since he could not be obligated while unknowing.

11 What they say — and therefore the will of the parents suffices in baptism, because it is ordered toward the deletion of sin contracted through the parents — should be answered as follows: even the will of guardians suffices in the absence of the parent; indeed, even the will of the one baptizing alone suffices, where no one else appears; and yet the child did not contract that sin from the guardians — indeed it is certain that the will of close relatives suffices, from whom the child has contracted no sin at all, nor perhaps even original sin itself. If it be said that authority is less fitting for boys than for women, because the boy has none of the use of reason and less bodily strength — and therefore if a woman cannot be ordained even de facto, neither can a boy — 12 the answer is: it is not analogous, because authority is less fitting to boys in act, but more fitting in aptitude.

13 Regarding the second, namely the impediment of fortune, specifically homicide, there are three conclusions. The first concerns what is to be understood by homicide. Indeed, insofar as it causes irregularity, it requires the death of a human being or mutilation; and mutilation counts as much as killing \[Extra, de clericis percussoribus, c. 1; dist. 55, c. ult.\]. By “death of a human being” is meant the death of one having a rational soul, without which there is no human being; for this reason, one who kills a foetus in the womb before that animation, or who hangs an already-dead corpse or mutilates it, does not become irregular. By “mutilation” is meant the mutilation of limbs properly so called, which when cut off do not grow back; it is otherwise with others, as if one has shaved the hair or cut the nails, or even if one has torn such things off — therefore these are not in view, but others are. Not only the principal members are meant: for not only if one has cut off a foot or hand, but even if a finger or joint, and not merely an entire finger or eye or nose, but also if one has cut off a notable part that cannot return — whether from oneself or from another \[dist. 55, Si quis semetipsum, c. Qui partem\]. Not so, however, if one merely wounds skin or flesh, which by nature grows back however much, or sheds blood: for since lost blood is restored by nature, one does not become irregular; nor even if one wounds the head such that a considerable piece of skull bone has been removed, since this pertains to the external members, and penalties are always to be construed narrowly.

14 The second conclusion is: who is a homicide in this matter? And the answer is: one who is a cause of homicide through counsel, assistance, deed, or in any manner whatsoever — provided that it be reducible to the genus of efficient cause.
