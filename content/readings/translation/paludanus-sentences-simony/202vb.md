Hence Augustine says that the sacrificial fire, which had survived under water through the seven years of the Babylonian captivity, was extinguished when Antiochus sold the priesthood to Jason. From this it is argued: the selling of the priesthood was not simony because the priesthood was instituted by God — for then selling every being would be simoniacal, since every being is instituted and created by God — but rather because the priesthood, by whomsoever instituted, was instituted for the worship of God, and from this it derives its spirituality.

## The priesthood under the old law was only a relation of reason.

That spirituality at that time was nothing but a relation of reason. So too, since every ecclesiastical benefice now orders a man toward divine worship and service, even if he were not required to perform the church’s office, the very canonical right is spiritual by reason of its ordering toward divine worship, even though it contains in itself no spiritual reality. Moreover, for a thing to be of itself simoniacal, it is not necessary that it possess that divine spirituality, but only that it be formally such in itself. For even if the Pope had made a chalice, it would not on that account be more consecrated than if a craftsman had made it, since its spirituality formally consists in the consecration. Hence things of this kind, which are annexed to divine worship by statute of the Church, are formally spiritual of themselves, even though they are so effectively through the Church. Just as it was once of itself simoniacal to sell the priesthood, by which a man was ordered to divine worship in the ceremonial rites, so now it is simoniacal to sell a canonry, by which one is ordered to divine worship in the ecclesiastical offices. §26. As for the argument about the chalice, it must be said that the case is not parallel: for when a chalice is sold, only the bodily matter is sold, not the spirituality of reason. But when a canonry is sold, what is sold is not the income itself — for that can be sold separately to a layman — but the right to receive it, not just any right, but that very same right of performing office in spiritual matters; for just as there is one priestly character with the power of consecrating and absolving, so there is one canonry that is the right of performing office and of receiving what is owed to that office. ## Against Durandus.

First, one might say that it is indeed true that it was once simoniacal for someone to become a minister of the temple for money, because the institution of the ministers of the Church was a matter of divine law, as was the sanctification of priests and Levites; the jurisdiction and ordering \[of the clergy\] was established by David. But the institution of canons is not a matter of divine law, as is the case with orders and sacred things. Second, \[the solution\] falls short because the authority cited speaks of a minister of a pagan temple, where nothing is holy but everything profane, and it does not say that the act was simoniacal, as is apparent — not just \[from\] the temple \[alone\]. Third, as to the authority *Freely you have received, freely give* \[Matt. 10:8\], it could be said that this pertains to those things received from God, not from man, such as simple canonries.
