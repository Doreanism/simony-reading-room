Sixth, the bishop dispenses when the simony was contracted through ignorance of positive law — as in the case of one who promised to ask nothing of the presenter. Ignorance does not excuse from simony (*Extra*, same title, *Per tuas* \[*X* 5.3.28\]); it does, however, excuse \[the ignorant party\] such that the bishop may dispense. Nor does the decretal *Cum illorum* (*de sent. excom.*) stand in the way, because if one could not absolve from excommunication, then neither could he dispense from the irregularity contracted on account of it, whether knowingly or unknowingly; otherwise, even in that case, he could act on grounds of ignorance of fact or of law. In the same way an abbot dispenses regarding an irregularity incurred by a monk through ignorance of law or fact on account of an excommunication from which the abbot himself could have absolved — but not for any other reason. And thus an abbot can do no more than a bishop in the matter of dispensation, except insofar as he has greater power of absolution: for a bishop \[may absolve\] only in cases of minor assault, an abbot \[only\] short of grievous harm. Yet this case is doubtful: for the bishop is not found to dispense with one who has received holy orders simoniacally, except when the simony was contracted by another party while he \[the ordinand\] was ignorant. But if the simony was perhaps contracted by him, ignorance of law does not excuse. Or one may say that when someone receives a benefice through simony contracted between the conferrer and a friend of his \[the recipient\] while the recipient was ignorant, the bishop cannot dispense — unless it is a simple benefice (*de elec.*, c. penult.) — because in that case the conferral was vitiated by the intention of the conferrer; but when neither party believed he was committing simony, that is, neither the giver nor the receiver, through an error of law, then the conferral is less vitiated, and thus even if major orders or a dignity or a cure of souls were involved, the bishop may dispense, if he himself did not commit the simony, or a superior if he himself did commit it.

The second principal article concerns the object and matter of simony. On this topic, first a probable opinion held by certain \[authorities\] will be set forth; second, arguments will be raised against it by way of inquiry; third, by way of epilogue certain conclusions will be gathered.

Regarding the first point, some hold that something can be called “spiritual” either by reason of the uncreated Spirit or by reason of the created spirit. If by reason of the uncreated Spirit, this can be in two ways: either it is spiritual by essence, such as grace and the gifts of the Holy Spirit, or by attribution, such as the sacraments, which are spiritual through \[grace’s\] causality — along with the things antecedent, concomitant, and consequent to these that are spiritual by essence. These last can in no way be sold, either in law or in fact. The reason is that in every sale the thing sold must be capable of being transferred from seller to buyer, whether in law or in fact; but grace and the spiritual gifts cannot be transferred from one person to another, either in law or in fact; therefore they cannot in any way be sold, and consequently no simony can be committed by the purported sale of such things. Sacred things, however, which are spiritual by \[causal\] attribution, can indeed be sold in fact, but never in law and licitly. The reason for this is threefold, corresponding to the three things to which the thing sold stands in relation in a sale: the price, the seller, and the act of sale itself. First, then, the proposition is evident from the comparison of the thing sold to the price: that sale is intrinsically illicit in which the thing sold cannot be recompensed by any price, for it is of the nature of buying and selling that the thing sold should be compensated by a price. But a spiritual thing cannot be compensated by any earthly or temporal price — as is said of wisdom in Proverbs 3 \[cf. Prov. 3:15\] — and with stronger reason this holds for grace and the spiritual things belonging to grace, because she “is more precious than all riches,” and “all the things that are desired cannot be compared to her.” Therefore the sale of any spiritual thing for any temporal consideration is intrinsically illicit. This argument is touched upon by the blessed Peter, condemning Simon, the originator of simony, in Acts 8 \[Acts 8:20\], saying: “Your money perish with you, because you thought that the gift of God could be purchased with money.”
