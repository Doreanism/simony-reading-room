\[Continuing the third conclusion:\] They may be sold intact. To laypersons they should not be sold except melted down, to whom they may then be pledged as well. The same holds for relics, which may be sold and pledged for the value of their gold and silver, but not otherwise, since “let no one sell the martyrs, let no one trade in them.” *C. de sacrosanctis ecclesiis*, l. Nemo \[*Cod.* 1.2.6\]. Second, concerning burial the same principle applies: a cemetery may be sold only for the necessity of the church, the sustenance of the poor, and the ransom of captives — and then only for the value of the land, not on account of its blessing. Burial itself, however, ought not to be sold; and one who refuses to bury unless money is first obtained, and anyone who improperly delays a funeral until something is given or promised to him, commits simony. Other things are annexed to spiritual acts, such as the right of patronage, which cannot be sold but passes with a whole estate when it is sold; and if the estate were sold at a higher price on that account, it would be simony, even though it may accrue to a layperson — because \[the right of patronage\] is not spiritual by its own nature but by reason of that to which it is ordered. Likewise, concerning a canonry: its fruits may be sold, but not the right of receiving them, which is spiritual and does not accrue to a layperson. It is also permitted to redeem tithes from the hand of a layperson and to remove an impediment to one’s right. 22 With regard to the third main argument — namely, in what ways simony is committed — one should know that it is committed in three ways, which must be examined in order: first, through the gift of the hand \[*munus a manu*\]; second, through the gift of the tongue \[*munus a lingua*\]; third, through the gift of service \[*munus ab obsequio*\]. 23 Regarding the first, concerning monks: first, those who give something to abbots in order to obtain priories. Concerning these it must be said that if there is anything spiritual involved — such as tithes, offerings, the obligation of celebrating the divine office, or anything of that kind — it is simony, whether the gift comes from the resources of the monastery or of the priory or from elsewhere, insofar as the preposition “for” \[*pro*\] here denotes appraisement, proximate cause, or primary \[cause\]. Small gifts, however, which are accepted without corrupt intention, do not pertain to simony; this is against those who say that a visitor may not accept even a pen, the contrary of which the gloss states. *Extra* de censibus, c. Fœlicis, lib. 6 \[*Sext.* 3.20.1\], which is also understood of visitations by ordinary right — and so not of visitors sent by a canon, a \[university\] master, or a provincial \[minister\]. Concerning those who by reason of their office may receive nothing: it must be said that they ought to consider the intention of the person making the offering — whether he is otherwise a friend or a kinsman. For if he is a stranger who would not give it except in view of some \[official\] judgment, the gift must be refused, however small, because — although the law distinguishes, for the presumption of law \[*praesumptio iuris*\], whether the gift is large (which is plausibly likely to move the heart) or small (which is not plausibly likely to do so), and on this account the law permits judges to accept such small things since it does not seem they would be corrupted by them — such as food and drink that could be consumed within a few days — nonetheless in the court of conscience, whether small or large, if it is \[given or received\] in exchange for something spiritual, it is simony. 24 Second, simony is committed through the gift of the tongue, and a distinction must be drawn: whether a person intercedes for another or for himself. In the first case: if he intercedes for an unworthy person, it is simony; if for a worthy person, it is not. In the second case: if the \[office at stake is one involving the\] care of souls, it is simony; otherwise not. This distinction holds as regards the presumption of law in the external forum; because in the court of conscience, anyone who grants \[a benefit\] in consideration of another’s intercession, or who petitions with the intention that this be done on account of the intercession, commits simony. But if a prelate grants something at someone’s request that he would not otherwise have granted, because he supposes that the petitioner would only intercede on behalf of a worthy person — so that the petitioner’s words serve as a kind of testimony — it is not simony. One who knows or believes that the \[superior\] who conferred an order or benefice upon him did so principally on account of services or carnal intercessions made by himself or another: if he was unworthy, he must resign, since \[the services\] then take the place of a price; but if he was worthy, \[the obligation to resign\] does not hold — though he ought to do penance for the corrupt intention if he procured it. *Extra* eodem \[tit.\] Mandatum \[*X* 5.3.28\] and de aetate et qualitate, Tuam \[*X* 1.14.14\]. 25 Third, \[simony is committed\] through the gift of service, where the distinction is drawn: whether the service was honorable — in which case it is permitted — or dishonorable — in which case it is simony.
