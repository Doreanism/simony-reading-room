But a man can give power even to one who is not his subject; however, he cannot compel him to receive it. The Church cannot prevent someone from being able to ordain another — which is to give power to a willing recipient — since the character is a power. But the Church can act in such a way that even an unknowing person cannot absolve another; for although every agent requires the subjection of matter, it does not require the subjection of jurisdiction or lordship, just as it is not necessary that the host or chalice which a priest or bishop wishes to consecrate be his own. Nor, similarly, is this required of the one he is to ordain, since he does not have power to bind him or impose anything upon him. But one who absolves does have power to bind and to impose penance, which he cannot do except to one who is subject to him in jurisdiction. Hence, etc. Therefore, if the ordaining minister is a heretic, or excommunicated, or suspended, it matters: for either the recipient knowingly receives from him — and then, on account of the fiction \[of consent\], he does not receive grace, and on account of the other’s suspension he does not receive the execution \[of orders\] — and likewise if the ignorance was crass and willful. But if the recipient was probably ignorant and bore no fault, he receives not only the character but also grace, since he placed no obstacle, as he did not approach with bad faith. If, however, the minister was publicly suspended, yet the one newly coming could probably have been ignorant of this, then he does not receive the execution of orders, but a dispensation can be granted to him by a bishop, as in the case of one ordained by a deposed person \[Extra, de ordinatis ab eo qui renunciavit episcopatui, c. si\]. Or if the suspension was occult, he then receives execution without dispensation. 15 To the third objection it must be said that, although a church consecrated by heretics must be reconsecrated, and every consecration that is not a sacrament \[must be redone\], since in these matters the Church wholly willed and was able to take power away from heretics — whence heretics can neither consecrate oil for the infirm nor chrism — they do nonetheless confer true orders, and likewise the episcopate, since this is a true part of a sacrament whose minister the Church cannot impede. Therefore one consecrated by them is a true bishop. Yet, as has often been said, excised heretics cannot absolve, because absolution requires a subject, and against the will of Peter regarding the person who is subject to him, one cannot submit himself to another — even though the jurisdiction required in the court of penance is spiritual and voluntary. In the article of death, an excommunicated person can absolve, as was said above in the proximate distinction; but not a heretic, since one who knowingly submits to one who would rather curse than absolve would be acting in bad faith. But if no bad faith on the part of the dying person intervened — for instance, because he did not know the minister to be a heretic, or if he knew it but in no way wished to assent to him, and received the sacrament out of necessity from him with detestation of the heresy — then it would seem a true absolution, as if made by a simple excommunicate. And it would not be necessary for one who recovered to confess again; which is certainly true in the case of ignorance. For it is lawful to receive baptism from the hand of a heretic in necessity, but not communion, nor consequently, as some say, penance — since penance and viaticum at death go hand in hand \[Extra, de penitentia, Quod in te\]. Yet it is truly the case that where viaticum is granted, penance is also granted, without which viaticum is not worthily received, but not conversely, since to one who is solemnly doing penance or under interdict, penance is granted at all times, but not the Eucharist, for it is always said: “Believe, and you have eaten.” And spiritual eating suffices for salvation. And although contrition likewise suffices, nevertheless, since a person does not know whether he is worthy of love or hatred — where he knows himself to be attrite but does not know himself to be contrite — and unless he is absolved, so that the power of the keys may perfect what is imperfect in him, he is in danger. Hence it seems almost a sacrament of necessity that it could lawfully be received from a heretic in that case, as has been said.
