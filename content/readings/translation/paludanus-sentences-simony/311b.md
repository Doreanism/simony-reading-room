The year \[within which one must be ordained\] for the subdiaconate or priesthood does not begin to run except from the time when he was in peaceful possession, or when it was his own fault \[that he was not\]; from that point the year is also a useful year \[*annus utilis*\], since it does not run except when a just impediment has ceased. Moreover, the church thus vacant cannot be conferred on him that same occasion — that is, during that vacancy. For if he gives it to someone who does not accept, it is still vacant for the first time of vacating, and so it cannot be conferred during that first vacancy. Similarly, if he arranges for it to be given to someone who immediately resigns in fraud, then although it vacates a second time on account of this resignation, this is not valid before God. But if in good faith it was conferred on another, and that person dies, is deposed, or resigns, so that it vacates a second time, then it can be conferred on him, for there is no prohibition against this. 24. The third conclusion concerns the cure of souls in non-simple \[i.e., collegiate\] churches, such as deans in colleges, priors and abbots in convents — and these are not bound by that statute. De electione, chapter *Statutum*, Book VI \[*Liber Sextus* 1.6.14\]. For there is not the same danger if these men are not priests, since there are many priests in colleges who, by concession of such a non-priest curate, would do what he would do. This is not the case in a simple cure, where there are no parish priests who can administer the ecclesiastical sacraments — which is why it was more necessary to impose a penalty there. Though these curates are more strictly barred from studying law or medicine than simple curates \[extra, *Ne clerici vel monachi*, c. 1 \[X 3.50.1\]\], because they are, as it were, religious, living in common — and if not in terms of food and clothing, at least in terms of the worship and service of God. The word *collegium* is taken broadly here, to include also a parish church that has chapels subordinate to it, in which there are priests who can supply \[what is needed\], as has been said. 25 To the argument on the other side, a response has already been given; but in connection with this, such a case in fact depends on circumstances: Canons ordained that there be certain chaplaincies, and that whoever held them should have themselves ordained within a year. Afterward, when one of these was conferred on a certain man, he appealed to the bishop to ordain him or refer him to another; the bishop, however, was unwilling to do either, saying that this man together with all the canons was excommunicated — while conversely the canons considered the bishop to be excommunicated. When the year had elapsed, the canons declared that this man had forfeited his chapel; the man pleaded impossibility, and the question is asked as a matter of law. The answer is that he is not deprived by the law itself \[*ipso iure*\], because the decretal that deprives one who has not been ordained speaks of a curate or another who is bound to be ordained by the statute of law, not of man; he would have to be deprived by sentence, unless he had just cause — and he appears to have had just cause. Nor is it an objection that he did not appeal, because he was not obliged to litigate or have recourse to a superior; for neither could a superior ordain him, nor license him, nor does it seem that he was obliged to go all the way to the Pope, since perhaps the income would not suffice for so many expenses, and he is not compelled to serve at his own expense.

## Third Question

### On the impediments to orders

Thom. 4. dist. 25, q. 2.

The third question concerns the impediment to orders. It seems that orders ought to have no impediment: for among beings, those of greater dignity have fewer impediments, as the heavenly bodies do; but orders, among the other sacraments after the Eucharist, is of the greatest dignity — and so on. 2 Against this: there are impediments that the laws set down. 3 I answer: three things are to be considered here. First, the impediment of nature. Second, the impediment of fortune. Third, the impediment of fault. 4 As to the first, concerning the impediment of nature, there are three conclusions. The first is that a woman cannot be ordained, either in law or in fact.
