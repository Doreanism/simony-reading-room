Now every order is a sacrament \[*sacrum*\], and not merely a sacramental \[*sacrale*\]. Hence if the opinion were true that holds that all orders other than the priesthood are merely sacramentals, related to the sacraments only as dispositive rather than essentially, it would seem that the Pope could change both the form and the matter and the recipient, just as he can in other sacramentals, such as the catechism and exorcism — unless one were to say the cases are not alike, because those sacramentals are instituted by God whereas these are not. The male sex is therefore a matter of the necessity of the sacrament, whose principal cause is the institution of Christ, both with respect to those who minister and to those who receive. For Christ ordained only men at the supper, when he gave them the power of consecrating, and after the resurrection, when he gave them the Holy Spirit, saying: “Whose sins you shall forgive” \[John 20:23\], etc.; nor did he advance to any grade of orders even the Blessed Virgin Mary, holiest of all women. The Apostle also, who handed on to us what he received from the Lord, as is found in 1 Corinthians 11, indicates that women ought not to be ordained or hold the grade of teaching in the church that belongs to the ordained, saying: “I do not permit women to speak in church” \[1 Corinthians 14:34\]. Nor can it be said that this is only from the ordinance of the apostles and not from the statute of Christ; because to take away from someone something useful for their salvation and granted by Christ cannot be done without injury — especially in spiritual matters. But the dignity of orders is useful for the advancement of salvation in those who use it rightly; therefore, if it had been granted by Christ that women could be ordained, it could not have been taken from them without injury. It must therefore be held that women cannot be ordained by Christ’s institution, nor preach, nor hear confessions, even an abbess. De poenitentia et remissione, c. *Nova* \[X 5.38.12\]. The fitting reason is: because through orders someone is placed in a grade of excellence above others who are not ordained; but such a grade does not befit women over men, but rather a state of subjection, on account of the weakness of the body and the imperfection of reason — on account of that word in Genesis 3: “You shall be under the power of your husband” \[Genesis 3:16\] — and so on. Thus therefore the female sex impedes the reception of orders, because the opposite is a matter of the necessity of the sacrament. Nor is the case the same for orders and the other sacraments, especially with regard to women, because the other sacraments are ordered against a defect common to men and women — such as baptism, the Eucharist, penance, and extreme unction — or else for an act common to both, such as confirmation, which is ordered toward the confession of faith, to which both men and women are ordered. But the sacrament of orders consists in a grade of excellence above others, and of teaching, which does not befit women, as has been said. Nor is there an obstacle in what is said at Distinctio 27, q. 1, chapter *Diaconissa* \[Decretum C.27 q.1 c.23\], nor Distinctio 32, c. *Presbyter* \[Decretum D.32 c.18\]: because the canons call a woman a deaconess not from the order of diaconate, but from a blessing by which it is fitting for her to read homilies at Matins — though not the Gospel at Mass, nor to minister around the altar at Mass as befits a deacon. And the canon calls a woman a presbyteress either as a widow who has custody of the church’s goods, as appears from the following chapter *Si mulieres*, or as the wife of a Greek priest, or as a *hebdomaria* \[a woman who leads weekly prayers\] who recites prayers.

### 5   The second conclusion is

A hermaphrodite ought not to be ordained, being a monster in nature and defective in body, and a disgrace to the clergy, and because of the danger: since we do not know whether such a person is a man or a woman. It must however be one or the other, because in higher beings the masculine and feminine characteristics can be perfectly conjoined — but never in perfect animals, and especially not in human beings. If therefore the male sex prevails, the person is a man and not a woman, and so can be ordained — though ought not to be; but if the contrary, then the person is a woman, and thus cannot be ordained even in fact. Digest, *De statu hominum*, lex *Cum hermaphroditu* \[Digest 1.5.10\].

### 6   The third conclusion is

Age impedes \[ordination\]: because no one ought to be ordained except at the appointed times. Thus childhood age impedes the reception of major orders, because the contrary is a matter of the necessity of precept — but not the reception of minor orders, except as a matter of suitability only. The reason for the first is that a vow of continence is annexed to major orders.
