If however it is manifest, he is suspended also as regards others; and this, says Innocentius, makes him irregular by consequence. For one who is unworthy in the possession \[of an order\] is much more unworthy in the acquisition of it. And one who is unworthy of a lesser dignity is unworthy of a greater. *ff. de senatoribus, l. Qui indignus.* Suspension, however, does not seem to follow except for simony in a benefice or an order. Hence in the *Summa*, in the gloss, it says that not every simony gives rise to irregularity — as, for example, if someone sells holy water or blessed bread.

The second penalty is restitution, which must always be made to the church in whose injury the thing was given or received: for instance, if it concerns a prebend, a parish, or an order, restitution must be made to that church by its bishop or by its judge — unless all members of the church share in the guilt. For then it can and should be distributed to the poor.

The third penalty is the resignation of a benefice obtained through simony, even if obtained in ignorance — as stated in the *Summa*, same title, *Pœna*, verse *Item qui recipit* — except in three cases. The first: if an enemy gave \[the money\] out of malice. The second: if a friend gave it against my will — in which case the recipient is not even a simoniac — *Extra eodem, Nobis*. The third: when \[the simony was\] without an agreement, whether tacit or express — *Extra eodem, c. ult.*

To the argument made in opposition \[in the opening objection\], it must be said that the restitution of usury is a penalty of divine law, which holds the interior act to the same standard as the exterior; but the resignation of a benefice is a penalty of human law, since the Pope, and sometimes even a bishop, can dispense from it. Now human law does not punish the interior act — *ff. de pœnis, l. Cogitationis pœnam nemo meret* \[“No one deserves punishment for mere thought”\]. Hence mental simony, which is an interior sin, does not oblige \[the offender\] as pactional simony does — although there is no purely interior simony when one has proceeded to an external giving, even if not to a formal agreement.

The third conclusion concerns dispensation: because the Pope can dispense in all things and through all means. A bishop can also \[dispense\]: first, in every simony other than that involving orders and benefices, as is said in the *Summa* of Raymund and in the gloss; second, with a simoniac who was ignorant of the simony contracted — provided however that he resign the simple benefice viciously received, and that it then be conferred on him again. But this is otherwise if it is a dignity, a rectory, or a benefice with cure of souls, and not merely a simple benefice without cure, as is also stated in the *Summa*. Third, \[a bishop can dispense\] as regards the reception of orders rightly received beforehand, so that the recipient may minister in them and retain a benefice previously legitimately obtained — since otherwise one is suspended from everything and subject to deposition, as is also said there. Fourth, \[the bishop can dispense\] with the ordainer, the promoter, and the petitioner, \[when acting\] without condition or agreement, as stated there. Fifth, \[the bishop can dispense\] with one entering religious life. Now just as \[the bishop\] can dispense with secular persons, so much the more with regulars subject to him. As for exempt persons, there is doubt, because no one can absolve one whom he cannot condemn; but since what has been introduced in favor of certain persons ought not to be turned to their injury — as stated in *C. de legi., l. Quod pro fauore* — it therefore seems that \[the bishop\] can dispense with them. And indeed it is certain that a bishop can dispense with those who are not exempt by the law itself, but only have by privilege the faculty of exempting themselves and declining his forum by way of exception — as scholars who have the option among three courts \[according to\] *C. ne filius pro patre, authentica habita super hoc* — or such as those who cannot be excommunicated or suspended by the ordinary. But as for those who are exempt by the law itself and subject to him in nothing, it is hard to see how he can absolve, bind, dispense with, or grant indulgences to them.
