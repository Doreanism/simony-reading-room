The reason for the second point is that it is fitting that no grade be conferred on one who cannot perform its act; but the acts of any orders whatsoever, even minor ones, cannot be duly performed except by those possessing the use of reason and discretion. Therefore it is not fitting that anyone be promoted before this time, even to minor orders — unless perhaps necessity be present and there be hope of progress. Nevertheless, boyhood age does not, by the necessity of the sacred rite itself, impede the reception of orders, as it seems to some: because one can hold a power whose use one does not yet have. And therefore, although boys lacking the use of reason cannot exercise orders, they can nevertheless receive the power and in due course perform their acts.

But against this, certain persons argue with some probability and subtlety, if by “little ones” we understand boys who entirely lack the use of reason. First, because it seems quite absurd that such a little one could be made a priest or ordained a bishop. Second, because for the reception of any sacred order a proper intention of receiving is required — either express, if the recipient is an adult, or interpretive, if he is a child; but in a child being ordained to a sacred order there can be no proper will to receive the order, since he lacks the use of reason, nor can there be an interpretive one: because, although the will of parents or of those acting in place of parents can be the interpretive will of children with respect to those things that are of necessity for the children’s bodily or spiritual salvation — for which reason the intention of parents offering them for baptism suffices for the reception of saving baptism — nevertheless the will of parents does not seem to suffice for the reception of the sacred order, which is not of the necessity of salvation but belongs to the works of supererogation and establishes one in a grade of dignity; for in this respect it is not clearly apparent how the will of parents should be interpretive of children, since children may lawfully dissent from such \[arrangements\] once they have reached the age of discretion. And further, because baptism is ordered toward the deletion of original sin, which the child contracts through the act of the parents: it is fitting that the will of the parents should suffice for the child’s receiving baptism; but this reason has no place in the other sacred orders.

But although these arguments are stated with some probability, the common opinion of theologians and canonists is to the contrary. One may therefore say that boys ought not to be ordained \[1 q. 1, c. Pueri\]. If, however, a boy were ordained, it would be valid as regards the character, provided he had the use of reason. But if he lacked the use of reason — for example, because he is a babe in the cradle — in that case, in which he could not be baptized, for instance if his pagan parents were unwilling, he could not be ordained either, just as he could not be baptized: for unless he first received baptism, he would not receive ordination. But supposing the boy were baptized, if the parents were unwilling that the one they wished to have baptized should be ordained, I still do not think he could be ordained: for parents have more right to prevent a boy from those things that are not necessary for salvation than in matters that are necessary. If therefore a boy cannot be baptized against the will of his parents — which is necessary for salvation — much less can he be ordained, which is not necessary. If one should say that this is not analogous because, from the moment he is baptized, he is a subject of the Church, which can do as it pleases with him in spiritual matters and take him from his parents if they wished to raise him in heresy — this does not hold, for this cannot be a just cause for taking him from his parents. But what if they ordain him with the consent of the parents? As regards the episcopate — with respect to the obligation that is created by consent and acceptance, by which one is called the spouse of the Church — it is certain that one is not that spouse until, having the use of reason, one consents; nor is one a parish priest, abbot, prior, or the like: for although by civil law legacies are acquired for one who is unaware of them, yet by canon law no ecclesiastical benefice is acquired for a man until he consents.
