\[Continuing the second conclusion:\] when an anniversary \[Mass\] pertains to the sustenance of life, those who accept it are obligated to perform it, just as a canon is obligated to the canonical hours. 20 Likewise, prayer should not be offered for money but for God and out of genuine devotion. If money is given by contract for the recitation of a psalter, that is express simony. For preaching, one may not receive money, but a preacher may receive his sustenance. 1 Cor. 9. Likewise, it is permitted to preach for the purpose of gathering alms for others and oneself — as Paul \[preached\] for collections to be made for the holy co-apostles and disciples. 1 Cor. 16. But it is not permitted to make the accumulation of money the end of preaching, as do those who count godliness as gain, preaching for no other purpose than to collect money, for which they even invent indulgences and compose many falsehoods in their preaching. Such men ought not to be called preachers but hucksters \[*quaestores*\], against whom the Apostle \[says\]: “I seek not yours but you” \[2 Cor. 12:14\]; “not because I seek the gift, but I seek the fruit” \[Phil. 4:17\]. Likewise, giving money for correction and visitation is simony, as is receiving money from an ecclesiastical judge to conceal a crime or to clear the accused. The same applies if done through intercession or service. Likewise \[simony applies\] to not punishing a crime, or punishing it less \[than it deserves\]; but \[a payment for\] procuration of visitation and for the consecrating bishop is permitted — yet not for one who does not visit, as in the case of exempt persons, because when the office is removed on account of which the \[right to the\] payment is owed, the payment is no longer owed. *Dig.* de adimendis legatis, l. Alune, § Seia \[*Digest* 34.4\]. For it ought to suffice them that they reap temporal things from places that have been spiritually visited, in proportion to the days on which they administer spiritual things. *Extra* de censibus, c. Fœlicis, lib. 6 \[*Sext.* 3.20.1\]. Yet there is a certain one who demands his procuration, saying, “I do not wish to correct you; let the Devil correct you — but I wish to exact my procuration,” arguing that the legal texts which say that one who does not perform the office of visitation should not receive the benefit of procuration speak only of a case where it is the visitor’s own fault. *Extra* de censibus \[et\] procuratione \[*Sext.* 3.20\], against which stands the passage cited, since the Pope in transferring visitation is compared to a testator who transfers the execution \[of a legacy\]. Hence, one to whom a benefit belongs on account of an office, if by his own fault he is deprived of or suspended from the office, is thereby deprived of the benefit — just as in the case of one who by reason of a benefice is bound to holy orders: if by his own fault he becomes irregular, he ought to lose the benefice. But if, after both rights have been acquired by him, he loses the \[exercise of the\] office through no fault of his own, he does not for that reason lose the benefit — just as one who has become irregular through no fault of his own: although he cannot be promoted, he ought not for that reason to be deprived of his benefice. Hence when a diocesan bishop is deprived of the office of visitation through no fault of his own by reason of exemption \[of a monastery\], unless this is expressly provided, he does not seem to be deprived of the benefit of procuration. Nor does the cited passage pose an objection, since it speaks of a case where neither right had yet been acquired: for in that case, when the principal \[right\] is transferred, the accessory seems to be transferred along with it; but it is otherwise when both have already been acquired and one is transferred for a specific reason by means of a comparison to which the other is not accessory — and such is the case here. For it does not follow that if a man is exempt from one thing, he is therefore exempt from another. Concerning admission to religious life, one must say, as Thomas says, that for the necessities of life one may tell a person to bring \[something\] with him if he wishes to live \[in the community\]; but there are other acts for which it is permitted to receive \[payment\] for one’s labor, such as for teaching and other spiritual things \[that proceed\] from the created spirit. As for teaching itself, some say it is not simony, because it relates not to the Holy Spirit but to the human spirit; others say it is. But regarding theology, simony seems evident; regarding the other sciences, it is not \[simony\] for anyone to give or receive even a freely offered \[payment\] for a license to teach in any faculty, nor \[is it simony\] for one’s labor — though for teaching itself it is likewise not permitted — but to receive what is freely offered for one’s labor \[is permitted\], though not to demand it. *Dig.* de variis et extraordinariis \[cognitionibus\], lib. 1 \[*Digest* 50.13\]. Hence it is not permitted to retain books \[as payment\], except perhaps for things freely promised; but one who is obligated by office to teach receives shamefully — except from the wealthy, whom he is not bound to teach without charge. *Extra* de magistris \[*X* 5.5\]. As for the judge, witness, and legally learned advocate, it has already been stated. 21 The third conclusion concerns things annexed to sacred \[objects and acts\]. First, regarding sacred vessels, which may not be sold except in necessity — and then not at a higher price on account of their consecration than they would be sold to clerics, to whom they may be sold intact.
