Our adversaries object, indeed, that we falsely claim the word of God, being the most wicked of its corrupters. Yet how malicious a slander this is — and how remarkable an instance of shamelessness — you yourself, most prudent king, will be able to judge by reading our confession. Something must nonetheless be said here that may either stir you to read it attentively and with care, or at least prepare the way. When Paul wished all prophecy to be formed according to the analogy of faith, he set down the surest standard by which the interpretation of Scripture ought to be tested. If our \[teachings\] are measured against this rule of faith, the victory is in our hands. For what agrees better and more fittingly with faith than to acknowledge ourselves stripped of every virtue, that God may clothe us; empty of all good, that he may fill us; slaves to sin, that he may free us; blind, that he may enlighten us; lame, that he may direct us; weak, that he may sustain us? To strip away from ourselves every occasion of boasting, that he alone may stand forth glorious and we may glory in him? When we say these things and the like, our opponents interrupt and cry out that we are somehow overturning that blind, supposed light of nature, fictitious preparations \[for grace\], free will, and the meritorious works of eternal salvation, together with their works of supererogation — because they cannot bear that the full praise and glory of every good, virtue, righteousness, and wisdom should reside with God \[alone\]. Yet we do not read that those who drew too deeply from the fountain of living water were rebuked; on the contrary, those who dug for themselves broken cisterns that cannot hold water are sternly reproved. What is more consistent with faith than to trust that God is a propitious Father when Christ is acknowledged as brother and propitiator? Than to await all things good and prosperous with confidence from him whose inexpressible love toward us advanced so far that he did not spare his own Son but delivered him up for us? Than to rest in certain expectation of salvation and eternal life when Christ, given by the Father, is contemplated as the one in whom such treasures are hidden? Here they lay hands upon us and cry that such confidence of assurance is not free from arrogance and presumption. But, while we presume nothing of ourselves, we ought to presume all things of God; and it is by no other means that we are stripped of vain glory than by learning to glory in the Lord. What more? Survey, most valiant king, all the particulars of our cause, and judge us more wicked than any class of criminal, unless you clearly discover that we labor and suffer reproach for this reason: that we place our hope in the living God; that we believe this to be eternal life, to know the one true God and Jesus Christ whom he has sent. For this hope, some of us are bound in chains, others beaten with rods, others led about in mockery, others proscribed, others most cruelly tortured, others escape by flight; we are all pressed by want of means, cursed with dreadful imprecations, lacerated with abuse, treated in the most unworthy manner. Look now upon our adversaries — I speak of the order of the priests, by whose nod and pleasure others wage enmity against us — and reckon with me for a moment what zeal drives them. The true religion, handed down in the Scriptures, which ought to be agreed upon by all, they easily allow themselves and others to ignore, neglect, and despise; and they think it matters little what anyone holds or does not hold concerning God and Christ, provided only that they submit their minds to the judgment of the Church by implicit faith, as they call it. They are not greatly troubled if the glory of God happens to be polluted by manifest blasphemies, so long as no one raises a finger against the primacy of the Apostolic See and the authority of holy mother Church.
