# TO THE MOST POWERFUL AND MOST ILLUSTRIOUS MONARCH, Francis, Most Christian King of the French, his prince, John Calvin prays for peace and salvation in Christ.

When I first set my hand to this work, most illustrious King, I had no thought at all of writing something that would afterward be offered to your Majesty. My purpose was only to convey certain rudiments by which those who are touched by any zeal for religion might be formed to true piety. And this labor I was sweating out chiefly for our French countrymen, of whom I perceived very many to be hungering and thirsting for Christ, yet I saw very few who had been rightly imbued even with a modest knowledge of him. The book itself speaks to what I had set before me as my aim — composed, that is, for a simple and unpolished form of teaching. But when I perceived that the fury of certain wicked men had so prevailed in your kingdom that there was no room there for sound doctrine, I was moved to think it worthwhile to use the same work both to give instruction to those people and to make my confession before you, so that you might learn the character of the teaching against which those madmen are inflamed with such great rage — men who today trouble your kingdom with sword and fire. For I shall not be afraid to confess that what I have here set forth is very nearly the sum of that very doctrine which they cry out must be punished with prison, exile, proscription, and fire, and exterminated from land and sea. I am well aware of how dreadful are the accusations with which they have filled your ears and your mind, so as to make our cause most odious to you. But this you must weigh, in accordance with your clemency: that no innocence, whether in words or in deeds, will stand if it is sufficient merely to accuse. Indeed, if someone should allege, for the purpose of exciting hatred, that this doctrine — the account of which I am now endeavoring to render to you — was long ago condemned by the votes of all the estates and has been pierced through with many judicial prejudgments, he will have said nothing more than that it was partly violently overthrown by the faction and power of our adversaries, and partly suppressed by lies, tricks, calumnies, and treacherous fraud. It is an act of violence that bloodthirsty sentences are pronounced against it without hearing of the case; a fraud that it is charged — beyond its desert — with sedition and wickedness. Let no one think that we complain of these wrongs unjustly: you yourself, most noble King, can be our witness of how daily, by lying calumnies, it is misrepresented to you as aiming at nothing other than to wrest the scepters from the hands of kings, to overturn all tribunals and courts, to subvert all orders and civil polities, to disturb the peace and quiet of the people, to annul all laws, to scatter all lordships and possessions, and finally to turn everything upside down. And yet you hear the smallest fraction of it. For certain horrors are spread abroad among the common people which, if they were true, would cause the whole world rightly to judge it worthy of a thousand fires and crosses, along with its authors. Who would now wonder that public hatred has been kindled against it, when faith is given to such utterly false accusations? Behold the reason why all the estates together consent and conspire to condemn us and our doctrine.
