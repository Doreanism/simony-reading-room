Shall we accommodate what was appointed only to confirm the truth to the confirming of lies? It is therefore right that the doctrine, which the Evangelist says must come first, be examined and tested before all else; and if it shall have been approved, then at last it ought to receive its confirmation from miracles. Now the mark of sound doctrine, on the authority of Christ himself, is this: that it tends not to the seeking of men’s glory, but of God’s. Since Christ affirms this as the test of doctrine, miracles are wrongly esteemed when they are drawn in any other direction than to the glorifying of the name of God alone. We should also remember that Satan has his miracles, which, although they are more properly illusions than true works of power, are nevertheless of such a kind as to deceive the unwary and unlearned. Sorcerers and enchanters have always been renowned for miracles. Idolatry has been nourished by stupendous miracles — which nevertheless commend to us neither the superstition of sorcerers nor that of idolaters. And it was with this battering-ram that the Donatists once shook the simplicity of the common people, namely, that they abounded in miracles. We therefore give the same answer to our adversaries now as Augustine once gave to the Donatists: that the Lord has put us on our guard against those workers of wonders, since he foretold that false prophets would come who by lying signs and various wonders would lead astray, if possible, even the elect. And Paul has warned us that the reign of Antichrist would come with all power and signs and lying wonders. “But these miracles,” they say, “are performed not by idols, not by sorcerers, not by false prophets — but by saints.” As if we did not know that this is Satan’s art, to transform himself into an angel of light. The Egyptians long ago buried Jeremiah among themselves and honored him with sacrifices and other divine honors. Were they not abusing a holy prophet of God for the purposes of idolatry? And yet by such veneration of his tomb they obtained what they thought was a just reward of it: a cure from the bite of serpents. What shall we say? Only that this has always been and will always be the most just vengeance of God upon those who have not received the love of the truth: to send upon them a strong delusion, that they should believe a lie. Therefore miracles are by no means lacking to us, and those certain and not open to cavil. But what our adversaries bring forward on their side are mere delusions of Satan, since they draw the people away from the true worship of their God toward vanity.

Moreover, they reproach us deceitfully with the Fathers — meaning the ancient writers of a better age — as if they had them as advocates for their impiety. If the contest were to be decided by their authority, the better part of the victory (to speak with all modesty) would incline to us. But since the Fathers have written many things wisely and excellently, while in some matters they have experienced what is common to all men, these godly sons of theirs, with the cleverness of wit, judgment, and disposition that they possess, adore only their lapses and errors; what is well said they either do not observe, or conceal, or corrupt — so that you would say their entire concern has been to gather dung from gold. Then they overwhelm us with shameless clamors, as though we were despisers and enemies of the Fathers. We, however, so far from despising them, could easily — were that my present purpose — confirm the better part of what we today maintain by the testimony of the Fathers themselves.

Yet we engage with their writings in such a way that we always bear in mind: that all things are ours, to serve us and not to rule over us; but that we ourselves belong to Christ alone, to whom in all things, without exception, obedience is due. Whoever does not hold fast to this dear Christ will have nothing settled in religion, for those holy men were ignorant of many things; they often conflict with one another, and sometimes even contradict themselves.
