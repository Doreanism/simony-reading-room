— but those who stubbornly reject the truth offered by God’s grace have nothing to offer in their defense. Their dilemma does not press us so hard as to compel us to confess either that the Church was at some point dead, or that we are now in conflict with the Church. To be sure, the Church of Christ has lived and will live as long as Christ reigns at the right hand of the Father — sustained by his hand, defended by his protection, preserved in safety by his power. For he will without doubt fulfill what he once promised: that he will be present with his own until the consummation of the age. We wage no warfare against the Church: for we worship and adore one God and Christ the Lord with one consent together with all the people of the faithful — as he has always been worshiped by all the godly. But our opponents stray not a little from the truth when they refuse to acknowledge a Church unless they can see it with their present eye, and when they try to confine it within boundaries within which it is by no means enclosed. On these hinges our controversy turns: first, they contend that the Church’s form must always be visible and manifest; second, they locate that very form in the see of the Roman Church and the order of its prelates. We on the contrary assert both that the Church can exist without any visible form, and that the form is not contained in that external splendor which they foolishly admire, but in a far different mark — namely, the pure preaching of God’s word and the lawful administration of the sacraments. They clamor unless the Church can always be pointed out with the finger. But how often did it happen among the Jewish people that it was so deformed that no visible appearance was prominent? What form do we suppose shone forth when Elijah lamented that he alone was left? How long after the coming of Christ did it lie hidden without form? How many times since then has it been so oppressed by wars, seditions, and heresies that it shone forth in no part? Had they lived at that time, would they have believed the Church to be alive? But Elijah heard that seven thousand men were preserved who had not bowed the knee before Baal. Nor need we doubt that Christ has always reigned on earth since he ascended into heaven. But if at those times the godly had sought any notable visible form, would they not instantly have despaired? And indeed Hilarius in his own age already counted it as the height of wickedness that, preoccupied with a foolish admiration for episcopal dignity, people did not perceive the deadly plague lurking beneath that mask. For thus he speaks: “This one thing I warn: beware of Antichrist; for the love of walls has wrongly taken hold of you; you wrongly venerate the Church of God in rooftops and buildings; you wrongly bring in the name of peace beneath them. Is it doubtful that Antichrist will sit in these? I have mountains for myself, and forests, and lakes, and dungeons, and chasms, which are safer: for in these the Prophets, abiding in them or cast into them, prophesied.” But what does the world today venerate in its horned bishops, unless because it supposes those to be holy rulers of religion whom it sees presiding over famous cities? Dismiss, then, so foolish an opinion. Rather let us leave this to the Lord — that since he alone knows who are his, he sometimes also withdraws the outward appearance of his Church from human sight. This, I confess, is a terrible judgment of God upon the earth: but if human impiety deserves it, why do we strive to resist God’s just punishment? Thus in past ages the Lord took vengeance on human ingratitude: for because they had refused to obey his truth and had extinguished his light, he suffered them to be blinded in understanding, to be mocked by absurd lies, and to be plunged in deep darkness — so that no face of the true Church remained visible. Yet in the meantime he preserved his own, scattered and hiding, in the midst of errors and darkness, from destruction. Nor is this surprising: for he has shown \[he knows\] how to save even in the confusion of Babylon and the flame of the burning furnace. Now as for their wish that the form of the Church be judged by some vain pomp, I will indicate rather than fully narrate — lest I extend my discourse immeasurably — how dangerous this is.
