Why, unless it is because for them God is their belly and religion their kitchen, without which they believe they would be not merely un-Christian but not even human? For although some gorge themselves splendidly and others subsist on thin scraps, they all live from the same pot, which without such fuel would not merely grow cold but would freeze entirely solid. Therefore, as each of them is most anxious for his belly, so he is found the most ardent champion of his faith. In the end they all bend their efforts to this single aim: either to keep their kingdom intact or their belly full; not one gives the least sign of genuine zeal. And yet they do not cease to assail our doctrine and to accuse and defame it by every name they can, to make it either odious or suspect. They call it new, and newly born, and cavil that it is doubtful and uncertain; they ask what miracles confirm it; they inquire whether it is reasonable that it should prevail against the consensus of so many holy Fathers and the most ancient custom; they press us to admit that it is schismatic, waging war against the Church — or else that the Church had been dead for many centuries, during which nothing of the sort was heard. Finally, they say there is no need of many arguments: the character of the doctrine can be judged by its fruits, seeing that it has produced such a heap of sects, such tumults of seditions, such license of vices. It is of course quite easy for them to insult an undefended cause before a credulous and ignorant multitude; but if we had equal turns at speaking, this bitterness with which they foam against us so loudly and as freely as with impunity would certainly subside. First, in calling it new, they do great wrong to God, whose holy word did not deserve to be accused of novelty. That it is new to them I have no doubt at all, since Christ himself is new to them and the Gospel is new; but those who know that old proclamation of Paul — that Jesus Christ died for our sins and rose for our justification — will find nothing new among us. That it lay hidden and buried for a long time is the fault of human impiety; now that through God’s kindness it is restored to us, it ought at least to recover its antiquity by right of postliminium. From the same source of ignorance they regard it as doubtful and uncertain: this is surely what the Lord complains of through his Prophet — that the ox knows its owner and the donkey its master’s crib, while he is not acknowledged by his own people. Yet, to mock the truth as uncertain — if they were required to seal their claim with their own blood and at the cost of their lives, one might see what store they set by it. Our confidence is far otherwise: it fears neither the terrors of death nor the very tribunal of God. That they demand miracles of us is a wicked demand; for we are not forging some new Gospel, but are holding fast to that very Gospel whose truth all the miracles that Christ and the Apostles ever performed were meant to confirm. Yet they claim this singular advantage over us: that they are able to confirm their faith with miracles unceasing to this very day. Rather, they produce miracles that might unsettle a mind otherwise well composed — so frivolous and ridiculous are they, or so vain and false. And yet, even if they were most prodigious, they ought to carry no weight against the truth of God, since it is fitting that the name of God be hallowed everywhere and always, whether by portents or by the natural order of things. The imposture might perhaps have appeared more plausible, were it not that Scripture warns us concerning the lawful purpose and object of miracles. For Mark teaches that the signs that followed the preaching of the Apostles were performed to confirm it; and likewise Luke relates that the Lord bore witness to the word of his grace when signs and wonders were done by the hands of the Apostles.
