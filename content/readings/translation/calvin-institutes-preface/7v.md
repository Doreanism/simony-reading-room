So it was for several centuries, when everything was submerged in deep darkness: nearly all mortals were sport and jest to that lord of this world, and he sat at ease and took his pleasure like some Sardanapalus in profound peace. For what else would he have done but laugh and play, in tranquil and undisturbed possession of his kingdom? But when the light shining down from above had somewhat dispersed his darkness, when that Strong One \[cf. Luke 11:22\] had troubled and overthrown his kingdom, then indeed he began to shake off his accustomed torpor and to take up arms. And first he stirred up the hands of men to violently suppress the truth as it was dawning; when through these means nothing was accomplished, he turned to ambushes — he stirred up dissensions and doctrinal controversies through his Anabaptists and other monstrous riff-raff, by which he might obscure it, and at last extinguish it. And now he persists in assailing it with both engines at once: for he strives to uproot that true seed by the force and violence of men, and labors with his tares (as far as in him lies) to overshadow it, so that it may not grow and bear fruit. But this is all in vain, if we heed the Lord’s warning, who has long since disclosed \[Satan’s\] arts to us lest he find us unprepared, and has armed us with defenses strong enough against all his stratagems. Moreover, what great malice is it to cast odium upon God’s very word because of the seditions which the wicked and rebellious stir up, or the holy \[contentions\] which impostors stir up against it? Yet the example is not new. Elijah was asked whether he was not the one who was troubling Israel. Christ was \[deemed\] seditious among the Jews. The charge of popular insurrection was laid against the Apostles. What else do those do today who impute to us all the disturbances, tumults, and contentions that boil up against us? But what answer should be made to such persons, Elijah has taught us: that we are not the ones who scatter errors or stir up tumults, but those very ones who struggle against the power of God. But while that one response is sufficient to blunt their rashness, I must also address the weakness of others, who are not infrequently shaken by such stumbling-blocks and waver in their perplexity. That they may not give way under this disturbance and be thrown off their footing, let them know that the Apostles in their own age experienced the same things that now befall us. There were the unlearned and unstable who, to their own destruction, perverted what Paul had written by divine inspiration, as Peter says. There were despisers of God who, when they heard that sin had abounded that grace might more abound, immediately objected: “Shall we continue in sin that grace may abound?” When they heard that the faithful are not under the law, they at once retorted: “We shall sin, because we are not under the law but under grace.” There were those who accused him of being an instigator of evil. Many false apostles crept in to tear down the churches he had built. Some preached the Gospel insincerely — even maliciously — out of envy and contention, thinking to add affliction to his chains. In some places there was not much progress of the Gospel. All sought their own things, not the things of Jesus Christ. Some went back, the dog to his vomit and the sow to the wallowing-place of mud. Many seized the liberty of the Spirit as a license for the flesh. Many crept in as brothers, from whom dangers afterward threatened the godly. Among the brethren themselves various contentions were stirred up. What was the Apostles’ course of action here? Should they not have dissembled for a time — or rather abandoned and deserted that Gospel which they saw to be the seedbed of so many quarrels, the material of so many dangers, the occasion of so many scandals? But in such straits this came to their aid: that Christ is a stone of stumbling and a rock of offense, set for the fall and rising again of many, and for a sign that shall be spoken against \[cf. Luke 2:34; 1 Peter 2:8\]. Armed with this confidence they pressed boldly forward through all the hazards of tumults and offenses.
