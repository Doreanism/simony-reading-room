They have transgressed the boundary by severely imposing celibacy upon their priests. There was a Father who held that Christ alone must be heard, of whom it was said, “Hear him”; and that one must not look to what others before us have said or done, but to what Christ — who is first of all — has commanded. This boundary they neither set for themselves nor allow others to hold, inasmuch as they prefer to set up their own masters rather than Christ — for themselves and for others. There was a Father who contended that the Church ought not to set itself above Christ, since he always judges truthfully, while ecclesiastical judges, being men, frequently err. Breaking through this boundary as well, they do not hesitate to assert that the entire authority of Scripture depends on the Church’s judgment. All the Fathers with one heart execrated and with one voice detested the contamination of God’s holy word by the quibbles of sophists and its entanglement in the wranglings of dialecticians. But do these men keep themselves within those limits, when all their lifelong effort is nothing but to involve and obstruct the simplicity of Scripture with endless controversies and more than sophistical squabbles? — so that if the Fathers were now raised up and heard the art of disputation which these men call “speculative theology,” they would be the last to believe that a discussion was being held about God. But our discourse would spread far beyond due bounds if I wished to recount how insolently these men shake off the yoke of the Fathers, whose dutiful sons they wish to appear. Months and years would fail me. And their shamelessness is so irretrievable and desperate that they dare to censure us for not hesitating to transgress the ancient boundaries. As for their appeal to custom, it accomplishes nothing. It would indeed be most unjust to us if we were required to yield to custom. Certainly, if human judgments were sound, custom ought to be sought from the good. But it commonly falls out far otherwise: for what is observed to be done by many quickly obtains the force of custom. Yet human affairs have rarely been so well ordered that the better things pleased the majority. Therefore from the private vices of many, a public error has commonly arisen — or rather a common consensus of vices — which these good men now wish to have the force of law. Those who have eyes can see that not one sea of evils has flooded in, that many spreading plagues have invaded the world, that everything rushes headlong — so that either human affairs are utterly to be lamented, or a hand must be laid upon such great evils, or rather force brought to bear. And the remedy is driven away for no other reason than that we have long since grown accustomed to the evils. But let public error hold its place in human society; in the kingdom of God, however, only his eternal truth is to be heard and regarded — a truth to which no succession of years, no custom, no conspiracy can prescribe \[a statute of limitations\]. So formerly Isaiah was teaching God’s elect not to say “Conspiracy” concerning all the things of which the people said “Conspiracy” — that is, not to conspire themselves in the people’s wicked consent, nor to fear and dread their fear — but rather to sanctify the Lord of hosts, and to let him be their fear and their dread. Now therefore, however they may cast at us the examples of past ages and the present, if we have sanctified the Lord of hosts, we shall not be greatly terrified. For whether many centuries have agreed in a like impiety, he is mighty who brings punishment to the third and fourth generation; or whether the whole world at once conspires in the same wickedness, he has shown by experience what the end is of those who sin with the multitude — when he destroyed the entire human race with the flood, preserving Noah with his small household, who by his faith alone would condemn the whole world. Finally, a corrupt custom is like a common pestilence, in which those who fall in the crowd perish no less \[than others\].
