Seized by this disposition, those who sit in judgment pronounce as verdicts the prejudices they brought from home, and think they have properly discharged their duties if no one is led to punishment unless convicted either by his own confession or by solid testimony. But convicted of what crime? “Condemn him!” — they say — “for this doctrine.” But by what right has it been condemned? Yet this was the chief defense: not to deny the doctrine itself, but to defend it as true. Here even the ability to murmur is cut off. Therefore, most unconquered King, I am not asking on behalf of the Gospel anything unjust — only that you take up a full cognizance of this cause, which has until now been handled in a confused manner, with no legal order, and by insolent cunning rather than by lawful gravity, or in whatever way tossed about and harassed. Do not think that I am here planning a private defense by which I might fabricate for myself a false return to my homeland — which, though I pursue it with the affection of humanity that is appropriate, yet in the present state of affairs I do not miss with great distress. Rather, I embrace the common cause of all the pious, and indeed the cause of Christ himself, which in every way lies today in your kingdom — torn apart and trampled down, left as if in despair — by the tyranny, that is, of certain Pharisees more than by your own conscience. But what brings this about, there is no point in saying here; certainly it lies prostrate and afflicted. For the impious have accomplished this much: that the truth of Christ, if not perishing as driven out and scattered, yet lurks as buried and obscure; while the poor little Church is either consumed by cruel massacres, or driven away by exiles, or struck down by threats and terrors, so that it does not dare even to open its mouth. And still they press on with their accustomed madness and ferocity, pushing hard against a wall already leaning, and pressing upon the ruin they have made. No one meanwhile comes forward to oppose such furies with his advocacy. And if any seem to wish to appear most favorable to truth, they think that the error and imprudence of ignorant men must be excused. For in such terms do temperate men speak — calling “error and imprudence” what they know with certainty to be the truth of God; calling “ignorant men” those whose intellect they see was not so contemptible to Christ but that he deigned to honor them with the mysteries of his heavenly wisdom. So much are all ashamed of the Gospel. But it will be for you, most serene King, not to turn aside your ears or your mind from so just a defense — especially where the matter at stake is so great: namely, how the glory of God may remain safe and unharmed on earth, how the truth of God may retain its dignity, how the kingdom of Christ may remain whole and intact among us. These things are worthy of your ears, worthy of your knowledge, worthy of your tribunal. For this very consideration also makes a true king: to recognize that in the administration of his kingdom he is the minister of God. Nor does he any longer exercise a kingdom but a robbery who does not reign to this end — to serve the glory of God. Moreover, he is deceived who expects lasting prosperity for that kingdom which is not governed by the scepter of God — that is, by his holy Word — since the heavenly oracle cannot fail, by which it has been decreed that the people will be scattered where prophecy has ceased. Nor should contempt for our lowliness draw you away from this concern. We are indeed most fully conscious within ourselves of how poor and abject little men we are: before God, certainly, wretched sinners; in the sight of men, the most despised; the filth of the world (if you wish) and its off-scouring, or if anything still more vile can be named — so that nothing remains for us to glory in before God except his mercy alone, by which we have been admitted, through no merit of our own, to the hope of eternal salvation; and before men not very much, except our weakness, which it is the greatest ignominy toward them even to acknowledge with a nod. But our doctrine must stand exalted above all the glory of the world, invincible above all power; because it is not ours, but that of the living God, and of his Christ, whom the Father has established as King to have dominion from sea to sea and from the rivers to the ends of the earth. And to have dominion in such a way that he might shatter the whole earth, with its iron and bronze strength and its golden and silver splendor, with the rod of his mouth alone —
