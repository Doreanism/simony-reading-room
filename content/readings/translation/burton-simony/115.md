Or Judas, for selling both his soul and his Savior for thirty pence? Or Gehazi, for selling his master’s gift for two talents? Or Ananias and Sapphira, for withholding what was vowed and consecrated? Now God is the same jealous God still that he was from the beginning. And the Apostle says concerning sacrilegious persons: *Be not deceived; God is not mocked; for whatsoever a man sows, the same shall he also reap.* But why all this? First, this concerns all patrons to look to it — such as account it no less lawful to sell a benefice, or to usurp by force or cunning either the whole or a part for their own proper yet improper use, than to sell their horse or house.

Now to all patrons is my speech chiefly addressed. The benefices in your patronage are but a pledge committed to you in trust, and it is no small trust — the souls of God’s people are engaged in it. Your first and chief care should be to make choice of a worthy minister; next, to collate it upon him freely, without either exacting or expecting of him anything more or less. Much less should you set a price upon it, or make a public sale of it to the one who will give most; or reserve your own tithes, or the glebe, or some such portion to maintain your household with grain for bread, or grain for drink, or some other commodities. The eagle snatching the sacrifice from the altar carried with it a coal that clung to it, which proved enough to set her nest and herself on fire. O look to it, if ever you look for God’s blessing upon you and your posterity. Gehazi’s curse was fearful — it clung also to his posterity. How many such merchants have been stripped of all, if we would but observe the examples? Nor is it sufficient that you shake your own hands of simony; see also that your family, your wife, your son, your daughter, or your servant, be free from it.
