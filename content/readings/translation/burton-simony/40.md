But all ambitious soliciting is judged and condemned as simoniacal by the ancient learned divines. Devout Bernard gives this advice to Eugenius: *Pro quo rogaris, sit suspectus; qui ipse rogat pro se, iam indicatus est. Clericum curiam frequentantem, qui non sit de Curia, ad idem noris pertinere ambientium genus. Adulantem et ad placitum cuiusque loquentem, unum de rogantibus puta, etiamsi nihil rogaverit. Non volentes, neque currentes assumito, sed cunctantes et renuentes. Isti sunt tui socii et collaterales. Ne te dixeris sanum, dolentem latera; ne te dixeris bonum, malis innitentem* — Let the man on whose behalf you are petitioned be held suspect; he who himself petitions on his own behalf is already judged. A cleric who haunts the court, not being of the court, you should know belongs to the same class of the ambitious. One who flatters and speaks to please everyone, reckon as one of the petitioners, even if he has petitioned for nothing. Take up those who are unwilling and do not run forward, but those who hesitate and refuse. These are your companions and associates. Do not call yourself well when your sides ache; do not call yourself good when you lean upon evils. Gregory thus: *Sicut is qui invitatus renuit, quesitus refugit, sacris est altaribus admouendus: sic qui ultro ambit, vel importune se ingerit, est procul dubio repellendus*, etc. — Just as he who when invited refuses, when sought out withdraws, is to be brought to the holy altars: so he who courts office of his own accord, or thrusts himself in importunately, is without doubt to be repelled. *Nemo sibi honorem sumit*, etc. — By this judgment are struck those who desire to seize ecclesiastical honors to which they are not divinely called. For he who thrusts himself forward and seeks his own glory does not receive honor when made a prelate: but making a robbery of the grace of God, he usurps another’s right. And therefore he does not receive a blessing but a curse. But he who is rightly and canonically called is called by God. Korah wished to take honor upon himself, and therefore he feels the divine sentence. I conclude this with that of Chrysostom:

What does it matter if you give no outright money, but instead flatter, suborn, and make a stir? “Your money perish with you” was said to Simon; and to these it shall be said, “Your ambition perish with you, because you have thought the gift of God may be obtained by man’s ambition.” It was one of the heavy curses upon Eli’s posterity that they should crouch and say, “Put me, I pray, into one of the priests’ offices, that I may eat a piece of bread.” 1 Samuel 2:36.
