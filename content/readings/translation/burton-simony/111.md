The Church of Rome being desperately and deadly sick with infinite enormous diseases, especially in her clergy — in whom that of simony might claim precedence — this Council, pretending but never intending (as the sequel proves) a reformation, after much ado and long debate about the cure of so inveterate and epidemic a disease, at length the Romish doctors, having beaten their brains about it, set down their prescription as an antidote against simony. At length, I say, after great travail and posting back and forth to Rome, this Catholic Mother brings forth a fair and well-featured child to look upon, which, had it not proved abortive at birth, might have lived to work wonders in the conjuring down of the spirit of simony which Simon the Sorcerer first conjured up. Wherefore, finding this infant lying stretched out dead and exposed in the eighteenth chapter of the twenty-fourth session of that Council, let me — not with Gehazi’s staff, but with Elisha’s spirit — try if any life may be fetched again out of it. Thus it lies: *Expedit maxime animarum saluti a dignis atque idoneis Parochis gubernari: id ut diligentius, ac rectius perficiatur, statuit Sancta Synodus, &c.* — (Lo, what a fair and well-favored face is here:) It is most behooving for the salvation of souls to be governed by worthy and fit pastors: which, that it may the more diligently and duly be effected, the holy Synod decrees that upon the vacation of any benefice, the bishop should presently, upon notice of it, take order for a fit incumbent rector; for which purpose the bishop and the patron, within ten days or such a time as limited by the bishop, shall nominate some fit clerks to govern the church before the examiners who are to be deputed and appointed. Yea, let it also be free for others who know any to be fit for that function to bring in their names, that a diligent inquiry may thereupon be made of every one’s age, manners, and sufficiency.
