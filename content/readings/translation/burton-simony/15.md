For as well the freewill offerings and oblations as the tithes were called holy. “If a man dedicate to the Lord any ground of his inheritance, the field shall be holy to the Lord when it goes out in the Jubilee, as a field set apart from common uses; the possession thereof shall be the priest’s.” Such glebe lands therefore, and lands commonly called temporalities, are in truth not temporal, having once been freely and solemnly given by vow of our noble predecessors unto the Lord for the more honorable maintenance of his Church; but they are now spiritual, sanctified and set apart from common use, as tithes are — they are holy to the Lord. Therefore, as it is a snare to devour that which is thus sanctified and after such vows to make inquiry \[about reclaiming it\]: so the simoniac only entangles himself in a snare, as the rabbit in her own burrow, when he makes this his loophole — that so long as he buys only the temporal lands of the Church he commits no simony. *Sed cominus res agatur. Simoniace, quid emisti? Glebam inquis, aut (te arbitro) Temporalia. Quanti emisti? Tantis. Agedum. Permittatur iam arbitrio calculorum, quantum inde sis lucratus, et num te negotiatorem satis prudentem cautumque præstiteris. Annui gleba tua reditus æstimantur forte 20 li. aut supra; ducentis vero, aut trecentis, aut quadringentis libris emisti. Na tu, hæreditatem tibi ac posteris, tantidem emere potuisses. Summa igitur, si computetur, huc recidit, quod hoc tuo mercimonio teipsum prodideris aut puerilem mercatorem, aut (quod magis suspicor) egregium veteratorem. Excute vero tuam tanta causa nimium indormientem conscientiam, et fatebere tandem, non te glebam tanti, nisi decimarum maior fuisset ratio, emisse. Vel sicut siquis domicilium charius emit, bonæ viciniæ gratia. Eadem prorsus ratio latifundiorum Episcopalium, vulgo Temporalium, habenda est. Emit quis, non Episcopatum, sed Temporalia, non quod sacrum est, sed quod seculare. Ista autem Temporalia partim ex latifundii utilitate, partim ex domini dignitate æstimantur. Quanti autem constet* \[But let the matter be brought closer to home. Simoniac, what did you buy? The glebe, you say, or — as you judge it — the temporalities. How much did you pay? Such and such. Come now. Let the arithmetic be left to reckon how much profit you have made from it, and whether you have shown yourself a sufficiently prudent and careful merchant. The annual revenues of your glebe are reckoned perhaps at £20 or more; yet you paid two hundred, or three hundred, or four hundred pounds for it. Why, you could have bought an inheritance for yourself and your heirs for the same sum. The sum total, then, if it be reckoned up, comes to this: that by this your bargaining you have shown yourself either a childish merchant, or — which I more suspect — a thoroughgoing swindler. Rouse then your conscience, which sleeps far too soundly in a matter of such weight, and you will confess at last that you would not have paid so much for the glebe, had not the tithes been the greater part of the calculation. It is just as if someone buys a house at a higher price for the sake of a good neighborhood. The same reasoning applies entirely to episcopal estates, commonly called temporalities. A man buys not the bishopric but the temporalities — not what is sacred, but what is secular.
