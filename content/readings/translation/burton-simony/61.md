For otherwise, by following such examples, you and those like you become examples to draw others after you, and so each for his part both maintains and increases that wicked trade, each striving who can run fastest to the Devil. Another says he buys of necessity, so that he may have a place in the Church to exercise those gifts God has bestowed upon him for the good of his Church. And if he does not buy, those gifts will lie buried in the earth. That would be a great pity. But what gifts? I doubt whether any sanctified and true priestly gifts would so easily be corrupted by simony. But as the king of Gath said to David, who was counterfeiting madness: “Have we need of madmen?” — so, have we need of simoniacs in the Church? Or as Philip king of Macedon, seeing his son Alexander by lavish gifts wooing the goodwill of the Macedonians, wrote to him in a letter: *Qua te malum ratio in istam spem induxit, ut eos tibi fideles putares fore, quos pecunia corrupisses? An id agis, ut Macedones non te Regem suum, sed Ministrum & præbitorem putent, quo quid Sordidius Regi?* \[What evil reasoning led you into this hope, that you thought those men would prove faithful to you whom you had corrupted with money? Is your aim that the Macedonians should regard you not as their king, but as a servant and paymaster — than which what could be more base in a king?\] — so we may turn it upon our simoniac thus: *Qua te malum ratio in istam spem induxit, ut populo te fidelem putares fore Pastorem, cum tuam ipse conscientiam pecunia tua corrupisses? An id agis, ut populus Dei non te Pastorem suum, sed Depastorem, non Concionatorem sed Corruptorem putent, quo quid Sordidius Rectori?* \[What evil reasoning led you into this hope, that you thought yourself a faithful pastor to the people, when you had corrupted your own conscience with your money? Is your aim that the people of God should regard you not as their pastor but as a despoiler, not as a preacher but as a corrupter — than which what could be more base in a rector?\] But if you have such a desire to exercise your gifts, *audiamus disertum* \[let us hear the eloquent man\]: bestow them where they are most needed, where the people perish for want of preaching, and preaching fails there because the tithes are impropriated. *Sed nullus ad amissas, ibit Amicus opes* \[But no friend will come to one whose wealth is gone\]. But you will say: *Dignus est operarius Mercede sua* — the laborer is worthy of his wages.
