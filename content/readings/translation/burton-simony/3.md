*ra alieni non proueniat: ita quia per electionem peruenitur ad consecrationem, perinde simoniacus habetur, qui pro Electione premia largitur, ac si pro consecratione munera dedisset* — But (says he) as buyers of ecclesiastical things are judged simoniacal, because those things are so annexed to ecclesiastical duties that one cannot have the one without the other: so because by election a man comes to his consecration, he is accounted no less a simoniac who bestows gifts for the election than if he had given his money for the consecration.

Now if the former definitions seem too broad — especially to those who would gladly pack up all simony in the same bundle as the gifts of the Holy Ghost, as commodities of one and the same kind — to gratify these, we might reduce everything to the same terms and say: simony is a desire or act of buying or selling the gifts of the Holy Ghost. So Master Calvin (on Acts 8) approves this as the most proper definition of simony. The only question is, what kinds of gifts of the Holy Ghost may be implied here, the trafficking and trading in which either is, or may well be called, either simony or at least a branch of it. Lyra (on Acts 8) says: *Simon volebat emere potestatem dandi Spiritum Sanctum, intentione Lucri inde recipiendi: propter quod ab ipso nominatur vitium Simonia, quo aliquis vendit aut emit Spiritualia* — Simon wished to buy the power of conferring the Holy Ghost, with the intention of gaining thereby; as Saint Augustine says: *Quod venderet, emere volebat* — he wished to buy what he intended to sell again.

For which reason, from him is named the sin of simony, when any man buys or sells spiritual things.
