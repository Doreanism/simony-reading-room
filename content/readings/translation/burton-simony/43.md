By this device the patron holds the incumbent in a miserable servitude, as his tenant at will; while in the meantime the patron may lord it as he pleases without restraint, for the incumbent is bound not to reprove anything his good master does or says, but must say amen to everything — or else farewell to everything. The patron has also another motive in this: that, lacking for the present a good buyer, he puts in his bondman to gain time and a better thing, until he is provided to his liking.

All non-preaching ministers who possess any good benefice are, by strong presumption, deep simoniacs. For let them tell me for what qualities or good gifts they were so preferred — unless it be that they and all their good gifts shook hands and parted the very moment they and their fat benefice met.

Some commit simony by mental reservation, when both the patron expects to receive and the presentee purposes to give some gratuity afterward, without any express promise or prior contract, relying only on trust — so that he may save his oath. This is a mock-simony and a mock-oath. For is this not Gehazi’s sin? Of which the Gloss says: *Hoc exemplum est argumentum contra eos, qui non exigunt, seu accipiunt, ante Collationem Beneficii* — this example is an argument against those who neither exact nor receive before the collation of a benefice. Gehazi asked but a gratuity after the healing — or call it his fee, for servants must live. But he had his gratuity with a vengeance: “Is this a time to receive silver and gold? The leprosy therefore of Naaman shall cleave to you, and to your seed forever.” Away with such gratuity. Indeed, if a benefice were a temporal benefit, it might call for a temporal recompense; but being of a spiritual nature, what gratuity or thankfulness should the patron expect, or the incumbent perform, but a painful and faithful discharge of that cure of souls committed to his trust?
