But \[the objection is\] that in the Old Testament it was customary to sell \[such gifts\]. Answer: We never read that the good prophets accepted any reward from those who brought it. Elisha flatly refused any share of the riches that Naaman would have presented to him. It was enough for Balaam and such wicked prophets to prophesy for hire, as the Lord complains of them, saying, “The prophets divine for money” — but we do not read that the good prophets received any such rewards. Indeed, far from receiving anything, Samuel feasted Saul and his company. *Sed fac eum accepisse* — say he did receive something. What was it? The fourth part of a shekel, a matter of twenty halfpennies; so the reward Saul brought to Samuel came to only five halfpennies. Yet even if Samuel did receive this at all, *Supes magis sunt estimande tabernaculi, quam munera Prophetae* — these are rather to be reckoned as maintenance for the Tabernacle than as rewards for the Prophet. *Et hec magis in sumptum Prophetae, quam muneri Prophetiae reputanda sunt: licet potuerunt hi, qui ariolos, &c.* — And these are to be counted more as provision for the Prophet’s expenses than as payment for prophecy; although those who were accustomed to go to soothsayers, and so on, might out of an evil habit suppose the prophets would do the same, namely, accept gifts. But Gratian concludes upon that fourth part of a shekel — containing five halfpennies — which Saul brought to Samuel: *Nostri ergo Sacerdotes si volunt vendere prophetiam, & columbam in Cathedris suis proponere, quas Dominus flagello subvertit: quinque tantum obolorum sint mercede contenti: qui utinam non pretium villarum, sed quinque obolos acciperent* — Therefore our priests (he says), if they will sell the gift of prophecy and set the Dove \[that is, the Holy Ghost\] for sale in their cathedrals or seats which the Lord overthrew with his whip, let them be content with the reward of five halfpennies only; would that they would not receive the worth of whole villages, but only five halfpennies. In a word, Gregory, speaking against simoniacal ordination reigning in France — our nearest neighbor — and exhorting Theodoric and Theobert, then reigning there, to convene a Synod for the reformation of such simoniacal heresy and corruption, writes: *In Sacerdotibus fides sit eligenda, cum vita.
