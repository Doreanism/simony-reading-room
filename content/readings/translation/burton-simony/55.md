*de regni vestri studeatis finibus prohibere, & nulla apud vos excusatio, nulla contra animam vestram suggestio locum inveniat: quia facientis proculdubio culpam habet, qui quod potest corrigere, negligit emendare.* — Wherefore (he says), greeting you with a fatherly affection, we pray you, most excellent sons, that you endeavor to banish this detestable evil from the borders of your kingdom, and let no excuse or pretext, let no suggestion, find place with you against your own soul; for he is without doubt guilty of the deed itself who, having the power to correct it, neglects to reform it.

Well, to conclude this point: whether this *morbus Gallicus* \[the French disease, i.e., simony\] was so purged and banished from the coasts of France that no remnants remain, I cannot say. But if it was, I pray God that, given the closeness of our neighborhood, it has not sailed over — as so many other French fashions have done — and set foot on Albion’s shore to spread the infection there. I trust that the Cinque Ports are so well fortified that they will not suffer such base wares to be entered at the Customs-house and so pass freely on sale here — where religion is purely planted, faith truly professed, the practice of piety so frequently professed, and well-charged Canons \[punning on both church canons and cannon artillery\] mounted. The Cinque Ports are the five senses, which religion so guards that no such simoniacal corruption can enter at any one of them. Would it enter at the port of sight, the Eye? That has learned to turn away from such vanity, or not to look with delight, or not to be dazzled — much less blinded — by such glittering objects, or to be enamored with the beauty of fair white-and-red earth. Or at the port of touch, the Hand? That has learned — not in superstition, “Touch not,” but in sincerity, “Touch no unclean thing” — yea, though a gift should leap up and fasten itself upon the hand, yet to shake it off into the fire as Paul did the viper, without any hurt or swelling whatsoever. Or at the port of hearing, the Ear? A religious ear does not love *male audire* \[to hear ill things\], so as to be charmed by any such silver-sounding music, however complete it may be in time, number, and measure.
