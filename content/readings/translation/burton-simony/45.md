Note here the tenderness of this Archbishop’s conscience: his brother gave but a horse, unknown to him, with no prior compact, and that after the election and in the Cardinal’s need at the time. And yet I do not read that this horse was of any extraordinary price — not worth five hundred or a thousand pounds, as patrons’ horses are estimated elsewhere.

Not very unlike this is a kind of simony not infrequent in the world, yet so refined that it disclaims and disdains all kinship with simony. A man has fairly and incorruptly (at least so far as any monetary transaction is concerned) obtained from the patron a free grant of some ecclesiastical preferment. But after the grant, before it passes the seals, the suitor must run the gauntlet of great difficulty — yes, and danger too — lest through some neglect or delay, some other person get between him and his goal. Like a spider that has entangled a sturdy fly in its subtle net and bestirs its nimble joints to secure the weakest places where there is any danger of escape. But the scholar, being of an ingenuous disposition — one who cannot dance attendance at every door, who blushes to appear an importunate suitor, and that in such places where all scholars, being not in ordinary attendance, are suspected — in order to cut off all impatience of delay and fear of dangers, chooses rather *Redimere vexationem suam quàm queat minimo; si nequeat paululo, at quantuli queat* \[to buy off his trouble as cheaply as he can; if not for a trifle, then for as little as possible\]. Say it be so, etc. This case was put to me by a learned man, to know what I thought of it. I told him frankly that for my part I could be content to redeem the credit of a minister’s modesty in such attendance with any reasonable ransom. But to give such a substantial sum — not only to purchase relief from impatience, but to forestall the peril of losing the preferment for want of provident, indeed importunate, pursuit — was in my judgment like the *secundine*, or afterbirth, of simony.
