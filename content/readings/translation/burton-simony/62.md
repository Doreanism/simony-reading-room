Another objects: I see the Church of God in great danger; the chief places are intruded into by those who will make no conscience of their duty, as is plain from those lesser charges and positions in which they have been most negligent and unfaithful; and so the ship of the Church shall be in danger of shipwreck, being governed by careless pilots. Therefore, for the love and care I bear to the Church, being conscious of my good conscience and that I aim chiefly at its good — why may I not ransom its danger with my money? A plausible reason, I confess; for our very lives are not too dear, much less our livings, to be spent and sacrificed for the Church of God. It was the Apostle’s joy and rejoicing to be offered up upon the sacrifice and service of the Philippians’ faith. And this ought to be the joy of every faithful minister of Christ, lawfully called and canonically placed in this or that position in the Church. But in this case the situation is otherwise. A man is not yet lawfully and orderly called to this or that charge in the Church. Well: the Church lacks a good pastor; wolves seek to enter. Shall I, to prevent them, climb up into the sheepfold by another way, and not by the door? What benefit will this be to the flock, when the thief and the robber comes to forestall the wolf? Why was Uzzah, being no priest, struck down? Was it not for laying his hand upon the ark, contrary to God’s commandment — Numbers 4:15: “They shall not touch any holy thing, lest they die”? But the ark was shaken by the oxen, and he only steadied it; yet he received his payment for his officiousness. So you see (at least in your own apprehension) that instead of the true Levites, the ark is drawn by oxen, the Church is swayed by those who ought not to sway it, and its state is shaken and in jeopardy.
