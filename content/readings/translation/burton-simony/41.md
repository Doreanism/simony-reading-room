Of such persons Cyprian speaks copiously and very aptly in his seventy-second Epistle, where he advises that after their return from their heresies they should not at once be admitted to places of preferment in the Church, but should first be proved by long humiliation. He gives the reason: because they often prove dangerous, since they cannot easily cast up the poison of heresy which they have once drunk in; so that they should be content only to be pardoned and received into the peace of the Church, and so remain until they have given sufficient trial and testimony of their sound repentance and reformation. The Council of Elvira, canon 22, says of such persons: *Placuit huic pænitentiam non esse denegandam, eo quod cognouerit peccatum suum, qui etiam decem annis agat pænitentiam: nisi Infantes fuissent transducti* — We are pleased not to deny penance to such a one, since he has acknowledged his sin; let him also do ten years’ penance — unless they had been carried away in their infancy. (Though such are most incurable, as the poet says: *Quo semel est imbuta recens seruabit odorem Testa diu* — the vessel will long retain the scent with which it was once freshly soaked. When they *cum lacte nutricis errorem surgere* — suck error from the nurse’s milk — it sticks with them. As we see the Jesuitical milk does, whereby men coming to some growth find their nursery has become a second nature.) Otherwise, as Boniface said well, *Nullum precio est inuitandus ad conuersionem* — none is to be invited to conversion by a reward. For, as Seneca says, *Precio parata, soluitur pretio fides* — faith that is purchased is easily sold again.

Others are said to commit simony when by mutual stipulation or covenant they make an exchange of benefices or prebends, arranging their transaction according to the value of the things. And if this is simony — as the ancients have defined it to be — it were to be wished that the exchange, at the very least, were free from stipulation, and that the main end of such permutations aimed principally at the common good of the Church.
