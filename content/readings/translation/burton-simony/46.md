But to come off so roundly — say it be to some courtier — what does it imply but either that the grand patron’s grant is of little validity unless seconded by the favour of some special servant about him (whose favour must be purchased), or that the suitor had a purpose rather to pay than to go without, or at least that some implied condition must be fulfilled? For I would ask this suitor — or rather at this point, this expediter — whether, before the obtaining of the benefice, this sum, whatever it may be (certainly not nothing), was not implied, if not expressed, to be bestowed in this way (so as to shift off the charge of simony): without which, why should not the suitor have fallen just as short of his preferment before the grant, as now, for want of extending his arm with a silver hand that cannot reach the seal, be in danger of being overreached by a longer arm? For so, *Multa cadunt inter calicem, supremaque labra* \[much falls between the cup and the lips\].

Another has found a neat way of simony: the patron and he are agreed concerning a vacant benefice that it shall come freely — but with a promise that he shall buy the next presentation, which pays for everything.

I might here speak of many other branches of this accursed tree: as if there should be any buying or selling, bartering and exchanging for college fellowships (wherein simony lays her first foundation and hatches her first egg, for who is fitter to traffic in the Church than one who has begun in a college?) — yes, even trucking for a porter’s or bell-ringer’s place in college or church. For *Simoniacum est emere officium ostiarii, seu pulsandi campanam* \[it is simony to buy a doorkeeper’s or bell-ringer’s office\]. But because every branch bearing the same fruit is known to belong to the same tree, it will be as superfluous to speak of more as it would be endless and impossible for an inexperienced simplicity to dive into the mystery
