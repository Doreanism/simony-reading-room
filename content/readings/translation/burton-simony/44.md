But as for any other gift, the very expectation of it is in danger of simony. I have heard of temporal men who have refused after-gifts from their servants whom they had freely preferred to some temporal office, taking the very offer very indignantly. How much more, then, should this purity be preserved in spiritual preferments?

But here some may object.

*Objection.* But having received a temporal benefit, shall I not — or may I not — in some measure recompense it with some temporal token of thankfulness? I answer that this benefit is not of a temporal nature but of a spiritual one, as we said before. Or, granting that it may be so far temporal as to call for some temporal recompense, yet it must be with certain limitations. Alexander III, writing to the Archbishop of Strigonia \[Esztergom\] concerning a horse which his brother, without his knowledge, gave to P., the Pope’s legate, for his election after it had been completed — which was nonetheless a scruple in the Archbishop’s conscience — resolves the matter thus: In giving or receiving, three things are to be observed: first, the quality of the persons by whom and to whom it is given, whether poor or rich; second, the quantity of the gift; third, the time of the giving, whether in time of necessity or otherwise. If therefore, he says, we consider the conditions of the aforesaid parties, the Cardinal and your brother, it was no great matter for the one to send the other a horse — which perhaps such a rich man as that would give to a jester without being asked. But if we consider the necessity of the occasion, it appears it was done with no other intention than to supply the Cardinal’s want at that time, when in an unfamiliar place he was without a horse. And inasmuch, he says, as it is written, *Beatus qui excutit manus ab omni munere* — blessed is he who shakes his hands from every bribe — this is spoken of those gifts which are wont to allure and pervert the mind of the receiver.
