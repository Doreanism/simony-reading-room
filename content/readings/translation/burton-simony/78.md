if a bishop is a name of labor rather than honor, and of duty rather than dominion; if it is not to be sought nor undertaken except for pure charity’s sake when necessity compels; if (as Saint Chrysostome) he bears the burden of all, is most inexcusable, exposed to all tongues, distracted with daily and nightly cares, subject to all censures, liable to all judgments; if others’ sins are imputed to his misgovernment; if the loss of each soul, so precious, shall be required of him; if to desire it is to cast oneself out of God’s favor; if a private life is much more sweet, more secure and safe; if it is not a bed of rest but of broken sleep; if their preaching is most solicitous and most assiduous; if he is like a ship continually tossed with waves and driven by contrary winds; if not many ministers are saved but far more do perish; if he is subject even against his will to being diverted and driven from his own disposition; if pride alone in such a man is enough to cause a universal decay and corruption; if their account is most severe — both for their doctrine (what \[they taught\], and how, with what diligence, with what devotion it was performed) and for their life (how answerable to their doctrine, how holy, how religious, how in every way exemplary to their flock; how careful in relieving the poor, how circumspect in electing good ministers and rejecting the unworthy); if this office makes men rather greater than better, hoisting up the sails of every passion to all winds of temptation — especially when it finds pride, avarice, ambition, and the like; if (as Erasmus) a bishop puts on the person of a father, a mother, a nurse, all importing manifold offices; if, as Aaron with his bells, he must be ever sounding forth the word of truth; if, for the multiplicity and difficulty of the duties of this calling — chained one within another — many wise and grave men of God have shrunk their shoulders from it;
