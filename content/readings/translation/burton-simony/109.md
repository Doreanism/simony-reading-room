But inasmuch as many take their first degree in simony before their commencement at the university, and perhaps before they have taken orders in the church — as some clever and promising scholar, who has learned to distinguish *per se, aut per alium* \[by himself or through another\], to contract by precontract, but ignorant of the nature of such bargains, falls unwittingly into the snare — and seeing also that if there were no sellers, there would be no buyers at all: therefore, for the prevention of much simony, whether between the patron and his schoolmaster, or between the patron and his neighbor, too provident for his son’s preferment, it were to be wished that the oath of simony might be administered to the presenting patron, so that the wicket being stopped up, the path would in time become so overgrown that men would be diverted from ever seeking to enter at the broad gate. Though if some wily dealer can, with his logic — or rather self-deceiving sophistry — find out evasions even beyond the pillars of Hercules, directly and indirectly, beyond which there can be no *plus ultra* \[further beyond\]: will not the merchant, do you think, by some trick or quibble in law, just as easily ferry himself out of that narrow-mouthed strait? Nevertheless, I say, it were to be wished that as well the presenter as the presentee might take the oath.
