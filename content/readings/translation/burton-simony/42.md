Now, what should move this preaching friar thus to color and gild over his simony with such a sacred gold is hard to say, unless some false gloss upon the Proverbs has made him believe that such preaching to such ends is to speak a word in season, which is, “Like apples of gold in pictures of silver,” as Solomon says. But the gloss is ill turned into a glose. The Apostle forbids such merchandising of the Word for filthy lucre’s sake. \[2 Corinthians 2:17; 1 Peter 5:2.\]

What should I speak of that more than common custom of matrimonial simony, no less odious than the rest, when a benefice or other ecclesiastical dignity must become the portion of some daughter or kinswoman — or at the least, an honest woman? And so if that gloss be true, that a bishop must be the husband of one wife — that is, of one benefice (as the Roman Church expounds it, not out of any zeal they bear toward singularity of benefices, but out of the hatred they have against the universality of priests’ marriages) — then it may also be equally true that a man making such a match for a benefice should be marrying two wives at once.
