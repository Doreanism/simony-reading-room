Objection. Some may here object that reciprocally Jacob also, in buying the birthright, committed simony. But Lyra answers well: not so; for the birthright belonging to Jacob by God’s own appointment and donation (Genesis 25:23), Esau having forfeited his right to it by selling it committed simony, whereas Jacob, by buying what was already his own by the best title, intended *non emere rem sacram, sed redimere vexationem suam* \[not to buy a sacred thing, but to buy his own peace\].

Objection. If Jacob in so buying committed no simony because he bought but his own, then why may not a minister buy and yet commit no simony, since tithes appertain to the minister as fully as the birthright did to Jacob, both by God’s donation? But the cases are not alike. For although tithes are by God’s own entitling tied and entailed to his ministry, yet this or that minister has no title to this or that tithe until he is lawfully possessed and invested in them. And when he is so, if he meets with a crafty or cruel patron — a profane Esau — who either fraudulently usurps or forcibly withholds any part of the tithes, then he may lawfully and commendably imitate Jacob’s wise example: *non emere rem sacram, iam suam, sed redimere vexationem suam* \[not to buy a sacred thing that is already his, but to buy his own peace\]. Yet if there were any such well-minded Jacobs, God’s ministers, who could and would redeem church goods to their true heir, they would draw upon themselves a blessing, and not a curse. Although I confess the Council of Mainz says: *Decimas, quas populus dare non vult, nisi quolibet munere ab eo redimantur, ab Episcopis prohibendum est, ne fiat* \[Those tithes which the people — or any patron — will not pay unless they are redeemed by some reward, the bishops ought to forbid such dealing\].

But some say it is not lawful for a man to redeem his spiritual right, because it is simony. But I think few will be so eager to redeem it as to need the bishop’s prohibition.
