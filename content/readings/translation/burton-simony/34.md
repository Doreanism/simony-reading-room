But where it is not lawful for me to do as I would, what place is left for the petitioner? — unless perhaps the petitioner would entreat this of me: that what he himself desires, it may be lawful for me to be all the more willing not to do. Espencaeus \[César Espence\] says: *Sunt adulatores potius, quam Doctores, qui*, etc. They are flatterers rather than teachers who teach that the Pope is lord of all benefices — and therefore, since as lord he sells by right what is his own, it necessarily follows that simony cannot be imputed to him. As though the Holy Spirit with his gifts were also his own! Were they not equally Peter’s \[i.e., possessed by Peter\]? Yet had he sold them, he would have been a simoniac. *Sed nemo potest retinere, quod vendidit; ut Judas Christum*: But no one can retain what he has sold — as Judas, by selling Christ, lost Christ. Gregory Nazianzen; Gratian. We will conclude this point with the words of Saint Ambrose: *Sunt quidem Decimæ Depositum seruorum Dei in Ecclesia seruatæ*: Tithes are kept in the Church as a deposit held in trust for God’s servants. Now for a man to take it upon himself to dispose of that for his own benefit which has been committed to him as a pledge in trust, to be delivered to the true owner when required — what this amounts to, let any reasonable person judge.

In the canon law, all advowsons, being contained under *ius patronatus* \[the right of patronage\], were forbidden to be bought and sold; as Gregory says (*Decret.* lib. 3, *De Iure Patronatus*, tit. 38, cap. 6), calling advowsons *Adnocatias*, *ius patronatus*, *advocationes*, *vice-dominatum*, *custodias*, or *guardias*: *Quia Clerici quidam Adnocatias Ecclesiarum comparant, vel quocunque modo possunt, acquirunt, ut postmodum eorum filii, vel nepotes, ad easdem Ecclesias præsententur, præcipimus ut id arctius inhibeatur; eosdem advocationibus taliter acquisitis, appellatione postposita, spoliando*: Because certain clergy purchase church advowsons \[*adnocatias*\], or acquire them by whatever means they can, so that afterwards their sons or grandsons may be presented to those same churches, we command that this be more strictly prohibited, stripping them of advowsons so acquired, with no right of appeal. So the canon law forbids the trafficking of advowsons.
