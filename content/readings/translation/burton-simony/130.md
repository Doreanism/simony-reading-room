But I know there is no one member of the natural body better able to distinguish between its own integrity and its neighbor’s infirmity than those of the politic body can. So that for me — being, as it were, but the simple apothecary of so many doctors — to go about protesting that this Censure is not general, as applied to the whole: what would that be but to argue as if any incapacity of sinister conception (where no just cause has been given) could take hold in such noble and generous breasts, the intact members of this beautiful body? Or as if I could be guilty of my own innocence. But here my protestation must break forth — nay, rather let it be turned into a joyful congratulation. How many are there, how many (I say) — not a few — both of the truly generous nobility and of the truly noble gentry, even all the truly virtuous of both, whose patronages are not tainted with the least touch of simoniacal corruption?
