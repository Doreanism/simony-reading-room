Cyprian *de Lapsis* says: *Non in Sacerdotibus religio devota, non in Ministris fides integra, non in operibus misericordia, non in moribus disciplina. Episcopi plurimi, quos et hortamento esse oportet caeteris et exemplo, divina procuratione contempta, Procuratores rerum secularium fieri, derelicta Cathedra, plebe deserta, negotiationis quaestuosae nundinas aucupari; esurientibus in Ecclesia fratribus non subvenire, habere argentum largiter velle, etc. Quid non perpeti tales pro peccatis eiusmodi mereremur! Adeo traditam nobis divinitus Disciplinam pax longa corrupit, etc.* \[That is: “Among the priests there is no devout religion; among the ministers, no sound faith; in \[their\] works, no mercy; in morals, no discipline. Very many bishops, who ought to be both an exhortation and an example to others, despising the management of divine things, have become managers of secular affairs; forsaking their cathedra and abandoning their people, they haunt the markets of gainful trade; they will not relieve their hungry brothers in the Church, but they desire to have money in abundance, etc. What suffering would we not deserve for such sins! So greatly has a long peace corrupted the discipline divinely delivered to us, etc.”\] And *de ieiunio et tent.* 6: *Simon Apostolorum temporibus vanalem putans Spiritum Sanctum, Petrum donis aggreditur, et tentat emere potestatem, per quam plura lucretur. Haec sacrilegi forma per omnia Officia, gradusque, discurrit, et nihil intentatum ambitio praetermittit. Nec dubitet quisquam Diaboli esse negotia, et nundinatores eius, quicunque, haec exercent commercia, nec quicquam huiusmodi ab eo, nisi praemissa Apostasia, donari.* \[That is: “Simon, in the time of the Apostles, thinking the Holy Spirit could be bought for money, approaches Peter with gifts and attempts to purchase a power by which he might gain more. This pattern of sacrilege runs through all offices and ranks, and ambition leaves nothing unattempted. Let no one doubt that these are the Devil’s business, and that those who practice such commerce are his merchants, and that nothing of this kind is granted by him without apostasy first being rendered.”\] So that simony and apostasy go together.

What should I speak here of the infinite and insupportable discouragements that both learning and honesty jointly suffer by reason of simony? — when an honest scholar sees before his eyes the impossibility of attaining any place in the Church, his honesty being in this respect so lame that it cannot stir either hand or foot to climb or scramble to any such place by by-paths.
