*honorem tanti Sacramenti convincitur obtinere per praemium. Ex qua re agitur, ut ipsi quod, qui sacros Ordines appetunt, non vitam corrigere non mores componere studeant sed divitias, quibus honor sacer emitur, satagant congregare. Hinc fit etiam ut innocentes pauperes a Sacris ordinibus prohibiti, despectique, resiliant. Et dum innocentia displicet, dubium non est, quod praemium illic delicta commendet; quia ubi aurum placet, ibi & vitium. Hinc igitur non solum in Ordinatoris & Ordinati animam letale vulnus infligitur, verum etiam excellentiae vestrae regnum Episcoporum culpa, quorum magis intercessionibus munari debuerat, pregranatur. Si enim dignus is Sacerdotio creditur, cui non actionis merita, sed praemiorum copia suffragatur, restat ut nihil sibi in honores Ecclesiasticos gravitas, nil defendat industria, sed totum auri profanae amor obtineat. Et dum vitia remunerantur honore, hinc Sacerdotes non proficere, sed perire potius indicantur.* — Let faith together with a good life be the criterion in choosing priests. But what works can a priest perform who is convicted of obtaining the honor of so great a Sacrament by bribes? By this means it comes to pass that even those who aspire to holy Orders take no care to correct their life or amend their conduct, but labor tooth and nail to amass riches, with which the sacred honor is purchased. And hence it also comes about that innocent and poor men, being excluded and despised, shrink back from holy Orders. And while innocence counts for nothing, there is no doubt that a bribe advances vice in its place, for where gold is welcome, there vice is welcomed too. And thereby not only is a deadly wound inflicted upon the soul of the ordainer and the ordained, but the kingdom of your Excellency is also burdened and oppressed through the fault of bishops, whose intercessions ought rather to have relieved it. For if one is deemed worthy of the priesthood not on the merit of his conduct but on the abundance of his payments, it follows that neither dignity nor diligence can secure any claim to ecclesiastical honors, and that the profane love of gold wins everything. And while vices are rewarded with honor, priests may well be judged not to prosper but to perish. And he concludes with an exhortation to the two kings: *Proinde Paterno salutantes affectu petimus excellentissimi Filii, ut hoc tam detestabile malum*
