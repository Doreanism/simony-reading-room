The third part — that virtual intention is required — is proved by exhaustive division: because intention is required by the third conclusion, and habitual intention does not suffice, as has now been shown; therefore virtual intention is required. This holds by way of a sufficient division. It suffices, therefore, in the one baptizing that he at some point had an actual intention to baptize, from which the act of baptizing proceeds, whether or not he has an actual intention to baptize in that very act — since perhaps he is attending to the washing as such a natural action, or to something irrelevant. It must be understood, however, that the act proceeds without any contrary movement of the will. Just as one who vests himself for Mass, intending to celebrate according to the usage of the Roman Church, and from that intention proceeds continuously — perhaps by force of some habit lodged in the imagination, amid great distraction — yet performs what is necessary for consecration: he truly confects the Sacrament of the Eucharist. Thus Scotus.

## Sixth Conclusion

If several persons baptize the same \[subject\], they truly confer the sacrament, provided that each of them performs the whole \[rite\]. This is proved because one person performing the whole does not annul the act of another. But if one person did everything that several persons do, the baptized would be baptized none the less with another performing the same. It is said, however, with the qualification that each of them performs the whole — that is, provided that each one washes and pronounces the words in their entirety with the intention of baptizing. For if one person washed while another pronounced the words, they would accomplish nothing. The case is commonly put thus: if a mute and a one-handed man came together, the mute washed, and the one-handed man pronounced the words, and both intended to baptize, it would not be a true sacrament. The reason for this, according to Scotus, is simply the divine institution, which, just as it willed there to be one principal agent, so too willed there to be one minister in the integral conferral of the sacrament — as is clear from the form we use, for the one who pronounces it signifies that he himself is washing. Nor should the formula in the forms of the sacraments be false in fact. The same must similarly be understood of the words of the Greeks, who say “let him be baptized,” which is not a petition but a kind of efficacious optative, the sense being: “let this one be baptized by him who wills to do this toward him.” Thus Scotus. See \[Scotus,\] Part \[IV\], Dist. 3, q. 2, art. 2. That consideration about the truth of the words counts for little or nothing, because if each of several baptizers baptizes saying “I baptize you,” the proposition is true, since each one is baptizing. What then if two persons baptizing one \[subject\] say “we baptize you in the name \[of\]”? Alexander laughs \[at this\].
