## The Fourth Conclusion

For the truth of the sacrament, the intention of the minister with respect to the specific effect of baptism is not required; it suffices to intend what the Church intends in a general way. This is proved because unbelievers and heretics, even regarding this article of baptism, as well as laypeople and women, can baptize, as was established by the first conclusion. Among these, some do not believe that baptism has any effect with respect to the purification of the soul; some do not understand what the effects of baptism are. And yet they can have the intention of baptizing as baptism was instituted, or as Christians use it. And this is what it means to intend what the Church intends — even if one does not believe that it accomplishes anything, as has been said.

## The Fifth Conclusion

For the truth of baptism, the said actual intention is not required, nor does habitual intention suffice; rather, virtual intention is required. The first part is proved: actual intention is not required, because God, in instituting the sacrament of baptism as a sacrament necessary for salvation, did not bind man to what is impossible or excessively difficult. But not to be distracted in any human act that interrupts or removes actual intention is either impossible or very difficult. For Ambrosius, following Augustinus, \[*On Free Will*\] 2, \[says\] that it is not in our power what impressions affect us. Therefore \[God\] did not oblige the one baptizing to refrain from being distracted during the act of baptizing by thinking about something else not pertaining to baptism. Now when one is distracted, he does not actually think about or intend \[baptism\] at that moment, because a person cannot simultaneously think about several things none of which is ordered to the other — and likewise he cannot simultaneously intend them, because intending presupposes thinking, just as an act of the will presupposes an act of the intellect.

The second part, that habitual intention does not suffice, is proved because such intention can exist in a sleeping person — indeed even in one who has a contrary actual intention; but neither of these baptizes. For if a parish priest who has the habit of baptizing were to baptize a child in his sleep — which is not impossible, since some walk in their sleep and perform remarkable actions — and similarly if such a person were out of malice to wash \[someone\] externally while actually intending not to baptize, neither would baptize, though both would have habitual intention. Moreover, an act is not called a human act by reason of a habit alone residing \[in the agent\], even though it is conformed by the habit to a human act. This is proved because an act proceeding from a habit without actual intention is a purely natural act, not an act of free will, since a habit inclines toward an act not freely but purely naturally — by which \[reasoning\] such an act is not a human act, since every human act is a free act.
