# The Single Question of the Fifth Distinction.

For the obligation itself is that he not direct \[the act\] toward something other than that to which he is obligated, and consequently it cannot be a fortuitous act, but an act proceeding from deliberate purpose, and thus a human act. Alexander also adds two further reasons for this: namely, out of reverence for the sacrament, and for the safety of the one being baptized. Hence an injury and irreverence would be done to the sacrament if it were administered without intention — rashly, impulsively, and by chance. Similarly, because the baptized person’s salvation is at risk if the sacrament is not rightly administered with proper care and foresight, lest anything be omitted that is necessarily required for the truth of baptism.

## The Second Part of the Conclusion

Namely, that intention is required not only with respect to the act of baptizing as its object. Such intention is indeed required, as the arguments for the first part of the conclusion prove — for otherwise it would not be a human act — but it does not suffice. For otherwise one who baptizes mockingly, as a joke, or washes while pronouncing the words for any other reason whatsoever, would truly baptize; and that conclusion is false on the basis of what has been said above, while the inference itself is evident — because the mocker does have intention with respect to the act: he wills to wash and to pronounce the words in ridicule and contempt of the Christian faith and the sacrament, and nothing else is lacking — neither matter nor form. The third part of the conclusion (that intention is required with respect to the effect, which is the end of baptism) is proved as follows: just as God wills that this act be exercised as a human act, and not merely insofar as it is such an act in nature — for in that respect it would not be distinguished from any other washing accompanied by other words spoken in jest — but insofar as it is the act of a minister exercising a sign of God; and one who performs a human act intends the end of that act as it is assigned to him. For this principle holds universally in the use of a practical sign. If a lawgiver were to establish in his polity that a touch of the hand is a sign of friendship, he would not intend that those using this sign should have merely a human act concerning it insofar as it is such an instituted sign; and if he had committed himself to giving or doing something upon that sign, he would neither give nor do it unless a person exercised that sign as it was instituted for that end. So too in the present matter: it does not suffice to have the intention of exercising baptism insofar as it is such an act in nature, but \[one must intend it\] insofar as it is a sign instituted for a definite end. And \[Biel\] affirms: the intention of the minister is required by the first part of the conclusion; the intention of merely such an act as it is in nature does not suffice, by the second part; therefore the intention of the act as ordered toward some end is required — and that end is indeed the end which the faithful (who use this sign, that is, the Church) intend. The inference holds, because nothing else can be assigned that ought to be intended beyond that act insofar as it is such in nature.

## Corollary

Which follows from the conclusion. A drunkard, a madman, and in general anyone lacking reason for the time during which he is deprived of the use of reason cannot baptize, because he does not then have freedom, nor the intention that has been described. Nor is this against Augustinus, 5, on Job: “I take no account of one given to drunkenness.”
