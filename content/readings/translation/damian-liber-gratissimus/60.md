For to come back to my own case: after the supreme pontiff Leo learned that my archbishop and consecrator had engaged in this commerce — though at that time a banker \[trapezeta, a money-changer\] presided over the apostolic see — he nonetheless granted to me, unworthy as I am, the exercise of the priestly office that the archbishop had held. But how can this iniquitous argument claim to exempt me from the category of those who received nothing, when it maintains that the stream of consecration dried up at the very original spring of the first see? Behold, then: not only is a conflict being mounted against all the statutes of the ancient fathers, but the judgment of this most blessed pope is also being cruelly assailed. Whatever the number of removes a given man is from a simoniac, what does it matter for the argument, so long as nothing flowed from that non-possessing original and therefore nothing passed into anyone thereafter? And if some churches can be found that were never corrupted by this plague, what benefit accrues to the sons of other churches in this regard, since canonical authority forbids anyone to be consecrated in a church other than his own? Therefore, according to the logic of this argument, the stream of divine grace dried up so completely among the simoniacs of the first age in certain churches that it could neither flow to them nor afterward pass through them to others. You see then, O devotees of perverse doctrines, where your murky wisdom tends? You perceive what fruits your sharpness of argument produces? Has the dispute at last come to this: that you presume to set a limit to the mercy of Christ and to strip his Church of the commerce of hope? But we confess faithfully to him that he is good, that his mercy endures forever.

## XXX. That those who had been promoted by simoniacs shone with miracles.

There is something else which I hear reported more and more frequently by many witnesses, and which I do not think should be passed over in silence. How are we to explain the fact that certain venerable men — who are established beyond all doubt to have been advanced to the priesthood by simoniacs, and who, as we know, persevered to the end of their lives in the unceasing offering of the sacrifice — are now clearly seen to shine with the glorious power of remarkable miracles? In our own age, blessed men such as Romuald of Camaldoli, Amicus of Rambona, Guido of Pomposa, Firmanus of Fermo, and many others flourished in the practice of holy life. Over their venerable remains, sacred altars have been erected by the authority of a priestly council, where, while the divine mysteries are offered at the urging of miracles, clear evidence shines forth of how sweetly pleasing the sacrifice offered by their hands is in the sight of God; and through them divine providence makes it plainer than light that no one is clouded by the darkness of another’s depravity whom the splendor of his own piety and uprightness illumines, nor does the guilt of those who promoted him overwhelm the man in whom purity and innocence stand firm.
