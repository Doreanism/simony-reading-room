For the dove is not repelled, nor does it recoil from the ministry of any unclean person, so long as He into whom it wholly descends alone holds the primacy of consecration. For the unity of the Church stands firm in this: that Christ kept with Himself the power of consecrating and transferred the rights of consecration to none of His ministers. For if consecration proceeded from the merit or virtue of the priest, it would assuredly in no way pertain to Christ. But though the bishop lays on hands and pronounces the words of blessing through the ministry enjoined upon him, it is certainly Christ who consecrates and sanctifies by the hidden power of His majesty. Hence it is commanded to Moses by the divine voice: “Speak to Aaron and his sons: Thus shall you bless the sons of Israel, and you shall say to them: The Lord bless you and keep you; the Lord show His face to you and have mercy on you; the Lord turn His countenance to you and give you peace” \[Numbers 6:23–26\]. And immediately it is added: “They shall invoke my name upon the sons of Israel, and I will bless them” \[Numbers 6:27\]. It is therefore the part of the priests to invoke the divine name over those who are to be consecrated, but it is the proper act of God Himself to bless inwardly. To ministers is plainly assigned the external order of consecration, but to the Lord alone is reserved the efficacy of that consecration. Hence He Himself said in Exodus: “I am the Lord, who sanctifies you” \[Exodus 31:13\]. If therefore it is the Lord who sanctifies, why should one fear that the guilt of any culpable servant can stand in the way?

## XIII. That neither a minister of the word nor a minister of the sacrament can obstruct the gifts of God.

The Apostle, however, complains that certain false brethren were preaching Christ out of envy and contention, not sincerely. But what of this? Does he decree that they are to be silenced? Hear then what follows: “What then? Only that in every way, whether in pretense or in truth, Christ is proclaimed; and in this I rejoice and will rejoice” \[Philippians 1:18\]. Now Christ has ministers of the word and ministers likewise of the sacrament — some of each kind faithful and good, others wicked and faithless; but it is received neither better from the good nor worse from the wicked. The ministers are various, but what is provided is assuredly one and the same. Good is the author of the gifts, and what He gives contracts no stain from the service of the ministers. The spring flows pure, and the leprous hand of the one who offers it is a matter of indifference, when what is drawn from it is clear and transparent. For what does it matter at any moment whether the one who ministers is good or bad, so long as one and the same is always He who is the principal author, whether of the word or of the sacrament? In the performing of miraculous signs, or in the oracles of prophecy, or even in the possession of virtues of holy religion, the grace of the Holy Spirit is diminished or increased. Just as Elisha asked of his master Elijah, saying: “I pray that a double portion of your spirit may rest on me” \[2 Kings 2:9\].
