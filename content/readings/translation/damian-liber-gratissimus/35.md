But this variation does not occur in the grades of office: one man is not more a priest or deacon than another, nor anything of that kind. In miraculous gifts, the faith and devotion of the one who asks are what count; in ecclesiastical offices, only the order established by the Church is observed. Let us hear what the Lord says about wicked preachers: “The scribes and Pharisees sit on Moses’ seat. Whatever they tell you, observe and do; but do not follow their example” \[Matthew 23:2–3\]. If therefore the wickedness of any priest does not prejudice the word that is given through him to hearers, how could it harm the sacrament, which is conferred not by human authority but by God? Both the preacher and the one who consecrates are ministers, not donors. Hence it often happens that a teacher’s tongue is one, yet his words do not bear fruit in the same way in the minds of his listeners: one heart lies torpid as if frozen with icy cold, while another’s spirit blazes immediately with the fire of divine love upon hearing the word. Whence does it come, then, that words proceeding from a single mouth and striking many ears alike do not reach the inner recesses of hearts in the same way — unless it is because one person ministers outwardly through the office of the tongue, while another by his own power distributes the voice of the preacher through the interior of souls? Whether in the giving of doctrinal words or in the conferring of ecclesiastical orders, the priest in no way employs his own merits, but only carries out the office of the ministry entrusted to him. Otherwise, if these things proceeded from the merit of the priest, they would utterly empty all faith in divine grace.

## Chapter XIV. That it was not Moses himself, but God, who gave of his spirit to the seventy men.

For a human being does not give what is divine; rather, the one God who makes good use of evil men is he who alone, whether through the good or even through the wicked, bestows his gifts. And therefore the gift of divine grace is neither diminished by the ministry of wicked men nor increased by the service of good ones. This is clearly recognized when the course of sacred history is recalled to mind. For the Lord said to Moses: “Gather to me seventy men of the elders of Israel, whom you know to be elders and leaders of the people, and bring them to the door of the tent of meeting and make them stand there with you, that I may come down and speak with you, and I will take some of the spirit that is upon you and put it upon them” \[Numbers 11:16–17\]. Here it should be noted that he does not say “you will take of your spirit and give it to them,” but rather: “I will take and give” — to show plainly that it is not a human being who hands on the Holy Spirit to another, but he alone who, as he wills, distributes the gifts of his grace. Hence a little later it is added: “Then the Lord came down in the cloud and spoke to him, and took some of the spirit that was on Moses and put it on the seventy elders” \[Numbers 11:25\].
