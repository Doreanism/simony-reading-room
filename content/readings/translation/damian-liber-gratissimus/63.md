The writer’s concern here is certainly not with simoniacs; rather, he is striking at that sect of heretics who are known to possess no faith or grace of the Holy Spirit whatsoever. Let them read the whole text, I urge, so that in a single view they may grasp the full import of what is said. Let them observe how, further on, the Paulianists and Novatianists are distinguished from one another according to the variety of their sects: those returning from the Novatianists to the catholic church may be received in their own orders, whereas those coming from the Paulianists must necessarily be baptized again. And let them gather from this the reason why — namely, because the Paulianists do not baptize in the name of the Holy Trinity, whereas among the Novatianists no question even arises concerning faith in the same Holy Trinity, since it is held in full. Let them also note that those who had been properly baptized by Philip the evangelist were completed by Peter and John through the laying on of hands alone; whereas those whom the apostle Paul found washed by the baptism of John, when he asked whether they had received the Holy Spirit and they confessed that they had not so much as heard the name, he ordered to be baptized. Since, therefore, when the decree passes from the general category of heretics to the specific, it mentions not simoniacs but rather Paulianists and Novatianists, it is established beyond all doubt that a judgment is not pronounced against those who are not mentioned. The meaning of this decree must therefore be traced through its order of argument. The Macedonians had inquired whether those returning from heretics, having been ordained by them, might be permitted to remain in their grades; or whether, since they could not, they might lawfully be consecrated again. In the course of their argument they maintained by a chain of reasoning that this could rightly be done, because the ordination of heretics, being invalid, was also null; since one who has lost honor cannot give honor, for there was nothing in the giver that the recipient could receive, and such persons would therefore seem to come to orders as newcomers. Moreover, to lend even greater force to their argument, they added that the true and righteous blessing of a lawful priest would salutarily drain away whatever corruption had been damnably infused by the heretic, so that in a manner the sacred hand would wash away whatever filth the sacrilegious hand had scattered. On these grounds they asserted that those ordained by heretics could reasonably be ordained again by catholic priests. But Innocent forbade all of this entirely, and decreed that it was in no way lawful for them to be re-consecrated. This is easily seen if the continuous sequence of the decree is carefully attended to. Let our opponents therefore be admonished to read this text more diligently, and let them clearly perceive that it applies not to simoniacs but to heretics of impious sects.

## XXXII. That rebaptism and reconsecration are an equal crime.

Since, then, the subject of reconsecrations has fittingly presented itself, and this alone remains yet to be treated in our little work, let us not be reluctant to address this theme as well, if briefly. Anyone who has been accustomed to dwell at the threshold of sacred Scripture has learned to weigh rebaptism and reconsecration on the same scale of judgment. For just as one who is rebaptized is shown to have stripped off Christ whom he had put on, so too one who is reconsecrated is clearly proven to have lost the Holy Spirit whom he had received. And since the divinity of the supreme Word and of the Paraclete is one, their essence is without doubt the same; therefore whoever loses the one will not possess the other. And just as it is impious if someone should presume through the repetition of baptism to drive Christ away from himself, so likewise it is execrable if through reconsecration he should not fear to deny the Holy Spirit. But if we do not find it burdensome to add a brief passage from the decree of Pope Felix, where he speaks of those who have been rebaptized, we can there clearly gather what we ought also to think about reconsecration.
