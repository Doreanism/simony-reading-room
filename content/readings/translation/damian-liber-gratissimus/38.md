Likewise, the Holy Spirit, in conferring the sacrament of spiritual grace, attends not so much to the merit of a person’s life as to the office of the grade assigned; so that it does not much matter for the effect of this thing whether the members of the Church are dishonorably or honorably composed, but rather where they are placed — and we must consider not so much their merits as their positions. This is especially so since the apostle says: “God has arranged the members in the body, each one of them, as he chose” \[1 Corinthians 12:18\]. But it often happens that the very members to which the more important offices of the senses are entrusted are found subject to deformity, while the others are adorned with conspicuous beauty. The beautiful members share in the utility of those offices along with them for the common benefit, though they admit no blemish of the others’ deformity into themselves. Hence the same apostle also says: “Our less honorable members receive more abundant honor” \[1 Corinthians 12:23\]. So too, what clerics possess in a special way they owe not so much to themselves as to the common use of the whole Church. And thus it comes about that if they are unworthy, they owe both the privilege by which they are preferred to others and, for the guilt weighing upon them, will not escape judgment. For if they received the ministerial priesthood for themselves alone, people would not be wrong to argue about their merits. But since that grace is conferred for the common salvation of all, attention must be paid not to the quality of those to whom it is given, but to those for whose sake it is given. Furthermore, the Truth says: “I am the vine, and you are the branches” \[John 15:5\]. From this vine various branches come forth — some indeed to be cut off at some point, others to be preserved forever for bearing fruit. But those that are to be cut off, so long as the evangelical or canonical pruning-knife has not yet come, whatever fruit they produce is not separated from the fruit of good branches, but is held to be of the same kind.

## Chapter XVI. On most wicked bishops whose ordinations were nonetheless valid.

Hence it is that all the ordinations performed by Liberius — who was both a heretic and a schismatic — are known to have been preserved in the stability of their standing. Now Liberius, deceived by the error of treachery, is recognized to have subscribed to the Arian heresy, and through his transgression most abominable crimes were committed. Many priests and clerics were killed by his malice, and the remaining Catholics were barred not only from the churches but even from the baths. Liberius, having become an apostate, survived for a space of six years. Yet whatever he did in the matter of ordinations remained fixed and valid in its firm force. What shall I say of Pope Vigilius? He, truly wicked and impious, whatever he established by ordaining, no pontiff presumed to annul. You should know that this is the Vigilius who first contrived plots against Pope Boniface, so that, while Boniface was still alive, he might sacrilegiously obtain the apostolic see — but, the senate resisting him, he was unable to carry out his unlawful and wicked designs. Afterward he attacked Pope Silverius by his customary machinations, seeking to strip him of the apostolic dignity.
