## XXXI. How what is frequently adduced as an objection is to be understood.

Now we have judged it necessary to set forth what our adversaries frequently cite against us from canonical authority, so that once what they put forward has been clearly refuted by evident reasoning, all contention of questions may rightly cease at once. For as often as we speak on behalf of those who have been ordained by simoniacs, they immediately come forward with “There came” \[*Ventum est*\], they press upon us “There came,” they thrust “There came” before our eyes to make us look. And we too will come to “There came” — but only in such a way that when we arrive at the words, we do not depart from sober understanding. Come then: Pope Innocent, the very one already mentioned, inserts among the many chapters of his decree the following words: “There came,” he says, “to the third question, which by reason of its difficulty demands a longer discussion. Since we say that those ordained by heretics have their head wounded at the point where the wound was inflicted, the medicine must be applied there that is capable of receiving the healing. That healing, coming after the wound, cannot be without a scar; and where the remedy of penance is necessary, there the honor of ordination cannot have a place. For if, as it is written, ‘whatever an unclean person touches shall be unclean’ \[Numbers 19:22\], how shall that be given to him which cleanliness and purity are accustomed to receive? But on the other hand it is argued that one who has lost honor cannot give honor, and that the recipient received nothing because there was nothing in the giver that he could receive. We agree — and it is true: certainly what he did not have he could not give. The damnation he did have he surely gave through the laying on of a wicked hand; and how one who has become a sharer in a damned man’s guilt ought to receive honor, we cannot discover. But it is said that the true and just blessing of a legitimate priest removes every vice that was introduced by a corrupt one. If that is so, then let sacrilegious men, adulterers, and persons guilty of every crime be admitted to ordination, since crimes and vices are supposed to be removed by the blessing of ordination. Let there be no place for penance, since ordination can accomplish what lengthy satisfaction has customarily accomplished. But the law of our Church is to grant to those coming from heretics, who were baptized in that communion, only lay communion through the laying on of hands, and to appoint none of them to any honor in the clerical order. Those, however, who have passed from the Catholic Church to heresy, who ought to be received only through penance — among us not only do they not do penance, but are even heaped with honor,” and other things of the like kind that follow. Now from this decree we have set out many passages here and yet omitted more that might seem necessary, both in order that the line of argument from which the error crept in may come to light, and also in order that a moderate prolixity may not pour the tedium of surfeit upon our readers. These words, the opponents dream, must be understood in such a way that the heretics mentioned therein are asserted to be simoniacs, and that those ordained by them have their heads so wounded by that laying on of hands that the honor of ordination can no longer have any place in them. But this interpretation is confounded, the truth being veiled, because they do not examine the text of the entire decree in order.
