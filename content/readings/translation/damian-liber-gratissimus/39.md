Indeed a genuine simoniac and usurper of the Church, he bound him with the bonds of anathema. Yet Vigilius, as he had conceived in his sacrilegious mind, wickedly condemned the aforesaid Pope Silverius through imperial power and the support of money and false witnesses, clothed him in monastic garb, and sent him into exile on Ponza, sustaining him on the bread of tribulation and the water of affliction. There that blessed pope died a confessor and was buried, and not a few of the sick were healed at his tomb. But Vigilius, caught in the just bonds of anathema and guilty moreover of homicide, by his nefarious audacity installed himself as pope. But — O the inestimable mercy of divine clemency! — Vigilius, who was already despaired of on account of his guilt, was preserved for repentance: he refused to enthrone Anthimus the heretical patriarch, whom he had promised to reconcile. Therefore, thrust into long exile and worn down by harsh afflictions, he received what his deeds deserved, and at last died far from his homeland and rested in the Lord. His ordinations, nonetheless, as has been said, remained in perpetual stability. Now these events concerning Silverius and Vigilius which we have recounted are found recorded partly in the synodal decree of that same Silverius and partly in the *Liber Pontificalis*. If, therefore, even the acts of such flagitious pontiffs are not annulled, by what reasoning could the hand of a simoniac harm an innocent man whose own conscience does not condemn him? How can another’s wickedness separate from divine grace one whose own life commends him to God? For the Holy Spirit is given through one by whom, beyond all doubt, it is not possessed. But so that our assertion, weak as it is, may gain credence among those who hear it, let the blessed Augustine himself come forward as a fitting witness. For in his work against the Donatist Parmenianus he speaks in these words: “What is read in the Book of Wisdom — ‘The Holy Spirit of discipline will flee the deceitful and will withdraw from thoughts that are without understanding’ — is to be understood as meaning that he is absent from that person’s salvation, yet does not forsake the mystery which through him works the salvation of others. Hence the apostle says: ‘For if I do this willingly, I have a reward; but if against my will, a dispensation is committed to me.’ As if to say: it profits those to whom I dispense this, not me who does it.” The same excellent doctor in his commentary on the Gospel of the blessed John speaks thus: “’Now you are clean because of the word that I have spoken to you.’ Why did he not say: ‘you are clean because of the baptism with which you have been washed,’ but rather: ‘because of the word that I have spoken to you,’ unless because the word also cleanses in the water? Remove the word, and what is water but water? The word is joined to the element, and it becomes a sacrament, itself as it were a visible word. For he had indeed said this when he washed the disciples’ feet: ‘He who has bathed does not need to wash except for his feet, but is entirely clean.’ Whence comes this great power of water, that it should touch the body and cleanse the heart, if not through the working of the word? Not because it is spoken, but because it is believed. For in the word itself one thing is the passing sound, another the abiding power. ‘This is the word of faith which we preach,’ says the apostle, ‘because if you confess with your mouth that Jesus is Lord and believe in your heart that God raised him from the dead, you will be saved.
