And they plunge into the peril of eternal death from the very source from which others grow more vigorously strong to grasp the rewards of life. Certainly whoever reads with a watchful eye that page of sacred scripture which speaks of the aforementioned Balaam will not at all doubt that this unhappy man was in every respect a true simoniac — except that Simon had not yet lived, from whom the detestable name might have been borrowed — that he held his prophetic gift for sale, and yet that the Holy Spirit spoke most manifestly through him; and, what is no less astonishing, even while his heart was burning with the fires of greed, God is found to have spoken to him nonetheless, familiarly as it were, by day and by night. And lest one might suppose that he possessed the Holy Spirit to a lesser degree on account of being called a diviner by Moses, read carefully the words of the same history, and you will find — with that same Moses bearing clear witness — that when he \[Balaam\] had seen Israel dwelling in their tents, the Spirit of God immediately rushed upon him and straightway roused him to the oracle of his prophecy as a true prophet \[Numbers 24:2\]. He is called a diviner, then, and yet he is a prophet: for the one whom supernal grace had raised to prophetic dignity, his own wickedness was defiling under the shameful name of diviner. Here one must consider how deep and subtle is the judgment of God — or rather how terrible God is in his counsels concerning the sons of men \[Psalm 66:5\] — in that this pitiable man neither lost, on account of the wickedness of his guilt, the grace he had once acquired, and yet was unable to escape the sentence of divine retribution on account of that very grace which he, unworthy as he was, presumed to exercise. What wonder is it, then, if almighty God — who always is what he is and knows no change — still maintains the ancient standards of his own judgment and holds to the same customary rule of discernment: so that what he did in Balaam’s case he works no differently today in those like him, displaying his gifts to the unworthy out of the generosity of his mercy while striking them with the stroke of fitting retribution out of the strictness of his justice?

## VIII. That the spirit of prophecy seized the reprobate Saul and his servants.

This too is not obscure: when Saul sent officers to seize David, those men — as the history relates — saw a company of prophets prophesying and Samuel standing over them, and the Spirit of the Lord came upon them as well, and they began to prophesy \[1 Samuel 19:20\]. And when Saul sent others a second and a third time, and the spirit of prophecy had likewise come upon them, at length — as scripture attests — Saul himself went in anger to Ramah. But before he arrived, the Spirit of God came also upon him, and he prophesied the whole day naked along with the others before Samuel \[1 Samuel 19:23–24\]. What wonder is it, then, if wicked men receive the gifts of God — which they are unworthy to receive — to the increase of their own condemnation, when even to Saul himself, whom God had rejected and whom a demon had grown accustomed to torment very frequently, it was granted not only to receive the Spirit of God for a time, but also to prophesy at length along with his attendants?
