Consider: the one who baptizes, after imparting the first rudiments of the faith which he has taught to the catechumen, straightway pronounces over him, in accordance with ecclesiastical custom, the prayers that have been composed for this purpose by priestly authority; and so, through the grace of exorcism, he expels the malign enemy who until that moment had held tyrannical sway over the candidate, and by calling upon the Paraclete he introduces him through devout intercession. But what else does the one who consecrates do, except that he likewise, by reciting the canonical prayers, invokes that same Holy Spirit upon the one being advanced and beseeches him to deign to descend upon him? — save that what is accomplished through the baptizer is in fact greater than what is performed through the office of the consecrator. For it is a greater thing for the Holy Spirit to descend with a new act of condescension upon one in whom he had not yet dwelt than to advance to the order of a higher grade one in whom he was already present through faith. For through the office of the one who catechizes and baptizes, both the unclean spirit is expelled and a dwelling is prepared in the new man for the God who comes to dwell there — as the words of the exorcism itself attest, in which the evil spirit is commanded by adjuration: “Flee, O devil, for the judgment of God draws near.” In consecrations, however, what is evil, which was believed to have already been destroyed and removed, is not expelled; rather, what is good, which had been conferred, is increased by an added fullness of grace. Are we to believe that one and the same man can accomplish what is greater and yet be unable to fulfil what is lesser? Perhaps it will be urged in reply that the rites of initiation in the faith contain certain elements that are in no way employed in the mysteries of consecration — namely, the salt placed in the neophyte’s mouth, the water in which the one to be baptized is immersed, and also the chrism with which he is anointed. But these things, considered in themselves, are bare elements and are known to contain no hidden mystery of sacred significance; it is through the prayer of the priest and the invocation of the divine name that they receive the sacrament of inward power. The sacraments of consecration and baptism alike consist, therefore, not in material and earthly things but in the prayers of priests and the invocation of God — though in the consecration of priests a sanctified oil is also seen to be used. Since, therefore, one and the same simoniac in both cases completes the order of canonical prayers and in both cases regularly invokes the name of God, it passes beyond the grasp of reason and exceeds human discernment how through him the Holy Spirit can be given in baptism but not given in priestly promotion. But it is urged on the other side that the simoniac cannot give what he does not have. Very well then — what is it that the priest breathes into the face of the catechumen, saying: “Depart from him, unclean Satan; give honor to the living and true God, give honor to the Holy Spirit”? By these words it is clearly shown that through that human breath — the hidden mystery of divine power secretly at work — both the evil spirit is expelled and the Holy Spirit deigns to enter.
