Among other things he continues as follows: Those who are established to have been bishops or deacons, and who are shown to have either willingly or under compulsion destroyed the unique and saving laver, and to have stripped off Christ — whom they had put on not only by the gift of regeneration but also by the grace of the honor they had received — since it is established that no one could come to a second immersion without having openly denied himself a Christian and professed himself a pagan: which, though universally execrable in all, is in bishops, priests, and deacons proved horrible even to hear and to speak of. But because the same Lord and most merciful Savior wills that none should perish, it will be fitting for them to lie in penance until the day of their departure, if they repent. And they are in no wise to participate in prayer — not only not with the faithful, but not even with catechumens — though lay communion is to be restored to them at death. In these words one must observe with particular care that, if one who is rebaptized is said to have denied Christ — because after receiving baptism he denied being a Christian — then likewise one who comes forward to be reconsecrated is shown to have denied the Holy Spirit, because even after his consecration he testifies that he has not received the Holy Spirit. The reconsecrated person is therefore by no means separated from the rebaptized by a different condemnation, since they are joined by an equal impiety of sacrilege — unless perhaps someone would argue that the reconsecrated person sins more gravely, because Truth says that whoever sins against the Father and the Son, it shall be forgiven him; but whoever sins against the Holy Spirit, it shall not be forgiven him, neither in this age nor in the age to come. And indeed, if we examine the decrees of catholic pontiffs with careful discernment, we find that license has sometimes been permitted in the case of rebaptism, whereas we clearly see that it has been utterly denied to reconsecration. For if we do not allow what was said above to slip from memory, we note that those baptized by Paulianists and by other heretics who do not possess the Holy Spirit ought by rule to be baptized; yet in that case baptism is not said to be repeated, because the prior unclean washing does not appear to have been a baptism at all. But those who are reconsecrated, from whatever type of heretics they may come, absolutely cannot be, for this the sacred canons entirely forbid. We wonder greatly, therefore, why some rage with such prodigious blindness as to decree that those ordained by simoniacs must be consecrated again. For they promulgate over the ordination of simoniacs a decree that is shown to be absurd even for those heretics who appear to have not even the slightest trace of orthodox faith. Among heretics, some hold the right faith and are to be received in their orders; others lack right faith and ought not to persist in their honor. And since we cannot drive off the charge of heresy from simoniacs, let us discern clearly to which heretics they may justly be assigned. For they must be said either to hold right faith like the Novatianists, or to be held fast in the errors of perfidy like the Paulianists or Arians.
