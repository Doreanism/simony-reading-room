## XXXVII. What Pope Leo finally decreed regarding those who were promoted free of charge by simoniacs.

Nevertheless we do not think it idle to set forth what the same venerable Pope Leo decreed in this matter. For when he had annulled all the ordinations of simoniacs by the authority of synodal force, immediately a tumult of great sedition arose from the multitude of Roman priests, so that not only they but many bishops as well were saying that almost all the basilicas had been stripped of their priestly services, and that the solemn masses in particular were to be entirely abandoned — to the overthrow of the Christian religion and the despair of all the faithful round about. What more? After long volumes of debate on all sides, it was at last suggested that the recently departed bishop of that same see, Clement of venerable memory, had decreed: that whoever had been consecrated by a simoniac, knowing at the very time of his ordination that the person to whom he had offered himself for promotion was a simoniac, should now perform forty days of penance, and should thereafter minister in the office of the order he had received. This sentence the venerable Leo immediately confirmed as binding, and under such penance he commanded all to continue henceforth in their received offices. For even today you can observe not a few who, having been ordained through various grades by such persons, were promoted by that same supreme pontiff to the summit of the episcopate. Nor was this done wrongly, because since canonical authority decrees that whoever has obtained a sacred dignity through money shall be deposed — both he himself and the one who ordained him — it is plainly evident that, condemning the one simoniacally ordained and the one who ordained him, the canon immediately fixed the limit of its judgment there, and did not extend the reckoning of the sentence to the one who might have been promoted by such a person. For if the author of this canon had thought that any person promoted by a simoniac ought to be deposed, he would, when noting the ordained and his ordainer, have consequently not been silent about those who could be promoted by him as well — so that he would naturally say: “Let him be deposed, and his ordainer,” and would straightway add: “and whoever has been ordained by him.” That he certainly did not say this signals to us that we ought not to think so either.
