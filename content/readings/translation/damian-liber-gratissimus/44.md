But these signs of miraculous power, which divine providence works either through heretics or through unworthy priests of the true faith, are granted not by reason of any personal merit of theirs but by virtue of their ministry; they by no means free such men from the punishments they deserve, nor do they excuse before the strict Judge the guilt of the crime committed. For where they reap glory, there they tumble into disgraceful ignominy, and the more they are exalted by the praises of flatterers, the more headlong is their plunge into the precipice of eternal damnation. And often such men, struck by a terrible death, show more plainly than daylight how truly reprobate they were, even while they appeared outwardly to be almost wonderworkers.

For the bishop of Fiesole, whom we mentioned a short while ago, was of such depravity that even his own brother — a layman — did not think himself worthy to maintain familiarity with him in honest decency; yet he blazed with such miraculous powers that, at the dedication of a single church, he is firmly attested by those who report it to have cured five demoniacs in the presence of all the people. But because he shone not by his own miracles but by those of another, at the approach of death he was marked beforehand by the punishing action of a burning fire upon his burning body. For a sore of malignant ulceration broke out pestilentially on his foot and spread to encompass the entire sole, shin, thigh, groin, and genitals together, creeping on toward the vital organs, so that — consuming his inward parts like the fuel of a dry log — it burned him with its hidden heat, so that this first fire might kindle what an unquenchable flame would afterward devour. Bound as he was by the excess of so intolerable and monstrous a disease, he is known to have neither undertaken proper penance nor been willing to remove from himself the company of women whose ministrations he continued to use shamelessly even then. Now, our Redeemer says that he cast out demons “by the finger of God,” and the finger of God is understood beyond doubt to be the Holy Spirit; it is therefore clearly recognized that demons are never expelled from the bodies of men except through the power of the Holy Spirit. From this it is plainly manifest that that bishop, though unworthy, though he was a simoniac and stained with the filth of licentious debauchery, could nonetheless not have put demons to flight in any way had he not possessed within himself the power of the Holy Spirit. For Samson, who contrary to the precepts of divine law had recourse to the harlots of a foreign nation — how could he have burst such mighty bonds, or slaughtered thousands of men unarmed and alone, had not the strength of the Holy Spirit fortified him? Moreover, that false prophet was not without the prophetic revelation of the Holy Spirit — he who, skilled in deceit, by carefully constructed lies persuaded the man of God returning from Bethel to eat and drink contrary to divine commandments, saying: “An angel spoke to me in the word of the Lord, saying: ‘Bring him back with you to your house, that he may eat bread and drink water’” \[1 Kings 13:18\]; and Scripture immediately adds: “He deceived him and brought him with him.” And a little further on: “While they were sitting at table, the word of the Lord came to the prophet who had brought him back, and he cried out” — namely the order of events by which he was to perish, because he had been disobedient to the voice of the Lord. Here it should be noted that the one whom Scripture first openly accuses of having deceived his brother is soon after unhesitatingly affirmed to have received the word of the Lord and prophesied most truly.
