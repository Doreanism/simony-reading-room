## XXIIII. That bishops who have been wrongly ordained possess the grace of consecration for others, but not for themselves.

Let us then grant — or rather assert — that a simoniac is a heretic; but a Novatianist is also a heretic, and a Donatist likewise a heretic. The simoniac sins against the Holy Spirit because he buys the gift of God; the Novatianist certainly sins against the same Holy Spirit because, even after penance, he despairs of the remission of the lapsed. The Donatist likewise sins against the Holy Spirit by separating himself from the Church, outside of which the Holy Spirit cannot be found. What reason is there, then, that while the ordination of those \[i.e., Novatianists and Donatists\] is decreed to be valid, that of the latter \[simoniacs\] is judged worthless — especially since we are not laboring to ensure that simoniacs remain in the honor they have wrongly purchased, but rather that those who were gratuitously consecrated by them should not lose the position of their rank? Here it must be noted how great is the difference in the situation of those of whom we are speaking: the Donatists promote Donatists, and the Novatianists promote Novatianists — that is, men entangled in the same errors — to advancement in ecclesiastical rank, whereas simoniacs ordain not simoniacs but rather innocent and Catholic persons. Against those men \[Donatists and Novatianists\] it could be urged not only that they were ordained by heretics, but moreover that they themselves were equally heretics. But the latter \[those ordained by simoniacs\] are by no means liable to the simoniacal heresy, even if they were regularly promoted by simoniacs with every element of venal commerce removed, and received their administrative office in those same churches where they also received the mystery of holy baptism. Whether persons of this kind should be barred from their honors can easily be weighed from the authority of blessed Leo, if we do not shrink from recording his decretal judgment here in his own words. For he says: “No reason permits that men should be counted among bishops who have neither been elected by the clergy, nor sought by the people, nor consecrated with the judgment of the metropolitan by the agreement of the provincial bishops.” Since, therefore, the question about a wrongly received honor often arises, who could doubt that those men \[so promoted\] are in no way able to confer what is not shown to have been properly conferred?
