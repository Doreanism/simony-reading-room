## X. That the Holy Spirit is given not by the merit of priests but by their ministry.

Was even that Caiaphas worthy to be the one through whom the Holy Spirit would speak the mystery of Christ’s life-giving death? For when he had said: *It is expedient that one man should die for the people, lest the whole nation perish* \[John 11:50\], the evangelist immediately added: *This he did not say of himself, but, being high priest that year, he prophesied* \[John 11:51\]. He plainly received the prophetic spirit only for a moment — not that he himself might understand what he was prophesying, but rather that he might more eagerly incite his fellow conspirators toward hastening the Lord’s death. He saw \[the truth\] for our benefit, not his own, and he opened a hidden treasury of wisdom which he himself did not know. For Caiaphas himself is found to be not altogether unconnected with simoniacs, if the course of the ancient history is carefully reviewed. Indeed, as Eusebius of Caesarea relates in his *Ecclesiastical History* — to repeat here the very same words — “The legal precepts having already ceased by that time through force and ambition, the honor of the pontificate was rendered to no one on the basis of merit of life or lineage, such that appointments were now changed year by year.” Then, adducing Josephus in evidence, he testifies that over a span of four years four high priests, each having served in the annual priesthood, succeeded one another in turn: “Valerius Gratus, having ejected Annas from the priesthood, designated Ishmael son of Phabi as high priest; but soon removing him as well, he substituted Eleazar, son of the high priest Ananas. After a year, however, he also removed him from office and handed the pontifical ministry to a certain Simon son of Camithus \[*Canifi*\], who having served no longer than a year himself, received as his successor Joseph, who also bore the name Caiaphas.” Behold — Caiaphas is understood to have received the priesthood neither freely nor in accordance with law, inasmuch as, with others ejected and violently cast out, he crept into the place of another’s dignity; and yet he received the spirit of prophecy — unworthy though he was —
