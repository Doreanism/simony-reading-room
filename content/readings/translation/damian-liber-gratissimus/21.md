## III. That just as there is one who baptizes, so too there is one and the same who principally consecrates.

If anyone here perhaps objects to me that baptism for the regeneration of humanity is one thing, and the consecration of ecclesiastical office another, I hold with respect to consecration entirely the same as I believe concerning baptism in this regard. For since baptism is the origin and beginning of every ecclesiastical sacrament, just as baptism belongs to God and not to man, so surely every ecclesiastical consecration belongs specially to him from whom the fullness of all blessings flows. Of whom the apostle says: “Who has blessed us with every spiritual blessing in the heavens.” Nor would baptism itself, or indeed any consecration, be held as something great, except in view of the Holy Spirit, who is given through those acts. For, as it is said, what is water but water? But the word is added to the element, and with the Spirit descending, it becomes a sacrament. Since therefore in both kinds of sanctification this is the whole and the magnificent thing — that the Holy Spirit is received both by those who are baptized and by those who are consecrated — just as baptism is ascribed not to human virtue or power but to God its author, so likewise every ecclesiastical consecration must in all respects be referred to him, since the manifold grace of God operates in both: that the former may obtain the remission of sins and the latter may advance to the orders of spiritual dignities; that the former, having put off the old self along with the deeds of error, may put on the new, while the latter, as priests of God, may be clothed with justice; that the former, like newborn infants, may desire the sincere and pure milk of the word, and the latter may attain to the measure of the full stature of Christ. It works in those so that they become children of adoption; in these, so that, as the same Spirit ordains, they may be set over others to beget the sons of God.

## IV. That the Lord received the rights of priesthood simultaneously with baptism.

Sincere and integral faith holds, therefore, that just as baptism, so likewise sacerdotal consecration is polluted by no stain of corrupt ministers and violated by no guilt of another’s offense. But however wicked, however entangled in crimes he who consecrates may be, he who is consecrated suffers no harm to the sacred gift on that account, nor is defrauded of any diminution of heavenly grace. For the mystery of consecration is conveyed to another not by the merit of the priest but by the office he performs, and one must not inquire into the manner of life of the one consecrating, but attend only to the ministry he has received.
