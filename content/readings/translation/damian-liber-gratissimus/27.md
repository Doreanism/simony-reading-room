It ought not therefore seem incredible if, in the holy Church — which is beyond all doubt the throne of God, the sanctuary of the Holy Spirit, and the vessel of all heavenly gifts — even an unworthy person receives that sacramental grace with which he does not accord either by intention or by the merit of his life; for assuredly this is not a matter of the merit of the one who gives or of the one who receives, but a gift of the supreme Bestower. For it is not written that the Spirit blows where there is worthiness, but rather it is said: *he blows where he wills* \[John 3:8\] — so that the spiritual grace conferred through ecclesiastical institution is attributed to the divine will rather than to human merits. Indeed, the Holy Spirit does not always come on account of merits, but he always abides in someone on account of merits — that is, to their salvation. Hence that word of the Truth: *If anyone loves me,* he says, *he will keep my word, and my Father will love him, and we will come to him and make our dwelling with him* \[John 14:23\]. For he who, after promising that he would come to those who love him, immediately added that he would also make his dwelling with them, makes it plain enough that God dwells in some and merely lodges as a guest in others. Similar to this is what he says: *Upon whom will my spirit rest, if not upon the humble and quiet one who trembles at my words?* \[Is. 66:2\] — as if to say plainly: he will indeed come upon others, but upon this one — because he is worthy — he will rest. For, as has also been said before us, the Holy Spirit, when welcomed and appeased, grants repose to the meek and humble of heart; but when opposed and resisted, he stirs up unrest in the harsh and proud. This unrest was signified by those tiny gnats under which the magicians of Pharaoh failed, saying: *This is the finger of God* \[Ex. 8:19\] — confessing that the Holy Spirit who was in Moses was working against them. It is fitting that the Holy Spirit — who is the third person of the Holy Trinity — is said to have been hostile to the unquiet in the third plague, where the plague of gnats is recounted. This unrest was experienced by Jehu, who, led by divine zeal, burned fiercely to avenge God’s enemies and especially the worshipers of Baal, yet did not abandon the golden gods that Jeroboam had worshiped.

## IX. What the doctors hold concerning baptism and the eucharist of the Lord’s body.

There are in truth three preeminent sacraments regularly celebrated in the holy Church: baptism, the saving mystery of the Lord’s body and blood, and the ordination of clerics. Now, concerning baptism, the blessed Augustine in his commentary on the Gospel of John, and concerning the eucharist of the Lord’s body, Paschasius in his own book, agree so closely in their treatment that they affirm neither that these mysteries are made better by good priests nor worse by bad ones. Whether these sacraments are performed by thieves or adulterers or even murderers, they differ in no respect whatsoever from the mysteries consecrated by holy priests.
