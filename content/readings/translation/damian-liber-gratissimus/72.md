If then the water that was despised was turned into a sacrifice to God, how precious a gift to God must we believe that money refused \[for simony\] to be? David made his libation by pouring out water; this emperor likewise offered a sweet sacrifice to God when he despised not water but gold for love of him. And because there were those who, set upon royal thrones, distrusted their ability to administer the governance entrusted to them unless they swelled the public treasury with the sale of churches — behold, almighty God, in whose hand rests the victory of conquerors, the scepters of kings, and the condition of all offices, without the revenues of such dishonorable acquisition subjected many foreign nations to his faithful servant, tamed under his rule the tyrannical ferocity of adversaries, bent the necks of proud enemies beneath him, granted him victory over the barbarian peoples round about, and gave him to trample the necks of rebellious and unbelieving subjects. So that it is clear that victory comes from heaven and is proved to depend not on gifts of money but to flow down from the bounty of grace above. Thus this emperor seems to have obtained a victory over the enemies of the catholic Church not inferior to that of Constantine Caesar. For Constantine crushed the dogma of the Arian sect with the arms of orthodox faith; this man destroyed the pestilence of the simoniac heresy by trampling down avarice. Constantine stood as champion of the faith so that Arius might not tear apart its unity; Henry came forward as conqueror of greed so that Simon might not occupy the seat of pestilence in the Church. Let others toil to weave with the pen of history the triumphs of kings, to proclaim with praises of studied laudation the glorious titles of wars and their brave deeds. To me this victory which we recalled above seems far nobler and incomparably more glorious — a victory in which no human blood is shed, no multitude of soldiers diminished, but the whole catholic Church spread throughout the boundaries of the Roman Empire is everywhere liberated from the chains of ancient condemnation as from a dreadful and very deep prison, so that, snatched to freedom by the emperor through divine power, she may freely sing to God: “You have broken my chains, O Lord; I will offer you a sacrifice of praise” \[Psalm 116:16–17\].

## XXXIX. The author inveighs against simoniacs.

I bring a charge against you, simoniacs, who have imposed upon us the heavy labor of this toilsome work. We do indeed defend your \[ordinations\], yet only in such a way that we condemn you utterly. We admit what is done through you in such a manner that we judge you abominable and deserving of the most extreme punishments of all the damned. Judas sold the Lord, believing him to be a mere man, but soon cast away the price of blood, about to pay a worthy penalty. You, however, having no doubt whatever about the divinity of the Holy Spirit, both attribute to him the transaction of sale and retain the profit of the sacrilege you have committed; and you who deserved to be punished make gain of the crimes you have perpetrated. To whom could I fittingly compare you, who hold the divine gifts not for yourselves but for others, and whose \[ministry\], though it brings an increase of salvation to others, turns to your own judgment and perdition? I see you plainly resembling the Jews, who, utterly ignorant of the divine law in their very marrow, have become throughout the world the scribes \[i.e., custodians of sacred texts\] for Christ’s faithful. You also seem to bear almost the likeness of bees, which, having labored to convey their wrought honey to sweeten human throats, themselves soon waste away and perish in their hunger.
