When the mother ran after her in a frenzy, she suddenly came upon the priest Marinus and, with great wailing and sobbing, burst out: “Alas, alas, father,” she said, “I could never have imagined that the wretched daughter of a wretched mother — the very one you yourself drew from the sacred font — should become the prey of blood-stained beasts, and, made food for wolves, should glut the greedy maw of savage rapacity.” The priest replied: “Fear not, woman, for if she is truly the one whom I baptized, no beast shall ever have power to devour her.” Then, with reckless and foolish boldness, he added: “For if it is I who washed her in the sacred laver of baptism, and the beast has torn her to pieces, then I will no longer believe that God reigns in heaven.” A wondrous thing: after nearly four hours, at a distance of more than eight miles, she was found unharmed and unhurt amid the thickets of the forest. Beside her stood the wolf, tame and harmless. And just as the lion was not permitted to violate the corpse of the prophet returning from Samaria, so this wolf did not dare to harm the girl. Indeed, as far as one can understand, it even appeared to stand guard over her willingly. When the girl was found, no injury whatsoever was discovered on her body, except that her legs and arms were scratched and bloodied by the thorns and brambles.

There was also another priest in these parts not long ago — a brother of that most reverend man Gerhard, who now presides over the monastery of Saint Donatus situated in the suburbs of the town of Imola. This priest led a common and nearly secular life, yet he had received from the Lord so great a grace that, whenever someone was reported to him as having been struck by a serpent, he would immediately hand to the messenger some water he had blessed, for him to drink; and once it was drunk, so great was the efficacy of divine power made manifest that the one who drank it, as if he himself had been specifically bitten by the snake, vomited out all the venom of the wounded man’s wound, and the one who had actually been struck was left entirely free of poison, his whole body purged of the pestilential fluid, safe and sound.

There was yet another priest of almost no account whatsoever, so limited in worldly wisdom that he did not even possess the least natural cleverness, and who could barely stammer out letters syllable by syllable in a rustic fashion. One evening a hawk carried off one of his hens, an injury which stirred him to the bitterest bile of rage — so thoroughly that he threatened the saint in whose name the church he presided over had been dedicated, saying that unless the saint restored to him the bird he had lost, he would no longer celebrate the ecclesiastical offices for him. And as if the madness of his tongue were not quite sufficient, he dared to go further with his hands and attacked the very altar with strokes of a rod. What more need be said? The next morning the hen returned, and except for its wings and head, stripped of every other feather, it bore not a single trace of injury on its whole body — demonstrating clearly that divine mercy does not entirely despise the cries of priests, however unhinged, seeing that the hawk received no license to devour the priest’s bird.

And what wonder is it if divine providence displays the power of miracles through priests of whatever sort, provided they are orthodox, when it often manifests signs of its power even through heretics themselves? For, to say nothing of other instances, what is written in the *Historia Tripartita* does not seem out of place to introduce here in its own words: “Meanwhile,” it says, “a fire that broke out in the city of Constantinople spread gradually until it reached the baths called the Achilleae, and then the district called Pelargus, so that the church of the Novatians was caught in the flames.
