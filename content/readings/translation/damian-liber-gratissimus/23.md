Add to this that, as canonical authority attests, the prohibition of rebaptism exists lest the name of the Holy Trinity, in whose name baptism was administered, seem to be annulled. But if this is the reason why no one ought to be rebaptized, to whom does it apply more plainly than to anyone ordained by a simoniac? — for the simoniac consecrates not merely in the name of the Holy Trinity but observes every detail of the Catholic order of consecration. For though a simoniac becomes a heretic through his corrupt commerce, he is nonetheless orthodox in faith, and his condemnation is seen to arise from ambition rather than from unbelief. Moreover, even if one examines closely Peter’s sentence pronounced against Simon himself, Simon’s faith is not found blameless: “May your money perish with you, because you thought the gift of God could be purchased with money” \[Acts 8:20\]. For when he says “you thought the gift of God could be purchased with money,” he clearly shows that Simon’s faith was such that, had he paid the price, he would not unreasonably have bargained to receive the Holy Spirit for the working of miracles.

## VI. That an ordination, if it is Catholic, is also valid.

The simoniacs of our own time, since they cannot hope to become distinguished by miracles, do not desire the Holy Spirit or his gifts; inflamed rather by ambition to obtain power, they pant only after the pinnacle of rank. And so in matters of faith they are sound, yet in their monetary dealings they are caught in the snares of simoniacal condemnation. Two founders of this heresy are recorded as having broken out disastrously — one under the prophetic, the other under the apostolic dispensation — namely Gehazi, who appears to be the master of those who sell, and Simon, who appears to be the founder of those who buy. Their followers, therefore, just as they do not differ in their error, so also are not distinguished in their condemnation. Yet if these men receive a Catholic ordination, they perfectly receive the sacred office of the dignity they undeservedly approach. For the Holy Spirit is of equal power whether his grace is sold or freely given. Nor does the corrupt traffic of human perversity cause divine power to lose the effect of its own authority. Indeed, even our Savior, after he had been sold and the bag of that most pestilential traitor \[Judas\] was already swollen with the price, restored the severed ear of the servant Malchus with his own hands while still in the grip of his persecutors.
