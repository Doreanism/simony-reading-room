For in that dove which descended upon the Lord after baptism, he received together with the sacrament of baptism also the rights of the true priesthood — the oil of gladness being poured upon him, of which the Psalmist sings: “God, your God, has anointed you with the oil of gladness above your companions.” Our Redeemer is shown to have received the mystery of priesthood simultaneously with baptism, because after his baptism he immediately began to preach, to choose disciples, and to shine forth with new miracles — which, if the Gospel narrative is carefully examined, he is nowhere recorded to have done before. Hence it is that the holy Church, taking her norm of institution from her own head, faithfully observes it: that, in the likeness of the Lord himself, she does not admit any person to the priesthood before the age of thirty. For unless sure faith held that the Lord had received the priesthood simultaneously with baptism, why would canonical authority so emphatically forbid anyone to aspire to the priestly office before the age at which he himself was baptized? Indeed, why should the example of the Lord’s age be applied to the reception of the priestly dignity, if it were not believed that the Lord had received his baptism without receiving the priesthood at the same time? For just as baptism, so also the priesthood he received on the side of his humanity for our salvation — of both of which sacraments he exists, insofar as he is God, both the author and consecrator. Of him the apostle says: “Where the forerunner has entered on our behalf, having become a high priest forever according to the order of Melchizedek.” Moreover, the holy apostles themselves are found to have been consecrated by the Lord nowhere other than in the reception of baptism. They are not recorded as having been visibly immersed in the water of baptism, but rather carried out the obedience of baptizing that was enjoined upon them, since they were indeed commanded to baptize believers in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit. Furthermore, when it was said to them by the Lord, “John baptized with water, but you will be baptized with the Holy Spirit not many days from now,” it is established that they then received the mystery of perfect baptism and of all-encompassing consecration together, when the Holy Spirit came upon them in the diversity of tongues. Since therefore at the very beginning of human redemption both kinds of sacrament appear to have been received simultaneously — by the Lord and by the apostles alike — it is clear that baptism is the foundation and beginning of ecclesiastical consecration in such a way that he who is acknowledged to hold the primacy of baptism is likewise, without any ambiguity whatsoever, to be believed the author of consecration.

## V. That there is no reason, since no one dares to be rebaptized, why one ordained by a simoniac should be deposed or reconsecrated.

This being so, I do not see what reason there can be — since one baptized even by a heretic is not rebaptized — why one promoted by a simoniac should, as is said, either be deposed or reconsecrated. For if a baptism given by a murderer, an adulterer, or even a heretic is to be held valid — on account of that Gospel text, “Upon whom you see the Spirit descending and remaining, this is he who baptizes” — there is absolutely no reason why we should not return equally in the case of consecrations to the same author of both sacraments, and say equally: “This is he who consecrates.” For did that dove come upon the mediator between God and humanity with the power of baptizing and not also with the power of consecrating?
