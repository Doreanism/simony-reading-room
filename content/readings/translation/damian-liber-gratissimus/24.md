For the one who desired them was unworthy; but in him from whom they were expected, the effect of his power was not lacking. Further, even when suspended on the cross — after he had given up the spirit, while the earth shook, while the sun was darkened, while the veil was moreover rent, the stones split, and the dead rose — all these things show plainly that he who hung in torment was no less powerful than he is now, exalted at the right hand of the Father’s glory. In the same way, therefore, we must believe of the Holy Spirit: that he is of one and the same power whether he is seen in some sense subject to purchase or whether he is bestowed by the free laying on of hands. Just as our Redeemer, even while he was being sold and suffering, could not fall short of his own majesty, so too the Holy Spirit, even when the bitterness of being put up for sale intrudes, knows no diminishment of his own power. For though any priest may appear to act externally, it is Christ himself — who is the true priest and supreme pontiff — who, as people approach him toward their various ends, dispenses his gifts to them. These gifts come to some as salvation, to others as condemnation. Yet what is given is altogether good, even if the one who receives it is guilty. For surely a good physician would not administer poison to the sick. Was not that morsel which the Lord offered to Judas good? But what was the means of salvation became for him the material of damnation, because one who was not at peace received the instrument of peace. We all know plainly that the mystery of the Eucharist, which we receive from the sacred altar whether we are righteous or sinners, is altogether good. The good man does not receive it better, nor the bad man worse. And yet the Apostle says that one who eats and drinks unworthily eats and drinks judgment upon himself, not discerning the body of the Lord \[1 Corinthians 11:29\]. If, then, what the unworthy person receives is still the body of the Lord, it is clear that a good thing is turned to the ruin of the wicked — a thing that was provided for the good as salvation. Nor is it to be called a bad thing because it harms, nor has it ceased to be a sacrament because one who is accursed received it. Rather it must be asserted that the same thing which was prepared for the good as a remedy of salvation became for the unworthy an occasion of death. We must therefore believe without hesitation that, if the consecration of any ecclesiastical order takes place within the Catholic Church — that is, in the unity of orthodox faith, so that faith is sound on both sides — whatever is conferred by a good man through a good man is likewise efficaciously bestowed by a bad man through a bad man; because this sacrament does not depend on the merit of the minister or recipient, but on the order of ecclesiastical institution and the invocation of the divine name. And since, just as there is one who baptizes, so beyond doubt there is one and the same who consecrates, this is bestowed by the generous kindness of Christ, which the recipient does not merit. For he who makes his sun rise over the good and the evil, and sends rain on the just and the unjust \[Matthew 5:45\], what wonder is it if he pours out the shower of his grace even upon a sandy shore? What is strange if he strikes even the eyes of the blind with the rays of his splendor, so that even they may rightly sing what that unhappy Balaam sang: “The man speaks, whose eye is opened; the hearer of the words of God speaks, who knows the teaching of the Most High and sees the vision of the Almighty, who falls down with his eyes uncovered” \[Numbers 24:3–4, 16\].
