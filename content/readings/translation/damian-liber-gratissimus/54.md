what we say may be seen to be drawn not from our own inventions but from the rules of the fathers. He says, among other things, to the Emperor Anastasius: “Let the most sacred breast of your serenity know, in accordance with the custom of the Catholic Church, that none of those whom Acacius baptized or whom he ordained as priests or deacons according to the canons shall be touched by any harm on account of the name of Acacius, as though the grace of the sacrament transmitted through a corrupt man might thereby appear less firm. For even baptism conferred far from the Church, whether given by an adulterer or a thief, reaches the one who receives it undiminished, because that voice which sounded through the dove excludes every stain of human pollution. This is declared by the words: ‘This is he who baptizes in the Holy Spirit and fire.’ For if the rays of this visible sun, when they pass through the most fetid places, are not stained by any defilement of contact, much less is the power of him who made this visible sun touched by any unworthiness of his minister. For even Judas, though he was sacrilegious and a thief, whatever he did among the apostles by virtue of the commission entrusted to him — the benefits given through an unworthy man suffered no detriment on that account, as the Lord himself declared in the plainest terms: ‘The scribes and Pharisees sit on the seat of Moses; do what they say; but do not do what they do, for they say and do not do.’ Whatever, therefore, any minister in the Church appears to perform for the benefit of people in the exercise of his office, all of this is entirely contained and fulfilled by the divine effect — as Paul, through whom Christ speaks, affirms: ‘I planted, Apollos watered, but God gave the growth.’ And a little later, the same Anastasius \[continues\]: Therefore even this man, whose name we say must be suppressed, harmed only himself by his wicked administration of good things. For the inviolable sacrament which was given through him obtained for others the perfection of its power.

Behold: this apostolic man is seen to have taken up our cause as his own, as though he were standing on our side, so that he appears to have left us almost nothing to say on the matter of this question. Let us go forth then, and shamelessly resist so great an authority; let the new censors not be satisfied with what seemed good to the ancient fathers, so that, while all discretion is abandoned, even piety itself is lost. For certain men, while they seek to appear holier than they are, strive so to cut away what is corrupted that they set about to tear apart what is sound as well; and in order to appear to persist rigorously in the censure of justice before the applause of men, they do not scruple to wander cruelly from the path of piety.
