The same punishment is not due to a servant who sins in domestic matters under his master’s authority and to one who has shaken off the yoke of servitude and chosen the dominion of a stranger. For although these men \[the Novatianists\] may in some respect appear to offend against the rule of faith, they do not do so to such a degree as to be charged with denying the Holy Spirit through heretical wickedness. And therefore, when they return, since they have sinned, hands are laid upon them; but because they have not denied \[the faith\], they are not deprived of the office of priestly rank. The Arians, on the other hand, who openly and utterly fight against the divinity of the Holy Spirit, cannot, upon returning to the Catholic Church, remain in the dignity of an order ill-received. For they believe, as their sacrilegious profession makes plain, that the Father is Creator, the Son a creature, and damnably assert that the Holy Spirit is a creature of a creature. Since, therefore, they do not possess in faith the power of the Holy Spirit by which every order of ecclesiastical dignity is perfected, an ordination performed among them is adjudged null and void by canonical authority. But it seems not beside the point, if Pope Innocent left anything on this matter in his decrees, to insert it here under the same headings. He says: “The Arians and other such pestilences — because we receive their laypersons converted to the Lord under the guise of penance and the sanctification of the Holy Spirit through the laying on of hands — it does not seem fitting to receive their clerics together with the dignity of any priestly ministry. For since we permit only baptism to those whose baptism was at any rate performed in the name of the Father and the Son and the Holy Spirit, we do not consider that they possessed the Holy Spirit from that baptism and those rites; since, when the authors of these sects departed from the Catholic faith, they lost the fullness of the Spirit which they had received, and they cannot give the fullness of the Spirit, which operates most especially in ordinations — a fullness which I would say they lost rather through the faithlessness of their impiety than through faith. How is it possible that we should consider their profane priests worthy of the honors of Christ, when we receive their laypersons as imperfect, as I said, with the image of penance in order to receive the grace of the Holy Spirit?” In these decretal words it is plainly conveyed that in the promotion of clerics the faith of the ordainer is not unreasonably taken into account, upon which it depends whether the ordination itself is judged valid or null and void. For what the ordainer holds in faith, the ordained receives as a gift. Indeed, even if the Arians possessed right faith, even if they were entangled in some error of any other kind of wickedness, a consecration performed among them would not perish. Here it is to be noted how great a reverence is owed to the invocation of the divine name, since even those baptized by men whose faith, so to speak, so perfidiously condemns them, are nonetheless not dared to be rebaptized.
