Since, therefore, all these men we have mentioned are said to have been consecrated by simoniacs, and yet, persevering in the offices they had received all the way to the end of their lives, they so pleased the divine judgment that wondrous signs have borne wondrous testimony to their admirable merits — it is established beyond all doubt that the purchase by the one who promoted does not prejudice the case where the one promoted was ignorant of the transaction of venality; nor does the guilt of the seller flow over onto him who is not bound by the crime of selling. And indeed it might in some manner be tolerated, however barely, if the claim of falsehood only overturned the order of the present life — were it not that it also swept away from the past age all hope and substance of Christian piety. For they say that what then appeared to be a church was a mere house of stone, what was believed to be an altar was nothing but bare rock, that priests and those seen constituted in the other orders were wholly laymen and utterly devoid of all the powers of spiritual sacrament, that what was believed to be the body and blood of the Lord was simply bread and wine — earthly substance, suffused with no power of the Holy Spirit. All of it, therefore, was false and empty, entirely removed from the truth of Christian redemption. It follows from this that our fathers, whether they recognized these things as false or whether they took falsehood for truth, have perished without distinction by an inescapable necessity. For what does it profit to know a peril from which there is no escape? Or how can someone reach the fountain of truth if he delights — though unknowingly — in wandering through crooked and winding paths? Was Simon alone, then, able to dam the flowing streams of divine grace, standing as a barrier between God and man to separate the human race from communion with its Creator? Far be it, far be it, that a false man should undo what the true God and man has established. Far be it that the wickedness of one man should overwhelm all humanity, and that human perversity should be able to blot out the divine gifts. But what, I ask, shall we say about those whom we now see occupying episcopal thrones for some forty or fifty years after their trafficking in this nefarious commerce? For, to say nothing of their predecessors, whether more recent or more remote, whom that same thief from another source produced, all those who had been ordained by these usurpers either have already been taken from this life or have been removed from the exercise of their offices by the impediment of dim-sighted old age. Hence it is necessary that in their dioceses the sacred mysteries are celebrated only by those clerics who received their order from those simoniacs who are still living. What then is to be thought about these clerics, what is to be admitted? Are the many thousands of souls in their dioceses to be said to perish, they who are being deceived by the empty show of a sacrament from these men who have no sacramental grace? Is the people to be told to seek out priests from other dioceses, or to travel abroad themselves to receive the sacraments from those who were not ordained by simoniacs? But the sacred canons powerfully resist this, and they exclude from communion those who cross the boundaries of their parishes without a formal letter from their bishop.
