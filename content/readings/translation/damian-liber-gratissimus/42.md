Hearing this, the companions of the wretched woman hastened at once to the church at Fiesole and there before the altar of the blessed Romulus awaited divine mercy. But there too the demon, when questioned, answered that he would by no means depart unless Bishop Raimbaldus came. And while the aforesaid bishop was making haste to come, in the meantime the demon was saying: ‘Woe, woe! See, the bishop is now mounting his horse; see, he is now passing before that house, and that one.’ And those present, taking note of the places, verified that everything was truly as the lying spirit claimed. At last the bishop arrived, invoked the name of Christ, and immediately drove the evil spirit from the possessed body and sent the woman home free and unharmed.

At that same time too — when the simoniac plague was spreading lethally throughout the whole body of the Church and freely sprouting up through every ecclesiastical rank — there was a certain priest named Marinus, the father of this most reverend and honorable Eleuchadius who now presides over the monastery of the Blessed Mother of God and Ever-Virgin Mary established near the walls of the city of Faenza. This Marinus, then, was not ashamed to hold publicly, as a lawful wife, the mother of the Eleuchadius we have mentioned. To this priest, divine providence had plainly granted such great grace that if he were to sprinkle holy water blessed by him upon crops in the fields, no further plague of worms, or caterpillars, or locusts would remain to harm the grain, but they would all depart in a mass as soon as the blessing of the holy water had been sprinkled upon them. On another occasion, a certain lord named Hieremias, a neighbor of the same priest Marinus — related to him by kinship of blood, but utterly hostile in will — was laboring under the bitterest hatred of a most severe quarrel with his adversary. Among the other losses inflicted by that enmity, fearing also that the keenness of his hunting dogs might be bewitched by a hostile spell, he asked the said priest to consecrate a bucket full of water by exorcism and bring it to him, saying he judged it quite necessary for certain uses of his own. And when the priest had done what was commanded in good faith, Hieremias — wise in doing evil, but utterly ignorant of the power of the sacrament — did not fear to cast pearls before swine. For the fool mixed the holy water with bran and offered it to his dogs to eat, so that they might not be put under a spell by witchcraft. But by a different judgment of divine providence, the outcome was the reverse of his intention. For the evil which he sought to ward off by evil means he incurred instead; and while he recklessly sought to look after his brute animals, he rather harmed his own soul. For when the dogs were admitted to feed and had barely parted their eager jaws at the first rush, not yet touching the foul food with their tongues, but having caught a whiff of it with their nostrils, they all fell dead to the ground at once. Then, doubly confounded — guilty of sin and stricken by the loss of his property — he made known to the priest Marinus the whole sequence of events as they had truly been, and tearfully sought that penance be given him for such audacity and such sacrilege. Marinus refused to arrogate to himself the judgment of so great a crime, and however much he was pressed by entreaties on all sides, he would agree to nothing else except that, if he truly sought penance, he must necessarily make his way to the apostolic see. Compelled by necessity, Hieremias at last obeyed, and at the Roman Church received the judgment of penance which he sought.
