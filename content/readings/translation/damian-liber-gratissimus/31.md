\[Caiaphas\] received the prophetic spirit though unworthy. Whence blessed Jerome in his commentary on the evangelist Matthew says: “Moses had commanded by God’s order that the high priests should succeed their fathers, and the line of descent be traced through the priests. Josephus reports that this same Caiaphas redeemed the high priesthood from Herod for a single year’s term by payment of money.” For, to put it thus, a lamp is placed in the hand of a blind man — not that he himself might see, but that he might minister the light to others. For Caiaphas received the prophetic spirit not by reason of his own merit, but by reason of the office he held. For kingship and priesthood are recognized as instituted by God, and therefore, even if the person of the administrator is found altogether unworthy, the office itself — which is wholly good — is sometimes attended by appropriate grace. Hence it is that Nebuchadnezzar, after he cast the three young men into the fiery furnace, though many others were present, was yet the only one able to behold the fourth, whose appearance was like to the Son of God. For the Holy Spirit is to be believed to have opened the eyes of his mind, so that he alone saw what was hidden from others, and what he saw he also of his own accord acknowledged. Hence Pharaoh saw the dream through which he took precautions against the coming famine, lest Egypt, unable to endure hunger, be ruined. Hence Abimelech merited the oracle of divine address and heard that he must return Sarah to her husband lest he suddenly die. For kings and priests, even if some of them are reprobate by reason of a notably wicked life, are nonetheless found to be called gods and anointed ones on account of the sacrament of the ministry they have received. Hence the Hebrew slave is commanded by the law to be presented to the gods — that is, to the priests; hence it is said elsewhere: “Do not speak evil of the gods” \[Exodus 22:28\], that is, do not detract from the priests; hence Saul, though he had already been cast down by divine judgment from the height of his royal throne, was nonetheless still called the Lord’s anointed until the very day of his death. In the law too it is commanded that every firstborn of the male sex be called holy to the Lord. But if the merit of each one is carefully considered, shall Esau rightly be called holy? Of whom God’s own voice says: “Esau I have hated” \[Romans 9:13\]. Shall Ammon be holy — who, when he burned shamelessly with lust for his sister, drenched with his own blood the sword of his brother Absalom, that avenger of incestuous crime? Shall Manasseh, shall Ahaziah rightly be called holy — one of whom, as Scripture testifies, worshipped all the host of heaven, the other of whom, when he consulted Baal-Zebub, the god of Ekron, regarding the length of his life, ended his life in a pitiable death, despairing in his own faithlessness? For it is one thing to be holy by the merits of one’s life, and another to be called holy by reason of the condition of one’s ministry.
