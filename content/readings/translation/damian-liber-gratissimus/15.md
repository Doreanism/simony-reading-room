# PETER DAMIAN’S LIBER GRATISSIMUS.

EDITED BY

L. DE HEINEMANN.

When in 1046, after the gravest and most lamentable upheavals in the Roman Church, Emperor Henry III had ordered the enthroned installation of Clement II, whom he had elected to the apostolic see, this most benevolent pope and the sound portion of the clergy who attended him — those inclined toward reform of the Church — labored with the utmost energy to correct the evils that had subverted the Church and to restore the ancient Catholic order. They therefore began most vigorously to inveigh against those who had received episcopal office or other orders by payment of money. Pope Clement had already treated the matter of what was called the simoniac heresy with his bishops at the Roman synod he held in January 1047. There a most difficult question arose: whether those who had been ordained by simoniac bishops — men who had themselves scorned to purchase their ordination with money, yet had not been ignorant that their consecrator was tainted with the simoniac stain — ought to be condemned and removed from office, or not. At that time it was decreed that whoever had been consecrated by a simoniac, not being ignorant at the time of his ordination that the one to whom he had presented himself for promotion was a simoniac, should now perform forty days of penance and thereafter minister in the office of the order he had received.

Afterward, however, Pope Leo IX annulled all ordinations performed by simoniacs. But since an uproar of great sedition immediately arose from the multitude of Roman priests — so much so that not only they but many of the bishops said that nearly all the basilicas were being stripped of their priestly ministers — the pope was compelled to confirm the decree of his predecessor Clement II. Nevertheless, at the councils held at Rome in 1050 and 1051 the question was again and again debated. For among the most distinguished of Leo IX’s advisers there were those who maintained that men consecrated by simoniacs must be reordained, the head and most eloquent champion of whom was Humbert, cardinal bishop of Silva Candida.
