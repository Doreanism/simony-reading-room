We have taken care to adduce this example from antiquity against those who assert that a consecration performed by simoniacs is altogether void, and who contend that those ordained by them differ nothing from laymen. For if that preliminary play, in which the boys had merely pretended to be priests or ministers, was held in such regard that the bishop committed them under oath to God to their parents to be raised by the Church, and did not think it right even for them to remain in the secular order — boys who had fashioned themselves as clerics under the mere appearance of a shadowy imitation — why are those said to differ nothing from laymen who are known to have received the mystery of consecration from whatever persons, in accordance with the rite of the Church? If a mere imitation sets those boys apart from laymen, how can the false vanity of men claim that those who have truly and regularly received all things differ nothing from them? For “whatever things,” as the Apostle says, “were written, were written for our instruction” \[Romans 15:4\]. Moreover, in his Moralia, if I recall correctly, blessed Gregory is found to have said that a simoniac is caught between two dangers: whether he abandons the flock entrusted to him or remains in an unlawfully obtained office, he cannot escape the snares of sin; and without defining either course of action explicitly, he asserts only that one should swim away as quickly as possible from whichever situation seems the less grave. If, then, a simoniac is, as they say, a layman, in what does he sin, I ask, if he abandons the exercise of priestly dignity? If he does not possess the sacrament of priesthood in any way, why is he separated from pastoral ministry with guilt — a ministry to which he is not bound by the bond of a sacrament? But tell me, brothers — I speak in all charity — if simoniacs and those ordained by them differ nothing from laymen, as you assert, what is it that the sacred canons decree that simoniacs are to be deposed in a synodal council? Surely if a layman is found to have usurped priestly rights, does he require a solemn synodal proceeding in order to be formally deposed? Will it not suffice if he is corrected by some private discipline, to be broken from his presumptuous audacity by simple words alone? After deposition, will a simoniac be able to receive the remedy of penance through the laying on of hands, to bear secular military service, to take a wife, to engage safely in commercial dealings? For even if they lose the exercise \[of their order\], they are nonetheless unable to lose the sacrament of ordination. Therefore, since you must necessarily hold that all these things cannot apply to simoniacs — things which you would not deny are available to any layman — you must confess that those for whom you plainly see different things are fitting differ from one another by the mutual variation of their conditions.
