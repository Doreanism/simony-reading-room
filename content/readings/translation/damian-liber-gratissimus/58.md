And lest he should leave even this point too lightly impressed, and lest he should more fully instruct a slower mind in what was being said, he did not shrink from repeating what he had plainly stated, saying: “The dove is therefore sold, because the laying on of hands, through which the Holy Spirit is received, is offered for a price.” If, then, the Holy Spirit is received through that laying on of hands which is offered for a price, what less of the sacrament is found in simoniacs than in priests promoted without payment — except that the very fountain which they pour out for others’ salvation flows to their own damnation, and, after the manner of vipers, they die in giving birth to their young? For what wonder is it if good ministers come forth from bad ordainers in spiritual ministries, when something not altogether unlike this often happens even in carnal generation? It is a well-known fact that sighted children are often born of blind parents, and that upright and vigorous offspring succeed parents who are crooked and infirm. Clearly, neither does the virtue of the ordainers provide any increase to those who do not fear to walk in crooked ways, nor does their depravity hinder those who walk in the path of upright conduct. For what is known to have harmed Samuel, because he was Eli’s disciple? Or what profited Nicholas, because apostolic election raised him to the grade of levitical ministry \[Acts 6:5\]? The latter indeed received the laying on of the apostolic hand together with Stephen and yet perished; the former lived amid the wickedness of Hophni and Phinehas, and because he was a prophet faithful to God, this was known to all. Wherefore it was only right that those who are all aflame to depose everyone together should at least preserve that dispensation of measured moderation \[noted\] above from Innocent \[I\]. For when that pope was disputing concerning those ordained by heretics, he added after much else: “Whenever sin is committed by the people or by a crowd, because the punishment cannot be visited upon all on account of their great number, it is customarily allowed to pass unpunished.”

## XXVIII. What great absurdity follows for those who dispute unskillfully.

When, moreover, in such matters was discernment ever more pressingly necessary than now, when scarcely any church is found that appears untouched by these persons? Is it judged more wholesome to be deprived of the divine mysteries than to make use of the ministry of a priest deemed invalid? But the world, now grown old, cannot embrace a new teaching, and apart from the custom it has grown up with, it considers it shameful to absent itself for long from the offices of the Church. Moreover, there arises among them an intolerable confusion, which casts no small darkness upon wavering minds and blunts the keenness of understanding for those who are excessively inquisitive. This occurs, certainly, when it is said that if any lower grade is received from a simoniac, then whatever is afterward conferred by a good bishop is properly to be counted wholly null and void. For, they say, one who does not hold the lower orders cannot aspire to the higher grades.
