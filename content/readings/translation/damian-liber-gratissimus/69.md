## XXXVI. He addresses the bishops, so that through them a discerning judgment may be suggested to the supreme see.

Wherefore I beg you, O faithful pontiffs, rise up against this impious teaching with the burning zeal of divine holiness and unanimously oppose this new heresy now first emerging. Let the mouth of those who speak iniquity be stopped, and let the hand of sinners not shake the foundation of the apostolic faith. Let the ecclesiastical teaching be preserved in its own purity, nor let it be permitted that the turbulent cunning of worldly-wise men disturb it. The enemy rises up at night to scatter tares through Christ’s field; you, walking in the day, uproot with the hoe of sound doctrine what has been evilly sown. The Philistines stop up the wells at night; let Isaac hasten to clear them out by day \[cf. Genesis 26\]. The nocturnal thief attempts to break into Christ’s sanctuary; let the tongue of his watchdogs be roused to bark protectively. But if perhaps someone from your own order should come forth who presumes to teach such impieties, the more harshly must you rebuke him, since he who ought to have been a guardian of the Church strives to attack it. Suggest, therefore, to our lord the most blessed pope that he extend the vigor of ecclesiastical severity against the guilty in such a way that, in accordance with the wishes of indiscriminate men, he does not also involve the innocent in the same sentence. For a judgment appears to run wild when it has been pronounced for the death of children on account of the father’s crime. Let him be animated by divine powers to destroy simoniacs, as he has already begun — but not to the extent of allowing their ordinations to fall under the immoderate judgment of certain men. Indeed, let him who holds the keys of the Church in Peter’s stead rise up most especially against this new teaching, and let him pierce with a sentence worthy of condemnation those who introduce this perversity; and let him who steers the helm of the net entrusted to the fishermen take care under this dispensation to separate the bad fish from the good, so that together with the bad he does not also add the good to the condemned. Let him say to indiscriminate men what the father of the household is read to have said to his servants: “Lest perhaps,” he said, “gathering the tares you root up the wheat along with them” \[Matthew 13:29\]. So that afterwards the tares may be gathered in bundles for burning, while the wheat is gathered into the granary. For surely when the blessed Apostle \[Peter\] struck Ananias and Sapphira with a sentence of fitting punishment \[Acts 5:1–11\], did he decree that their children or kinsfolk should also be punished? The sentence leveled at Simon himself \[Acts 8:20\] did not pass over to those who were perhaps able to draw out something of the truth from him. For when it is said: “Your money be with you in perdition” \[Acts 8:20\], the money is struck along with the dealer. But where the money is absent, the blow is not to be feared.
