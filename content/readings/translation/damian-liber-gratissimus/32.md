For the sacrament of grace is one thing, and grace itself is another. The sacrament of grace, God Almighty gives even through the wicked, but grace itself He gives only through Himself. For God often, as has been said, bestows His Spirit from the bounty of His clemency even upon those whom He does not acknowledge by reason of the merit of a depraved way of life. Did not those men undoubtedly have the Spirit of God, who come before the tribunal of the judge crying: “Lord, did we not prophesy in your name,” and the rest that follows \[Matthew 7:22\]? To whom the reply is immediately given: “Depart from me, you workers of iniquity; I do not know you” \[Matthew 7:23\]. Since these men testify that they possessed both the prophetic spirit and yet undergo the sentence of rejection, it is plainly shown that in some cases the Holy Spirit is not excluded on account of the wickedness of a life, nor is a damnable life excused on account of the Holy Spirit that they have irreverently misused. For just as, by the Lord’s testimony, “you do not know where \[the wind\] comes from or where it goes” \[John 3:8\], so too you cannot know why the Holy Spirit sometimes comes even upon reprobate men and through them brings forth an indication of His will. For since the Apostle says that the spiritual man judges all things and is judged by no one \[1 Corinthians 2:15\], who can judge the Holy Spirit Himself if He does not shrink from descending, by some arcane discretion known to Himself alone, even into the wicked — He who did not even will to deliver from the hands of the impious the Son of God, coessential and coeternal with Himself? Hence it is said through Solomon: “For the spirit of wisdom is benign, and did not deliver the accursed man from his lips” \[Wisdom 1:6\]. The Holy Spirit is fittingly called benign, for He renders good to those who deserve evil and bestows the gift of His grace upon those deserving punishment. But who under the name of the accursed one in this passage is to be understood, if not our Redeemer? — who, so that He might loose us from the bond of the curse, did not refuse to be subject to curses. Hence Paul also says: “Christ redeemed us from the curse of the law, having become a curse for us — for it is written: ‘Cursed is everyone who hangs on a tree’ — so that the blessing of Abraham might come to the Gentiles in Christ Jesus, that we might receive the promise of the Spirit through faith” \[Galatians 3:13–14\]. The benign Holy Spirit, therefore, “did not deliver the accursed man from his lips,” because He effectively fulfilled in due time, through the exhibition of events, all the sufferings and torments of the Lord’s passion and death which He had previously foretold concerning Christ through the mouths of the prophets. For in a sense He would have delivered Him from His lips, if He had not brought to pass through the order of events what He had announced concerning Him in prophetic oracles. Here it should be noted how deeply and mystically the statements of the two teachers harmonize. For what the one said — that the spirit did not deliver the accursed man from its lips — is the same as what the other asserted: that we might receive the promise of the Spirit through faith. And to show the wise man of what accursed one he was speaking, having set forth the preceding sentence, he immediately added: “For God is witness of his reins and the true searcher of his heart, and the hearer of his tongue” \[Wisdom 1:6\].
