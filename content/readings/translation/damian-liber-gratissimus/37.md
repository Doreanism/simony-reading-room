Leaving aside the mystery by which divine providence is understood to have worked first in hidden ways within their hearts, they are recorded to have received the Holy Spirit by these manifest gifts: first when the Lord breathed on them and said, “Receive the Holy Spirit” \[John 20:22\]; second when fire appeared over them in the form of tongues \[Acts 2:3\]. Yet even before these signs of so evident a bestowal — indeed at the very beginning of their new calling — they are known to have been divinely inspired. For how could they have left behind all that they saw with bodily eyes and hastened toward invisible things at the command of a single voice, unless the grace of the Holy Spirit had already shone within their inner eyes? Hence Paul also says: “Whoever does not have the Spirit of Christ does not belong to him” \[Romans 8:9\]. This apostolic verdict, although it had not yet been written down, was nonetheless without doubt already true. They would in no way have given themselves to be disciples of Christ in order to belong to him, had they not already possessed the first-fruits of his Spirit. The hand of divine generosity, therefore, gave again and again what it had already given to his faithful, so that by repeating its gift it might kindle more fervently and flood more abundantly those cold and parched hearts. So indeed, so is human nature — long consumed by the squalor of harmful old age — to be reformed to the flower of new life by certain stages of growth. If therefore, according to what has been set out above, not only can no one be established in any lower grade but no one can even be baptized without the Holy Spirit, then whoever is promoted to the office of the priesthood receives not the Holy Spirit anew, but rather an increase of the Spirit he already possesses. For just as one soul is diffused through all the members of the body, so the holy universal Church is quickened by the one Spirit of God. Hence Paul also says to the Corinthians: “For as the body is one and has many members, and all the members of the body, though they are many, are one body, so also is Christ. For in one Spirit we were all baptized into one body” \[1 Corinthians 12:12–13\]. Now the soul, being one, gives life to all the members, but does not impart the same faculty to each; to some it grants by a kind of prerogative what it does not give to others. For while the other members live through it in common, the eye receives from it specially not only life but also sight; the ear receives hearing; the heart receives understanding; the nostril receives smell; the tongue receives speech. And though the same soul is in no way different in itself, it nonetheless assigns different offices to the individual members. So too the Holy Spirit, while bestowing faith on all who are reborn in Christ — as it were infusing life into all the members of the Church, as the divine voice says: “The righteous shall live by faith” \[Romans 1:17\] — when it promotes certain ones to the ministries of ecclesiastical dignity, grants special gifts to those higher members, as it were. Furthermore, the soul does not discern whether the eyes are bleary, the ears misshapen, or the nose crooked.
