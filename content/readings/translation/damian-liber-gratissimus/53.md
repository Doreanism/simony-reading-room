Behold: by the authentic judgment of Pope Leo, whoever has not been chosen by the consent of clergy and people, nor consecrated by the agreement of the provincial bishops with the judgment of the metropolitan, is not to be counted among bishops; and yet those who were ordained by such men in their proper churches with the judgment of the presiding authorities are by no means to be deprived of the honor of the rank assigned to them — that is, so that one who rushed to the episcopal office with such great usurpation that the wishes of nearly all concurred against him, what he received profited him nothing, since he is not counted among bishops, and yet profits others, so that those promoted by him are in no way barred from their honors. But who would deny that a venal transaction could also have intervened there, where a place of honor was seized with such stubborn and importunate ambition? Yet he who possesses for others what he does not possess for himself is permitted to consecrate others, even though he himself is not counted among bishops. For often a fruitful branch is grafted onto a barren trunk, and in the growth it produces, it preserves the rights of its own nature, not of the trunk.

## XXV. That those ordained by impious heretics are often not removed from their offices.

What shall I say of the fact that ecclesiastical authority seems to come to the aid even of those who were ordained by the most impious heretics, and that in pronouncing judgments on this controversy it has regard not so much to the old faithlessness of those who advanced them as to the new faith and repentance of those who were advanced? For whoever diligently attends to the decrees of the pontiffs knows that the same Pope Innocent whom we mentioned a little while ago granted permission for all who had been ordained by the heretic Bonosus before his condemnation — if, having abandoned and condemned his error, they resolved to join themselves to the unity of the Church — to remain in their honors. And indeed Bonosus, as is found in those same decrees, is recognized as having been a Photinian \[follower of Photinus\]. The Photinians deny that Christ God, begotten from the substance of the Father before the ages, existed \[before his human birth\]. These men, since they do not fear to deny the Son of God together with the Jews, incur together with them a share in no different condemnation. Since, therefore, those who are reported to have been consecrated not only by so pernicious a heretic but were also entangled in his errors are permitted to remain in the ranks they had received, it is easily grasped what judgment is rightly to be passed on those who were advanced by simoniacs without simoniacal intent. Not unlike this is what Pope Anastasius likewise decreed concerning the heretic Acacius. His decretal judgments are so carefully composed and in all respects so consonant with our allegations, that, even if everything we have set forth at greater length were lacking, they would be fully sufficient to establish what we are arguing in the present disputation.
