## \[XLI.\] Appended to the preceding work.

I, unskilled and unlearned, wrote this little book under a certain hesitation of scrupulous uncertainty and, as may be seen at the end, reserved the matter for the definitive judgment of apostolic authority. In the time of the venerable Pope Nicholas, however, this question was again raised and debated at length. But after very many waves of contention, after the tangled and winding volumes of wavering questions, the matter was at last resolved and reached this boundary of judicial sentence: that those who had hitherto been consecrated gratis by simoniacs should remain in the honor of the dignity they had received, while those not yet advanced by them should not henceforth have license to be promoted to orders by them — observing this consideration, namely, that neither should the entire ecclesiastical order collapse all at once because of the severity of the sentence, nor should the simoniac plague gain the force of strength in conferring the grades of honor because of a lenient indulgence; by a certain dispensatory principle \[*iure dispensatorio*\], so that what is valid for the past should be entirely forbidden for the future. We therefore, not stubbornly pressing our own arguments, but humbly obeying synodal decrees, follow the edict of the apostolic see — both what has already been promulgated and, if anything still more refined and wholesome is to be established in the future, we profess our obedience. So that, just as we received from the blessed apostles, while the Gospel shone forth, the first rudiments of a nascent faith, so henceforth we may hold every rule of living and thinking as handed down to us by apostolic men.
