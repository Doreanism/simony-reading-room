C1) Codex Vindobonensis \[Vienna\] no. 2177, twelfth century, quarto, fols. 92–122, containing our work, which I myself transcribed.

C2) Codex Monacensis \[Munich\] no. 22272 (Windberg 72), parchment, twelfth–thirteenth century, quarto, fols. 51–75: Peter Damian’s *Liber Gratissimus* in a hand of the twelfth century is extant here. This manuscript I also examined personally.

C3) Codex Monacensis no. 14451 (S. Emmeram E 74), parchment, thirteenth century, quarto, fols. 105–134; it swarms with so many scribal errors that we have refrained from noting them all. It was collated by our colleague O. Holder-Egger.

Codices C2 and C3 derive from a single exemplar that had been drawn from the same source as Codex C1.

Among Peter Damian’s opuscula, the *Liber Gratissimus* was most widely known to medieval writers: Cardinal Humbert cites it at I, cc. 7 and 15 (below), and Deusdedit and Bernold likewise mention it.

An edition was prepared by Const. Caietanus in 1602, in the third volume of Peter Damian’s *Opera*, subsequently reprinted several times, most recently at Bassano in 1783, pp. 85–146. This edition was reprinted by Migne, *Patrologia Latina* CXLV, cols. 99–154.

Berlin, January 1888. L. de Heinemann.

## Verses on Simoniacs.

God curses Simon’s forge-anvil and the recesses of his mint, overturning his plague-ridden throne.
For the thief creeps in by back alleys and crooked paths,
while the shepherd approaches the open door freely, with untroubled step.
But where the corrupt dealings of traffickers are absent,
and no heavy, saleable talent swells the treasury,
no broker does harm, since commerce brings no defilement.
For what can men with money not accomplish, where money itself can wound?
Nay, how can a withered hand harm the trees it plants?
Often the sick man has given health-bringing medicine to the physician;
the enslaved have begotten a champion, the blind a man of sight;
a cleric has sprung from noble parents, the ugly have produced the beautiful;
nor should offspring be condemned for the faults of their fathers.
Each man is weighed down by his own deeds;
he is not held by the fault of his companion.

## BOOK OF PETER DAMIAN, HUMBLE MONK, CALLED THE LIBER GRATISSIMUS, SENT TO HENRY, ARCHBISHOP OF RAVENNA.

To Lord Henry, venerable bishop of the see of Ravenna: Peter, most humble servant of the life-giving cross of Christ, sends greeting in the same.
