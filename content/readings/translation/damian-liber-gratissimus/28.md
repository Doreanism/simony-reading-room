Whoever has applied himself to those aforementioned books has no doubt that the matter is there worked out fully and clearly. Now, regarding the ordination of clerics, no extended controversy has arisen — because, once those two sacraments had been plainly expounded, doubt found no foothold for hesitation about the third. But now the excessive curiosity of men thrusts a new question upon the world, and by staring too hard at what is perfectly clear it contrives, by seeing less, to bring on darkness. “How,” they ask, “can the grace of the Holy Spirit either be given through evil men or be received from evil men?” — not considering that the grace of the Holy Spirit is received precisely in the ecclesiastical order of rank itself, which — whether an unworthy person confers it or an unworthy person receives it — the Holy Spirit, who governs the laws of his Church, decrees to be valid. For, as the blessed Jerome says: *Bishop, presbyter, and deacon are not titles of merit but of office.* It is as if a tribune himself were unworthy, or were to advance some unworthy person to the military rank: the emperor would nonetheless establish as binding everything that was done, in order to preserve the order of his army; nor does he overthrow the provisions of tribunicial law who has not yet deposed the tribune himself from the dignity of his rank. But the objection is raised: “Behold, the Gospel cries out: *He who does not enter through the door but climbs in by another way is a thief and a robber* \[John 10:1\].” Granted — let that man who crept in secretly by another way be unfailingly charged with theft and robbery; he has not, however, made me a sharer in his crime simply because he admitted me through the door. Let him therefore suffer the punishment for the crime he committed, provided only that the prejudice of another’s guilt does not ensnare me — provided that another’s fault does not render damnable the one whom the purity of his own innocence keeps undefiled. Furthermore, concerning the claim that an unworthy man cannot receive the grace of the Holy Spirit: it is beyond all doubt established that the body of the Lord, which is consecrated on the sacred altars through the ministry of the holy priest, is vivified and sanctified by the power of the Holy Spirit conceived \[in the words of institution\], so that it may have power to vivify and sanctify us. As is said in those very venerable secrets of the Mass: *Through whom you, O Lord, create, sanctify, vivify, and bless all these good things.* For it cannot be believed that it can be vivified in any other way than by the Holy Spirit, since, as the Truth attests, *it is the Spirit that gives life* \[John 6:63\]. I ask, then: when a holy priest extends that heavenly gift — which has been vivified and sanctified by the power of the Holy Spirit and, to speak confidently, is truly full of the grace of that same divine Spirit — to some wicked person, does the Holy Spirit therefore forsake the Lord’s body and withdraw, nauseated by the filth of a squalid vessel? But if that is so, how is it true what is said through the Apostle, that *one who eats and drinks unworthily eats and drinks judgment upon himself, not discerning the body of the Lord* \[1 Cor. 11:29\]? For if, when the Spirit withdraws, it is no longer the body of the Lord but simply ordinary bread, then one who receives no sacrament at all does not incur judgment.
