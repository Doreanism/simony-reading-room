## XXIII. That the effect of consecration rests on the foundation of faith.

Concerning the Donatists also, we know that it was granted by the Council of Carthage that, when any of them resolved to return to the unity of the Catholic Church, if this seemed to benefit the peace of Christendom, he should be received with his rank. Now as we have learned in the general enumeration of heresies, the Donatists were not led astray by any very great error of faith, but rather, being angered that one Caecilianus had been enthroned at Carthage against their wishes, they made a schism and were cut off from the communion of the Church. And although it is certain that no one can receive the Holy Spirit outside the Catholic Church, yet any one of them who had been ordained, if this served the peace of the Church, was not deprived of his honor, because he persevered in the faith. Since, therefore, a Donatist, who is beyond all doubt a schismatic and is proved to be a stranger to the gift of the Holy Spirit, can sometimes remain in the order of the honor he has received, it is clearer than light that consecration does not depend on the merit of the one ordained or of the one ordaining, but rather the whole sacrament has regard to the faith of both. Otherwise, whether a Donatist promotes a Donatist to the rights of clerical rank, or a Novatianist a Novatianist, what worthiness of merit does either possess that he should be able either to give or to receive the mystery of divine grace? But between the evil giver and the evil receiver, the good stands in the middle, knowing how to be deterred from its own nobility by the vice of neither. A leper holds out gold to a leper, and that gold shines bright, untainted by any filthiness of scabrous hands. If a blind man holds out a lamp to a blind man, the light, knowing nothing of blindness, does not draw in darkness from the hands of its bearers. Therefore, if right faith is present — that one believes rightly in the Father and the Son and the Holy Spirit — the consecration even of an unworthy priest is fulfilled without distinction. Nor does the gift of divine grace suffer any diminishment of its perfection on account of the merit of the unworthy minister. Hence the aforesaid pope Innocent, when he was discussing together the two heresies — those of the Paulianists and the Novatianists — why he decreed that those coming from the Paulianists should be baptized but utterly forbade it in the case of the Novatianists, gave his reason in these words: “Because the Paulianists,” he says, “do not baptize in the name of the Father and the Son and the Holy Spirit, and among the Novatianists the question about the unity of the Father and the Son and the Holy Spirit was never raised.” And therefore, from all the separated groups, this one alone was chosen, to which the privilege was believed due, because they do not sin in any way in the sacrament of the Father and the Son and the Holy Spirit.
