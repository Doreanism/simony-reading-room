For the same great Leo, pope as mentioned above, commands that all clerics coming over from heretics be received in the orders they have already obtained, yet forbids their promotion to higher grades. We are content here to provide only the heading of this decree, referring those who have leisure to read the full text there at greater length: “That every cleric of whatever order, who deserts the Catholic communion and joins himself to heretical fellowship, if he returns to the Church, shall remain without promotion in the grade in which he was.” Let us therefore grant that simoniacs differ in nothing from other heretics and, by their schismatic faction, similarly withdraw far from the communion of the Church: nevertheless, by Leo’s judgment, all those who have already been promoted by them are decreed to remain in their priesthood or other grades.

## XXVIII. Here it is plainly proved that the Holy Spirit is received through simoniacs.

But it is objected on the other side: Behold, blessed Pope Gregory cries out: “If anyone, out of consideration for his office, does not burn ardently to correct the sin of simoniac heresy or the ordination of neophytes, let him not doubt that he shares the lot of him from whom this wicked offense took its beginning.” We grant it — and, God forbid, we think nothing different from the holy man — certainly that every one of the faithful should strive with all his strength to correct the plague of both offenses and should burn with the zeal of right judgment to have simoniacs deposed together with neophytes. We therefore do not stand in defense of simoniacs, whom the entire authority of sacred scripture testifies must be condemned; but, as we gather from the manifold decrees of the holy fathers, we freely profess that those ordained gratuitously by simoniacs are rightfully to be retained in their orders. Therefore, let those who invoke Gregory as the judge of their proof not disdain, in reciprocal exchange, to admit Gregory’s testimony as well — and not just any testimony, but one that, as they say, cuts the matter to the quick and henceforth puts a term to the verbose contention. In a certain homily, then, while speaking of simoniacs, he added: “For who are they today in the temple of God who sell doves, if not those who in the Church receive money for the laying on of hands, by which laying on of hands the Holy Spirit is given from heaven?” Behold — if the authority of this holy man is adjudged worthy of trust, there is no reason why the controversy of disputation should be drawn out any further. For what is more evident, what more plain, than the statement that by that very laying on of hands which is sold, the Holy Spirit is given from heaven?
