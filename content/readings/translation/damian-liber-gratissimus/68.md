But now, while I was staying in the city of Ravenna on account of the very frequent necessities of the Roman people, Germanus, who asserts himself to be a priest, and Lupentius, who calls himself a deacon, having undertaken what seemed to them a legation for many such persons, poured forth their petition and sought to express their own grievances: asserting that they held churches situated in your charity’s parish, but had been unable to merit your communion — on this ground, namely, that one Rusticus by name had received the presbyterate through a repeated ordination, and that this was no small impediment, since either they grieved that such a man should be retained in the Church, or he \[Rusticus\] judged that he ought to sin against others in the same way as he acknowledged it to be a sin in himself.” Behold, here it is plainly known what opinion Innocent held concerning re-consecration: he was eager to have restored to communion with his bishopric those who in their zeal for justice grieved that a re-consecrated man was being retained in the Church; he declares that the other \[Rusticus\] thought that the bishop ought to sin against others in the same way as he had sinned against himself — that is, the one who had consecrated him anew. For by the familiar eagerness of the wicked, he was trying to entangle others in the same snare of condemnation by which he had not feared to entrap himself first. Hence those men not unreasonably held that such a one should be expelled from the Church as one utterly to be condemned as a heretic — one who neither spared himself nor provided \[others\] from danger. But lest anyone accuse us of producing a tangled volume, complaining that we intermingle perhaps too many lengthy canonical examples, let him know this is certainly the reason: namely, that we are very often unable to persuade or exhort those against whom we are arguing to examine the sacred canons. And therefore we judge that the sentences of the holy Fathers must be set down here, so that we may place them in excerpted form before their delicate and tender eyes, in order that, while they consider in what we have brought forward what we hold to be true, they may also yield to the authority on which we rely. But that everything may appear confused to them, they also act against the canons in this: they receive all the orders together in re-consecration. Wherefore when I had learned of a certain man known to me, that he had been recently re-consecrated, I confess I shuddered at the deed. What more? At last I met the man: “Had you,” I said, “already within you any of those orders which you recently received from the bishop?” “Nothing at all,” he said, “for why would I have received them, if it were established that I already had them?” And I added: “Therefore you differed nothing from a layman — indeed you were altogether a layman.” “Yes,” he said, “a pure layman, as one who had nothing of the cleric in me.” “But if a layman,” I said, “on the very day on which he is a layman, rushes forth to the rights of priesthood, then by your own judgment he becomes a neophyte and, consequently, must without doubt be deposed.” At this he blushed with confusion and, because he could not shake the force of the conclusion, confirmed it by his silence.
