For even baptism — God forbid! — if it were sold for money, though it would be gravely contrary to the sacred canons, is not to be believed to have lacked the mystery of full and perfect regeneration. And therefore one who received baptism under such a payment of money is affirmed to have no need of rebaptism, nor is he judged to be without the divine gift. But if the madness of such obstinacy breaks forth to the point that even the baptism of simoniacs is judged null and void, here let not our own insistence exhaust itself — rather let the entire discipline of the Catholic faith universally rise up against him, and let the whole Church arm itself against one who strives to overturn all the institutions of the holy fathers.

## XXI. That one must attend to what is received, not from whom it is received.

But perhaps the objection is raised of what the blessed Pope Gregory testifies regarding the condemnation of simoniacs, saying: “The blessing is turned into a curse for him, because he is advanced only to become a heretic.” We grant the judgment and hold it unlawful to dissent from it. The man is indeed accursed, he is indeed a heretic, who received badly what was good. Yet what was received is in itself good, though it has become evil for the one who received it. For even the morsel handed to Judas did not become evil from having been good; rather it became evil for the one who received what was good unworthily and not well. And therefore, if Judas had handed that same morsel to another, it would have been necessary for the sacred gift to draw no stain upon itself from the guilt of the giver, and for no contamination to overflow onto the recipient. Even today, if some unworthy person were to receive the Lord’s body from the most holy altar and hand it to another, what is received is without doubt clean, and the gift of divine grace is not defiled by the filth of a corrupt minister. And though any wicked person eats and drinks judgment to himself, yet that which is eaten and drunk is the grace of God. For the grace of God — that is, the humanity of Christ — tasted death. “To the clean, all things are clean; but to the defiled and unclean, nothing is clean” \[Titus 1:15\]. If, then, what is clean is for the defiled itself unclean, it must be acknowledged that the priesthood, which is for the good man the cause of spiritual dignity, has become for the simoniac the material of condemnation — so that what makes this man assuredly a Catholic priest makes that other man truly a heretic. Nor is it remarkable that this happens in spiritual things, when the same diversity can easily be found in bodily matters as well. For a food that strengthens one man often burdens another, and from the same medicinal antidote one person perishes while another recovers.
