Sixtus, bishop of the Roman see, having assembled a council of seventy-six bishops, condemned him and deposed him from his own see, and assigned him merely three estates of the Church for his use by usufruct. He nonetheless ordered that his position be kept vacant, suspended his \[right of\] ordination, and left a certain bishop named Theodolus as his vicar in that see. After nine months, however, when the city of Jerusalem was being oppressed by famine, Polychronius sold those very estates which he had received for his use and lawlessly distributed their proceeds as alms to the poor. When Bishop Sixtus learned of this — with the deacon Priscus accusing him and charging the pious deed as a crime, saying that he had not been permitted to alienate the Church’s goods, which he had received only temporarily for his use — the holy pope rejoiced greatly and straightway informed the pious Augustus Valentinian of the deed. Then, with a synod assembled and Valentinian sitting in joint session, the Roman pontiff began to deliberate what should fittingly be done for Polychronius, who, in the very poverty of his deposition, distributing all he had, had laudably imitated the evangelical widow praised by the mouth of the Redeemer. By appropriate judgment it was therefore decreed that he should not be widowed who had followed the widow by piously dispensing his livelihood. Thus Polychronius, who had previously and deservedly lost his rank through pride and avarice, afterward became generous and humble and recovered for himself the insignia of his former dignity. If, then, holy conduct could excuse Anatolius — who, as was said above, was ordained by heretics while the bishop \[Flavian\] was still alive and had been deposed — and if a single act of almsgiving could restore the manifestly simoniac Polychronius to his former honor, by what reasoning is it said that one promoted by a simoniac will by no means attain the fruit of the honor he has received?

## XVIII. That miracles are often performed even through unworthy priests.

Indeed, what wonder is it if almighty God permits the office of priesthood to be spread in his Church even through evil ministers, since through those same ministers he also frequently displays signs of power — not, obviously, on account of the merit of a holy life, but on account of the sacrament of the priestly ministry received? For, to touch on a few from among many examples: Raimbaldus by name, the bishop of Fiesole, was most manifestly simoniac in a twofold sense — because not only had he himself obtained the summit of the episcopate through money, but he also seldom if ever consecrated clerics or churches without payment. To this was added the fact that, besides other \[women\], he had one publicly and intimately attached to him as if she were a wife bound by lawful marriage, and their sons and daughters, several of them, still live today, both united in wedlock and blessed with growing children. Through such a man as this, they say, many miracles were performed. Of these it will suffice to recount one here, so that from the one example the mind may gather what it may reasonably believe of the others which report has made known.
