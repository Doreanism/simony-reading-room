Hence the blessed Augustine, in his commentary on the epistle of the holy apostle John, speaks as follows: “An evil man can possess baptism; an evil man can possess prophecy. We find that King Saul possessed prophecy — he who persecuted the holy David was filled with the spirit of prophecy and began to prophesy. An evil man can receive the sacrament of the body and blood of the Lord. For of such persons it is said: *He who eats and drinks unworthily eats and drinks judgment upon himself* \[1 Cor. 11:29\]. An evil man can bear the name of Christ — that is, an evil man can be called a Christian. Of whom it is said: *They profaned the name of their God* \[cf. Lev. 22:32\]. Therefore an evil man can possess all the sacraments; but to have charity — that an evil man cannot do.” If then, according to the judgment of so great a doctor, an evil man possesses all the sacraments, how is it that the laughable foolishness of men shamelessly babbles that a simoniac does not possess the sacraments? Let us hear what that same distinguished doctor says elsewhere on the same subject, so that the position he held on this question which we are examining may become all the more clearly known the more frequently it is cited. For when he was speaking about the blessing of Isaac upon his son, he added: “The will of a just man, so far as conscience is concerned, is good; but so far as foreknowledge is concerned, he is not immune to adversity, for God alone judges concerning future things. And therefore Isaac, just as befits present human understanding, thought it more fitting to bless his elder son. But God, who is the knower of hidden things, showed that the younger deserved the blessing — in order to demonstrate that the benefit in a blessing is not that of a man but of God, since it is the dignity of the office, not the merit of the man, that the blessing of God follows. And indeed it was said by God in Numbers to the priests Moses and Aaron: *You shall put my name upon the children of Israel; I, the Lord, will bless them* \[Num. 6:27\] — so that the transmission of grace flows to men through the ministry of the ordained, and the will of the priest can neither harm nor benefit, but only the merit of the one seeking the blessing.” How great the dignity of the priestly order is, let us consider from this: it was said of the wicked Caiaphas, the murderer of the Savior, among other things: *This he did not say of himself, but, being high priest that year, he prophesied* \[John 11:51\]. By this it is shown that the spirit of graces does not follow the person or the dignity, but the ordination of tradition — so that, although someone may be of good merit, he cannot bless unless he has been ordained to exercise the office of ministry. But it is God’s to bestow the effect of the blessing.” These words of the blessed man are so plain and so transparent that whoever, after once having attended to them, nonetheless prepares with obstinate mind to persist in contending, is proved to be attacking not Peter — who is nothing — but rather the great Augustine himself.
