“Cleansing their hearts by faith.” And the blessed Peter in his epistle says: “Baptism saves you in like manner, not as the putting away of the filth of the flesh, but as the answer of a good conscience toward God.” “This is the word of faith which we preach,” by which, without doubt, baptism is consecrated so that it may be able to cleanse. For Christ, with us as the vine and with the Father as the vinedresser, loved the Church and gave himself up for her. Read the apostle and see what he adds: “That he might sanctify her,” he says, “cleansing her with the washing of water in the word.” The cleansing, therefore, would by no means be attributed to the flowing and perishable element unless “in the word” were added. This word of faith has such great power in the Church that through the Church itself — believing, offering, blessing, baptizing — it cleanses even the smallest infant, however little yet capable of believing with the heart unto righteousness and confessing with the mouth unto salvation. All this is accomplished through the word, of which the Lord says: “Now you are clean because of the word that I have spoken to you.”

According to the judgment of this blessed man, then — or rather according to the salvific teaching of our Redeemer himself — the mystery of any sacrament depends not on the merit of the one who consecrates it but resides in the word of consecration, so that what lies hidden in the consecrator by way of merit matters little; what matters is what descends through the words upon the one who is to be consecrated. The same Augustine in his commentary on Psalm 10 \[Psalm 11\] also says: “But what has the righteous done? If Caecilianus has offended you, what has Christ done to you? He who sent his betrayer — whom he called a devil, who before the Lord’s betrayal could not even be trusted with the Lord’s money-bag — along with the other disciples to preach the kingdom of heaven, in order to show that the gifts of God reach those who receive them with faith, even if the one through whom they receive them is like Judas.”

## XVII. On Anatolius and Polychronius, who, having made evil beginnings, by good conversion showed themselves worthy of their orders.

Why, then, does the vain rashness of men boast that the contagion of another’s sin can defile the purity of an innocent person, and that the crime of traffickers overwhelms those who are wholly ignorant of any trafficking — when even those who have begun badly often end well, and sinister beginnings issue in good outcomes through the satisfaction of an amended life? But to confirm with an example what we assert in words: it is not obscure that when the holy Flavian, bishop of Constantinople, was sent into exile on account of the Catholic faith, Anatolius was ordained in his place by heretics while Flavian was still alive. To this man, the holy Pope Leo speaks in these words: “For since your predecessor of blessed memory Flavian was expelled for the defense of Catholic truth, there was not unwarranted reason to believe that your ordainers had consecrated one of their own kind, contrary to the decrees of the holy canons. But the mercy of God was present, guiding and strengthening you, so that you made good use of your evil beginnings and showed yourself advanced not by human judgment but by divine goodness. And this must truly be understood as such, provided you do not lose this gift of divine grace through evil transgression.” What further shall I say also of Polychronius, the bishop of Jerusalem? He, as is found in the *Liber Pontificalis*, while arrogantly claiming for Jerusalem the primacy of sees and contumaciously proclaiming himself supreme pontiff, also fell into the abyss of the simoniac heresy, to such a degree that he would promote no clerics without payment and would accept not less than ten pounds of gold — trading wickedly — from the dedication of any basilica whatsoever.
