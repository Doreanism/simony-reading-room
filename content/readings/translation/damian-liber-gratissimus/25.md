## VII. That Balaam was a simoniac and yet did not lose the spirit of prophecy.

Consider: how could this son of avarice have commemorated such great marvels and announced so expressly and so clearly the redemption of the world — yet to be accomplished so far in the future — if he did not possess the presence of the Holy Spirit? For as the distinguished Doctor \[the Apostle Paul\] says: “Who knows the things of a man save the spirit of the man that is in him? So likewise the things of God no one knows but the Spirit of God” \[1 Corinthians 2:11\] — especially since the same scripture says that, with the Spirit of God rushing upon him, he took up his parable \[Numbers 24:2–3\] and said what we have just cited above. Do we say that the gift of the Holy Spirit is not given through a simoniac? Surely one recognizes that the simoniacal heresy was already flourishing in this Balaam? Was he not attempting to sell the grace of the Holy Spirit when, at the promise of payment, he produced the oracle of his divination? For scripture attests that the elders of Moab came to him with the fee for divination in their hands \[Numbers 22:7\]. And again through other messengers Balak son of Zippor sent to him, saying: “Do not delay coming to me; I am prepared to honor you, and whatever you wish I will give you” \[Numbers 22:16–17\]. Inflamed with desire for money he burned with eagerness, and yet through him the Holy Spirit was speaking profound mysteries. In the end, both Balaam and Balak were plainly hostile to God and proved to have been enemies of God’s people — one eager to buy, the other no less eager to sell, the gift of divine grace. And yet the secret of prophetic revelation did this man utter beyond his own merit, and that man \[Balak\] heard, quite unworthy as he was. And — that we may marvel all the more at the generosity of the divine gift — Balaam was striving to curse, yet against his will he blessed instead: “I was brought to bless,” he says, “and I am powerless to withhold blessing” \[Numbers 23:20\]. The bridle of divine power was bound upon his mouth, and he could not direct the impulse of his tongue anywhere except where the presiding Holy Spirit turned it. His tongue fought against his will, and the turbid bitterness that his foreordained-to-damnation mind had conceived could not flow out through the channel of his mouth. Therefore, since he could not hurl curses upon the people of God as he had been asked, he turned himself to devices of deception and placed a stumbling-block of Midianite women before them \[Numbers 25:1–2; 31:16\]. If, then, he who even desired to curse could by no means obtain the power to curse, what can be concluded about simoniacs, where at least the mind accords with the blessing of the tongue? But he who irreverently abused the office of prophetic dignity — what end finally closed the prerogative of the grace conferred upon him? For after scripture has recounted the victory of the people of Israel, it immediately adds: “Balaam also, the son of Beor, they slew with the sword” \[Numbers 31:8\]. Just so, whether simoniacs or any other criminals apply themselves to receiving ecclesiastical ministries, they do receive the gift of heavenly grace through the generosity of the Giver, yet they do not escape the fitting wages of their own transgression.
