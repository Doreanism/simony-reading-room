Let the simoniac burn, burn, that the Catholic may be illuminated; and by the same light by which he ministers to others, let him himself tend toward the darkness of his own destruction — so that what burns may perish and what is illuminated may live. For the light of priests is light to those who seek light, but to those burning with the fire of ambition it is a conflagration. What one man possesses unto the ruin of his own downfall, another receives unto the advancement of his salvation. Samson drew honey from the mouth of a dead lion and slaked his thirst with flowing water from a dry jawbone of a donkey. Yet he tasted neither the laziness of the little donkey in the water nor the ferocity of the beast in the honey. In both cases, what was consumed tasted according to its own character in his mouth; from what source it had been taken, reason did not greatly distinguish. Why should it be remarkable, if we judge that what we observe happening in the water of baptism has an analogous counterpart in the case of simoniacs? For through that water we are reborn anew in Christ, while that same water, once its work is done, is poured contemptuously down a drain.

## XXII. That neither Novatianists are to be deposed nor Arians rebaptized.

But let us now examine the sacred canons with what brevity is available, and, even though the explicit ruling on the question at hand is not frequently found therein, let us demonstrate by certain comparisons of cases what position is to be held. We know that it was decreed by the Council of Nicaea that Cathari \[Novatianists\] returning to the Catholic Church should, upon receiving only the laying on of hands, be received back in their orders. Why? — except because that type of heretic remained sound in the integrity of the Catholic faith, even though they strayed in certain other superstitions. For it is well known that Novatus, whose error these men followed, taught that neither should communion be given to the twice-married, nor ought those who had lapsed in persecution to be admitted even after penance. If, therefore, even those who were ordained by these heretics are in no way to be stripped of their office — precisely because their consecrators did not waver in the faith — what is to be thought of the ordinations of simoniacs, who observe nearly all the rights of ecclesiastical institution? But perhaps it is objected that simoniacs are by no means perfect in faith, since they manifestly sin against the Holy Spirit, whom they think can be purchased with money. To this we easily reply that, if this strict scrutiny is to be applied, a fault of faithlessness directed against the Holy Spirit is in a certain sense found even among the Novatianists. For since through the Holy Spirit, who is the remission of sins, forgiveness is granted to the penitent, it is without doubt established that whoever denies communion to the lapsed through penance is fighting against the Holy Spirit.
