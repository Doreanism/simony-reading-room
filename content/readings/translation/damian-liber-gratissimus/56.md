That is surely a harsh verdict, an utterly inhumane censure of indiscriminate judgment — that those whom conscience renders innocent should be punished by the imposition of penalty, and that judicial severity should be extended against those whom the boldness of presumed wickedness does not accuse. For where no crime has been committed, why should penal punishment be inflicted? Where there is no guilt, why is an innocent person presented as a defendant? What would be considered a legal prejudice even in the courts of civil judges — why should it seem right in the council of holy priests? It is the custom indeed of devout pontiffs to draw a sigh from their inmost hearts at the deposition of any single priest, to drench their sorrowful faces with copious streams of tears, and all those present are compelled by the fervor of their pious compassion to grieve together. And now, behold — almost the entire Church of Christ is stripped of its priests at the stroke of a single sentence, and this is to seem tolerable? The whole body of priests is collectively deposed, and this is held in contempt? Why is not that at least recalled to mind, which the divine voice proclaims through Ezekiel, saying: “The soul that sins shall die; the father shall not bear the iniquity of the son, nor the son bear the iniquity of the father; the righteousness of the righteous shall be upon him, and the wickedness of the wicked shall be upon him” \[Ezekiel 18:20\]? Yet these men, greedy for fraternal destruction, strive and press toward this goal: that the soul which has not sinned should perish, and that the innocent person should pay the penalties for another’s crime. They are zealous to have sons adjudged to the iniquities of their fathers, and the just condemned for the crimes of the wicked; and — to speak more plainly — they condemn those who are not simoniacs to the punishments of simoniacs, and sentence them for a trafficking which those who are utterly ignorant of the crime of that execrable trading have never engaged in.

## XXVII. That the simoniac heresy has flourished up to this time.

For who does not know that, through the Western kingdoms, up until the reign of this most clement king Henry and the pontificate of Pope Clement of revered memory \[Clement II\], and of this most blessed apostolic Leo \[Leo IX\] — under whose pontificate, as all rejoice, the holy Church is now governed — the poison of the simoniac heresy boiled forth lethally, so that what was openly and commonly done was in no way deemed liable to retributive punishment, and what was shared by nearly everyone was held as a rule, as if decreed by legal sanction? What, then, did he deserve? What sin did he commit — the man who simply approached his mother the Church, in which he was reborn of water and the Holy Spirit, and received there, where he had received baptism, the grace of consecration as well? It was certainly not his responsibility to have been obliged to inquire into the character of the person about to consecrate him. Therefore, whoever he found presiding in the episcopal chair in his own church, he thought it proper to be promoted to orders by him.
