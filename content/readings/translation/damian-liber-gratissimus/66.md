For those who have been baptized or ordained by such persons can be neither faithful nor clergy. Now if anyone objects that Formosus, bishop of the Roman see, was reconciled after deposition, let him know that very many priests have indeed been reconciled who are nevertheless nowhere recorded as having been consecrated again for the same purpose. For it is one thing for someone to be restored by synodal sentence to the rights of a lost order, and another to merit the grace of the Holy Spirit through the mystery of consecration, which cannot be repeated — especially since in one who is deposed, though he loses externally the privilege of exercising his dignity, the sacrament of the order once received nonetheless remains; just as, with the sacrament of regeneration remaining intact in him, a person guilty of any crime is excommunicated, and does not lack that same sacrament even if he is never reconciled to God.

## XXXIIII. That it is false that one consecrated by a simoniac differs in no way from a layman.

But though we have said much about the impiety of reconsecration, lest the matter still hang in doubt if the authority of recorded deeds is not also adduced, let us supply an example from ecclesiastical history — which seems, as is commonly said, to illustrate to some degree the consecration of any priests whatsoever. To set down the very words used there: At the time when Bishop Alexander was celebrating the feast day of Peter the martyr at Alexandria, having finished the solemnities and waiting for his clergy to gather with him for the banquet, he saw in the distance, in a place near the sea, a group of boys playing on the shore in the manner that commonly occurs — imitating a bishop and performing what is customarily done in churches. But as he watched the boys intently for a long while, he saw them performing certain things of a more hidden and mystical character. At once, greatly disturbed, he ordered his clergy to be summoned to him, and showed them from a distance what he himself was observing. He then commanded them to go and seize all the boys and bring them to him. When they had come, he inquired of them what their game was and what they had done and how. They, as children of that age tend to do, first denied it in fear; then they set forth in order what had been done, and confessed that certain catechumens had been baptized by Athanasius, who had been playing the role of bishop in that game. He then inquired diligently of those who were said to have been baptized as to what questions had been put to them and what they had answered, and likewise of the one who had put the questions. When he saw that everything conformed to the rite of our religion, he took counsel with his council of clergy and is recorded to have decided that baptism ought not to be repeated for those upon whom water had been poured with proper interrogations and responses, but that what is customarily completed by priests should be completed.
