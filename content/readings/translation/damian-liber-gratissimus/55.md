## XXVI. That those whom Acacius ordained even after his condemnation are not to be deprived of their honors.

What will these men say when they hear what the same pope decreed concerning those whom Acacius ordained after his condemnation? For I do not think that those who are manifestly at variance with his judgment by reckless presumption have read these passages. Let them therefore hear the clemency of the apostolic see, and let them be softened from the harshness of their own verdict. For after the words we have already quoted, he \[Pope Anastasius\] adds the following: “But if the curious suspicion of some extends so far that they imagine the sacred acts performed by Acacius in the sacraments he usurped were rendered ineffectual after the sentence pronounced by Pope Felix — and that therefore those who received the mysteries transmitted in consecrations or in baptism should be afraid lest the divine gifts appear null and void — let them remember that the earlier discussion applies equally to this case as well: because he performed these acts without usurpation of the priesthood being adjudicated against him, in those sacraments whose power holds good, his condemned person did not harm others in this respect either. For the sentence of the Davidic trumpet applies to him: ‘But God shall shatter the heads of his enemies, the crown of hair of those who walk in their iniquities’ \[Psalm 68:21\]. For pride always brings ruin upon itself, not upon others. This is attested by the entire authority of the heavenly scriptures, as it is also said through the Holy Spirit in the prophet: ‘He who practices pride shall not dwell in the midst of my house’ \[Psalm 101:7\]. Hence, since he claimed for himself the name of priest while condemned, the tumor of pride was inflicted upon his own crown; for the people who thirsted for the gift of his ministry in the sacraments were not excluded, but that soul which had sinned was, by just judgment, properly liable to the penalty — as the manifold witness of the scriptures everywhere confirms.”

What is to be said to all this? What cleverness will be able to prevail against it? What arguments will be available to those who resist the truth? Behold, the case here is argued specifically concerning one person, yet the cause that is settled is universal, applying generally. We do not, however, write out these things as though we were standing in defense of heretics — so as to destroy the remaining sentences of the holy fathers, which more severely punish them and their ministry — but plainly in order that, as attention is given to the measure of discernment present in the former saints, those who live now may likewise in no way exceed the boundary of right judgment through immoderate zeal for justice; lest that word of the Apostle be seen to apply to them as well, which is said of certain of their like: “They have a zeal for God, but not according to knowledge” \[Romans 10:2\]. Those therefore who were promoted by a heretic condemned by the authority of the apostolic see do not fall from the exercise of their rank; and yet those who were consecrated by simoniacs without simony are to bear the danger of the honor they have received?
