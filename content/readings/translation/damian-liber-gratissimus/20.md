Hence it is not inappropriately said through Solomon: “All rivers enter the sea, yet the sea does not overflow; to the place from which the rivers go forth, they return so that they may flow again.” This outflow and return of spiritual rivers Luke the Evangelist describes mystically when he says that Jesus, having called together the twelve apostles, gave them power and authority over all demons and to cure diseases, and sent them to preach the kingdom of God. Behold the going forth of the rivers. And afterward he adds: “And the apostles returned and recounted to him all that they had done.” Behold the return. The rivers return therefore to the place from which they went forth, because all the elect are in no doubt that they are debtors to him from whom they had drawn everything they pour forth spiritually. Hence Paul also says: “There are distributions of graces, but the same Spirit; and there are distributions of ministries, but the same Lord; and there are distributions of works, but the same God who works all things in all.” Indeed, by first naming the Spirit, then adding the Lord, and finally God, he shows that the Holy Trinity — that is, the one God — is without doubt the author of all graces. For he himself distributes his gifts, who by hidden providence discerns the merits and the diversity of works of each individual.

## II. That the priest ministers outwardly, but God invisibly consecrates.

Therefore it must be believed with full faith that Christ delegates to his ministers the office of ecclesiastical consecration in such a way that he nonetheless principally retains in himself the sacrament of all orders, and grants to servants the ministry of advancing their fellow servants in such a way that he transfers to no one the right and power of consecrating. For although bishops may appear to consecrate through the office of the ministry entrusted to them, it is he who truly consecrates who invisibly gives the Holy Spirit. One is he who prays, another who hears the prayer; one is he who petitions, another who grants the petitions. For who would dare to compare himself to Peter and John? And yet it is said of them that, “sent to Samaria, laying hands on those who had been baptized, they prayed for them; and they received the Holy Spirit.” Not therefore from their own generosity, but through their ministry — and accordingly not with them as givers, but as those praying — did the Holy Spirit descend upon the believers. For there is one great priest, one supreme high priest, who entered once not into any holy of holies, but into heaven itself, that he might appear before the face of God on our behalf. From him, as from a certain summit, all priesthood is diffused through the members of the Church, and all that is sacred is propagated in an ineffable manner. Hence when he sent the disciples to baptize, he did not transfer into them the power of the sacrament, but implanted obedience, and made them not authors of baptism but its ministers. For when he says, “Go into the whole world, baptize all nations in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit,” he clearly shows that it was not they but he who was beyond doubt the author of baptism, in whose name they baptized.
