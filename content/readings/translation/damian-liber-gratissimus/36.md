Moreover, just as those seventy whom the Lord appointed after the apostles — and similarly those whom he willed to share with Moses the burden of governing the people — prefigured the order of priests, so likewise Moses first, and afterward the apostles, appear to have held the dignity of bishops. And just as those seventy could not receive the Holy Spirit from the apostles, nor those elders from Moses, so also it must without doubt be understood regarding bishops and the other ranks of ecclesiastical dignity: that the one almighty God, who distinguished them through the various grades of the ordained ministry by the ordering of his governance, alone also, as he himself knows, poured out the grace of his Spirit upon each one. For in that giving of the Spirit, Moses could claim for himself no other privilege than what the divine voice commanded him — namely, to choose the men and, leading them to the door of the tent, to stand with them. This is the sum total of Moses’ work. What merit of his own could Moses recognize in this gift of God, when moreover two of those who had been enrolled, according to the testimony of scripture, remained in the camp and did not come to the tent, and yet, without his knowledge, received the Spirit equally with the others? In what is said about taking from the spirit of Moses and giving it to others, Moses must be believed to have suffered no loss of his own spirit — just as when any material borrows light from a burning lamp, the lamp is by this not in the least diminished in its own brightness.

## Chapter XV. That those who come forward to be consecrated already possess the Holy Spirit.

Indeed, corresponding to the seven gifts of the Holy Spirit, there are likewise seven orders of ecclesiastical dignity. As for those who are placed above all these grades — namely, patriarchs, archbishops, and bishops — they are seen not so much as receiving a new order as being more excellently elevated within the same priesthood. For since a priest is called such because he gives what is sacred \[*sacrum dat*\], that is, because he offers sacrifice to God, what in the Church can be found more exalted or more eminent than the priesthood, through which the mystery of the Lord’s body and blood is proved to be offered? Although those of higher rank may each enjoy certain privileges proper to their ministry, yet since they share in common with the remaining priests what is greater than all else, they rightly bear along with them the name of priest. When ministers of the Church are promoted to these grades, they must not be thought to receive the Holy Spirit newly and all at once, as if that heavenly inhabitant were then for the first time beginning to visit them as previously empty vessels; rather, it is in this manner: that he already dwells in them and leads them through a more abundant grace to the advances of a yet higher grade as well. Otherwise, how could anyone newly receive him at the assumption of honors, when the grades themselves are conferred by rule at lengthy intervals of time, and baptism — to which the first-fruits of the Spirit are ascribed — is seen to have been received long before?
