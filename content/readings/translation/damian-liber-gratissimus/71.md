## XXXVIII. Where the Emperor Henry is extolled with the glory of well-deserved praise.

Moreover, as we recall the deeds of the venerable pope, reason next persuades us to turn our minds also toward considering the illustrious renown of this great King Henry. For after God it was he who rescued us from the maw of the insatiable dragon; it was he who, with the blade of divine virtue, cut off all the heads of the simoniac heresy as though of a many-headed hydra. He indeed may rightly say to the glory of Christ: “All who came before me were thieves and robbers” \[John 10:8\]. For until his reign the falseness of priests furnished, if I may say so, the inexhaustible expenses of Babylonian Bel. But after he obtained, by God’s design, his father’s sovereignty, he straightway cast a ball of pitch into the dragon’s jaws and thus, like a second Daniel, extinguished the fearsome beast. For by pitch, which burns and constricts, the love of money may not unreasonably be represented — a love that burns in the furnace of the covetous heart and at the same time constricts it from any open-handed bestowal of mercy and pious compassion. What is it, then, to cast a ball of pitch into the dragon’s mouth, if not to say openly with Peter: “Your money perish with you” \[Acts 8:20\]? This the prince truly proclaimed when he overturned the seats of those who sold doves, drove out the money-changers, and refused to allow commerce to be transacted in God’s temple.

Nor does it seem out of keeping with this image that Daniel is said to have mixed fat and hair along with the pitch. For what do we understand by fat but the pleasure of the flesh? What by hair, which grows on the outside of animals’ bodies, but external wealth? The king mixed fat and hair with pitch and thus burst the dragon, because, utterly abhorring the simoniac poison, he not only refused to yield to avarice but also sought no enticement of the flesh nor any gain from outward profit whatsoever in his dispensing of ecclesiastical dignity. So now, with the dragon cast down from heaven and bound in the abyss by an angel with a chain — the dragon who had formerly fought against Michael and dragged a third of the stars in his wake — let us taunt him exultingly with Isaiah: “O Lucifer, you who rose at dawn, how have you fallen from heaven; you who struck down nations, you have crashed to earth” \[Isaiah 14:12\]. Indeed, until the time of this august emperor all the decrees of the canons that the fathers had issued concerning this plague seemed to have been erased from the memory of many by long forgetfulness. But he, like that illustrious Josiah of old, as soon as he found the book of the law of the Lord, tore his garments because he grieved, overthrew the altars, cast down the abominable idols, and demolished all the sacrilegious superstitions of the kings before him. And because he refused to keep the practice of his predecessors in order to observe the commandments of the eternal King, divine Providence — not ungrateful toward him — granted him something that it had not until then conceded to most of his predecessors: namely, that the holy Roman Church is now ordered at his nod, and that no one at all may elect a priest for the apostolic see without his authority. Surely if David, because he slew the Philistine Goliath, earned the king’s daughter as his bride, what wonder if this emperor too, as the victor over Simon the heresiarch — who truly defied the array of the living God — received the holy Church, which is the daughter of the supreme King?
