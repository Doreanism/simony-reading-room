8. 4. A will that is in some measure ready to undertake and discharge the Ministry — a will that must not arise from fear of poverty or similar inconvenience, nor from a desire for superiority, fame, gain, or the like, etc. (1 Peter 5:2); for such a will is in some sense constrained and is not properly and directly directed toward the work of the Ministry, but must arise from pure love and zeal (John 21:15–17).

9. 5. The difference among these qualifications is such that, first, a knowledge of the truth together with some aptitude for teaching is necessarily required for the very essence of a Minister, because these are the proper foundations of that vocation; whereas integrity is required only as a means to the well-being \[of the ministry\], though it is necessary by God’s precept even for the bare being of a Minister.

Secondly, with respect to doctrine and aptitude, judgment may be rendered by others; but with respect to the disposition of the will, no one besides the candidate himself can always and with certainty judge.

Thirdly, doctrine and aptitude cannot suddenly be supplied by others; but a will held back by fear or modesty may and ought to be roused and stirred up by exhortation. As for those who are able but unwilling, effort spent in calling them is well employed; not so for those who are willing but unable.

Quest. 3. Whether those who can read, or recite a sermon from memory out of the writings of others, are furnished with the necessary gifts of Ministers?

10. *A.* 1. In no way. For first, the gift of reading and reciting from memory is not a gift of the Ministry but a gift of wisdom and knowledge (1 Corinthians 12:8).

11. 2. A Minister must necessarily have a faculty for applying the Word according to the diversity of hearers, times, places, and other circumstances, with the variety that different occasions require (2 Timothy 2:15); but this cannot be done by reading or reciting from another’s prescription.
