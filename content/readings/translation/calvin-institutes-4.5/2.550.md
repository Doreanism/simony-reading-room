But how little the priests of that time wallowed in superfluous wealth is made plain enough by that single utterance of the Synod of Aquileia, over which Ambrosius presided: *Glorious in the priests of the Lord is poverty.* The bishops of that day certainly had some resources with which they might have rendered the Church conspicuous in outward splendor, had they thought such things to be the true ornaments of the Church. But since they knew that nothing was more contrary to the duty of pastors than to shine and to swell with pride in the delicacies of the table, in the elegance of clothing, in a great retinue, and in magnificent palaces, they followed and cultivated humility and modesty, and indeed poverty itself, which Christ consecrated among his ministers.

18 But lest we be too lengthy here, let us gather again into a brief summary how far removed is that dispensation — or rather dissipation — of ecclesiastical goods which now exists from the true diaconate that both the word of God commends to us and the ancient Church observed. What is devoted to the adornment of churches, I say is wrongly applied, if that measure is not observed which the very nature of sacred things prescribes, and which the Apostles and other holy Fathers prescribed by the examples of their teaching. But what of this kind is seen in churches today? Whatever is not arranged to conform — I will not say to that ancient frugality, but to any decent moderation — is rejected. Nothing whatever pleases unless it savors of luxury and the corruption of the times. Meanwhile, they are so far from having proper care for the living temples \[of God\] that they would rather see many thousands of the poor perish of hunger than break the smallest chalice or flagon to relieve their want. That I may not pronounce any harsher judgment of my own, I would wish only that pious readers would consider what those men would say if that Exuperius, bishop of Toulouse, whom we have mentioned, or Acatius, or Ambrosius, or someone like them, were to be raised from the dead. Assuredly they would not approve of resources being transferred elsewhere as though they were superfluous, while the poor are in such great necessity. To say nothing of the fact that the purposes to which they are devoted (even if there were no poor) are in many respects harmful, and in no respect beneficial. But I leave men aside. These goods are consecrated to Christ: they must therefore be dispensed at his discretion. And that portion of expenditure which they have lavished contrary to his command, they will in vain represent as an outlay for Christ. Although (to speak the truth) not much of the ordinary revenue of the Church is spent on these purposes. For there are no bishoprics so wealthy, no abbeys so rich, no benefices either so numerous or so ample, as to suffice for satisfying the gluttony of priests. But while they wish to spare themselves, they lead the people into superstition, so that what ought to have been distributed to the poor is diverted to the building of churches, the erecting of statues, the purchase of vessels, and the procuring of costly vestments. Thus in this whirlpool the daily alms are swallowed up.

19 As for the revenue they derive from fields and possessions, what else shall I say than what I have already said, and what is before the eyes of all? We see with what fidelity the greater part of those who are called bishops and abbots administer it. What madness is it to look for ecclesiastical order here? Was it fitting for men whose life ought to have been a singular example of frugality, modesty, continence, and humility, to rival the magnificence of princes in the number of their retinue, the splendor of their dwellings, and the refinements of their dress and table? How far also did it depart from the duty of those whom the eternal and inviolable decree of God forbids to be lovers of base gain, and commands to be content with a simple maintenance — not merely to lay hands on villages and fortresses, but to swoop down upon the most ample satrapies, and finally to seize dominions themselves?
