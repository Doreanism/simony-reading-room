Is it even bearable to hear that men are called Pastors who have broken into the Church’s possession as into an enemy’s spoil? who have prevailed through bitter litigation? who have purchased it with a price? who have earned it through base services? who, as boys barely able to stammer, have claimed it as an inheritance from uncles and kinsmen, and sometimes even from their fathers — though illegitimate?

## Would the license of a people, however corrupt and lawless, ever have gone so far?

But this too is a greater monstrosity: that one man (I will not say of what sort — certainly one who cannot govern himself) is set over the governance of five or six Churches at once. One can see today in the courts of princes young men who are thrice abbots, twice bishops, once archbishops. And everywhere there are canons burdened with five, six, seven benefices, of which they take absolutely no care except in receiving the income. I will not cite the Word of God speaking everywhere against this — which has long since ceased to carry even the least weight with them. I will not cite the many most severe sanctions against this wickedness made in many Councils — for these too they boldly despise whenever it pleases them. But I say that both are prodigious enormities wholly contrary to God, to nature, and to ecclesiastical governance: that one plunderer should prey upon several Churches at once; that a man should be called Pastor who cannot be present with his flock even if he wished — and yet (such is their shamelessness) they cloak these abominable foulnesses with the name of the Church to shield them from all reproach. Indeed, if it please God, in these wickednesses is contained that sacrosanct succession by whose merit they boast that the Church has been kept from perishing.

## Now (which is the second mark in judging a legitimate pastor) let us see how faithfully they discharge their office.

Of the priests created there, some are monks and others are those called seculars. That first class was unknown to the ancient Church, and to hold such a position in the Church is so at odds with the monastic profession that when men were formerly co-opted from monasteries into the clergy, they ceased to be monks. And indeed Gregorius, whose time contained much of the dregs \[of decline\], nevertheless did not permit this confusion to occur. For he requires that those made abbots renounce the clerical order, because no one can rightly be both monk and cleric, since the one is an impediment to the other. Now if I ask how someone can properly fulfill his office whom the Canons declare unfit — what, I ask, will they answer? They will cite for me those abortive decrees of Innocentius and Bonifacius by which monks are received to the honor and power of the priesthood while remaining in their monasteries. But what sort of reasoning is it that any ignorant ass, the moment he has occupied the Roman see, should overturn all antiquity with a single little word? But of this matter later. Let it suffice for now that it was regarded as a great absurdity in the purer Church if a monk were to serve as a priest. For Hieronymus declares that he who dwells among monks does not perform the office of a priest, and counts himself among the laity to be governed by priests. But even granting them this, what duty do they perform? Among the mendicants, some preach; all other monks either drone through or mutter Masses in their lairs. \[One must ask\] whether Christ ever willed, or the nature of the office permits, that presbyters should be made for this purpose. Since Scripture plainly testifies that it is proper to presbyters to govern the Church, is it not impious profanation to transfer — indeed wholly to transform — the sacred ordinance of God? For when they are ordained, they are expressly forbidden to do what God enjoins upon all presbyters. For this refrain is sung to them: “The monk, content within his cloister, presumes neither to administer the sacraments nor to discharge any other public function.” Let them deny, if they can, that it is open mockery of God to make someone a presbyter for the purpose of abstaining from the true and genuine office.
