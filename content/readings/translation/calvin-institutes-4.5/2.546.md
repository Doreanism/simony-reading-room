9 I come to the seculars, who are partly beneficed (as they say) — that is, holding benefices that provide their living — and partly hire out their daily labor by singing Masses or chanting, living as it were on the alms collected thereby. Benefices either carry the cure of souls, as bishoprics and parishes, or they are stipends for leisured men who earn their living by chanting — such as prebends, canonries, personages and dignities, chaplaincies, and the like. Although, with everything now turned upside down, abbacies and priorships are conferred by privilege (that is, by common and established custom) not only on secular priests but even on boys. As for the mercenaries who seek their daily living, what else would they do but what they do? Namely, prostitute themselves in a servile and shameful manner for gain — especially given the enormous multitude with which the world now swarms. And so, since they dare not beg openly, or reckon that this way profits them too little, they go about like hungry dogs and by their importunity extort from unwilling men, no differently than by barking, what they stuff into their shriveled bellies. Were I to pursue at length in words how great a disgrace it is to the Church that the honor and office of the presbyterate has sunk to this, there would be no end. Readers need not therefore expect from me a discourse equal to so shameful an outrage. Briefly I say: if the office of a presbyter is (as both the Word of God prescribes and the ancient Canons require) to feed the Church and administer the spiritual kingdom of Christ, then all those sacrificers who have no work or stipend except in the trafficking of Masses not only fail in their duty but have no legitimate office at all that they exercise. For they are given no place of teaching; they have no people to govern. In short, nothing remains to them except an altar at which to sacrifice Christ — which is to make offering not to God but to demons, as we shall see elsewhere.

10 I am not touching here on extraneous vices, but only on the inward malady that is rooted in their very institution. I will add a word that will sound harsh in their ears — but because it is true, it must be said: canons, deans, chaplains, provosts, and all who are supported by idle benefices must be reckoned in the same category. For what ministry of the Church can they render? They have cast off the preaching of the Word, the exercise of discipline, and the administration of the sacraments as burdens too onerous to bear. What then do they retain by which to boast that they are true presbyters? Chanting, forsooth, and the pomp of ceremonies. But what has that to do with the matter? If they appeal to custom, to usage, to long prescription of time — I in reply cite Christ’s own definition, by which he both set before us the true presbyters and specified what those who wish to be so regarded must possess. And if they cannot bear so hard a law as to submit themselves to Christ’s rule, let them at least allow the matter to be decided on the authority of the early Church. But their condition will be no better if their standing is judged by the ancient Canons. Indeed, they degenerated into canons when they ought to have remained presbyters, as they once were — those who governed the Church together with the bishop and were, as it were, his colleagues in the pastoral office. Those capitular dignities, as they are called, look not at all toward the true governance of the Church; far less do chaplaincies and the rest bearing names of the sixth order \[of lesser offices\]. In what position then shall we place all of them? Certainly both the Word of Christ and the observance of the ancient Church excludes them from the honor of the presbyterate. Yet they contend they are presbyters — but the mask must be stripped away: so we shall find that their whole profession is most alien and remote from that office of presbyters which both the Apostles describe for us and which was required in the early Church.
