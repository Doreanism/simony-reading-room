“We,” they say, “are the pillars of the Church, the overseers of religion, the vicars of Christ, the heads of the faithful — for the Apostolic power has passed to us by succession.” As though they were speaking to blocks of wood, they puff themselves up perpetually with these absurdities. But as often as they boast of this, I will in turn ask them what they have in common with the Apostles. For it is not a question of some hereditary honor that can be conferred on those who are asleep, but of the office of preaching, which they themselves flee with such great care. Similarly, when we assert that their reign is the tyranny of Antichrist, they immediately retort that this is that venerable hierarchy so often praised by great and holy men. As if, forsooth, the holy Fathers, when they commended the ecclesiastical hierarchy or spiritual government as it had been handed down to them through succession from the Apostles, were dreaming of this misshapen and ruin-filled chaos — where Bishops are for the most part like rude asses who do not even hold the first rudiments of faith common among the people; or at times are boys still fresh from their nurse; and where those who are more learned (which is however a rare example) think the episcopate to be nothing other than a title of splendor and magnificence; where the rectors of churches think no more of feeding the flock than a cobbler thinks of plowing; where all things are so confused in more than Babylonian dissolution that no whole vestige of that ancient order any longer appears.

## What if we descend to morals?

Where will be found that light of the world which Christ requires? Where the salt of the earth? Where that sanctity which could serve as a perpetual censure? There is today no class of men more notorious for luxury, softness, and pleasures, and every kind of lust besides; from no class are fitter or more skilled masters of all imposture, fraud, treachery, and perfidy to be found; nowhere is there so much cunning or audacity for doing harm. I say nothing of arrogance, pride, rapacity, cruelty. I say nothing of the unbridled license in every part of life. With enduring these things the world has been so wearied that there is no danger I may seem to exaggerate too much. I will say one thing which even they themselves will not be able to deny: of the Bishops almost none, and of the prefects of parishes not one in a hundred, is such that, if judgment were to be rendered upon his morals according to the ancient Canons, he would not be either excommunicated or at the very least removed from office. I seem to be saying something incredible, so thoroughly has that ancient discipline — which required a more exact scrutiny of the morals of the clergy — fallen into disuse; but the matter stands exactly so. Let those now go, who serve under the banners and auspices of the Roman see, and boast of the sacerdotal order they possess. Certainly the order they have is clearly not from Christ, nor from his Apostles, nor from the Fathers, nor from the ancient Church.

## Let the deacons now come forward, together with that most holy distribution of ecclesiastical goods which they possess.

Although they no longer appoint their deacons for that purpose at all — for they impose upon them nothing other than to minister at the altar, to recite or chant the Gospel, and to perform various trifles of I know not what kind. Nothing concerning alms, nothing concerning care of the poor, nothing concerning that whole function which they once performed. I speak of the institution itself; for if we look at what they do, in reality it is not truly an office for them but merely a step on the way to the presbyterate. In one respect, those who hold the place of deacon at the mass represent an empty image of antiquity: they receive the oblations before the consecration. For it was the ancient custom that before the communion of the Supper the faithful would kiss one another, and offer alms at the altar — thus declaring their charity, first by the symbol \[of the kiss\] and then by the act of generosity itself. The deacon, who was the steward of the poor, received what was given, to distribute it. Now from those alms no more reaches the poor than if they were cast into the sea. They therefore make a mockery of the Church with this lying diaconate. Certainly in it they have nothing in common either with the Apostolic institution or with the ancient observance.
