For when they are given to hunters and fowlers, the outcome is to be accounted excellent. To excuse so great an indignity in any way is the work of utterly shameless men. The people, I say, once had an excellent canon prescribed to them by the word of God: that a bishop must be blameless, an apt teacher, not quarrelsome, and so on. Why then was the right of election transferred from the people to these men? Because, of course, amid the tumults and factions of the people, the word of God could not be heard. And why today is it not transferred back from them, since they not only violate all laws but, having cast off all shame, lasciviously, greedily, and ambitiously mingle and confound things human with things divine?

3 But they lie when they say that this was devised as a remedy. We read that cities were often in uproar over the election of bishops in former times; yet no one ever dared to think of removing from the citizens their right. They had other means by which to prevent these evils or to correct them once committed. But I will say what the truth is. When the people began to grow more careless in holding elections, and pushed off this responsibility onto the presbyters as less fitting for themselves, the presbyters abused that occasion to usurp tyranny for themselves, which they afterwards established by issuing new canons. As for ordination, it is nothing other than pure mockery. For the semblance of examination they display there is so empty and hollow that it lacks even a pretense of substance. Therefore, where princes have in some places obtained by compact from the Roman Pontiffs the right to nominate bishops themselves, the Church has suffered no new injury thereby — since the election was merely taken away from the canons, who had seized it by no right, or rather had stolen it outright. It is indeed a most shameful example that bishops are dispatched from the court to take possession of churches, and it would become godly princes to abstain from such corruption. For it is an impious despoiling of the Church whenever a bishop is thrust upon a people who did not seek him, or at least did not openly approve him. Yet the disordered practice that had long prevailed in the churches gave princes the occasion to take to themselves the presentation of bishops. For they preferred this to be their own benefaction rather than belong to those who had no better claim to it, and who abused it no less perversely.

4 Behold, what a splendid calling, on the ground of which bishops boast that they are successors of the Apostles. They also claim that the right of creating presbyters belongs to them alone. But in this they most perversely corrupt the ancient institution — in that by their ordination they create not presbyters to govern and feed the people, but priests to offer sacrifice. Likewise when they consecrate deacons, they do nothing about their true and proper office; they ordain them only for certain ceremonies around the chalice and the paten. Yet in the Council of Chalcedon it was decreed to the contrary, that absolute ordinations are not to be performed — that is, not without simultaneously assigning to the ordained a place where they may exercise their office. This decree is most useful for two reasons: lest the Church be burdened with superfluous expense, and lest what ought to be distributed to the poor be spent on idle men. Further, so that those who are ordained may consider that they are not being advanced to an honor, but that an office is being entrusted to them, to the discharge of which they are bound by solemn pledge. But the Romanist masters — who think nothing in religion need be attended to except the belly — first interpret “title” as an income sufficient for sustenance, whether from a patrimony or from a benefice. And so when they ordain a deacon or presbyter, with no concern for where they ought to minister, they confer the order upon them, provided only that they are wealthy enough to sustain themselves. But who could accept this — that the title required by the council’s decree is an annual income for sustenance? Moreover, because the more recent canons condemned bishops to support at their own expense those whom they had ordained without a suitable title, in order to restrain their excessive readiness to ordain, a device was contrived to evade the penalty.
