Or (if they prefer me to speak more bluntly and plainly) since Chaplains, Canons, Deans, Provosts, and all the idle bellies of that flour do not touch with so much as a little finger the smallest part of the office required of Presbyters, it is not to be borne that by falsely usurping the honor they should violate the holy institution of Christ.

11 There remain the Bishops and rectors of parishes: who, one would wish, would at least contend to retain their office. For we would willingly grant them that they hold a pious and excellent charge, if indeed they discharged it; but when, by abandoning the churches committed to them and casting their care upon others, they still wish to be counted pastors, they act as if the office of pastor were to do nothing. If some moneylender who never stirred from the city were to profess himself a plowman or a vine-dresser; if a soldier who had served constantly in battle and camp, and had never seen a forum or a book, were to sell himself as a jurisconsult — who would endure such rotten absurdity? Yet these men act somewhat more absurdly still, who wish to appear and be named legitimate pastors of the Church, and yet are unwilling to be so. For how many of them discharge even the appearance of governing their own church? Most devour their churches’ revenues throughout their whole lives, and never once approach them even to take a look. Others come themselves once a year, or send a steward, lest anything be lost in the leasing. When this corruption first crept in, those who wished to enjoy this kind of leave exempted themselves by special privilege; but now it is a rare example for any to reside in his own church — they value them no differently than country estates, over which they set their vicars as farm managers or tenants. Yet even the natural sense itself repudiates this: that one is the pastor of a flock who has never seen so much as a single sheep of it.

12 Already in the age of Gregorius it is apparent that some seeds of this evil had taken root, so that the rectors of churches were beginning to be more negligent in teaching — for he complains of this bitterly in certain places. “The world,” he says, “is full of priests; and yet in the harvest a laborer is rarely found — for we take up the priestly office indeed, but do not fulfill the work of the office.” Likewise: “Because they have not the bowels of charity, they wish to be seen as lords, but they in no way acknowledge themselves as fathers. They exchange the place of humility for the pride of domination.” Likewise: “But we, O pastors, what are we doing, who receive the reward and are no workmen? We have slipped down into outward affairs; we undertake one thing and perform another. We abandon the ministry of preaching; and, to our punishment as I see it, we are called Bishops — we who hold the name of honor, not of virtue.” When he uses such harsh words against those who were merely less diligent or assiduous in their duty, what, I ask, would he say if he saw that from among the Bishops almost none, or certainly very few, and from among the rest scarcely one in a hundred, ever once in their whole life mount the pulpit? For matters have come to such madness that it is commonly reckoned a thing beneath the dignity of a Bishop to deliver a sermon to the people. In the time of Bernardus things had fallen somewhat further; but we see also with what bitter reproaches he inveighs against the whole order — an order which at that time we may well believe was considerably more sound than it is now.

13 But if anyone rightly weighs and examines the entire face of this ecclesiastical government that exists today under Popery, he will find that there is no den of thieves in which bandits range more lawlessly and without measure. Certainly all things there are so utterly unlike, yea wholly alien from, the institution of Christ, have so degenerated from the ancient institutions and customs of the Church, and so war against nature and reason, that no greater injury can be done to Christ than when his name is held out in defense of so disordered a government.
