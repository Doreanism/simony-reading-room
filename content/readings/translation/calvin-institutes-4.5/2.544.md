5 But even if those grosser abuses were tolerated, is it not always absurd to appoint a presbyter to whom you assign no place? For they ordain no one except to perform the sacrifice. Yet the legitimate ordination of a presbyter is to be called to the governance of the Church; of a deacon, to the administration of alms. They do indeed clothe what they do in much pomp, so that by its very appearance it may command reverence among the simple — but among sane men, what weight can those masks carry when there is nothing solid or true beneath them? For they employ ceremonies either borrowed from Judaism or fabricated by themselves, which it would be better to leave aside altogether. Of a true examination, however (for that shadow they retain I do not trouble with), of the consent of the people, of other necessary matters — not a word. By “shadow” I mean those ridiculous gesticulations composed in feeble and frigid imitation of antiquity. Bishops have their vicars who are to inquire before ordination into \[the candidate’s\] learning. But what do they ask? Whether he can read his Masses; whether he can decline some common noun that comes up in a reading, or conjugate a verb, or knows the meaning of a single word — for it is not required that he be able to render the sense of even one verse. Nor are those rejected from the priesthood who fall short even in these childish rudiments, provided they bring some recommendation of money or favor. Of the same stamp is the fact that when those to be ordained are brought to the altar, a question is put three times in a voice that no one understands, whether they are worthy of the honor. One man who has never laid eyes on them answers (but lest anything be wanting to the formality, this part is assigned to him in the play), “They are worthy.” What is there to fault in these venerable Fathers, except that in sporting with such open sacrileges, they mock God and men without any shame? But because they have been long in possession of this practice, they think it is now their right. For whoever dares open his mouth against these offenses so manifest and so atrocious is dragged by them to a capital trial — like one who in former times had revealed the sacred rites of Ceres in public. Would they do this if they believed there was any God?

6 Now in the conferral of benefices (which was once joined with ordination but is now entirely separated from it) — how much better do they conduct themselves? Among them the procedure is manifold: for not bishops alone confer benefices, and even among those who are called patrons, they do not always have full right; but some hold the right of presentation, while the patrons retain the title of collation as a matter of honor. To these are added nominations from the schools, resignations — whether simple or made by way of exchange — letters of commendation, preventions, and whatever is of that kind. Yet they all conduct themselves in such a way that none of them can reproach another for anything. My contention is this: scarcely one in a hundred benefices in the Papacy today is conferred without simony, as the ancients defined simony. I do not say that all purchase with a price paid out in coin — but show me one in twenty who reaches the priesthood without some crooked recommendation. Some are advanced by kinship or family connection; others by the authority of their parents; others win favor through services rendered. In short, benefices are conferred not so that the Churches may be provided for, but for those who receive them. They call them “Benefits,” by which name they declare plainly enough that they regard them in no other light than as the gifts of princes, by which they either purchase the goodwill of soldiers or reward their labors. I say nothing of the fact that these rewards are bestowed on barbers, cooks, muleteers, and men of that sort of dregs.
