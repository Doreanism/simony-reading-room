For as bandits, after slaughtering men, divide the plunder among themselves: so these men, after extinguishing the light of the Word of God and, as it were, slaughtering the Church, thought that whatever had been dedicated to sacred uses lay open for plunder and pillage. And so, once the division was made, each seized for himself as much as he could.

16 Here all those ancient arrangements which we described were not merely thrown into confusion, but utterly erased and annulled. The urban Bishops and Presbyters, who had grown rich from this plunder and had transformed themselves into canons, divided the greater part among themselves. Yet that the partition was tumultuous is evident from the fact that they are litigating over the boundaries to this very day. Whatever the case, by this settlement it was ensured that not a single farthing of all the Church’s goods should revert to the poor, to whom at least half of it had rightfully belonged. For the canons expressly assign a fourth part to the poor; and the other fourth is designated for Bishops, that they may expend it on hospitality and other offices of generosity. I say nothing of what the clergy were obligated to do with their own portion and for what use they ought to have devoted it — for it has been sufficiently demonstrated that the remainder, which is set apart for churches, buildings, and other expenses, ought in time of need to be open to the poor. I ask: if they had one spark of the fear of God in their heart, could they bear this on their conscience — that whatever they eat and wear comes from theft, yea from sacrilege? But since they are little moved by the judgment of God, let them at least consider that they are men endowed with sense and reason, and that they are trying to persuade us that their ecclesiastical orders are as fine and well-ordered as they are wont to boast. Let them answer me briefly: is the diaconate a license for stealing and robbery? If they deny this, they will also be compelled to confess that they have no diaconate left — since among them the entire administration of ecclesiastical wealth has been openly turned into sacrilegious plunder.

17 But here they employ a very fine pretext, saying that the dignity of the Church is not unfittingly maintained by this magnificence. And they have from their own sect certain men so shameless that they dare to boast openly that those prophecies in which the ancient Prophets describe the splendor of Christ’s kingdom are then at last fulfilled, when that royal pomp is seen arrayed in the sacerdotal order. “Not in vain,” they say, “did God promise these things to his Church: ‘Kings shall come, they shall worship in your sight, they shall bring gifts to you.’ ‘Arise, arise, put on your strength, O Zion; put on your garments of glory, O Jerusalem.’ ‘All they from Sheba shall come, bearing gold and frankincense, and shall proclaim the praise of the Lord.’ ‘All the flocks of Kedar shall be gathered to you.’” If I were to dwell at length in refuting this shamelessness, I fear I might seem foolish. I therefore have no desire to waste words thoughtlessly. Yet I ask this: if some Jew were to abuse these testimonies, what answer would they give him? They would rebuke his stupidity in transferring to flesh and the world what was spoken spiritually of Christ’s spiritual kingdom. For we know that the Prophets, under the image of earthly things, portrayed for us that heavenly glory of God which ought to shine in the Church. For in those blessings expressed by their words the Church was never less abundant than under the Apostles; and yet all acknowledge that the power of Christ’s kingdom then flourished most vigorously. What then do those passages mean? That whatever is anywhere precious, lofty, and excellent, must be won for the Lord.
