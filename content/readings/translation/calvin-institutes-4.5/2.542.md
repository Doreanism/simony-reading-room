We shall give the first place to bishops — would that this could be an honor for them, to hold first rank in this disputation. But the matter itself does not allow me to touch even lightly upon this subject without the greatest disgrace to them. And yet I shall remember what kind of writing I am now engaged in, and I shall not allow my discourse, which ought to be composed for plain instruction, to overflow its proper bounds. Yet let anyone of them who has not altogether lost his sense of shame answer me: what sort of men are commonly elected as bishops today? Certainly any examination of doctrine is too antiquated a practice; and if any regard for doctrine is shown, they choose some jurist who knows better how to argue in court than to preach in the Church. This much is certain: that for a hundred years, scarcely one in a hundred has been elected who had any grounding in sacred doctrine. I spare the preceding centuries not because they were much better, but because the question before us concerns the present state of the Church. If a moral censure were conducted, we should find few or virtually none whom the ancient canons would not have judged unworthy. The one who was not a drunkard was a whoremonger; the one who was free from that vice too was either a gambler, a hunter, or dissolute in some part of his life. These, after all, are the lesser vices which according to the ancient canons exclude a man from the episcopate. Most outrageous of all is the fact that boys barely ten years old have been made bishops by papal dispensation. They have advanced to such lengths of impudence and stupor that they did not shrink even from that extreme and so monstrous an outrage which nature’s own sense utterly abhors. From this it is apparent how devout the elections were where such gross negligence prevailed.

2 As for the election itself, the entire right of the people has been abolished. Votes, consent, signatures, and everything of that kind have vanished; full power has been transferred to the canons alone. They confer the episcopate on whomever they please, and presently bring him before the sight of the people — but to be adored, not examined. Yet Leo protests that reason allows no such thing, and pronounces it a violent imposition. Cyprianus, when he testifies that it flows from divine right that nothing be done without the consent of the people, shows that the contrary practice conflicts with the word of God. The decrees of so many synods most strictly forbid that it be done otherwise, and command that if it be done \[otherwise\] it shall be void. If these things are true, then today there remains in the whole Papacy not a single canonical election valid either by divine or ecclesiastical law. But even if there were no other evil, how will they be able to excuse this — that they have so stripped the Church of its right? Their reply is that the corruption of the times required it: since among the people and the magistrates, hatreds and partisan interests prevailed more than sound and right judgment in the appointment of bishops, the discretion in the matter was therefore entrusted to a few. This may indeed have been the last resort in a desperate situation. But where a remedy has proved more harmful than the disease itself, why is not relief provided for this new evil as well? But, they say, it has been precisely prescribed to the canons themselves what they ought to follow in an election. Yet can we doubt that the people of old understood themselves to be bound by most holy laws, when they saw a rule set before them from the word of God, as they gathered to elect a bishop? For that divine voice of God in which he describes the true character of a bishop rightly ought to count for more than countless thousands of canons. And yet, corrupted by base affections, the people had no regard for law or equity. So today, even though excellent laws are written, they remain buried in their parchments. Meanwhile it has in most cases become established practice — and (as though it were done by good reason) even approved — that drunkards, whoremongers, and gamblers are everywhere advanced to this honor. I say too little: that episcopates are the rewards of adulteries and pimping.
