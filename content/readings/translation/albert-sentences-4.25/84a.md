Rather, just as baptism remained whole in them, so in them the ordination remained whole: because the defect lay in the separation, which is corrected in the unity of peace, not in the sacraments, which, wherever they are, are themselves. And when it seems expedient to the Church that their prelates, upon coming to the Catholic community, should not there administer their honors, nevertheless the sacraments of ordination themselves are not taken away from them, but remain upon them; and therefore the hand is not imposed upon them, lest an injury be done not to the man, but to the sacrament itself. Now just as in baptism there is a right by which it can be given through them, so in ordination there is a right of giving—each, indeed, to their own ruin. But it is one thing not to have, another to have ruinously, and another to have salutarily.
