If, however, it is the will that strives and does go forth into the act of buying or selling, then many simonies are committed in the sale of chalices and other vessels of the Church, which again is false, since such things can lawfully be done.

2. Further, it seems that the will alone, without eagerness, suffices for simony: for simony is more shameful than usury, and one is a usurer from the mere hope of gain; therefore it seems that one is also a simoniac from the mere will or hope of buying.

Further, this seems to be shown at Acts 8:18ff., where Simon never in fact bought something spiritual, but only willed to buy it, and yet he was a simoniac, such that all simoniacs are named after him. Therefore it seems that the will alone makes a simoniac.

3. Further, it is asked why it says *eager*. For what is done out of ignorance is done without eagerness; but there are many who are ignorant that spiritual things ought not to be put under a price. Therefore, if those men put spiritual things under a price by buying or selling, they do not have an eager will in this; therefore they do not commit simony, which is absurd.
