This form of religious life would not be impeded by diversity of place or rite. Therefore, in order to exclude the troublesome who refuse to work but demand their portion inordinately, it is safe and evangelical to be content with the bare necessities of life. And thirdly, the reason for this papal counsel is evident. “For we know,” he says, “that ‘every best gift and every perfect gift is from above, descending from the Father of lights,’ from whom he received the gift of good will who by the holy deliberation of free choice freely determines to serve God. It remains, therefore, that whoever has received or given a gift for the receiving of any divine gift sells or buys the gift of God simoniacally.” And if this law were maintained, few would be seduced into entering private religious orders for temporal advantage, because I do not know whether the Lord, the Father of lights, aspires to this; and if he does, one who is thus fitted by God ought not to be moved by temporal recompense or advantage, and much less ought the buyer of a corrody to be accepted in place of a religious man who prays, at a price. Nor do the infamous disguises avail by which this simony is covered: first, that a purely temporal thing is bought in a corrody. For in Gal. 6, the apostle writes: “Be not deceived, brethren; God is not mocked.” That disorder, I say, by which a gift of God is exchanged in temporal commerce, is condemned, as is plain in Causa I, quaestio 3, in the opening, and in the same quaestio, cap. *Si quis obicerit*. Secondly, it is objected that the monastery, to which the goods belong, is compelled by the accidents of fortune to help itself with its own goods — but this is the same argument as that of the false prelate who sells churches or other gifts of God.
