Others who, from their temporal alms, have endowed colleges of priests in a certain perpetuity, are called the patrons of those places, whose office it is to govern their almsmen lawfully according to the rules of the first patron and the private conditions \[of the endowment\], and to correct offenders according to godly reproof — not by killing or burning, as is done in conquests — but by withdrawing alms in a medicinal manner, and in times of necessity by receiving from the alms a moderate amount for one’s own use, as is evident in C. 16 q. 7, c. *Filiis* and the same question 5, c. *Quicunque*, and the glosses of the doctors agree.

Hence concerning the description and the whatness of the property of our avaricious \[patron\] they grope around saying that it is a temporal thing annexed to the spiritual, and others that it accrues to lordship as a passion to its subject — and so parish churches are said to have their glebe as a fundamental endowment. And these men feign that all patronage principally pertains to the pope, following the text at the beginning of the second book of the Decretals, *De Judiciis*, c. *Quanto divina* \[X 2.1.13\]: “The cause of the right of patronage,” it says, “is so conjoined and connected to spiritual causes that it can be determined only by ecclesiastical judgment” — not that it pertains to the pope or bishop to be the patron of a bodily alms, although he sometimes distributes to the poor the alms he receives as a servant of Caesar or another secular lord.
