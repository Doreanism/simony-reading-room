## CHAPTER II.

But so that the aforesaid pestilence may be the more fully exposed, objections are raised against what has been said. For it seems that the definition of simony given by the laws of the Church is: the deliberate will to buy or sell something spiritual or something annexed to the spiritual — a definition set forth in a papal statement \[in Gratian’s Decretum\], Causa 1, questio 1, *Qui studet*, where it speaks thus: “It is beyond doubt that one who strives to purchase a gift of God by price in holy orders can by no means continue therein or be restored thereafter.” And since one might sophisticatedly argue that he is not buying the spiritual but the temporal annexed to the spiritual, the final clause “or something annexed to the spiritual” is added to remove this evasion, in accordance with the law \[in Gratian’s Decretum\], Causa 1, questio 3, *Si quis obicerit*. From these passages it seems a suspicious presumption to set aside so approved a definition, since Saint Thomas \[Aquinas\], Secunda secundae, questio 5 and 8, approves and expounds it; hence it is said that the sense of the aforesaid definition is to be defended as orthodox. And with this conceded, it is permissible for fuller exposition to vary the formulation in logic, just as it is varied in the logic of scripture; nor have I seen this definition expressed in church law or by any holy doctor except perhaps by way of recitation. But this entire definition tends toward the good, since it applies to the selling and buying of the kingdom of God, although the ordinary gloss on the Decretum appears to say the contrary
