And notwithstanding that Christ not only exposes himself in the midst of the doorway but makes himself the door to exclude the unworthy, yet thieves and robbers and adulterers slip in who steal and plunder the treasury of the Church, and — what is worst — beget sons both carnally and spiritually from the patrimony of the Crucified, who all too greatly disturb the peace of the Church; for the sons of Belial, estranged from the God of peace, must needs generate discord. Let no one be troubled that Christ appears powerless because he permits scoundrels thus to enter and to afflict his bride with such insults, for he who now acts according to the law of one who endures gives to wisdom and discipline its law, placing in the free power of man the capacity both to do well and to do ill. But there will come a day on which the King, furious against those who defile his bride, will judge them most severely. For, as Parisiensis \[William of Auvergne\] says, the simoniac makes God nourish adulterous sons and permits the sons of the devil to disinherit his own sons from the patrimony of the Crucified; he allows God to be simoniacked by his idol, and from the bosom of a revered patron or place he allows simony to be committed; for when anyone celebrates or ministers for money in an office wherein the Holy Spirit is given, he not only makes money his god but immolates both persons to the idol he thus honors. Nor is there any fear, even in the presence of Christ or an angel, to commit simony at the very altar — which exceeds the wickedness of the devil; and this is the original mine \[*minera*\] of the cooling of charity. This is noted by Parisiensis as having been prefigured by the fact that the fire of sacrifice, which had survived for seventy years of Babylonian captivity under the waters, was extinguished when Antiochus sold the priesthood to Jason (2 Maccabees 1 and 2). For under the waters of poverty and tribulation the Holy Spirit sustained martyrdom and gave life to the Church; but once the Church was endowed, dignity became venal and charity was extinguished, in accordance with the prophecy of Christ in Matthew 24. And notwithstanding that so frequently on the basis of scriptural faith this danger is declared to temporal lords and an easy, wholesome, and profitable remedy is pointed out to them, yet the god of this world has so blinded them — in the cause of God — by the dust of temporal affairs and attachment to their own gain, that in the cause of the devil — to make war on Christians for the sake of gain and pride — they rush headlong. Simony, therefore, is the cause why a great part of what is called the Church lies scattered and dead.

## CHAPTER II.

But so that the aforesaid pestilence may be the more fully exposed, objections are raised against what has been said. For it seems that the definition of simony given by the laws of the Church is: the deliberate will to buy or sell something spiritual or something annexed to the spiritual. This is based on a papal statement \[in Gratian’s Decretum\], Causa 1, questio 1, *Qui studet*, where it speaks thus: “It is beyond doubt that one who strives to purchase a gift of God by price in holy orders can by no means continue therein or be restored thereafter.” And since one might sophisticatedly argue that he is not buying the spiritual but the temporal annexed to the spiritual, the final clause “or something annexed to the spiritual” is added to remove this evasion, in accordance with the law \[in Gratian’s Decretum\], Causa 1, questio 3, *Si quis obicerit*. From these passages it seems a suspicious presumption to set aside so approved a definition, since Saint Thomas \[Aquinas\], Secunda secundae, questio 5 and 8, approves and expounds it; hence it is said that the sense of the aforesaid definition is to be defended as orthodox. And with this said, it is permissible for fuller exposition to vary the formulation in logic, just as it is varied in the logic of scripture, nor have I seen this definition expressed in church law or by any holy doctor except perhaps by way of recitation.
