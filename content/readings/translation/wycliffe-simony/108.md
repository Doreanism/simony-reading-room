Since therefore, before the appropriation, both the rector and his vicar were present together in the appropriated church, caring for and feeding their subjects — as I suppose — it is therefore not lawful for a man without compensation to withdraw the cure belonging to the rector. For the prayer of a simoniac in particular cannot be equivalent to the office of preaching, since in that case God would have ordained preaching to no purpose.

Nor does the fiction avail whereby it is said that preaching is now superfluous, since the community knows the faith of Christ sufficiently — for undoubtedly those who ignorantly babble such things are worse than the faithless, and they persecute the faithful who preach Catholic conclusions following from the principles of faith, to the point that they do not believe in God; therefore there has never been greater necessity to preach the Catholic faith. Likewise, according to this folly, a bishop and the pope would be most fully discharged from the cure, because through them the cure of the entire people is distributed in parts to rectors and vicars — and so the bishop and the pope, according to the interpretation of their names, would deprive themselves of the episcopate and the papacy. But these are delirious ravings; for the pope and the bishop and the rector and the vicar each has a cure over the sheep, and the higher one is more fully charged with care — as God is, for whom, according to the philosophers, there is care for all things, although he is most fully charged in an equivocal sense. Hence that God cares for men the apostle implies in 1 Corinthians 9, asking: “Is it for oxen that God has concern?” Hence that supreme shepherd has deigned to have men as helpers in evangelizing the people; in 1 Corinthians 3 the apostle writes: “For we are fellow workers of God.” And therefore, just as God chooses the more fitting helpers, so ought the bishop, knowing that he bears the burden of his curate and of all his sheep; he ought therefore to examine diligently the curate to whom he has committed the cure, knowing that on account of the greater failing of his vicar he bears \[it\] more heavily, and on account of the more diligent discharge of his office he will bear it more tolerably.
