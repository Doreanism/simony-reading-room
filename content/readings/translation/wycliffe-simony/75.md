But here it is commonly said that it is bodily labor that is being sold, not the blessing; and thus the payment is given for expenses, not for the consecration. This, however, is analogous to the act of a servant of a certain wealthy but miserly master: for the servant is reported to have said to his master at a market, that the whole community of that village was giving away the fattest geese free of charge, but was selling beef and other common foodstuffs at a high price; and the master is said to have ordered his servants to eat the donated geese, while he himself wished to be fed on the thick, customary fare; but in the end, when the accounts were tallied, the beef had consumed the entire value of the geese that were pretended to be given freely. And so it seems that the Church is being deceived by this sophistry in its reply. Therefore in \[causa 1,\] quaestio 3, at the beginning, this fiction is destroyed under these words: “Those who purchase temporalities, not spiritualities, are by no means to be counted as simoniacs.” And the reply is that not only those who receive spiritualities, but also those who receive the temporalities attached to them, for a price, are adjudged to commit simony. Whence Malachi, chapter 1, speaking in the person of the Lord, says: “Who is there among you who will shut the doors and kindle my altar freely? I have no pleasure in you, says the Lord of hosts, and I will not accept an offering from your hand.” “To shut the doors,” says Gratianus, “is not the office of the sacred ministry, but merely attached to it.” The payment is therefore not excusable — for in this way any simony could be excused. Likewise, when a bishop basely sells his bodily labor, it seems that he ought to provide it to his subjects for a fair price. But it is clear that a parish could purchase that same bodily labor from an outside worker at a far lower price, which a bishop would take ill with his dependents.
