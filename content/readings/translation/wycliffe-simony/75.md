Likewise one may ask how much such bodily labor of a bishop is truly worth; and since it was once worth less than it is now, and is worth less in a suffragan than in the lord bishop himself, it seems that it is worth more according to the gravity of the person and the need of the parish for that labor. Since then one bishop is weightier than another, and the need for such a consecration can be imagined without limit and priced ever more dearly, it seems that the labor of such a bishop can be raised in price at will; and if expenses are to be reckoned, the bishop receives a rich endowment from king and realm for such labors — indeed the disordered extravagance of the bishop in horses and superfluous household would redound to the costly harm of the Church, which, though true, is altogether unreasonable. And in the same manner simony is committed if a suffragan is hired to collect money in such petty and abject little works. Likewise it seems that the bodily labor of such a bishop is of less value than the same labor of a layman; but such labor of a layman is worth a farthing, as I suppose; therefore that labor of the bishop is worth the smallest coin. Either, then, let him give it freely, or let him justly sell it for a fair price. The assumption is plain, for in marking out according to the measure of reason one must consequently grant, in the given case, that the bishop’s blessing is worth fairly a farthing — suppose, that is, that he went about performing exactly as many blessings as he would receive farthings, all but one; and yet a steward would deny that such a blessing is worth even that.
