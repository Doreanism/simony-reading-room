And notwithstanding that Christ not only exposes himself in the midst of the doorway but makes himself the door to exclude the unworthy, yet thieves and robbers and adulterers slip in who steal and plunder the treasury of the Church, and — what is worst — beget sons both carnally and spiritually from the patrimony of the Crucified, who all too greatly disturb the peace of the Church; for the sons of Belial, estranged from the God of peace, must needs generate discord. Let no one be troubled that Christ appears powerless because he permits scoundrels thus to enter and to afflict his bride with such insults, for he who now acts according to the law of one who endures gives to wisdom and discipline its law, placing in the free power of man the capacity both to do well and to do ill. But there will come a day on which the King, furious against those who defile his bride, will judge them most severely. For, as Parisiensis \[William of Auvergne\] says, the simoniac makes God nourish adulterous sons and permits the sons of the devil to disinherit his own sons from the patrimony of the Crucified; he allows God to be simoniacked by his idol, and from the bosom of a revered patron or place he allows simony to be committed; for when anyone celebrates or ministers for money in an office wherein the Holy Spirit is given, he not only makes money his god but immolates both persons to the idol he thus honors. Nor is there any fear, even in the presence of Christ or an angel, to commit simony at the very altar — which exceeds the wickedness of the devil; and this is the original mine \[*minera*\] of the cooling of charity. This is noted by Parisiensis as having been prefigured by the fact that the fire of sacrifice, which had survived for seventy years of Babylonian captivity under the waters, was extinguished when Antiochus sold the priesthood to Jason (2 Maccabees 1 and 2).
