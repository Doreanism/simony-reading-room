And this seems to be the sense of C. 16 q. 7 \[c. *Monasterium*\]: “A monastery,” it says, “or an oratory canonically constructed shall not be removed from the lordship of its founder against his will, and it shall be lawful for him as patron to commend it to whatever priest he wishes for sacred office with the consent of the bishop of that diocese, lest an evil \[priest\] be appointed.” Behold that a religious house stands in the just discretion of its founder, and much more so in the just judgment of the kingdom and king. Therefore the immediately following decree of Gregorius says: “The order of reason does not permit that a monastery should be claimed by any person and governed at his own discretion, against the will of its founders and apart from their disposition.” There are many canons testifying that whoever has lawfully founded a bodily alms \[i.e., a charitable endowment\] for the Church will retain in himself and in his heirs the care that the rational purpose of the endowment be fulfilled. Therefore many following decrees say that the act of the patron or of his heir ought to be guided by the provident care and wisdom of the local bishop, and that if the wisdom as well as the goodwill of any bishop living among them is lacking, the authority and decree of the bishop of the Church Triumphant cannot fail. And the law of Scripture together with the law of nature — which is the law of the Trinity and of reason — shall serve us as our rule, nor can any law of the Church or any other law that is to be believed Catholic contradict this judgment. Likewise the whole genus of patronage and its right was originally in lay hands, and it has not since been licitly derived from lay hands to the Roman bishop or his satellites; therefore it still remains in lay hands as it was from the beginning. The minor \[premise\] is evident from the fact that the pope acquired no such right either from Christ or from Caesar, since it is a lay office. Hence a patron is called *patronus* from *pater*, as if a defender, and from this he is said to have many offices, as the gloss records on c. *Pie mentis* \[C. 16 q. 7\]: “*Dos*, *edificatio*, *fundus* make the patron” — and from these three: honor, obligation, and benefit.
