Indeed it seems that it would be far less evil to celebrate \[the liturgy\] in the open air than in a place so blessed; for spiritual leprosy infects walls and distant places, just as God, for a lesser sin, submerged Sodom and Gomorrah. On this point it seems to me beyond doubt that in such consecrations the heresy of simony is committed; for whenever the aforesaid definition truly applies to the one who consecrates, then that bishop’s consecration is not a true consecration, but a desecration and a curse, retarding the faithful from the ministry rightly owed to their God. Nor is it easy to imagine any reason for the introduction and reservation of such consecration to the bishop, other than profit and the proud display of episcopal preeminence.

Nor does Scripture furnish any reason why a holy layman or any priest could not bless and consecrate in like manner. For with an abundance of priests assisting, Salomon consecrated and blessed the Temple, 3 Kings 8 \[= 1 Kings 8\]. And priests bless and consecrate the Eucharist, which is infinitely more excellent, as Hieronymus notes and as is set forth in the Decreta, Distinctio 95, chapter *Ecce ego dico* \[*Behold, I say*\]; indeed it seems that if a place is blessed or consecrated on account of so foreign and so lightly grounded a work of the bishop, it is all the more so on account of so present a blessing of a priest when he consecrates the body of Christ. And it seems burdensome to encumber the Church with so many ceremonies, as is apparent in the matter of the cessation of legal rites, and especially from the apostolic synod in Acts 15, and from the decree of Augustinus set forth in Distinctio 12, chapter *Omnia* \[*All things*\], and from the saying of the blessed Isidorus in Book 8 of the *Etymologiae*, chapter *On Heresy*: “It is not lawful for us to introduce anything freely on our own judgment, nor to choose what others have introduced from their own judgment.
