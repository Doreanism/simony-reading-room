Nor is any external act culpable or meritorious except insofar as it proceeds from a disordered or good will. And it is clear that a false assumption is made; although one uses equivocal language in calling simony an external act, in response to the first confirmation it is said that the assumption is true: for Symon the heresiarch, of whom we read in Acts 8, is not recorded to have actually disbursed that gift of God, nor merely to have willed in a disordered way. Yet commonly, in the perpetration of an external act, a disordered will is generated and a new kind of sin is added in manifold ways — which, since it more perceptibly harms the Church, is therefore more heavily punished among men, though commonly less than it deserves. Concerning that matter — how the external act aggravates the sin — it has been said elsewhere. As to the second, the conclusion ought to be conceded. For nothing is more contrary to sacred scripture than the simoniacal appointment of an unfit person, as is plain elsewhere; because thereby the ordinance of God is annulled at the root, the simonist — whether active or passive — is a capital heretic, and especially one who teaches by papal bulls that an ecclesiastical benefice is to be conferred on such an unfit person, and above all one who in teaching this also executes it, as are commonly notaries and others called jurists, who promote this in a disordered way. For since every such person says that he himself, having simony even hidden in his soul, thereby falsely teaches contrary to sacred scripture and defends his simony under some degree of pertinacity — even if he be a simonist only for an instant — therefore no conclusion is more frequently impressed upon ecclesiastical law teachers than this: that simony is a heresy; concerning which I rejoice, on account of the other things that follow.
