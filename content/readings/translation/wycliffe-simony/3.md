— that \[deliberate will\] there is no simony, and through it simony is completed without any external act. Also, because equivocation and sophistry consist in buying and selling, and the rules of definition do not permit a disjunction to be placed in a definition, therefore in place of the disjunct “buying or selling” the term “exchange” is used, which embraces both, as well as private contracts — including deceitful ones — that are called neither buying nor selling. For the same reason, it is not necessary to append at the end that disjunct “spiritual or annexed to the spiritual,” but the whole difficulty lies in the scope of the definition, which is of similar analogy, and in the precise nature of a spiritual thing, since every thing seems to have some quality that is spiritual; but neither of these objections undermines the foregoing position, since this is a common objection against crude philosophers. For whoever sins mortally sells to the devil his own soul and the dignity of the human race. But simony is called more specifically and notoriously the disordered transaction of men concerning the spiritual office of the house of God, which — since it is dangerous with respect to so precious a ministry — is contracted along with any mortal sin committed in connection with that office. And this is the reason why the saints taught the equivocations of signs, so as to drive out this poisonous spiritual trafficking, as Blessed Gregorius says in Homily 42 on the Gospels: “There are,” he says, “some who do not receive monetary rewards from an ordination, and yet bestow sacred orders on account of human favor, and seek from their holiness only the recompense of human praise; these do not give freely what was received freely, since for the office of holiness performed they seek the coin of human favor.” Hence, when the prophet, describing the just man, rightly said: “He who shakes his hands free of every gift.” Notably, he says “every,” because one kind of gift is a gift of service, another a gift of the hand, and yet another a gift of the tongue.
