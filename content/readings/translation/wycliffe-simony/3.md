In treating of simony, one must first presuppose that it exists — attested not only by the testimony of Scripture in Acts 8, but also by public report, which affirms that after the endowment of the Church, simony has come to occupy a large part of what is called the Church. For Scripture recounts how Symon Magus wished to purchase from the apostles the power to confer the Holy Spirit upon whomever he laid hands. And from this very Symon Magus the plague of buying is called simony, just as from Gezia, of whom we read in 4 Kings \[2 Kings\] chapter 5, the plague of selling is called Gezia. And these two poisons, new and old, of the chancery, each embraces the name of the other. The learned define simony as the disordered desire to exchange spiritual things for temporal ones. This is clearer than the genus-definition “deliberate will,” because it is not only lawful for every man, but he ought to buy blessedness, and consequently ought to deliberately \[studiose\] buy what is spiritual — as the Lord speaks through the prophet Isaiah 55: “Come and buy without silver” — and as Blessed Gregorius teaches in his fifth Homily on the Gospels; and Blessed Bernardus and other saints taught the same, since no one attains blessedness otherwise, not even Jesus Christ himself. Likewise, a master ought to exchange his learning deliberately with his disciples, just as a preacher or minister of the sacraments ought deliberately to exchange the spiritual office or merit for a modest temporal subsidy. Therefore, in such an exchange there is no vice as such, as the Apostle says in 1 Corinthians 9: “If we have sown spiritual things for you, is it too much if we reap your material things?” Likewise, it is lawful to exchange deliberately by way of selling and buying tithes, offerings, and other sacred things, as is evident in a thousand cases; therefore the sin does not consist in the exchange as such, but in the disorder of the exchange, which is not primarily in the external act itself but radically in the act of willing. And because an error could arise from another source in the volitional power called the will, it is expressly said that simony consists in the act of the volitional power.
