And since the members of the devil sin more grievously in the time of the law of grace than they sinned in the time of the law of nature, Christ notably says to simoniacs in Matthew 10 that it will be more tolerable for the land of Sodom on the day of judgment than for the people who cast out worthy overseers. And as a sign of his detestation of this kind of sin, Christ is recorded to have entered the temple twice as lord — namely in John 2 and Matthew 21 — and to have cast out the merchants, in a most notable and just figure. And from this, someone says that with the help of the secular arm, the source of simony, the nest of Avignon \[*Avinonicus nidus*\], is in some way scattered of itself. For those who used to sell doves as regards the richer merchandise were cast down from their seats, and the money that used to be formed from the goods of the poor through them, Christ poured out through hidden paths, lest they cast it onto their own tables. There is fear concerning that prophecy of Elizeus in 4 Kings \[2 Kings\] 5: “that the leprosy of Naaman shall cleave to Jezi and to his seed forever.” For since the leprosy of simony must needs be spread through the Church and must have, as it were, one capital chamber as its mine, with one part of that mine weakened, another creeps in; and there is fear that in punishment for sin both or one of them will grow stronger and infect the Church all the more in punishment for the earlier hidden sin.

But one thing I venture to prophesy: that wars will continually endure, whereby nation shall rise against nation and kingdom against kingdom, for as long as this plague of simony lasts. For through this plague the members of the Church are above all set at discord, defiled, and infected, and thereby separated from the head, the king of peace. And this is the reason why it is so frequently called heresy by the doctors and by the laws of the Church, as is evident from 1, questio 1, “Qui studet,” and “Quicunque” and “Cum liqueat,” and the chapter “Quociens” in the fifth book of the Decretals, De Symonia, with many similar laws in these sources. And hence it is said in 3 Kings \[1 Kings\] 13: “Whoever wished to, filled his hand and became a priest of the high places.” And it follows: “For this cause the house of Jeroboam was overthrown and wiped from the face of the earth.” He must therefore avenge the selling of the priesthood of Christ who thus avenged the selling of the priesthood of idols. The same consideration applies to religious orders, to universities of study, and to the disordered exchanges of private colleges. For in all of these simony must be present whenever they deviate from the law of Christ, and thus simoniacal wickedness in them is the harbinger of simoniacal weakening or future ruin. For among the religious it is said that spiritual gifts of God are illicitly exchanged; in universities it is said that the trading of graces \[*gratiae*\], the dealing in halls and other resources, is conducted more fraudulently, so that money may be more craftily drained from poor boys under the pretext of selling \[services\]; and in private colleges it is said that favoritism and the sin of those who build Zion in bloodshed are actively at work. And since all these dissolve the order of the Holy Spirit and thereby the peace, it is necessary that they be laid waste, according to the testimony of Christ in Luke 11: “Every kingdom divided against itself shall be laid waste, and house shall fall upon house.” For since the spirit of discipline will flee the deceitful, it is clear that kingdoms thus divided against themselves must, through the absence of their Comforter, be laid waste. And since everyone who is at discord with God must also be at discord with himself, it is clear that one household will fall upon another and they will destroy each other in turn. And thus nothing destroys contracts and kingdoms — indeed, Christianity itself — more than simoniacal heresy, because it removes the grace of the Holy Spirit through which the members would be held together among themselves and with Christ. Therefore, to sow heresy in a good community is the same as to withdraw moisture from a grassy garden, and thereby to destroy its greenery at the root; and thus woe to the nation, woe to the place, and woe to the man who strives to sow, defend, or foster simoniacal heresy in a community.

For arguments can be made to detest this heresy: first, from the fact that it is impossible for peace or salvation to be present to a Christian except through good will toward the mediator Christ.
