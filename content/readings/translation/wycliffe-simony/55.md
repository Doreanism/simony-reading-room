Likewise, by the same reasoning it is evident that conducting papal progresses is to disturb the church — when the clergy of kingdoms thereby subvert the alms of kings and the lords of secular deadhands.

The first consequence is evident from the fact that the pope can augment the number of his college at will and assign to them a boundless number of benefices without measure, since otherwise his power in this matter would be restricted, and it would be doubtful and troubling to the conscience whether there were some rational limit to it; and the inconvenience is plain to the faithful. Likewise no layman is obliged to give alms to his parish priest beyond what is necessary for his office, and consequently — the pope having thus designated or falsified the fruits of such a benefice — \[the layman\] can be held not bound to provide beyond what is necessary for his \[the curate’s\] spiritual office. From which it seems that a layman may lawfully annul reservations and foreign procurations and provisions of this kind; which would not be the case unless they were unlawful — therefore the conclusion holds. And by the same reasoning it follows that when a benefice is vacant in respect of its due ministry, the instrumental ministry ought also to be vacant. Moreover, since in such a preferment there are two elements — namely, the receipt of temporal fruits and the spiritual ministry — if the pope can separate one from the other, then the worthier second can exist without the first, as was once the case, while the first, being accidental, can exist without the second on an equal footing. From which it seems that the pope can at will abstract from a layman such a portion and consequently dissolve the entire endowment of the church. And further it follows that by delaying this he harms the holy church, since the spiritual minister would then be purer and the alms of the church would then be more abundant; but this judgment would be burdensome to the prelates.
