he says, “who has been ordained, if not by compact, nor under compulsion nor at another’s request, but after having received his letters and pallium freely wishes to offer something to any member of the clergy solely as a matter of grace, we in no way prohibit this from being accepted, for his offering incurs no stain of guilt, since it did not proceed from any solicitation by one seeking such.” As regards the first point, it is clear that the bishop who knows this, or ought to know it, and does not correct or remove it — being like another Elizeus who punished and removed Gezi — must be reckoned as a partaker of the crime through consent. And as regards the law, it is clear that the deed and will of the bishop’s minister contradicts it. For few or none of those ordained — some from need and some from awareness of the leprosy of simony — freely offer the ministers of the bishop any gifts whatsoever; but the ordinand who refuses such \[payment\] either puts up a pledge, or is excluded at the door, or is otherwise penalized to an amount far exceeding what was exacted from his fellow; and because this manner of extortion is of the most shameful sort, it avails nothing to appeal to so greedy an extortioner or to the aforesaid law. For in this way infinite excuses can be contrived: that the cleric is not buying or coveting the spiritual dignity, since it is a burden and not a favor, but that a contract is being made concerning the temporal thing annexed to it; or if this is contested, a fiction is contrived that there is no purchase even of the use of the temporal profit, with its ownership surrendered; and so on with countless other foolish fictions. Therefore the best judgment concerning a simoniac is to observe whether he fulfills the ministry that his office requires, and to judge his simoniacal wickedness by that standard. For one could contrive a third fiction: that he is not buying the temporal thing annexed to the spiritual office, but only its use; and such doubly cunning men do not buy blessedness, and then, according to the blessed Gregory in his *Homilies on the Gospels*, Homily 5, they shall never have it.
