A thousand such laws exist by which it is plainly proven that from the pope and all bishops down to the lowest priest, all purely clerical persons ought to live a common poor life; and if this is perverted, it is clear how widely the simoniacal heresy has spread today. Nor do the veiled procurations heal the disease, of which \[it is spoken\] in Book V of the *Decretales*, title *De Symonia*, chapter *Cum sit Romana* \[*Since it is Roman*\], for these are understood as referring to things freely given, not to stipends exacted from those faithfully laboring, when the goods of the Church do not otherwise suffice. Nor is the second infamous fiction of any avail, whereby it is said that by the law of nature a man must first provide for his parents and kinsmen, for this has some color of justification only for those placed in greater or equal necessity, so that they may be relieved from want — not that they may be exalted as illustrious and noble in the eyes of the world. Nor is the third fiction of any avail, whereby it is said that the dignity of the episcopal office requires a household, adornments, and sumptuous expenditures, for the dignity of Christ and the apostles shone forth in the greatest poverty without these things.

And the simony of the bishop in his entry and in his progress is evident; and concerning simony in his departure the greatest fear is warranted, because all those who defend such an opposite position in life and deed — persecuting simple people who proclaim this judgment as heretics — and do so right up to the end of their lives without repentance, appear to be foreknown sons of the devil, hardened \[in sin\].
