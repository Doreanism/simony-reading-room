For it is not in the people’s interest to attend tortures or spectacles; therefore, much less \[is it in their interest to attend\] an office in which so great a dishonor is done to Christ and in which the priest kills himself and harms the people. The refusal, therefore, of communion with such persons, by turning those who associate with them back to themselves \[i.e., to the faithful\], would avert from the people the participation in crime contracted through consent, and in both respects would avoid idolatry.

As regards the ministry of bishops, it is plain that, with simony reigning in them, whatever they do is blasphemous. They therefore do not then bless, nor consecrate, nor ordain, nor confirm, but in their attempts they accomplish the complete opposite — as is clear from countless testimonies. Concerning orders, it is clear at question 1, chapter Si quis episcopus: “One who has been ordained \[by such persons\], let him profit nothing from this ordination or promotion.” And the decree of the Eighth Council follows, chapter Qui: “Whoever has consecrated anyone by means of money, or has been consecrated by another \[for money\], is a stranger to the priesthood.” And a third testimony is borne by the same decree of Gregory, who writes in chapter Quicunque: “Whosoever sells or buys holy orders cannot be priests. Hence it is written: ‘Anathema on the giver and anathema on the receiver — this is the simoniacal heresy.’ How therefore, if they are under anathema and are not holy, can they sanctify others?
