### CHAPTER VI.

It remains to examine more particularly how a bishop may incur simony; and it is clear from what has been said that he can be simoniacal in his entry, in the course of his tenure, and in his departure. In his entry: whenever, by whatever means, a man takes episcopal governance preferentially or rather principally for the sake of temporal gains — whether worldly display or profit — rather than for the merit of blessedness or the seeking of the people’s souls for God through the teaching of his law in word and deed; and this is easy to judge from his manner of living. Whether therefore the gift proceeds from the hand, from the tongue, or from an intermediary, whether through a curate or through another, so long as this disordered will is present, simony is committed. For in every act of the will concerning sin, in every positive \[enactment\], something divine lies hidden; therefore, in the disorder which neither pope nor God can justify, simony lurks. The manner of proceeding must therefore be conformed to the divine will, not by seeking pretexts that all such things may be done rightly, for while God authorizes and acts, whatever is enacted is rightly done. For it happens that a man, attending to the lawful manner, may take pride meritoriously; as Isaiah 61 \[says\]: “But you, my sons, shall be called priests of the Lord, ministers of our God.
