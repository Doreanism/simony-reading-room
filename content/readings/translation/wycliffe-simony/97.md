And the faithful are thus astonished that papal laws which savor of profit, though they be without foundation and contrary to the faith of scripture, are reckoned, observed, and practised more than the Gospel; but papal laws founded in the Gospel that savor of religion are set aside as heretical or profane — and this is a manifest mark of heresy and infidelity in the highest degree. Nor is valid the excuse by which so many titles of commendation are attached to them; for they say — and perhaps it is true — that unless they bestow the goods of the poor upon these lawyers and mercenaries, these would circumvent and despoil them of their revenues; therefore, for the sake of retaining their possessions, it is necessary to impart the goods of the poor to such men. But those who argue thus are worse than the infidel described in that chapter of infidels, Rom. 3, who say: “Let us do evil that good may come”; yea, they say something worse: that sacrilegious and heretical things must be committed in their interest so that still more execrable and heretical things — such as churches and estates simoniacally held — may continue in their possession. For according to that sophistry they would perpetrate the greater evil and again a greater, and so without end, so that the evil which was reckoned the greater might be avoided.

Therefore this excuse is contrary to the apostle’s principle, and is without doubt an incitement of the devil to lead his own more deeply into error. For since by decree of the Church this was in holy times sacrilege and heresy, it does not become lawful on account of a multiplication of tyranny or of sin; it follows that it remains heretical just as it was at first, for evil days do not excuse but increase malice.
