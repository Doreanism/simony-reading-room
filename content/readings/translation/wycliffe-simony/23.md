And it is clear that for the satisfaction of a simoniacal cleric who perceives himself to be unfit to occupy that office, resignation of the benefice is required; for otherwise he persists continuously as a simonist and consequently as an obstinate heretic, since he continuously commits simony through the exchange of a temporal thing and of the prelate’s dignity — which, continuing to hold without the required condition \[i.e., fitness\], he does not satisfy by shaking off the heresy, but persists in the initial incapacity contracted simoniacally. But if one who was previously fit becomes incapacitated by misfortune, by right the Church alone is bound to provide for him in the necessities of life — with the full proviso that the Church not be deprived of one discharging the curate’s office. Nor is such a person’s habitual penalty or punishment to be weighed by will or by arbitrary judgment, but according to the law which the Holy Spirit set forth in his apostle, as is made clear at length elsewhere, *De Officio Episcopi Et Curati*. Nor is simony known otherwise.

Secondly, it is doubted whether it is lawful to aspire to the aforesaid perfection. And it seems that it is, because that is a perfect state which no one enters except willingly, and it is not lawful to lie for the sake of piety or at the command of law or of a lord. And this is confirmed by the definition of the apostle in 1 Timothy 3, who says: “Whoever desires the episcopate desires a good work.” Here it must be said, following Crisostomus in Homily 18 of the *Opus Imperfectum*: in such terms there are two things to consider, namely the ministry and the dignity. The first is to be desired, as the apostle specifically says; but the second is to be fled — and much more \[to be fled is\] dignity without office, which is a lie of the disciple of Antichrist; and entirely doubled dignity derived from temporal endowments, because this would be to will in a disordered way, on account of the abundance of the Lord’s ordination, that which \[the Lord\] would destroy. And it is clear how the laws and the sayings of the saints in this matter are to be understood. For a man ought not merely to be unwilling \[for\] temporal honor — and especially \[honor arising\] from an abundant endowment of temporalities — but to grieve continually \[over it\].
