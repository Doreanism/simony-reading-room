For beginning from the lowest it is plain that the Masses and prayers of a simoniac are turned to his own sin and to the detriment of the Church. Hence in the fifth book of the Decretals, concerning simony, it is written in the chapter Tanta: “So great is the corruption of this crime that even slaves after their masters, and any criminals whatsoever, are admitted to bring an accusation.” And it follows in the same place: “Every sinner may sing Mass except the simoniac, whom anyone — even a harlot — may remove and accuse as one ordained to orders wrongfully.” Since therefore this law is infallibly true, it seems that few \[persons\] would hear a priest’s Mass, because far too many are simoniacs in deed or by consent. Nor is it necessary to wait until the case is brought before the canonical or civil court against such simoniacs, because — as the gloss teaches — such a simoniac is suspended ipso facto from the outset; for since a harlot or another unfaithful person can instruct a judge and so the Church concerning the vice of simony, the danger of communicating with simoniacs \[exists\] even before human judgment, just as if it had been continuously feigned — since the truth of the danger is no less known to the Church.
