Moreover, by some worldly contingency or heavenly event the monastery may easily be rendered incapable of repaying the temporal goods it thus sells. For in ordinary commerce the buyer commonly receives everything purchased together before giving the price. But in this foolish exchange, the sale is insufficient to secure the buyer, and the patron is bound to dissolve the contract of his alms — and so the private buyer of a corrody, when the contract would be illicit, would never taste anything of the corrody he had thus bought; hence it is said that rather than make such a contract in a state of poverty, a man would permit and bring about the complete dissolution of his house in the world, being thus relieved. For the first \[course\] is repugnant to the law of God, involving a sin that a man would not commit even for the salvation of the whole world, still less for the salvation of a foolish new human invention, since there is no precedent in the law of Christ for making such perpetual private alms — therefore without doubt the ordering of God would be in every way better, as is plain elsewhere. Whence the aforesaid papal counsel would dictate that such private conventicles be dissolved, since they dissipate the law of Christ. But leaving this matter for those who study it in particular, it is plain how great a simony is committed by the aspiring prelates of religious houses: for all who, by temporal gift, human favor, or worldly means, have wished to be thus preferred in their order, incur as excommunicated heretics the leprosy of simony at entry; and likewise any novice who has desired such an order for temporal or bodily quiet, for lavish food, or for worldly excellence beyond what he had in secular life, is straightway a simoniac, because he desires the spiritual dignity or status of the order inordinately and excessively for temporal advantage.
