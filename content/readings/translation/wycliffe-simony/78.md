And if other honorable signs implicit in Scripture are sought out, care must be taken, first, that they be observed in the order and form in which they were originally instituted, and second, that — the law of Scripture not being set aside — they not be given excessive weight. A consecration restricted to the bishop can rightly be performed alongside these; indeed a bishop may in case of necessity lawfully receive from those he ministers to the necessities of life — exaction being set aside — as a freely given blessing, as many laws in Book V of the *Decretals*, *De Symonia* \[*On Simony*\], teach. But such reception without simony you will find rarely or never, and indeed it seems incompatible with endowment. Whence in causa 1, quaestio 3, final chapter, Pope Innocentius writes as follows: “If any person shall have acquired prebends, or a priory, or a deanery, or an honor, or any ecclesiastical promotion, or any ecclesiastical sacrament — such as chrism or holy oil for the consecration of altars or churches — through the execrable fire of avarice by means of money, let him be deprived of the honor so wickedly acquired, and both buyer and seller and intermediary shall be struck with the mark of infamy. And let nothing be required, either for sustenance or under pretext of any custom, before or after, nor let the recipient presume to give anything, since this is simoniacal; but let him freely enjoy, without any diminution, the dignity and benefice conferred upon him.” From this holy law it is clear, however the doctors may gloss it, how perfectly simony is committed in these matters, and especially under the guise of custom, of honor, or of the reservation of first fruits,
