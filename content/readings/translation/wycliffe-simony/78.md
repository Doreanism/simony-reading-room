And the same consideration applies to the other ministries of the bishop, such as confirmation, ordination, and the consecration of chrism, vestments, and ornaments. In confirmation, because it is light, brief, and public, a bodily payment is not commonly received by the bishop or his minister. But in the others the Church is deceived by sophistry, with ministers receiving simoniacally and the bishop becoming simoniacal through consent; for it is said that in the ordination of clerics, the clerk of the bishop who inscribes the name of the ordinand — and the barber too, even if he has not shaved the ordinand, which is very remarkable — and even he who presents the letters dimissory will not have them returned before the fee has been paid to the devil, so that with the simoniacal receipts accumulating in the smallest transactions of the ministers, the great heap of simony grows at an annual episcopal ordination into many pounds. Nor does the amount paid matter, since just as the poor widow who cast a farthing into the treasury gave more than all the others, so a cleric despoiled of a farthing from a more disordered will is despoiled through a greater simoniacal heresy. Moreover, since all the aforesaid plunderings by ministers redound upon the master as arch-heretic, it is clear how blameworthy is their master, the bishop, who permits himself to be struck by so manifold a simony for so small a price. Whence in causa 1, quaestio 2, in the chapter *Sicut* \[*Just as*\], there is a general precept: “Just as it does not befit a bishop to sell the hand that he lays on, so too a minister or notary ought not to sell his voice or his pen in an ordination”; for in both cases the reasoning of simony is identical.
