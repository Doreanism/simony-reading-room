For if God has commanded every man concerning his neighbor, the servant to whom God has given the talent of knowledge ought to assist his superior by correction; and the one who instead, anointing him with the oil of the sinner and eating with him of idolatrous things, will say with Isaiah at the last: “Woe is me, because I have kept silence” — for the faithful man in whom the love of God predominates will not keep silence so foolishly in the cause of God against Symon. Nor does God weigh what or how much of his goods his servant has consumed, but in what manner and by what means he has sought them; otherwise robbers, thieves, and criminals would be excused for every abuse of the goods of the Lord.

### Fourthly, it is devised that cloisterers to whom churches are appropriated are, by virtue of the appropriation, discharged from the cure committed to vicars, and that the office of preaching does not belong to them but only that of devout prayer.

In this excuse of Lucifer I marvel, first, how the accursed appropriation — with no foundation in the law of God, in which all truth is contained — has such power to discharge mercenaries from the cure of their sheep, when a benefice and the corresponding office follow inseparably upon one another. Likewise, since nothing in the house of the Lord ought to be disordered, and the office of praying is least burdensome, one who flees the tumults of the world seems most disorderly to appropriate so many churches to so few merely for that bare office; for in the primitive Church, and even today, that office is discharged more easily and more fruitfully. Likewise, since the office of preaching is the highest duty of a superior, as is here supposed, if by virtue of appropriation it can be withdrawn from subjects, then by the same reasoning the entire pastoral office \[can be withdrawn\]; how therefore will those rectors answer on the day of judgment for the souls on whose tithes they feed, if they have not governed those souls by preaching the way of virtue and the law of the Lord? It would indeed be a simoniacal exchange to purchase prayers for so great a temporal stipend; for the parishioner would obtain those prayers more fruitfully by prudently shaking off this servile commerce himself.
