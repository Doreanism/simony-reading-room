Infected, I say, with the stinking filth of temporalities plastered upon it; for with that removed, bishops would not be so disorderedly attached to those things, nor so improvidently burdened with so great a lay retinue in distributing the goods of the poor, as the Decree says in causa 1, quaestio 3, chapter *Ex multis* and causa 1, quaestio 1, chapter *Non est putanda* \[*It must not be supposed*\], with the testimony of Scripture: “One who wrongfully receives something in order to dispense it well is burdened rather than helped.”

In the third way a bishop commits simony by inordinately accumulating money or some other craved temporal thing; and this is done in countless ways, according to the wiles of the devil who has long studied this art. For there is no doubt that whatever bishop inordinately occupies that office for the sake of worldly praise or honor is a simoniac, as is clear from causa 1, quaestio 3, chapter *Non solum* \[*Not only*\], because he inordinately exchanges with the devil the spirituality of the episcopate for the most worthless temporal advantage. And as regards the accumulation of money, there are a thousand paths machinated by the devil: at one time bishops, by blessing churches, altars, or cemeteries, take excessive sums; at another time they sell the sacraments or sacramentals they administer at so high a price as to suffice for themselves and their associates. As regards the consecration of places, it is said they commonly take five marks or more, and observe this more rigorously than they observe the Gospel, punishing those who are slow to pay more savagely. Do we believe that this can be excused before God from the heresy of simony, when they are manifestly selling a blessing or a consecration for temporal payment?
