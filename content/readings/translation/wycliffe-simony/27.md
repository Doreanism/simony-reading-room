### CHAPTER III.

It remains to descend more particularly, step by step, to the parts of the Church, in order to see whether they contract simony — and first, whether the pope or his curia could commit simony. It seems not, because the pope could not be enticed by the aforementioned heresy, nor could it be committed in his presence, since it is lawful for true lords to accept from their own property as they wish, and much more clearly it is lawful for the pope to receive from what has been conferred upon the bride for the governance of his bride. But the pope is the principal lord of all the goods of his Church; therefore it is thus lawful for him to manage the goods of his bride, and nothing therefore that he licenses with respect to the goods of his bride is to be deemed simoniacal — for this is the reason why God cannot commit simony. In reply, it is said first that the pope together with his curia can very easily commit simony; the conclusion is evident in fact, since many popes were simoniacs and were accordingly deposed, as I have recounted elsewhere from many chronicles. And the same is made evident by numerous papal laws: Dist. 23, *In nomine domini*; Dist. 4, *Si papa*; Dist. 79, *Si quis peccatum*.
