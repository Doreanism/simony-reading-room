But against this it is argued: it seems that every patron, after the malice of the community has become hardened, ought to dissolve his patronage — which is almost contrary to the law of C. 17 q. 4, where the decree speaks thus: “All those who plunder churches and alienate their revenues — we anathematize them from the threshold of Holy Mother Church and cast them out by apostolic authority, and condemn them, and judge them to be sacrilegious; and not only them, but all who consent to them, because not only those who commit \[the act\] are judged guilty, but also those who consent to those committing it — for equal penalty comprehends both those acting and those consenting.” Here I say that it would be useful and honorable for the Church to dissolve many of its endowments; and in this, patronage is not destroyed but improved, being converted from an irreligious temporal tradition to a spiritual patronage. But such a change would be blessed and meritorious for patrons, because there is no doubt that Christ — omnipotent, omniscient, and all-willing — ordained and fitted the life and state of his bride most suitably for the acquisition of blessedness. And he ordained the apostles as poor, propertyless \[persons\] dispersed throughout the world to preach the Gospel; therefore such a propertyless state would be more open and exalted for the acquisition of blessedness. And all the semi-learned \[*scioli*\] brothers or priests attest to this judgment, and the decree of Nicolaus III in the Sixth \[Book\], *Exiit qui seminat* \[VI 5.12.3\], affirms it. Since therefore Christ did not teach his apostles to be more beloved as propertied men or as assembled in cloisters, it seems that this \[propertied state\] is a burden and a detriment to the Church. A patron, therefore, who would dissolve this imperfection — as a helper of God, not as a deceitful earthly founder \[seeking to perpetuate\] his own name — would greatly benefit those so imprisoned and oppressed by earthly things, and consequently the whole Church. For if the entire clergy lived in a propertyless state, as they lived by the ordinance of Christ in the primitive Church, it would in itself be disposed toward holiness
