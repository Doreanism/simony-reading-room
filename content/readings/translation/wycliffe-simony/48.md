in De Consecratione, distinctio 5: “He errs not a little,” it says, “who prefers a mediocre good to a greater good”; and natural reason concurs. The minor premise of the argument is evident from the fact that God cannot elect anyone other than the most apt for the office to which he himself elects, since his appointment makes one more fit; and otherwise God would be irrational or foolish. Likewise, calibrating the fitness of the person as required to the office for which he is elected, it is evident that it is not sufficient for electors to choose a good person while setting aside another who is better fitted to such an office — because if another is more suited to that office, God readily makes that person fit, adapting that person to that office, and consequently wills the end for the sake of which he so adapts; therefore the one who thwarts this fitting and the will of God acts wrongly in this matter. For thus an assembly of electors could on one occasion deviate a little from the divine ordering and will, and on another occasion deviate just as much again, and thus by degrees, until it had secured the most unfit person in the prelacy. For the person who is now foreknown to be a member of the devil becomes immediately afterward a nefarious hypocrite subverting the church; and in this manner one believes it to be so with the election of the pope and other prelates of the church, which proceeds to a worse outcome as a punishment for prior sin. Who, I ask, knows the capacity and perseverance in good of a prelate, or his cunning by which, while harming the church, he will not submit to correction? Moreover, it is often according to the divine will, as a punishment for the people’s sin, to give them a foreknown reprobate as prelate, as is plain in the case of Saul, who was such a one chosen by the Lord, 1 Samuel 8, whom God said through the prophet Hosea 13 that he would give to the people in his anger.
