For there is a reason why he receives first fruits specifically from one whom he promotes rather than from one previously promoted by a secular lord. And no reason for this can be imagined other than that he promotes the one in this way; therefore he sells or exchanges a spiritual benefice for temporal advantage. Nor is there any doubt that this is a disordered exchange, since often, on account of the inadequacy of the instrument for spiritual ministry, many souls perish who are more precious than all this sensible world. Moreover, temporal lords, even kings, are permitted in great necessity to receive a fifteenth, with the consent of those to be taxed having been sought; but in the matter of these first fruits the pope frequently takes everything for himself, receiving either the assessed value of the benefice, or the tax, or the separate valuation of the whole. Likewise, however capable a person may be, if he is unwilling to pay first fruits he is expelled; and however incapable, if he is willing to pay he is quietly inducted — for which no reason can be imagined except papal profit as one of the goods exported. And this is plainly evident from the fact that when a single bishopric in England falls vacant, the pope shifts three or four bishoprics, so that he has served the cause of election and despoliation with respect to each of those bishops, since each has been further promoted; with the result that the pope receives first fruits from each of them, and neither believer nor unbeliever can imagine any reason except that so much shifting is multiplied in order that the pope may collect first fruits many times over. For the fitness of the person is not weighed, as if a more richly endowed person were proportionately better promoted.

Nor are the fictitious pretexts adduced in this matter of any avail. The first claims that the pope, by virtue of his preeminent universal lordship, may take from the goods of the church — and especially from mortmain — whenever and however he wishes. But this lordship blasphemously invented for the pope would take from the king and the secular arm all civil dominion, because, the king’s supreme dominion being removed, the king and all his tenants would be mere vassals, clients, and bailiffs.
