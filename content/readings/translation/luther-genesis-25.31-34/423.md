For they swept the Word, the name of God, and the Church into this trade — and yet nothing was truly bought; they merely invented a god of that kind, one who is willing to sell grace. The intent and will is simoniac, but in truth it is only a sieve and a he-goat \[i.e., a futile enterprise — milking the he-goat into a sieve, as the proverb has it\].

Yet this evil is naturally implanted in the hearts of all people. If God were willing to sell grace, we would receive it more eagerly and gladly than when he offers it freely. Previously, when simony reigned, all people were most avid to build endless monasteries and churches, because it was a buying and selling of the kingdom of heaven. Now, when it is offered freely and proclaimed: “God sent his Son in the flesh, to give eternal life to all who believe. You have merited nothing; it is given freely — liberation from death and from sins, the Holy Spirit, and the kingdom of heaven.” This mercy and divine generosity the world despises. Before, when we were taught how we could earn grace by our works, everyone competed to pour out all their zeal and resources. Just as this little town of Wittenberg gave a thousand gold coins a year to the monks.

Everywhere the maxim of Ambrosius is celebrated — most perniciously: “The kingdom of heaven belongs to the poor,” from the saying of Christ: “that they may receive you into the eternal tabernacles.” This saying, like a sharp razor, pierced the minds of all. “If the rich wish to be saved, let them buy from the poor,” they cried; “for the poor are the Friars Minor and other religious brothers — they hold the kingdom of heaven; from them you must buy.”

What kind of monstrosity, portent, and prodigy is this, then, that the world spurns redemption from sin, death, and hell, freely offered through Christ? If simony were to reign again, all would lavishly pour out their wealth. But when we teach from the word and command of Christ: “Receive freely, and give freely,” it falls on deaf ears — for parish priests, ministers, and schoolmasters. Indeed, they plunder those whom they ought to adorn and support. Are these not horrible frenzies of darkness? Thus every person is a simoniac, and yet no such sale can actually be made. For God has given nothing to any person, nor to the monks for their vows, save flames and the eternal torments of hell.

One can also see how wretched and dangerous a thing it is to have fallen from the cardinal doctrine of justification by faith apart from works. For when that doctrine is obscured and extinguished, the whole world becomes simoniac. Thus the Turks too strive to appease God by their works, and to buy spiritual things with money and with merits. Therefore simony exists in name, as it is described in canon law. For they redeem nothing and obtain nothing by price and merits; but that intent and will has sunk its roots deep in the human heart, by which every person is naturally a simoniac — wishing there were such a god as could be appeased by works and human merits, to whom one could present the account: “I have done many things, I have fasted much — look upon these, and sell me salvation and eternal life.” But God does not acknowledge or hear such workers of iniquity.
