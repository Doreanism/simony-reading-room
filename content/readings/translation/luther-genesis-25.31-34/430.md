This is how hypocrites and false brothers typically behave — tragen den baum auff beiden Achseln \[carrying the beam on both shoulders\]. They are shape-shifters: at one moment they pretend to yield and give something; at the next they snatch back what is owed them by no right — and yet they have their glosses by which they cover and excuse their hypocrisy, so as to retain the red stew along with the birthright. For they wish to serve both God and their belly, or Mammon, at the same time.

So Esau reasoned: Even if I sold my birthright, swore an oath, and gulped down the morsel, and even if my most contemptible brother now appears to have taken the birthright — still, I will keep it and not hold to my promise. For he had not sold it in earnest, but meant to mock his brother and put on a show as if he had sold it, while nevertheless retaining his rights. But the Letter to the Hebrews testifies that he was afterward rejected, and that although he tried to recover the birthright — for he repented of the sale — yet that repentance availed nothing: not that he was not saved, but that by no tears could he recover the blessing once forfeited. I therefore hold that the morsel was the price of this sale, and I do not accept the gloss of the Rabbis, since they are carnal men who understand nothing in Holy Scripture.

## Chapter XXVI.

There arose a famine in the land, besides the former famine that was in the days of Abraham; and Isaac went to Abimelech, king of the Philistines, in Gerar.

It has often been said in this whole book that the histories of the fathers are described as if in a very cheap and coarse rind, and set forth without any splendor or pomp of religion, righteousness, and wisdom — indeed under the most abject appearance of household management and bodily life. For what else does Moses recount of Isaac than that he was born of his father Abraham, that he begat sons, tended flocks, and wandered through various regions? There is little or nothing there about the prayers or the monstrous religious observances of monks. And what is it to me that he was a husband, that he slept with his wife? Surely these things are not what are to be taught in the Church? For when the flesh looks upon the outward and very common appearance of the fathers’ lives, it clings to that, perceives no edification from it, and is only offended. Then afterward it thinks of learning about the life of Saint Bernhardus, of Antonius, and others like them, where there are marvelous and incredible feats of abstinence, fasting, and vigils — where there is no use of women, servants, still less of livestock. The flesh laughs at the commonplace and lowly things in the household life of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob, and instead pursues what is splendid and magnificent. Or if these histories are ever read in churches, no one marvels at them, because no one perceives the true dignity and the true adornments of these stories.
