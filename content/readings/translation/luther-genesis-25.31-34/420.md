As for Jacob, he denies that he sinned by buying it, because by divine ordinance he was the firstborn, in accordance with the declaration: “The elder shall serve the younger.” Esau was in possession of a birthright that was not rightfully his: for although he claimed it for himself by right of birth, he did not hold it by divine will. But as the common saying goes, the position of the one in possession is the stronger. Therefore Jacob could not disturb him; and Esau defended himself by human law, divine law, and birth order, all of which, among all peoples, assign possession and lordship to the firstborn twin. Moreover, the Law grants a double portion of the paternal estate. Unless therefore God had changed and revoked that right, Esau’s birthright would have remained intact. But because Jacob knows by a divine voice that it belongs to him, he does not sin by acquiring through the sale what is rightfully his own; indeed, he did well to wait patiently for every opportunity to acquire the birthright that was owed to him.

No other more convenient means could have been found to accomplish this than through a purchase. For although that cooked dish or morsel is not a just price for a birthright, it nonetheless pertains to the settlement of a dispute — or, as the jurists say, to “buying off a vexation.” For it often happens that someone is harassed by sycophants \[i.e., vexatious accusers\] who drag him into court, and, by producing false witnesses or devising other tricks, attempt to seize his property as though unjustly occupied by him. Those who love peace, though they could defend their right, prefer to throw money at the sycophants rather than pursue litigation, in order to escape their calumnies. In such a case, the common proverb holds: *Man muß dem teufel den fegen auffleben* \[“One must give the devil his broom”\]. In this manner Jacob gave a small scraped morsel and freed himself from his brother’s harassment — who had been boasting about the birthright — so that henceforth he might hold his property and his right in peace. These are roughly the points Lyra brings forward at this place, quite well and piously for that time.

Above, however, in the history of Abraham, who bought a field for burial in Genesis 23, I spoke about simony. The question was: should Abraham therefore be called a simoniac, since burial is among sacred things, and the buying or selling of sacred things is called simony? But we heard that that purchased field was a profane thing, subject to a carnal lord. And the sale of a field is an entirely civil transaction. Abraham therefore did not sin when he bought the field and established a tomb there; otherwise, everything that Christians buy for necessary purposes — bread, meat, wine, all of which are turned to the use of the saints — would be simoniacal, which is absurd.

Now, laws and canons call it simony when spiritual things are bought for money: they say that selling a cemetery is simony, because it is sacred to Christians. Likewise, selling a portion of a parish, or lands belonging to what they call ecclesiastical benefices — this was that great sin against which theologians and canonists cried out with one voice in earlier times, and by which they condemned bishops and Romanists who bought or sold bishoprics.
