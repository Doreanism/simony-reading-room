But they accomplished nothing with their outcries, and now they are laughed at by the whole papacy, which is substantially filled with simony. Indeed, if you were to remove simony from the papacy, you would have removed the sun from it. For the courtiers have no other business than buying and selling priesthoods.

To reform the Roman Church from simony is therefore to lay it utterly waste. Where would be the splendor and pomp of the Cardinals of the Roman Curia, if you were to take away simony? Indeed the whole Curia itself would collapse entirely. For the number of Cardinals is far too great — they have already devoured all the goods of the Church. The monastery of \[Saint\] Agnes in Rome, which once supported 160 persons, the Cardinals have plundered and consumed. All things therefore are full of simony according to canon law. And whoever would wish to remove simony from the papacy would do the same as one who wished to place the Devil in heaven. John Huss and others who taught at that time taxed this vice severely, but in vain. For the Roman Curia is a condemned thing, which all ought to shun and detest. What then could it prescribe concerning faith and good morals? The papacy is a congregation of demons and most wicked men. They themselves condemn simony, yet they live and are sustained by it. Therefore we are rightly warned in Revelation 18: “Come out of her, my people, that you be not partakers of her sins, and that you receive not of her plagues.” All the colleges are of the Devil, and therefore let us flee, lest we become partakers of their plague.

We could, however, excuse these thefts of theirs by some gloss, so that they would not be called simony — because they are civil seizures. Nor is there any difference between such simony and civil occupation. Just as we see nobles today striving among themselves to seize estates, farmlands, and villages. Our own benefices and stipends, from which we draw benefit, are also simoniacally obtained, “gathered from the wages of a harlot,” as it is said in Micah 1. But if they plunder the goods of the Church not for the use of godly priests, but turn them to luxury and pomp, we turn them \[to good use\] — namely that the ministers of the Church may be sustained and the schools preserved. And so we take no account of this simony. But since they burden themselves by their own canon law, we say to them: “Out of your own mouth I judge you.”

True simony, however, is the buying and selling of a spiritual thing for money. As if someone wished to purchase grace, faith, charity, miracles, or the virtues of the Holy Spirit with which the Church of God is adorned — which is what Simon attempted in Acts 8. Not that the thing itself can be conveyed by buying or selling sacred things. For the Holy Spirit does not give his gifts for money; nor does God accept gold and silver in exchange for the forgiveness of sins, for life, eyes, senses, and all his benefits. But the divine munificence scatters his gifts upon the grateful and ungrateful alike; he requires only thanksgiving, that we say: Blessed be the Lord God in his gifts. That divinity be ascribed to him — that is, that we acknowledge him as God, whom we confess with our whole heart, mouth, and deed to be the Creator. For no other price can be paid for such great gifts.
