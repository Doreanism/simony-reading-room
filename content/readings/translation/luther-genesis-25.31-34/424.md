The simony of the canonists is the lesser kind and purely corporeal, belonging to the category of civil plunder. But when the Pope receives crowns, cities, and territories from kings and monarchs, and does so under cover of masses offered to strengthen their cause — presenting himself with an appearance of piety, wishing to seem the best of men — there he is at his worst and utterly simoniacal. For in the other case he does harm only by example and confesses simony through acts of civil robbery; here he does not acknowledge sin but praises and proclaims justice and religion. These things, then, I wished to note, so that it might be understood what true simony is, and so that we might remain in the pure doctrine which teaches how God has given us his Son, and through him all heavenly and spiritual goods — which the world wishes to purchase at a price rather than receive freely.

Let us return to the narrative. It is apparent that Jacob pressed the purchase in such a way that he cannot be excused or freed from the charge of simony, because he says openly: “Sell me your birthright.” By what right, then, does he demand this? And does he commit so great a sin as to attempt to buy the blessing, which is spiritual and pertains to Christ and to future things — all of which are sacred and look toward the life to come? In this life all things are profane and polluted. The sacraments and sacred things, however, were not instituted for the sake of the present life, but in order to be a certain sacred preparation for future things. For by the Gospel I am prepared, reborn, and renewed for the life to come. It was not handed to me so that I might grow rich from it. But whoever is a pastor and administers these things in order to acquire the wealth and glory of this age is a simoniac. So too the use of Baptism is this: that I might be transferred and reposited from the womb of my mother and from the grave into paradise, from death into life. I do not therefore seek there the pleasures and delights of the world. Rather I use spiritual gifts in such a way that, when bodily things cease, I might be led from this life to eternal life and immortality. Whoever plays the pastor, or absolves, or administers sacred things in order to gain glory and power thereby, is a simoniac according to that gross definition of the canonists — not among those of the subtler sort who seek eternal life through the sacraments.

But why does Jacob demand that the birthright be sold to him? And since he wishes to buy it, it follows that there is a transaction. I answer: Jacob perceived that occasion by which he might obtain the birthright, and he wished to make use of it.

Furthermore, brother Esau belongs to the number of those who are grossly simoniacal — that is, he sought nothing other than his belly in the spiritual blessing. For he did not attend the tabernacle but despised the worship of God. He first neglected small things, until he fell into contempt of greater goods, just as Cain, the firstborn \[did\].
