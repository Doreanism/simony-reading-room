We therefore teach — and this must be impressed upon people diligently and often — that in the examples of the holy fathers what is to be regarded as the chief and sum of their commendation is this: that God spoke with them, that they had the Word of God. That is the point which illuminates these histories and provides the true understanding of what and how great these things are — base dregs and contemptible in outward appearance, as they seem — of those most holy men. For where the Word of God is, there also is true faith and there are true works, because everything is done in the Word and under the Word. All other things that are done apart from the Word and without the Word by human discretion are in truth nothing other than dregs and dung before God.

Therefore all the lives of all monks, however splendid in the eyes of the flesh, are nevertheless utterly nothing. Great was the appearance of holiness in Antonius, in Hilarion, and in many others, some of whom spent their lives to the age of seventy in fasts, others in prodigious vigils — portents by which the flesh, the heart, and the eye of man are captivated. But look and see whether the Word of God is joined to these things. Ask Antonius whether he has a word by which he was commanded to go into the desert and mortify the flesh. He does not. “But it seemed good to me in this way, and pleasing to God,” he will say. Yet that is the very head of the matter, which ought to stand out in your works, Antonius — without which that entire life is death, and nothing but the choice of the flesh, self-will, and sheer display and the self-fashioning of carnal men.

Holy Scripture therefore commends in the histories of the Fathers above all faith in the Word. For the Word of God sanctifies all things. Indeed it is holy — rather, it is holiness, truth, and wisdom itself. And that life which is governed by the Word is a true life, righteous, wise, and eternal. But if it lacks the Word, it also lacks truth, light, and wisdom before God, and all its actions are works of darkness.

Thus marriage, although it is an unclean kind of life — since the union of male and female cannot take place without carnal impurity — and tending livestock is a sordid thing, and likewise the life of magistrates and of their subjects is most impure and full of vices, with many sins converging there, both private excesses and personal faults beyond the common ones: nevertheless God has magnificently adorned and ordered all these things with his Word. And if you cling to the Word, you are already cleansed from all your impurity.

In sum, no one lives without sins. But the power of the Word is so great that it absorbs all these things, so that you can say: I live the married life with wife and children in peace, in your fear and trust, and thus I know that all is well. So Paul speaks of the life of married people in 1 Timothy 2: “The woman will be saved through the bearing of children.” How? “If she remains in faith.” This is the first power of the Word.
