But all these people finally hear that sad verdict which stands in the Epistle to the Hebrews concerning Esau: “Know that afterward, when he desired to inherit the blessing, he was rejected; for he found no place of repentance, though he sought it with tears.”

So the Antinomians enjoy the comforts of the world, that they may be happy in this life, and say they intend to convert in their own good time; they despise their blessing — the Church, Baptism, the keys, the forgiveness of sins, eternal life; they receive the grace of God in vain, “neglecting the acceptable time and the day of salvation” (2 Corinthians 6). But in vain and too late will they one day seek the opportunity they neglected. As the bride laments in the Song of Songs: “I opened the bolt of my door to my beloved, but he had turned and gone.”

When Esau therefore says, “I must die; what use is the birthright to me?” he shows that he thinks only of his belly and rejects the promise as empty and useless for the life to come — precisely as the Epicureans say today: what is the point of hearing the Gospel, of using the keys, or of the Lord’s Supper? Jacob, however, thinks differently and knows that sacred things are preparations for the life to come; therefore he magnifies them and burns with zeal for them. Those who wish to use them only for this life are rightfully stripped of them by divine authority and right: for there freedom of will operates, so that men knowingly and willingly sell or cast them away.

Therefore, when Jacob sees the opportunity turning on the hinge and moment of time, he presses his petition and wants the contract confirmed: “Swear to me,” he says, “at this time” — that is, at this very hour. Because the opportunity must not be neglected. Then Esau swears, and so sells his birthright. In the same way we ought to deal with the Papists: if they would give an oath, hostages, and seals \[pledging\] that they would not make war against us, we would at once set the red pottage before them.

The remaining question, which others raise here, we shall also treat, lest it hinder the reader. Was that red dish the price of the sale and purchase? They themselves answer that it was not, because the birthright was a very great and most precious thing, and therefore it is not credible that Esau received this meager dish as the price. They therefore want the dish to have been only an earnest \[arrha\] confirming the contract, which Esau received as a token of the sold birthright — just as it is customary among us for a cup or two of wine to be set between buyer and seller as a sign of the sale, which the Germans call *gleichtauff*.

This opinion arose from the commentaries of the Rabbis, and we reject it. Just as all their opinions must be handled in this way: whatever good there is in them should be retained, but what is bad and false must be guarded against. For it conflicts with the plain text in the Epistle to the Hebrews, where it is openly said: “Esau sold his birthright for a single meal.”
