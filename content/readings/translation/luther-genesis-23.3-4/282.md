In this way he pretended to have the power to remit sins, seized the keys for himself, sold forgiveness of sins, and promised liberation from hell and purgatory. But he only took away the money, leaving the wretched souls under the power of sin, hell, and the devil.

This is simony in the proper sense — namely, to sell the gift of the Holy Spirit, which can only be received freely. It can indeed be put up for sale, but God cannot be purchased. The thing itself does not follow the simoniacal contract. Therefore, in terms of the actual reality, simony is impossible; but in terms of fraud, it is most commonplace — because the name of the LORD is prostituted and sold. But that very act is nothing other than an empty pretense. Therefore, in spiritual matters the Pope is a simoniac, selling forgiveness of sins and righteousness; in temporal matters he is a sacrilegious plunderer who would sell Christ himself if he had him in his hands, just as the Jews did — for the will is certainly not lacking.

A like simony occurred in the monasteries, on the part of both buyer and seller. For whoever paid a hundred gold coins had four anniversary [masses] celebrated for him. The entire papacy, therefore, is a Satanic church, full of plundering, sacrilege, and unspeakable abominations and blasphemies.

But from this there arises a question: whether it is lawful for a minister of the Word to ask or receive sustenance for his office. I answer: it is entirely lawful, just as a poor man may accept a gift. If I had that power — that no one could be absolved of sins without paying me a certain number of gold coins — I could in a single day scrape together all the wealth of the world. But this is not permitted, and the Pope with Satanic audacity established this scheme. We are commanded to teach, comfort, and absolve all who receive and believe, and all of them receive these things from us freely, according to the saying: “You have received freely; give freely” \[Matthew 10:8\].

But just as Christians freely enjoy the ministry of the Word, so in return they are obligated to freely feed and support the ministers on account of their office, providing food and clothing. Galatians 6: “Let the one who is taught the word share all good things with the one who teaches.” 1 Timothy 5: “Let the elders who rule well be counted worthy of double honor, especially those who labor in preaching and teaching,” and Christ himself says: “The laborer is worthy of his wages.” Likewise, the Lord says through Isaiah that princes and kings will give gifts to the Church. But these gifts are not prices in a transaction of buying and selling. We need daily sustenance, food and drink. But absolution is not paid for by these. For who could pay for it? What are a hundred or a thousand gold coins compared with that inestimable gift of the forgiveness of sins? When, therefore, we receive support from the Church, it is not a price equivalent to this gift — which is of such value that it cannot be paid for with all the wealth of the world. But because this vast and inestimable gift cannot be administered except through human beings who need food and clothing, it is necessary that they be fed and sustained. That, however, is not recompense for the gift, but for the labor and the work.
