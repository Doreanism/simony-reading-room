Among the canonists there is another question: if a minister of the church has saved two hundred gold coins from his labor, may he buy a farm or a property with that money? They say no, on the grounds that it would be ecclesiastical goods. But I affirm that this is permitted, because it is not a spiritual matter, even if the person administers spiritual things. The canonists are wrong; they do not know what simony is. They confuse spiritual and material goods in the most disgraceful manner, and by their own law and their own conscience they are notorious simoniacs themselves — they seize and devour the goods of pastors and churches, worse than Verres and Dionysius, and they distribute truly spiritual things like spoils for money. Yet these same men claim for themselves the governance of the whole world and judgment over our doctrine and the word of God, and command us to obey the Pope, though they neither understand nor observe their own statutes and decrees.

Lyra touches briefly on this dispute and simply wishes to slip away from it by noting that Abraham did not buy a sepulcher but rather a place in which a sepulcher is located. But this is sophistry even by their own terms: he did not buy a sepulcher, it is true, because there was none there yet — but he was permitted to make one wherever he wished.

This example also teaches that the dead are to be buried with singular honor and reverence, on account of faith in and hope of the coming resurrection. Before the age of the martyrs, people had tombs in individual fields, gardens, and homes; but afterward greater reverence prevailed, when distinct places and κοιμητήρια \[cemeteries, literally “sleeping-places”\] were designated for burial. Just as even among the pagans there were respectable funeral ceremonies, so much more should a certain reverence be added among Christians on account of the article of faith concerning the resurrection of the flesh, lest we seem to pass away and be buried like horses and mules.

The sections that follow are largely moral teachings, except for one particular passage — “Give me a possession or property” — for what our translator renders as “right of burial” is not in the Hebrew. Abraham is saying, as it were: I would not ask for so much as a footstep of ground in this land for my own life, but I seek only a place where I may dig a grave and lay my dead Sarah to rest.

The whole land had been promised to Abraham, and it belonged to him by every right as his own gift, and yet he does not occupy so much as a footstep of it. Rather, he lives as an exile, and he dies along with Sarah as a stranger in his own land.

The prophets and apostles perceived these things with spiritual eyes and looked somewhat more deeply than others did. For the fact that Abraham, who is the lord of this land by divine authority, is an exile in it with Sarah and his son Isaac, signifies in the spirit that we are guests on earth and live as though in exile.

So Paul speaks in 2 Corinthians 5: “As long as we are away from home in the body.” If we are exiles in the body, which is most properly our own, and our life in the body is nothing other than a pilgrimage — how much more are the things we possess on account of the body, the field, the house, the money, nothing other than places of exile and sojourning.
