For it is not a time for laughter, and that iron and stony spirit displeases God. Yet grief must nonetheless be moderated, lest you be overwhelmed by excessive sorrow.

The separation from beloved parents, a spouse, or children does indeed cause pain, because, as Sirach says, their light has gone out — that is, they no longer enjoy this light and our companionship. But against grief must be set what follows: “He has passed into his rest.” The cause of mourning is the extinction of light; the cause of comfort is rest. You will therefore think: May he rest in peace — for I know that he is well, not in sadness or calamity, but in quiet. There he sleeps and awaits a better life, just as we who still live also await it. Our consolation, then, is that a spouse, a son, or parents sleep; they are not held in torments and agonies, but rest sweetly in peace.

But when those terrible and tragic cases occur — when David loses his son Absalom, dying in mortal sin and damned — that is the sharpest grief and sorrow of all, and yet what else can you do but commit the matter to God? Where, however, the departure is peaceful and calm, the dying sleep in peace. And this is what Moses intends when he says, “Abraham arose”: he felt grief and sorrow at the death of his dearest wife, but he overcame that natural emotion and turned his thoughts to burial.

Yet this too is remarkable: that while Sarah was alive, Abraham was never concerned with acquiring possession and property for himself and his son, until now that Sarah had died. It seems a superfluous concern to worry about a place to lie \[in burial\], when you cannot possess it — and yet Moses records this with singular care and with more detail than usual: that Abraham purchased the plot not for the living, but for the dead.

Until now he had been a sojourner, and he had been unable to change this sufficiently harsh condition of life. After his wife’s death, he thinks of a proper burial site. Before this, certainly, during the roughly 70 years he spent in Canaan, many from his household undoubtedly died — male and female servants — but he was so concerned for none of them as for Sarah. Some he buried in Gerar, others in Egypt. Here he seeks a definite and proper burial possession.

The Hebrew word *ger* means “sojourner.” Psalm 39: “I am a sojourner with you, and a guest, as all my fathers were.” So Paul speaks in Ephesians 2: “You are no longer strangers and foreigners.” It denotes one who is from outside and has come from another place. So Abraham was a stranger, for he was not born in Canaan but had come from Chaldea.

*(Toschaf)* \[Hebrew term for “resident alien”\] is one who is not a lord but a guest in the land where he dwells, possessing nothing of his own. Christians are strangers and guests in this world, whose homeland and inheritance lie elsewhere. For according to the birth of the flesh they come into the world out of nothing.
