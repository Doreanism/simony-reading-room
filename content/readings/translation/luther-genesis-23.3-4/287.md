But where such harmony exists — where the princes of the land honor, protect, and support ministers for the sowing of spiritual things — there you may truly call it a paradise on earth. If, however, ministers are despised, mocked, and trampled underfoot, as happened to Isaiah and the other prophets (Isaiah 57: “against whom do you open your mouth and stick out your tongue”), even while they were teaching best and most faithfully — that is the surest sign of God’s wrath and impending calamity. For contempt of so great a benefit as is rendered to men through godly ministers has never gone unpunished. By contrast, impious and wicked priests generally fare better: princes are most obsequious and eager to serve their every need. Just so, the Pope had all the kings and princes of the world subject and lavishly devoted to him — not only in goods and treasure, but even in their very bodies. Yet for all these benefits he seduced them and won them for the Devil. Today the ministry has been happily purged of all error and idolatry — but how is it received and treated in the world? It is blasphemed, driven out, trampled down, and its ministers are killed. To us God has granted, by an immeasurable act of kindness, lodging under the most illustrious princes of Saxony, Duke Johann Friedrich, the prince-elector, and his brother Ernst. Yet as great as the clemency, favor, and goodwill of the princes is, so great is the savage hatred, malice, and contempt of the nobles, magistrates, townspeople, and peasants — who, if they could do what they wish, would long since have driven us from these seats. Remarkable, therefore, was the piety, humanity, and reverence of the Hebronites toward Abraham: they recognized that he was a guest who enriched all the inhabitants and citizens of that place — not with wealth, gold, and silver, but with heavenly and spiritual gifts — one who showed the way of salvation and through the word freed souls from sin and hell, who was, in short, full of every blessing. In return they embrace him, love him, and gladly listen to him, just as he found similar goodwill earlier from king Abimelech. A fine and splendid title it is — “prince of God” — by which the Hebronites address Abraham. Not that he governed them in civil matters, but because he had in his own household a great family of more than a thousand men; they therefore call him a prince of God who governs the best and most holy men in his house — as if to say: We have no people so faithful, no servants so holy, obedient, and humble as you have. Your principality is divine, for it makes men holy. And indeed it is a singular gift of God when princes govern their subjects wisely and well, and in turn subjects obey.
