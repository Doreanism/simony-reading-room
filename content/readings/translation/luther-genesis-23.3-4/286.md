Learn therefore from this example to govern your conduct in such a way that you grow accustomed to humanity, modesty, and reverence toward all. For Moses sets before our eyes so diligently and at such length the example of Abraham, who with the utmost modesty and humility asks that a portion of land be sold to him, pressing his suit with no impropriety or importunity. Although he may seem to do this out of necessity, being a foreigner, yet one so laden with virtues and gifts had grounds for self-exaltation. But forgetting all of these, he addresses the Hebronites respectfully and courteously.

They in turn, though he is a foreigner, greet him as lord, and display in their speech and bearing a singular humanity and kindness — themselves forgetting their own right and dominion over the land in relation to the stranger and newcomer.

These are truly good works — civil ones, to be sure, yet to be set against all the monstrous practices of the monks, who have as their sole object to accustom the young men they have received into their order to a certain inept and deformed humility, foreign to the manners of honorable and holy men. Let young people rather learn humility, courtesy, and reverence from this passage.

And yet hypocrites such as Wicelius the apostate, who charged that we teach things too civil, despise these things as commonplace; and if you ask them what else should be done, they say one must go to the temple, bellow in the choir, and mutter prayers. But this example and others like it plainly refute their hypocrisy, because the Holy Spirit does not disdain to commemorate and adorn these civil matters with such precision.

Now it is apparent that God adorned Abraham and his church with singular authority and magnificence among the Gentiles. So it was earlier with king Abimelech, who grants Abraham the use of the land and holds him in great honor — though Abraham possessed nothing of his own and was compelled to depart at the king’s death.

So too the Hebronites, a pagan people, honor him and call him a prince of God; and it is implied that they had heard the discourses of Abraham and believed in the God of Abraham. For wherever the most holy patriarch sojourned, he brought forth abundant fruit through word and spirit — teaching, exhorting, reproving — and the nations through his preaching heard and witnessed the mighty works of God, and therefore believed and were saved. It was right, therefore, that they honored him as a lord, just as Sara called him lord, and named him a prince of God.

It is moreover a great felicity when princes have such guests dwelling with them, acknowledge God and Christ in them, and treat them with reverence; and when the guest and priest in turn faithfully and devotedly performs his office of teaching. It is exceedingly rare that devout priests should have a safe and tranquil place in which to teach; it is a singular gift of God, which the Devil — the most bitter enemy of the Word and of human salvation — frequently hinders and disrupts.
