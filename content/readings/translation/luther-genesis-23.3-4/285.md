Christ surely wished his grandmother to be buried with honor, as her virtues deserved. For she governed the household in discipline and piety; she was an abbess far more excellent than ours are in external and domestic affairs — before whom the abbesses of our monasteries are not merely incomparable but must be reckoned utterly obscure. What are they, set beside Sara, mother of the most illustrious princes and kings, who administered her household throughout her life with such great distinction? And what are the works of abbesses other than horrible idolatry, whatever they do?

Now because this entire passage is moral in character, we must first note the example of civility and reverence in this people. For there shines in their speech, manners, and bearing a remarkable humanity, such as you would scarcely find in our age — neither among the old and elderly, nor among the young. For how do they address Abraham? “My lord, you are a prince of God.” This is a signal act of reverence, which these men show to Abraham — the very men who are lords of the land and possess it by divine right.

Next, consider the singular modesty in Abraham’s petition. For I believe that Ephron — of whom more will be said below — was the chief man in Hebron; yet Abraham does not approach him directly at first, but at the outset addresses those of lesser rank and insinuates himself very humbly.

The Holy Spirit did not wish to pass over these things, to signify that he requires and magnifies these virtues. For he sets forth the examples of great men so that we may learn that mutual reverence among men is necessary and pleasing to God — that we yield to one another, and, as Paul teaches, “outdo one another in showing honor” (Romans 12), and (Philippians 2) “in humility count others more significant than yourselves.”

Thus this remarkably detailed description is by no means idle. For we were not born as swine, mules, stumps, or blocks, but erect of face toward the stars; and God has implanted in man a rational soul that understands the difference between honorable and shameful things, so that it may recognize what is good in others and pay them due honor. Therefore an uncouth and brutish life does not befit Christians — such as appears in the savage and barbarous manners of our own people.

And above all, our soil has produced men entirely without culture, discipline, and civility, so that you would say the peasants, townspeople, and nobles of these regions resemble swine more than human beings, so utterly are they estranged from all humanity. I have therefore often marveled why God should have first opened the light of the Gospel to people so uncouth and savage.

The poets tell the fable that Ulysses once lost his companions and all were transformed into swine. The same metamorphosis seems to have befallen our people as well. Among the coarser sort, however, there is less danger. But if this evil spreads further to the Doctors of the Church, who on account of their singular gifts arrogate everything to themselves, and with a certain inept and rustic arrogance despise all others in comparison with themselves — others whom they ought to embrace and love with humanity — that is a great plague upon the churches.
