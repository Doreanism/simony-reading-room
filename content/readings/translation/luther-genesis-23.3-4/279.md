Abraham therefore says: I have come from a foreign land, and here I hold nothing as my own; I am a stranger in a double sense, and so I have no place to bury my dead. These words David carefully weighs in Psalm 39, and the matter deserves close consideration. For so great a man wandered and roamed so long with so large a household — roughly four hundred men, to say nothing of the women and children. If you count rightly, he had around a thousand persons in all; for where there are four hundred men, they bring with them an equal number of women and children.

The governance of this patriarch was therefore remarkable — something that, if you consider our own times, would today be altogether impossible. It is indeed a miracle of miracles that God nourished him in a foreign land with so great a household, and that Sarah administered all of this. Thus Abraham’s household arrangement \[*politia sive oeconomia*\] was no less remarkable than the Church itself; he was a stranger and sojourner with his entire family. Yet he surely sustained and upheld himself by the promise.

Now from this passage concerning the purchase of a burial place, a vast sea of questions and opinions arises among the doctors and the Master of the Sentences. For here in scholastic theology the material was taken for disputing about simony, which they handle so perplexedly and confusedly that it is unworthy of the light of day or of memory. Some two hundred years ago simony was a dreadful crime, but the popes have scarcely retained even the shadow of the word — as Julius boasts in Erasmus’s dialogue.

Simony is the buying or selling of something spiritual for money: when someone receives money and gives a gift or spiritual thing. So Simon Magus, when he saw that the Holy Spirit was given through the laying on of the apostles’ hands, offered them money, saying: “Give me also this power, so that anyone on whom I lay my hands may receive the Holy Spirit.” He desired to purchase the Holy Spirit with money and to have it under his own control, to do with it as he pleased. There Peter rebukes him sharply: “Your money perish with you, because you thought you could obtain the gift of God with money.”

From that Simon the crime is called simony — when someone, following Simon’s example, thinks that the gifts of God are to be bought or sold with money. For Christ does not sell his gifts and grace, but has redeemed us freely.

This is the true definition, which the canonists afterward subjected to remarkable torture. For that part of the definition — “the gift of God for money” — they apply to everything that people give to God. So now they call ecclesiastical revenues “spiritual goods,” because they are gifts of God — not gifts that God gave, but gifts that people have offered to God. Is this not immense blindness and folly?

They drag in a text from Moses for this purpose, from Leviticus 27: “Whatever has once been consecrated shall be most holy to the Lord.” They want gifts offered to the Church to be of this kind — benefices, sacred vessels, and other things originally established for the support of ministers
