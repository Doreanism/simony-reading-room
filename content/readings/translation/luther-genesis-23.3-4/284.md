It is therefore sufficiently clear that the patriarchs, in the promises, explained the kernel itself and looked within — they did not merely handle the outer shell. Thus Abraham understood that the promise given to him contained a true homeland, a truly promised life, and one better than this present life, which is servitude and captivity of the soul — namely, the life to come.

So also when David calls himself a pilgrim in Psalm 39, one might wonder why he says this, since he was the most powerful king of Israel and held the promised land. But he despises all this, and his mind was turned toward another and far better dwelling — unlike those who in this life, as though it were to last forever, devote themselves to plunder and usury with no concern. How empty and fleeting is the glory and pride in the goods and riches of this world, since no human being can be certain of a single moment of his own life! As each of us individually feels, sees, and understands — we carry about a body that is half a corpse and already dead — and yet a great many persuade themselves that this life is the best and will last forever, as is plain from that wicked zeal and insatiable desire to seize by fair means and foul. Why do they do this? Because, of course, they are in the devil’s exile, wandering and entangled in the cares and anxieties of this life.

The faith and patience of Abraham are therefore to be commended: he looks beyond this promised land for another and eternal possession. He wishes to bury Sarah in this land, so that she may be raised when Christ rises from the dead. Meanwhile he himself wanders as a stranger in his own land. God grants him not a footstep of ground, nor a grave; instead he buys a place from the inhabitants at a price. He therefore did not labor under that base desire for accumulating wealth, as though he were to remain in this life forever. But with his whole heart he was focused on the promised Christ, and he gazed with joy upon him as coming in the promise, and he awaited this land in that most excellent promise. Moral teachings follow, but useful ones.

23:5–6 And the sons of Heth answered Abraham, saying to him: Hear us, my lord; you are a prince of God among us. In the choicest of our tombs bury your dead; no man among us will withhold his tomb from you, so that you may bury your dead.

Moses is quite verbose in his description of this transaction. The Holy Spirit does this, first, in honor of that most noble matriarch Sarah, who is the mother of all the patriarchs, prophets, and most distinguished leaders and kings, and whose like the histories have none. Therefore no funeral in the sacred scriptures is described as magnificently as that of Sarah.
