# Chapter XVI.

53. *”Do not therefore be anxious,”* he says, *”saying, What shall we eat, or what shall we drink, or what shall we wear? For the Gentiles seek all these things. For your Father knows that you need all these things. Seek therefore first the kingdom of God and his righteousness, and all these things shall be added to you”* (Matthew 6:31–33). Here he shows most clearly that these things are not to be desired as goods that belong to us in such a way that we ought to do good for their sake — if we do good at all — yet that they are nonetheless necessary. For the difference between the good that is to be sought and the necessary that is to be taken up is declared by this very sentence, when he says, *”Seek first the kingdom of God and his righteousness, and all these things shall be added to you.”* The kingdom and righteousness of God, therefore, is our good; this is what is to be sought, and in this our end is to be placed, for the sake of which we do whatever we do. But because in this present life we are on campaign so as to be able to reach that kingdom — and this life cannot be lived without these necessary things — he says, *”These things shall be added to you; but seek first the kingdom of God and his righteousness.”* For when he said *”first,”* he indicated that the other thing is to be sought second — not in time, but in dignity: the kingdom as our good, these necessities as our necessity; and the necessity exists for the sake of that good.

54. For we ought not, for example, to preach the Gospel in order to eat, but rather to eat in order to preach the Gospel. For if we preach the Gospel in order to eat, we hold the Gospel cheaper than food, and our good will then consist in eating while our necessity lies in preaching. The Apostle also forbids this when he says that it is indeed permissible for him, and has been allowed by the Lord, that those who proclaim the Gospel should live by the Gospel — that is, should obtain from the Gospel what is necessary for this life — yet that he himself had not made use of this right. For there were many who were eager to find an occasion to acquire and sell the Gospel, and wishing to cut off that occasion from them, the Apostle supported himself by his own hands (Acts 20:34). Elsewhere he says of these people, *”In order that I may cut off the occasion from those who seek an occasion”* (2 Corinthians 11:12). And yet even if, like the other good apostles, he had lived by the Gospel with the Lord’s permission, he would not thereby have placed the goal of preaching in that sustenance, but would rather have placed the goal of his own sustenance in the Gospel itself — that is, as I said above, he would not preach the Gospel in order to arrive at food and whatever other necessities there are, but would take these things in order to fulfill that task, lest he preach not willingly but out of compulsion.
