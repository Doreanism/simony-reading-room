“Do you not know that those who work in the temple eat what belongs to the temple, and those who serve at the altar share in what is offered at the altar? In the same way, the Lord ordained that those who proclaim the Gospel should live by the Gospel. But I have made no use of any of these rights” (1 Corinthians 9:13–15). Here he shows that this \[living from the Gospel\] is a permission, not a command — for otherwise he would be held to have acted against the Lord’s precept. He then continues: “I have not written these things so that they may be done in my case. It would be better for me to die than to have anyone make my glory empty” (1 Corinthians 9:15). He said this because he had already resolved, on account of certain people who were looking for an opportunity, to earn his living by his own hands. For if I preach the Gospel, he says, that is no glory to me — that is, if I preach the Gospel so that these things may be done in my case, if I preach the Gospel for the sake of reaching those things and place the end of the Gospel in food, drink, and clothing. But why is it no glory to him? Because, he says, necessity is laid upon me — that is, to preach the Gospel because I have nothing to live on, or to acquire a temporal reward from the proclamation of eternal things; for in that case necessity will govern my preaching, not free will. Woe to me, he says, if I do not preach the Gospel. But how ought he to preach? Precisely so as to place his reward in the Gospel itself and in the Kingdom of God — for in that way he can preach not under compulsion but willingly. For if I do this willingly, he says, I have a reward; but if against my will, a stewardship has been entrusted to me (1 Corinthians 9:16–17). That is: if I preach the Gospel driven by want of those things that are necessary for temporal life, others through my preaching will receive the reward of the Gospel — those who will love the Gospel itself as I proclaim it — but I will have no reward, because I do not love the Gospel itself but have set its price in those temporal things. And it is a shameful thing for anyone to minister the Gospel not as a son but as a slave to whom a stewardship has been entrusted, dispensing what belongs to another and receiving from it nothing for himself except rations — which are given from outside, not as a share in the Kingdom, but as a support for wretched servitude. Though in another place he also calls himself a steward. For a slave who has been adopted into the number of sons may faithfully dispense to his fellow heirs the very thing in which he has merited the lot of a co-heir. But here, where he says, *but if against my will, a stewardship has been entrusted to me*, he wished to be understood as the kind of steward who dispenses what belongs to another and receives nothing of it for himself.

55. Therefore, whatever thing is sought for the sake of something else is without doubt inferior to that for the sake of which it is sought; and so that thing for whose sake you seek this other thing comes first, while this thing that you seek for its sake comes second. Wherefore, if we seek the Gospel and the Kingdom of God for the sake of food, we put food first and the Kingdom of God second — so that if food is not lacking, we do not seek the Kingdom of God at all. That is to seek food first and the Kingdom of God afterward — to put the one in the first place and the other in the second. But if we seek food in order to have the Kingdom of God, then we do what was commanded: *Seek first the Kingdom of God and his righteousness, and all these things will be added to you* (Matthew 6:33).
