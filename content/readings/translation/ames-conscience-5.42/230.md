6. *A.* 3. Yet this must be understood as follows: First, that the fear is induced for that end alone — namely, to force consent. For if it is brought on for another purpose, and the one who fears makes a contract in order to escape that evil, the contract will be valid. For example, if someone captured by a thief promises a sum of money to be released, the fear was not the cause but only the occasion of the contract.

Secondly, the fear must be unjustly inflicted. For if someone, out of fear of punishment established by law, bargains with the party he has wronged, such a bargain cannot be annulled.

7. *A.* 7. From the second condition it follows that a promise extorted by guile, or made under error, or so imperfect that it contains no intention of obligating oneself, does not properly constitute a contract. Nevertheless, if someone is deceived by another’s words to his own loss, and takes those words as a promise, he is bound to perform what he promised in that form of speaking, lest he become the cause of damage that would follow.

8. *A.* 8. From the third condition it follows that an internal promise made to another person does not bind; the reason is that internal acts are not fit instruments for conveying our meanings and consents to others.

## Question 2. *What is the law concerning the subject matter about which contracts are concerned?*

9. *A.* 1. Lawful contracts are properly exercised only in lawful matters. The reasons are: first, because in every contract consent is given, and consent to an unlawful thing is sin; second, because a contract has in itself the force of a promise, and it is not lawful to promise what it is not lawful to perform; third, because from a contract an obligation arises, and no obligation can be lawful that obliges to sin, since under that very title it is repugnant to the obligation of the divine law.
