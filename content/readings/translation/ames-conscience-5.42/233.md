## Question 3. What law governs the form of Contracts?

20. A. 1. The external form is not fixed and determined by any law, whether natural, divine, or civil. For it may consist in any declaration of mutual consent whatsoever, whether expressed by words, gestures, writing, or the delivery of the thing itself. Therefore, although such form has the power of obliging, yet because it carries no force in itself but only in relation to the internal court, it belongs less to the internal court of conscience.

21. A. 2. The internal form of a lawful Contract is upright dealing, by which one sincerely intends to bind himself to the performance of what he professes, and afterwards to perform it as much as lies within his power. The reason is that a Contract includes a promissory consent. Now a promise is a testimony by which one binds his faith to deal uprightly with another in the performance of this or that; and therefore the form essentially and internally requires that the upright dealing of the contracting party be true and sincere.

22. A. 3. Hence that division of Contracts — by which some are said to be according to upright meaning and others according to the strictness of the law — is not accurate, and has no place either in the court of conscience or before God.
