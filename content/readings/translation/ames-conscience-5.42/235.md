For such a contract is rendered void or voidable, because the party who was drawn into the bargain by fraud suffers injury in the transaction, and must therefore be fully redressed by the annulment of the contract.

28. *A. 9.* When this fraud bears only on the contract and concerns its accidents — as when a person is deceived regarding the quality of a thing and is induced by deceit not merely to buy, but to buy at a price above its value — it does not render the contract wholly void or altogether voidable; nevertheless, the deceiver is bound in conscience to compensate the other party’s loss according to equity.

29. *A. 10.* Of this kind is the deceit in which a seller conceals from the buyer some hidden defect in the thing sold, which brings damage or loss to the buyer.

30. *A. 11.* To this also belongs the case in which a seller, knowing that within a short time there will be a great abundance of such goods — by which occasion the price will fall, bringing great loss to whoever buys beforehand at the current price — neither warns the buyer of this nor reduces the price; and likewise the case in which a buyer knows that within a short time there will be many buyers and a scarcity of goods, by which the price of things will rise and the seller be brought into poverty by selling at the ordinary rate. For in such cases we are bound, at least by the law of charity, to look after our neighbor’s good, so long as this can be done without great loss to ourselves.

31. *A. 12.* Those civil laws which uphold a contract made by circumvention, provided it does not fall below half of the just price, are merely permissive in order to reduce disputes, and do not justify the contract, but leave it to be judged in the court of conscience by the law of nature and the divine law.
