23. A. 4. Sometimes failing to stand by promises is not repugnant to honest dealing — namely, when the promise ceases to bind:

First, if the thing promised becomes unprofitable, unlawful, or impossible.

Secondly, if the state of things and persons has so changed that in the judgment of wise men the promisor is held to have not intended to encompass such an outcome.

Thirdly, if the other party, in whose favor the thing was promised, should release it.

Fourthly, if the one who promised on the other side will not fulfill his promise.

Fifthly, if something was promised for a cause that is afterward found not to exist.

24. A. 5. Repugnant to honest dealing is dissimulation — that is, craftiness, subtlety, fraud, cheating, plots, or contrivances whether in words or deeds, applied to deceiving or circumventing others. 1 Thessalonians 4:6; 1 Corinthians 6:8. It is commonly called evil craft, to distinguish it from an honest device by which one is willingly beguiled into right reason.

25. A. 6. It is repugnant to honest dealing insofar as it removes the clear intention of performing what was professed and seemed to be promised.

26. A. 7. This guile, if it concerns the substance of a thing — as if one should sell glass for a pearl — renders the Contract void by its own nature, because in such a Contract consent is wanting. For one who intended to buy a pearl does not intend glass.
