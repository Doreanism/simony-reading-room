# Chapter XLII. On Contracts.

## Question I. What is the law concerning the efficient causes of contracts?

1. *A.* 1. A lawful contract requires, first, persons fit to contract — that is, those who have administration of their own goods or the power to bind themselves in such matters (Acts 5:4).

2. *A.* 2. Hence infants, the insane, and prodigals are not fit to make a contract; and under that principle, their contracts are rightly counted nullities by the civil law.

3. *A.* 3. Those who are minors but have come to the full use of reason — although they still have guardians — if upon mature deliberation they do bargain, that contract considered in itself, according to the court of conscience, appears to be binding. Accordingly, lawyers teach that minors who contract without the authority of their guardians are not bound by the civil law, yet they are bound by the natural law.

4. *A.* 4. In the second place, either formal or virtual consent of the contracting parties is required, and that with three conditions: first, that it be free — at least insofar as one is bound freely to will and agree, even if one will not consent to an unlawful act; second, promissory — that is, with a mind to be bound by the terms of the contract being made; third, expressed by some outward sign.

5. *A.* 5. From the first condition it follows that consent wrested by extreme fear is not sufficient to make a firm contract.
