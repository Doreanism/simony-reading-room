15. *A. 7.* Such simoniacal bargains consist not only in money, but in all things that can be valued and assessed in terms of money, or that bear a similar nature to money with respect to spiritual things. Hence a simoniacal gift is not unreasonably divided into a gift from the hand, a gift from the tongue, and a gift from observance. A gift from the hand is money, or something of equivalent value. By the gift from the tongue are understood prayers, praises, entreaties, and flatteries, insofar as they have the power of obtaining spiritual things. By the gift from observance is understood some temporal deed that induces the bestowal of spiritual things — as the payment of a debt — whether the bargain be expressed or tacitly understood.

16. *A. 8.* To intend only such giving or receiving, even if it consists on one party’s side alone, is nevertheless a degree of this sin, and is therefore not improperly called mental simony — distinguished by some as confidential simony, in contrast to conventional and real simony.

17. *A. 9.* That which is given for the sustenance of those who are helpers and ministers of spiritual things contains no simony whatsoever. The same principle applies here as is said of the payment of physicians and schoolmasters: *We do not pay them the price of the thing itself, but their labor — because they serve, and being called away from their own affairs, they attend to ours. They do not receive the reward of their merit, but of their employment.*

18. *A. 10.* Nor is it properly simony when something is given out of pure gratitude, without any agreement — tacit or expressed, direct or indirect. Nor when something is given by one who has a certain and undoubted right to the spiritual function, for the purpose of avoiding unjust troubles. In these cases, however, we must beware of deceit, and the very appearance of evil is to be avoided.
