It is opposed to honest dealing, not because it simply excludes sincere intent, but because it shows that diligent and due care in performance was lacking. The Apostle rightly calls it *lightness*, 2 Corinthians 1:17.

33. *A.* 14. This fault is divided into gross, slight, and slightest. The gross fault is the omission of that diligence which people in like circumstances ordinarily apply — as when one leaves a borrowed book in an open place from which it may be taken away. A slight fault is the omission of that diligence which careful people in like circumstances commonly exercise — as when one leaves an item of great value entrusted to one’s care in an unlocked room from which it may be taken away. The slightest fault is the omission of that diligence which the most careful people in like circumstances are accustomed to apply — as when one leaves an entrusted item and supposes the door to be locked, yet does not know this with certainty.

34. *A.* 15. For the gross fault, all contracting parties are held liable for the damage that ensues therefrom, because ordinary diligence is contained in the obligation of every promise, and its omission is manifestly opposed to honest dealing. Hence it is that this gross fault is customarily ranked in nearly the same place and order as outright fraud.

35. *A.* 16. For the slight or slightest fault, they are bound only so far as either the nature of the contract, the agreement of the parties, or human laws established in equity require.

## Quest. 4. *What law is there concerning the end of contracts?*

36. *A.* 1. The end of a contract ought always to be some good, for neither obligation, promise, nor consent ought to tend toward evil.

37. *A.* 2. Contracts ought chiefly to serve the good of the contracting parties, for they are employed regarding the parties’ own goods and not those of others. No one can bargain concerning goods that are not within his own power.
