## Fourth Supposition

It is further supposed, as being sufficiently and expressly determined by divine Law through Christ and through the Apostle, that to an ecclesiastical Office—taken in the threefold manner just described, for right, for obligation, and for the work itself—there corresponds a Benefice taken proportionately in a threefold manner, namely for right, for obligation, and for the work itself. Hence the Benefice, taken for the right itself, may be described as the right of receiving sustenance for temporal life from the one granting it, by reason of one’s spiritual office. The obligation of providing the Benefice arises from the obligation to officiate. Hence those who minister spiritual things purely of their own accord are not always under obligation to those to whom this ministration is made; rather, the latter have Curates or Prelates obligated to minister this very thing to them, for otherwise they would be pressed by a twofold crushing or burden. Furthermore, the act of the Benefice corresponds to the act or exercise of the Office, and this indeed by divine Law, and inseparably, as the Apostle deduces from a manifold root drawn now from the Law of Scripture, now from the Law of nature. Hence he speaks thus in the passage already cited: “Who ever serves as a soldier at his own expense? Who plants a vineyard and does not eat of its fruit? Who tends a flock and does not drink the milk of the flock? Do I say these things according to man, or does not the Law also say the same? For it is written in the Law of Moses (Deuteronomy 25:4), ‘You shall not muzzle the ox that treads out the grain.’ Is it for the oxen that God is concerned? Or does he say it for our sake? For our sake it was written, because he who plows ought to plow in hope, and he who threshes, in the hope of receiving fruit. If we have sown spiritual things among you, is it a great matter if we reap your carnal things? If others partake of this power over you, why not rather we? But we have not used this power; we bear all things, lest we give any hindrance to the Gospel of Christ. Do you not know that those who work in the sanctuary eat of the things of the sanctuary, and those who serve at the altar share with the altar? So the Lord has ordained that those who preach the Gospel should live by the Gospel.” Thus the Apostle, 1 Corinthians 9:7, 8, 9, 10, 11, 12, 13, 14—indeed, the Lord through the Apostle.

## Fifth Supposition

It is further supposed that, in those matters which are simoniacal by divine Law—and not only because forbidden by human Law (to speak in the manner of the Jurists)—the Pope can commit simony; for he is subject to the divine Law like any other, and when he transgresses it he sins the more gravely, the greater he is held to be. Now it is established that it is forbidden by divine Law for that which is truly spiritual to be sought out for buying or selling, as Sacred Scripture openly hands down concerning Gehazi and Simon Magus. And as Christ, speaking to the Disciples, determines: “Freely you have received, freely give” (Matthew 10:8). Where in the same chapter he had determined what has been said concerning the right of the Benefice with respect to the Office, when he says: “The laborer is worthy of his food” (verse 10). And in Luke 10:7 it is said: “of his wages.”

## Sixth Supposition

It is further supposed that, although to the Papal Office—by reason of right, of obligation, and of spiritual work—there corresponds a Benefice taken proportionately in a threefold way; nevertheless the Pope himself cannot demand or extort that Benefice in whatever ways he pleases, but must justly carry out, according to the divine Law, what is just, and must abstain “from every evil appearance” (as the Apostle commands in 1 Thessalonians 5:22).
