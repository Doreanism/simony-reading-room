### Seventh Objection.

Further: how would provision be made for those thus promoted by the Pope, or by any other promoter who commits simony?

## RESPONSES TO THE FOREGOING OBJECTIONS.

In reply to such objections we say first that they imply and include certain truths: namely, that a sufficient and fitting benefice is owed to the office of the Pope; and that support is owed to him by his inferiors; and that such support can, or could, with the due circumstances observed, be taken from the fruits of lesser benefices, where no other sufficient provision has been made for the Pope — as the first argument sufficiently concludes, both by divine law and by natural law, the divine being presupposed. But that there should be exaction or extortion, as the conclusion speaks of, the argument does not establish.

### To the Second Argument.

Next, where the opposite seems to be concluded by the second argument concerning canons, the reply is that there is a great difference: for such monies are not exacted from canons for a benefice or an office, but for the supporting of common burdens, in which that canon will share as to the common emolument — such as for the fabric, for bread, for the choir-boys, for retainers, for the cope, and the like. We do not deny, however, that among some persons the manner of exacting is not altogether pure and becoming, though not properly simoniacal.

### To the Third Argument.

Next, the third argument concludes in conformity with the first. Therefore a like conformity ought to hold as to not exacting the fruits before they have been received, just as they are not exacted from those of whom the argument speaks.

### To the Fourth Argument.

Next, concerning the fourth argument we can speak as we did of the second, and likewise of those that follow, to this effect: that very many things (alas!) are done, and have been done, bearing the evil appearance of shameful gain, and in many cases simoniacal depravity, just as we see other vices also reigning everywhere among men, while virtues rarely do. Therefore the old Law says: “Thou shalt not follow a multitude to do evil” (Exod. 23:2); for this is a most wicked form of argument: “The multitude does this, therefore it is lawful”; since the opposite of the consequent frequently, and almost always, stands together with the antecedent.

### To the Fifth Argument.

Next, reply is made by this also to the fifth. Adding that among the good, the conscientious, and the learned, whom malice — whether of pride, or of flattery, or of avarice — has not blinded, this simoniacal plague has been reproved by preachings and teachings, by words and by writings; and most recently, by this sacred Council, namely the Council of Constance, it was condemned with judicial severity in the Head.
