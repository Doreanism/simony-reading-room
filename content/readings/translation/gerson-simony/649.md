## To the Sixth Argument.

Finally, from what has been said above, some answer to the sixth \[objection\] becomes apparent: adding that some, though not all, could have held a true title to their benefices and a true Order, as is evident; and this on account of certain excuses probable in the eyes of some, both prelates and subjects. For ignorance of law and of fact could excuse the subjects, in which cases the supreme Pope Christ supplied, and still supplies, the defect of the prelates, so long as the good faith of the subjects stands. Again, those promoted through this simoniacal exaction could perhaps be excused on the ground of redeeming their own harassment; just as the theologians likewise say in a parallel case, that where a priest would refuse to baptize a child at the point of death unless money were first received, and no other person capable or knowledgeable in baptizing appears, because of a lack of water or of instruction, the parents of the child may give the money — not, indeed, with the intention of buying Baptism, but of redeeming their own harassment and providing for the child. Nor are they on this account to be said to consent to the sin of simony of that curate; just as in the matter of usury one may lawfully borrow at usury in certain cases, even though it is in no way lawful for the other party to lend. Hence redemption of harassment seems to have its place — not indeed in acquiring a new right to an office or benefice of this spiritual sort, particularly with respect to the conferrer; but it is found to have its place when one seeks to remove an impediment that is placed \[in the way\], so that one may not peaceably possess or use his own right or his own property. Thus perhaps the subjects would be excusable with respect to curates or prelates, when they are compelled to hand over money before spiritual things are duly conferred upon them; as in the earlier example concerning the Baptism of infants, concerning burials, concerning the ministration of the Body of Christ, concerning sacramental Absolution, concerning the conferral of Orders, of Matrimony, and the like. Finally, by way of answer to the seventh argument, a sixfold Consideration will be set down, sufficiently in conformity with what has gone before.

## TO THE SEVENTH ARGUMENT.

### First Consideration.

The stain of simony, just as of itself it is not heresy, likewise does not necessarily take away from the Pope his power or the plenitude of ecclesiastical power, nor indeed the exercise of it. The first part is therefore manifest, because it stands that someone may be a simoniac from a mere depravation of affection through avarice, while the right judgment of the intellect remains — namely, that to act thus is a sin. But there is no heresy without a depravation of the intellect through error concerning those things which the right Faith delivers. Nevertheless, by presumption of law or of the deed, every simoniac may be held suspect in the Faith. The other part is clear, since the Pope is not subject to his own positive laws, insofar as they are such. But that the simoniac be suspended from all exercise as one excommunicated ipso facto is a positive \[matter\] which does not bind the Pope, since an equal has no authority over an equal: since, moreover, he is in this way loosed from the laws — understand, purely positive penal laws, as above — taking law in this sense. Thus (as I judge) the distinction of the lords Jurists is to be understood, when they say that something is simoniacal because it is prohibited, and that in such a matter the Pope cannot commit simony — plainly there is properly no simony there; except insofar as it is prohibited by divine law, that is to say, where there takes place that contract of spiritual things for something commensurable with money: “I give if you give,” or “I give that you may give.” Nevertheless, in some contracts which are not of themselves simoniacal, the penalty of simony is attached, where it is true that the thing is called simoniacal for this reason, that it is prohibited by human law — just as someone may be punished as a heretic by presumption of positive law or of the deed, who is truly a Catholic.
