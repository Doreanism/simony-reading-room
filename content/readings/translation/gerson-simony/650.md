### Second Consideration.

The stain of simony in a Pope conferring a prelacy or a benefice does not necessarily take away the right to the benefice, nor does it suspend the exercise of ecclesiastical power. This consideration follows from the preceding, since the Pope can will that one so promoted does not incur the sentence of positive law, even where he would commit true simony by consenting to his own simoniacal promotion — although in God’s sight he would not escape every penalty. The consideration is also clear from another angle, in line with what has been said concerning the sixth objection about the redemption of harassment; or likewise, where there is good and simple faith in the one promoted, this would save him.

### Third Consideration.

The stain of simony is more perilous — even if not with respect to God, yet with respect to the ecclesiastical forum — in those promoted by lower prelates than by the Pope. This is plain from the preceding consideration: attending to the fact that every simoniac is ipso jure excommunicated, with the other penalties, from which he is not freed by the Ordinary, as has been touched upon concerning the Pope.

### Fourth Consideration.

Although the stain of simony may be excused, or may not be found in the receiving of monies, or of those things which are measurable in money (such as services or favors and binding promises concerning the administration of spiritual things, in the conferring of letters testimonial and the like) — nevertheless it is expedient in all these matters that the apostolic rule be observed: “As far as can be done, abstain from every appearance of evil.” 1 Thessalonians 5:22. On the first part a deduction could be made through many \[examples\] concerning the various Orders and estates, concerning religious convents and secular churches, concerning prelates and their officers, concerning curates and their vicars or subjects. Many things, then, are done by such persons in this way, which can have a right intention, although the manner of doing them gives scandal — as when a bargain seems to be made concerning temporal things, or a denial of spiritual things, or a delay of them on account of temporal things; or conversely, the conferral, exercise, or frequenting of spiritual things seems to be done for the sake of temporal things, as in Masses to be said, as in the profitable Hours being frequented while others are not. Let this manifold appearance of evil therefore be guarded against by the appearance of good set against it, so far as it shall be granted to do so — for it is not within our power to meet all possible scandals.

### Fifth Consideration.

The stain of simony can more conveniently be avoided by the rectification of intention, namely, by desiring or accepting temporal things not as the price of spiritual things or as nourishment of cupidity, but as the necessary support of bodily sustenance. Thus it will be preserved that there is no simoniacal exchange there, even where this concurs: “I give if you give” or “I give that you may give”; since these conditional and causal expressions — “if,” “that,” “on account of,” and the like — will not denote the principal cause, but a consequent and less principal one, or a motive, auxiliary, or meritorious \[cause\], as in that passage: “I have inclined my heart to perform thy statutes for ever, for the reward.” Psalm 119:112. Which reward was indeed referred by the Prophet to God, as an end under \[a higher\] end. For where one thing is for the sake of another (as the Philosopher says), both are one.
