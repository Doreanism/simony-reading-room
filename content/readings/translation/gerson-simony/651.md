## Sixth Consideration

The stain of simony can, as regards the past, admit of provision and remedy through this sacred Council, for the calming of consciences and the salvation of souls, in such a way that by a general edict any penalties or sentences of positive law enacted against simoniacs be abolished, together with a restoration of eligibility for benefices already obtained, granting in addition a license for the past whereby anyone may choose a suitable confessor who shall absolve him, in the forum of conscience, from all doubts or scruples. This provision may perhaps seem too broad or lax when measured against the severity of ecclesiastical discipline; but if regard is had to the immensity of God’s mercy and to human frailty — which would often prefer to renounce its own salvation rather than its benefice or status — this provision will appear tolerable. Or if of necessity some who are too notoriously scandalous must be punished, let thought nonetheless be given to the welfare of the community and of the many.

## Seventh Consideration

The stain of simony will be able to have a safeguard for the future. Let care be taken by this sacred Council, in renewing, altering, or interpreting the due laws or those already instituted concerning the collation of all spiritual things — whether by the head or by the members — that it be done with such moderation that there be neither a stricter prohibition of temporal things than is fitting, in the head or in the members (as many judge has been made in certain matters), nor again such a relaxation permitted as would introduce either true simony or a scandalous appearance of simony. But to set these matters forth in particular belongs to those who have been specifically commissioned for this purpose. As for the further question, by what authority a general Council may prescribe laws of this sort — especially to the Supreme Pontiff, without injury to the plenitude of his power — the practical working out of this is useful, and, if God grant it, will be examined elsewhere and at another time, harmonizing the plenitude of papal power with the very plenitude of power of the general Council.
