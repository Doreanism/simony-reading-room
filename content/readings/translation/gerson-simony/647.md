### Seventh Supposition.

It is further supposed that this is unjust, and such as no one would wish done to himself: namely, that in regard to a benefice rightfully owed to Peter himself, he should pay money before he enjoys the benefice, lest perchance he become a usurer. Now this is just the case in the conclusion, whose truth seems sufficiently established from these suppositions taken together. We omit the application of this deduction, which, virtually and by way of resolution, comes down to this: that the right of receiving temporal goods, however temporal they may appear, is purely spiritual by reason of its end and its object; and therefore an ecclesiastical benefice can no more be sold than the office to which it is necessarily connected; and that such an office is not saleable, no one is unaware.

## OBJECTIONS.

### First Objection.

But it is objected on several grounds. First: just as under the old Law the tithes of tithes were owed to the High Priest by the Levites (Num. 18; Deut. 16), so no less under the new Law is this tithing, or sufficient recompense, owed to the Pope by those beneath him. Now this recompense seems reasonable, namely that the fruits of the first year be paid by every newly beneficed person; since in this the Pope serves the Church, and therefore, on account of this office of promoting men, there is owed to him \[a payment\] for the fitting sustenance of his state; and for this both persons and benefices can be charged.

### Second Objection.

Further: the Pope can no less receive or request money from a beneficed person, even before he is in possession, than can the canons of a cathedral church, who nevertheless by custom receive both money paid down and a bond of obligation before possession is taken.

### Third Objection.

Further: no less reasonably, indeed more reasonably, can the Pope receive the first annates of benefices than is received by lesser prelates, or by cathedral churches, or by certain religious orders — and yet this is found to be done quite frequently.

### Fourth Objection.

Further: many benefices are handed over at farm, or for an annual pension, or on title, and sometimes part of the pension, or the whole pension, is received before anything has been taken from the revenues.

### Fifth Objection.

Further: because it is not probable that the Church would have tolerated for so long a time, among those promoting and those promoted, such an exaction of money, if it had been simoniacal by divine law; for not only those who commit evil deeds, but also those who consent to those who commit them (according to the Apostle, Rom. 1:32) are worthy of death.
