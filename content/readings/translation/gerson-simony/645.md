# Treatise on Simony

Concerning the matter of the simoniacal stain in the conferral of benefices by the Pope: resolving the matter down to the root of Sacred Scripture—since, if that is set aside, to run through the branches of particular opinions has no end—a single Conclusion is here set down: It is a simoniacal stain contrary to the divine Law, or at least suspect of the simoniacal species, when the one conferring an ecclesiastical benefice (even if he be the Pope) demands or extorts from the one to be beneficed ready money under the title of first-fruits, and hinders his taking possession and exercising his office until he has paid that money. We shall deduce this conclusion first from seven suppositions founded in divine Law; then we shall reply to seven objections; thereafter we shall add seven considerations or teachings.

## First Supposition

It is supposed, first, for the sake of proof, that in a benefice—especially one having the cure of souls—there are these two things, namely Office and Benefice. The Benefice, moreover (as is commonly said, and rightly), is given on account of the Office. Hence to the right and obligation of officiating there corresponds the right of receiving the Benefice, as will be shown below from every branch of law. For this reason no one ought to be bound to officiate by the conferral of a sacred Order, or otherwise, unless the Benefice—that is, the right of receiving sustenance for life—is annexed to it at the same time.

## Second Supposition

It is further supposed that an ecclesiastical Office is sometimes taken for the right to officiate, sometimes for the obligation to officiate, and sometimes for the very act of officiating. Now the act of officiating in this sense may be described as some exercise expended for the benefit of another or of others, for the attainment of eternal happiness and the avoidance of damnation. Hence by this last clause the ecclesiastical Office differs from a temporal or civil office, namely by reason of its proximate or principally intended end; for the proximate end of a civil office is the happiness or political tranquility of the present life, whereas an ecclesiastical Office has a higher end, namely the eternal salvation of souls.
