Gratian. Indeed, those who are chosen to preside over others must excel in holiness just as they are set apart in dignity. Otherwise, why are they preferred to others when they are raised above others by no grace of merit? Hence Symmachus to Laurentius, bishop of Milan:

### C. XLV. As in honor, so too must one excel in knowledge.

He who is more eminent in honor must be considered most base unless he surpasses \[others\] in knowledge and holiness.

VII. Part. Gratian. It is therefore clear that the statement of Augustinus — that the sacraments of Christ administered through heretics do not lack their effect — is not to be understood generally of all sacraments, but of the sacrament of baptism. Hence the same Augustinus says:

### C. XLVI. Baptism, whether administered by a good or by a wicked minister, should not be repeated.

Iudas gave baptism, and there was no rebaptism after Iudas; Iohannes gave it, and there was rebaptism after Iohannes — because the baptism given by Iudas is the baptism of Christ, whereas the baptism given by Iohannes was Iohannes’s. We rightly place not Iudas above Iohannes, but the baptism of Christ — even given through the hands of Iudas — above the baptism of Iohannes.

Item: §. 1. Those whom Iudas baptized, Christ also baptized; thus, those whom a drunkard, a murderer, or an adulterer baptizes, Christ baptizes.

§. 2. Item. The sacrament of the Church is so great that it is not polluted even when administered by a murderer.

### C. XLVII. On the same.

Item, against the Donatists.

Just as I am pressed when it is said to me: “then does the heretic remit sin?” so too do I press when I say: “then does the miser, the dissembler of religion, remit sins when he baptizes justly?” If through the power of the sacrament, then both alike; if through their own merit, then neither. For that sacrament is recognized to belong to Christ even in evil men; but in the body of the one spotless dove, neither of them is found.
