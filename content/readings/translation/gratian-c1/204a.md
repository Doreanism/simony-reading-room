## QUESTIO II.

GRATIANUS.

I. Part. There follows the second question, in which we ask whether money may be demanded for entry into a monastery, or whether money that has been demanded must be paid. That both of these things may be done lawfully is demonstrated by the sequence of both Testaments. For it is read in the first book of Kings that Anna brought Samuel with her, after he had been weaned, with three calves, and three measures of flour, and a flask of wine, to the house of God in Shilo. §. 1. In the Acts of the Apostles, moreover, we read that “the multitude of believers was of one heart and one soul,” and no one said that any of the things he possessed was his own; but all things were common to them. Each sold his estates and laid the proceeds at the feet of the apostles, of whom one, named Ananias, while keeping a part for himself along with his wife Sapphira, having received the sentence of a curse, fell dead at the apostle’s feet.

§. 2. From this it is clearly apparent that those who are about to enter a monastery ought to offer their possessions to the superiors, and are not to be received otherwise than if they have made such an offering. But there is a difference between offering spontaneously and paying what has been demanded. Anna, the mother of Samuel, offered gifts to the priests not because they were demanded or requested, but of her own accord. The believers offered to the apostles of their own will, so that the needs of the poor might be served — possessions which, under the pressing persecution of the unbelievers, they were not permitted to keep. And Ananias was not condemned because he was unwilling to offer his own goods, but because, having lied to the Holy Spirit, he was defrauding in part the proceeds of what he had offered to the apostles. Therefore these authorities do not permit superiors to demand anything from those about to enter, but to receive what is voluntarily offered, because the former is reprehensible, the latter not at all.
