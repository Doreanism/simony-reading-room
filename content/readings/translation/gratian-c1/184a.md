By the example of Saul and the wife of Jeroboam, it is evident that buying or selling a gift of the Holy Spirit is not a sin. By the example of Christ it is clear that the Holy Spirit, even if unjustly treated as something for sale by simoniacs, does not forget its own power, nor does it cease to produce the effect of its grace even when sold. But concerning prophecy, Hieronymus, commenting on Micah, provides a fitting resolution, declaring that it was evil men, not good, who accepted payment:

### C. XXIII. Evil prophets, not good ones, sold prophecy.

“Its leaders,” he says, “judged for bribes, its priests responded for hire, and its prophets divined for money, and they leaned upon the Lord, saying: Is not the Lord among us? No evil shall come upon us. Therefore on your account Zion shall be plowed like a field, and Jerusalem shall become as a watchman’s hut in an orchard.” \[Micah 3:11–12\] Let no one doubt that Jerusalem was overthrown on account of the crimes described in this chapter. And a little later:

§. 1. Petrus could have sold to Symon Magus what he requested; indeed, he could have pretended to sell it — for the Holy Spirit can be neither sold nor bought — but he condemned the offered money along with its offerer.

§. 2. Nor should anyone be troubled by what we read in the first book of Kings: that Saul, wishing to go to Samuel, said to his servant that he could not go to him “because there is no payment in my hand,” and added: “I have a quarter of a shekel of silver in my hand; I will give it to the man of God, and he will tell us our way.” \[1 Samuel 9:7–8\] For it is not written that Samuel received it, or that they offered it; rather, these are better understood as offerings for the tabernacle than as payment for prophecy. For a shekel has twenty obols; a quarter of a shekel is five obols. Let our priests, then, if they wish to sell prophecy and display doves upon their seats — which the Lord overturned with a whip — be content with a payment of only five obols; and would that they received not the price of estates, but merely five obols!

§. 3. The same point is made by the third book of Kings, concerning the wife of Jeroboam going to the man of God, who offered only ten loaves, a cake, and a jar of honey.
