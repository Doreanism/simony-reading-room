God may be purchased with money.” For the Holy Spirit is indeed a gift of God, and the property of the church that has been offered is likewise a gift of God. And, if you reflect carefully, Simon Magus — who had come to the faith — did not wish to buy the Holy Spirit himself (for he was unworthy of it, since, as it is written, the Holy Spirit of discipline flees the deceitful), but rather the gift of that Spirit, so that from the sale of the signs that would be performed through it he might profit by multiplied money. Nor did the Apostle abhor and smite with the dart of his curse the buying of the Holy Spirit itself — which he well knew could not rightly occur — but rather the ambition of such gain and the avarice, which is the service of idols, in that same Simon. Whoever, therefore, sells or buys ecclesiastical properties (which are the gifts of God — given by God to the faithful, and by the faithful to God; which are received freely from him and therefore must be given freely) for his own profit, is with that same Simon reckoning that the gift of God may be purchased with money. And therefore whoever dispenses or acquires them not for the purpose for which they were established, but for his own profit — whether through the gift of flattering speech, or of undue service, or of money — is a simoniac, since the primary intention of Simon was solely the cause of money, namely avarice, that is idolatry, as the Apostle Paul says. Otherwise, why did the Council of Chalcedon of 630 bishops so strictly forbid the steward or defender of a church, or anyone subject to its rule, to be ordained through money, that it anathematizes even the intermediaries of so great a crime — unless it judges these same persons to be simoniacs? And if the soldiers of the Church are struck in this way for such a crime, no wise person will deny that those who are not soldiers of the Church deserve to be struck much more harshly for this same cause — that is, of selling or buying. But our blessed predecessor Pascalis affirms concerning consecration, that whoever sells either one of the two, without which the other does not come about, leaves neither of them unsold — and through this understands that the one who sells or buys a church’s property is a simoniac. §. 2. Under the name of steward \[*procurator*\], the aforesaid council understands any administrator of ecclesiastical properties — for example, a provost, administrator, or *vicedominus*; under the name of defender, an advocate, or *castaldus*, and a judge; under those subject to the rule, an archpriest, archdeacon, canon, monk, or anyone bound to an ecclesiastical office. §. 3.
