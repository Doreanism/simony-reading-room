### C. XXV. On the same.

Also, Pope Damasus, \[epistle IV, on chorepiscopoi\].

Those who seemed to have received something possessed \[only\] a head wounded by an illicit laying on of hands; and where the wound was inflicted, it is necessary to apply the medicine by which the inflicted stain may be healed — that is, it is necessary to repeat what is not shown to have been legitimately performed or conferred. For I cannot discover how one who received \[an order\] from someone who had no legitimate power to give it can retain the honor.

### \[C. XXVI.\] Book 9 of the Code, title on the Julian law of extortion \[*Lex Iulia de repetundis*\].

Emperors Theodosius and Valentinianus \[Augusti, to Florentinus.\]

VII. Pars. We decree that men of this kind shall be appointed to govern provinces — men who are accustomed to be advanced to the distinctions of honor not through ambition or purchase, but by the testimony of their proven life and of your greatness — on this condition, that those to whom these honors shall have been entrusted through the election of your seat or our own, shall solemnly declare in the public records under oath that they have given nothing whatsoever for obtaining their offices and will never give anything thereafter, whether through themselves or through a person interposed to defraud the law and the oath, whether under the title of donation or sale, or under any other cover of any contract whatsoever; and on that account they shall receive absolutely nothing — whether while holding office or after laying it down — for any benefit rendered during the time of their administration, which they earned gratuitously (salary excepted). And although we believe no one who fears God would be so forgetful as to despise the oath and set any advantage before his own salvation, yet so that he may be bound by fear for his salvation out of the necessity of danger: if anyone shall dare to disregard the oaths he has given, we grant to all the right to bring an accusation — not only against the one who received but also against the one who gave — as a public crime, the one convicted being struck in every way with a penalty of fourfold \[the amount\].

### C. XXVII. On the same.

Also, Pope Pascalis.

It is plain that simonists, as the primary and foremost heretics, are to be rejected by all the faithful, and if, when admonished, they do not repent, they are to be suppressed by external powers. For all crimes, in comparison with the simoniac heresy, are reckoned as if for nothing.
