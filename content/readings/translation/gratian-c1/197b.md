The disposition of the one who remembers and repents corrects the usurpation of the illicit act. But if he does not correct it, what was given will remain for the punishment of the one who usurped it — whether of the one who gave illicitly or of the one who received illicitly — yet it will not be regarded as not given. §. 4. For if some persons secretly and irregularly, not in the public mints, stamp gold or silver with a seal, when they are caught and punished or pardoned, surely the recognized royal stamp would be required to be brought into the royal treasury. And a little further on: §. 5. If perhaps a soldier in a panic shrinks from the military brand upon his body, flees to the emperor’s clemency, and, having obtained pardon, enters military service, surely when the man is freed and reformed the brand is not repeated but rather, once recognized, is approved? Do the sacraments of Christ possess less, then, than this bodily mark — since we see that even apostates do not lack baptism, to which, when they return through penance, it is not restored, and therefore it is judged that it cannot be lost? §. 6. Concerning those who have separated themselves from the unity of the Catholic Church, there is now no question but that they have it and can confer it — though they hold it destructively and confer it destructively, because they are outside the bond of peace. §. 7. Wrong must be done to neither sacrament. §. 8. Just as one who departs from unity does not hold it rightly, yet holds it nonetheless, and therefore it is not given back to him on his return, so too one who departs from unity does not confer it rightly, yet confers it nonetheless, and therefore what he received from such a person is not repeated when he comes to unity. Further: §. 9. Just as what a person also held outside is not restored to him on his return, so what he received outside need not be repeated when he comes \[to unity\]. Hence it is understood consequently that the perversity of persons is to be corrected, but the holiness of sacraments is not to be violated in any perverse person. For it is established that holiness remains undefiled and inviolable in perverse and wicked persons — whether among those who are within or those who are without — and when the wicked are said to defile it, this is said with respect to what is within themselves, while the sacraments themselves remain undefiled; but in the good they remain for reward, in the wicked for judgment. Further: §. 10. How do Catholics not glorify the Lord, who follow his sacraments with such fitting veneration, that even when handled by the unworthy — those persons being condemned for their perversity — the sacraments are shown to remain in their inviolate holiness?
