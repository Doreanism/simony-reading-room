Concerning these persons it is further added in the same book of Prosper:

### C. VIII. One who is unwilling to relinquish his own possessions ought not to be supported at the church’s expense.

If someone is unwilling to relinquish his possessions for this reason — so that he may have something to live on — why does he accept, for which he must render an account? Why does he multiply his own possessions through the sins of others? §. 1. It is not for me to say what kind of sin those commit who presumptuously take the food of the poor; those who burden the church, which they ought to help from their own resources, with their own expenses on top of everything else — perhaps living in the community for this reason: so that they need not feed any of the poor, nor receive those who come, nor diminish their own wealth by daily expenditures. §. 2. But if they have contributed something from their income to the church, as it were for their own board, let them not set themselves above with vain boasting those whom the church, though they have nothing, feeds and clothes; for more perfect is the one who strips himself of worldly goods, or who, having had nothing, does not desire to have anything, than one who, out of much that he possesses, has contributed something to the church and perhaps boasts about what he has contributed.

Gratianus. See who are those forbidden to be received by the church. Those, however, who relinquish all their possessions — whether they distribute them to the poor or join them to the resources of the church — are both to be received by the church with praise and to be sustained by its resources.

Hence in the same book we read:

### C. IX. Those who relinquish their possessions, whether distributing them to the poor or joining them to the resources of the church, laudably administer ecclesiastical goods.

The priest to whom the care of distribution has been entrusted receives from the people what is to be distributed not only without greed but even with the praise of piety, and faithfully distributes what he has received; for he has either left all his possessions to his parents, or distributed them to the poor, or joined them to the resources of the church, and has placed himself in the number of the poor out of love of poverty, so that from what he ministers to the poor he may himself likewise live as a voluntary poor man. Clerics, too, whom birth has made poor, whether living in their own homes or in community, may with the perfection of virtue receive the necessities of life, since it is not greed that leads them to receive these things, but the necessity of living that compels them. §. 1. Of such persons the Apostle seems to speak: “Those who serve in the sanctuary eat of the things of the sanctuary, and those who serve at the altar share with the altar.” He would never have gone on to add in what follows, “So also God has ordained that those who preach the gospel should live by the gospel,” unless he had wished this to be understood of those who hold their own resources in contempt. Those live by the gospel who wish to have nothing as their own; who neither have nor desire to have anything, possessing not what is theirs but what belongs to all.
