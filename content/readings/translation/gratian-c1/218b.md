V. Pars. Gratianus. Behold, for what reasons certain things are tolerated in the church — things which, when those reasons cease, are punished with holy severity. But when they are to be tolerated and when they are to be cut away, Pope Gelasius distinguishes, saying to the bishops throughout Lucania, epistle I, chapter 11:

### C. XXIII. When certain things in the church are to be tolerated.

Although those things which, if the integrity of the rest stands firm, are by themselves unable to cause harm, are sometimes to be permitted; yet those things are greatly to be guarded against which cannot be admitted without manifest defilement. And if the consideration of circumstances or times, or regard for expedient provision, sometimes excuses the allowance of those things which are thought to do no harm at all — how much more are those things in no way to be tampered with which neither necessity nor any ecclesiastical utility whatsoever demands?

For canonical precepts are sometimes curtailed and relaxed out of necessity or for the utility of the church.

VI. Pars. Gratianus. §. 1. We have briefly shown which persons returning from heresy may be received in their orders, and which may not. Now the question arises concerning those ordained by heretics: if they return from heresy to the unity of the Catholic mother Church, may they be validly ordained again in the same order? “For one who has once been consecrated,” as blessed Gregorius says, “ought not to be consecrated again.” §. 2. Likewise, the sacraments which are administered by heretics in the form of the church — as Augustinus testifies — ought not to be repeated. §. 3. But lest injury seem to be done not to the man but to the sacrament, that statement of Gregorius is understood of those who have received priestly or episcopal consecration, who, when they return to the church, either receive the effect of their anointing through the laying on of hands, or are commanded to cease permanently from its administration. Similarly, that statement of Augustinus is understood of the same mystical anointing and of the sacrament of baptism. §. 4. There are, however, other orders in the church which are conferred not by sacramental anointing but by the bishop’s blessing alone, with a certain distribution of vessels or vestments — such as Levites and those established below them. These, although ordained by heretics, yet on returning to the church are \[to be\] ordained by the church in the same order (if they are otherwise worthy), and there will be no repetition of the gift in them, since nothing is judged to have been conferred upon them by the heretics.

Hence Urbanus II writes to Petrus, Bishop of Pistoia, and Rusticus, Abbot of Vallombrosa:

### C. XXIV. Those returning from heretics are to be reordained.

We find that Daibertus was ordained a deacon by Negzelonus — though \[Negzelonus was\] a simonist — yet not simoniacally, by Daibertus’s own confession; and it is established, declared by the sentence of blessed Pope Innocentius, that Negzelonus the heretic (who is known to have been ordained by heretics) is to be regarded as if for nothing.
