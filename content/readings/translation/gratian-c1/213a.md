Hence Symmachus writes to Bishop Caesarius, epistle I, c. 5:

### C. I. A bishop’s election shall not be subscribed to out of fear, bribery, or the intercession of the powerful.

If anyone desires a bishopric, let him by no means employ influential persons as advocates on his behalf through the giving of money; let him not compel clerics or citizens to subscribe to the decree made on his behalf by threats of any kind, nor encourage them with any gifts whatsoever.

### C. II. One who bestows gifts in order to be elected is a simoniac.

Likewise, Gregorius to the inhabitants of Salona, Book V, epistle 26.

But I, having regard for your charity and fearing the judgment of omnipotent God for my own soul, desire that the case of this same Maximus be carefully examined: whether he is burdened by no crimes that are contrary to holy orders, and whether he is not attempting to reach the priestly office through the simoniac heresy — that is, by offering gifts to those who elect him. For he will be a free intercessor before the Lord on your behalf if he comes to the place of intercession without the obligation of his own \[sins\].

### C. III. As ordination, so also election or consent must be offered without venality.

Likewise, Gregorius to Syagrius, Bishop of Autun, and to other bishops, Book VII, epistle 110.

Just as the one who refuses when invited, who flees when sought, is to be brought to the sacred altar, so the one who of his own accord pursues \[office\] or thrusts himself forward importunately is without doubt to be repelled. For one who strives to ascend to higher things in this manner — what else is he doing but growing outwardly while diminishing inwardly, and while ascending externally descending into the depths within? Therefore, dearest brother, let sincerity prevail in the ordaining of priests; let the consent be simple and without venality; let the election be offered in purity, so that one advanced to the summit of the priesthood is believed to have come there not by the patronage of sellers, but by the judgment of God.

Gratianus. If, however, money was promised or paid entirely without the knowledge of the person elected or ordained, and he did not thereby attain the election or ordination \[through that money\], it appears in no way to harm him: it does not pertain to the guilt of a crime of which he had no knowledge, nor to the annulment of an election or ordination, to which he is shown to have arrived by other means than through their wicked greed.
