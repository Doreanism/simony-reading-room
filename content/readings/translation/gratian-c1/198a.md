XII. Part. Gratian. From these words of Augustinus it is established that in all persons — apostates, heretics, and the condemned alike — the sacraments of Christ remain true as regards themselves, and holy; and unless relief comes through penance or pardon, they will tend toward the condemnation of the one who usurps them, whether in holding, giving, or receiving them. What benefit is it, then, that they are true and holy, when they destroy their usurpers just as surely as if they were evil and harmful? Crassus thirsted for gold and drank gold; he perished just as surely by true gold as he would have by true poison. §. 1. Likewise, gold is equally true in a thief’s strongbox as in the king’s treasury. But by a thief it is held illicitly, given illicitly, and received illicitly; by the king it is held, given, and received licitly. Therefore the thief himself and his accomplice in the theft (unless perhaps he has cleared himself from knowledge of the theft) will be condemned, as Gelasius declares to the emperor Anastasius. §. 2. Now against this opinion of Augustinus it is objected: The power of conferring baptism, the right to consecrate the Lord’s body, and the authority to bestow holy orders differ greatly from one another. For when a priest is suspended or deposed, no power of sacrifice is left to him. Yet the sacrament of baptism will not be violated by any repetition even if it has been administered by a deposed priest, a Catholic layman, or even a heretic or pagan; but no reason allows that those who receive the oil of sacred (or rather, execrable) anointing from the hands of a layman or a pagan are to be counted among priests. It does not therefore follow consistently that, if the right to baptize is retained by those who depart from the faith, the power to distribute holy orders should be retained by them also, even though both arise from consecration. For a deposed bishop has no power to bestow holy orders, yet he has not lost the faculty of baptizing. §. 3. But lest we entirely repudiate Augustinus on this point, let us understand that the power to distribute holy orders is one thing, and the exercise of that power is another. Those who, remaining within the unity of the Catholic Church, receive priestly or episcopal anointing, acquire the office and the exercise of their office from the consecration. Those who depart from the integrity of the faith retain the power they received at the sacramental level, but are entirely deprived of the effect of that power — just as spouses who separate from one another do not dissolve the marriage once contracted, yet are found to be estranged from the conjugal act. §. 4. Augustinus therefore speaks of those who, having received priestly power, depart from the unity of the Catholic Church — not of those who receive priestly anointing while already placed in schism or heresy; otherwise he would be contradicting the Council of Chalcedon \[451\], in which those ordained by simonists are judged to profit nothing, and Innocentius, who testifies that those ordained by other heretics through the laying on of a corrupt hand have obtained only condemnation and a wound upon the head. §. 5. Although it may be stated generally that sacraments celebrated among heretics in no way different from \[those celebrated\] in the Church of God are true and valid as regards themselves, yet false and empty as regards their effect — both in those who handle them ill and in those who receive them ill. Nor is this surprising: our salvation itself gave a morsel to Iudas; immediately with the morsel not a good spirit but an evil one entered. Why? Not because the good one did not give good, but because the wicked one received good wickedly; and thus what was good had no good effect, because it found no place \[in him\] where it might act. §. 6.
