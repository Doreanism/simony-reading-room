### C. XIX. Donatists and Novatianists, though ordained reprehensibly, are nevertheless tolerated out of necessity.

Same \[pope\], to the bishops of the Moors.

Maximus also, though he was ordained from the laity in a blameworthy manner, yet if he is no longer a Donatist and has been separated from the spirit of schismatic wickedness, we do not repel him from the episcopal dignity which he has obtained by whatever means — provided that he makes clear by a letter sent to us that he is a Catholic.

### C. XX. On the same.

Same, in the same letter, at the beginning of chapter 3.

We wish Donatus of Seiacum, converted from Novatianism (as we have learned) together with his flock, to preside over the Lord’s flock on condition that he remember to direct to us a statement of his faith, in which he both condemns the error of Novatus’s teaching and most fully confesses the Catholic truth. Those matters which we have now remitted with deliberate consideration of circumstances are henceforth to be observed according to the ancient rules, lest what we have granted for a time by pious leniency we should thereafter have to punish with just retribution.

### C. XXI. Those returning from heresy after public profession may be received in their orders.

Same \[pope\], to Ianuarius, Bishop of Aquileia, epistle III.

For it is most salutary and filled with the benefit of spiritual medicine that priests, deacons, subdeacons, or clerics of any order who wish to be seen as corrected and desire to return again to the Catholic faith which they had long since lost — let them first declare without ambiguity that they condemn both their errors and the very authors of those errors; and once the perverse opinions are also destroyed, let no occasion for despair remain, nor shall any member \[of the faithful\] be able to be harmed by association with such persons, when their own profession shall have come to oppose them in all things. With respect to these persons we also prescribe that the canonical constitution be observed: that they count it a great benefit if, with all hope of advancement taken from them, they remain in perpetual stability in the order in which they are found — provided they have not been stained by repeated anointing.
