### C. XCV.

Item, On the Trinity:

For \[the sacrifice\] cannot rightly be offered except through a just and holy priest, nor unless what is offered is received by those for whom it is offered, and \[the sacrifice\] itself be without fault, so that it may be offered for cleansing those who are faulty. This is certainly what all those desire who wish a sacrifice to be offered to God on their behalf.

Gratian. When the life of priests is said to be necessary for the eucharist, this is to be understood of its effect, not its consecration; for the effect of salvation is not conferred upon one whom his own merits make unworthy; rather, in such a person is fulfilled that word of the Apostle: “He who eats and drinks unworthily, eats and drinks judgment upon himself.” Therefore, the statement of Ieronimus is to be understood as directed against those who think they can purify themselves by frequent offering of sacrifice, while not ceasing to act impiously against the law of God. But that a priest, even if wicked, nonetheless through the office of his dignity pours grace upon men, Augustinus attests in the Book of Questions on the Old Testament:

### C. XCVI.

Priests invoke the name of the Lord; it is the Lord, however, who bestows the blessing.

It was said by the Lord in Numbers to Aaron: “You shall put my name upon the children of Israel, and I the Lord will bless them” — so that the handing on \[of the sacrament\] through the ministry of an ordained \[person\] pours grace upon men, and the will of the priest can neither harm nor benefit \[the recipient\], but only the merit of the one seeking the blessing. §. 1. Let us also notice here how great is the dignity of the priestly office or order. For it was said of the most wicked Caiaphas, the killer of the Savior, among other things: “This he did not say of himself, but being high priest of the priests of that year, he prophesied.” By this it is shown that the Holy Spirit of graces follows not the person of the worthy or unworthy, but the order of the tradition \[i.e., ordination\]; so that, even if someone is of good merit, he cannot nevertheless bless unless he has been ordained to perform the office of ministry. But to God belongs the giving of the effect of the blessing.

Gratian. But the claim that sacraments of \[holy\] orders administered through heretics lack their effect is disproved by the authority of Pope Anastasius, who approves the ordination of Acacius — though a heretic — as valid, asserting that the condemned person caused no harm even to those whom he baptized or ordained after his own condemnation. But that he acted illicitly and not canonically — indeed, contrary to the decrees of his predecessors and successors — is proven by Felix and Gelasius, who excommunicated Acacius before Anastasius, and by Hormisda, who, third after Anastasius himself, condemned this same Acacius. Hence Anastasius is also repudiated by the Roman church, and it is read in the Gesta of the Roman Pontiffs that he was struck down by God, in these words: “Anastasius II, a Roman by birth, etc.” Seek this back in the treatise on decretal epistles.
