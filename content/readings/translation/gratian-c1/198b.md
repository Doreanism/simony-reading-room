And in order to make this plain in all sacraments, he was transfigured twice while in the flesh. In one transfiguration he appeared wonderful as the sun, so that the faith of believers might be increased toward contemplating the heavenly glory elevated above them. In the other, as a stranger and unrecognized, he feigned to go further from those who doubted him and held back their eyes so that he would not be recognized — to what end, if not to show that, since he was not present in their hearts through faith, he was withdrawing further from them, and that, because they did not believe him to be what he was, they should see him also as other than he was? Behold: in each vision his presence and truth were real; but in one, faith was being exercised so that they might believe more than they believed, while in the other, unbelief was being restrained lest they recognize even what they saw. Therefore the sacrament of his body and blood is for some unto salvation, for others unto judgment. Faith too, even though it is true, is nevertheless dead without works. So also all ten virgins were equally virgins, but among them five were foolish and five were wise. The truth of their virginity was equal, yet on account of the intention of that same virginity there was not for all an equal identity of merit. So also is the sounding brass or the tinkling cymbal the martyrdom or almsgiving that charity does not know. If a blind man ministers the truth of a lamp to a blind man, neither thereby illuminates his blindness the more. So too the wicked man ministers true sacraments to the wicked, but the spiritual gifts are not therefore \[given\], because by their evil merits the Holy Spirit is prevented from working in them what is his. Hence Innocentius, while conceding that the baptism of heretics is valid, does not concede that the Holy Spirit is obtained from that baptism. And Leo does not permit those who have received the form of baptism \[from heretics\] to be rebaptized, but orders that the Holy Spirit — which no one has received from heretics — should be obtained through invocation of him and the laying on of hands by Catholic priests. It must be known, moreover, that the sacraments of heretics are called void, or even to be condemned, false and empty — not as regards themselves, since they are holy and true even when celebrated by a heretic, but because, since they are unto judgment for the faithless who give them illicitly, they do not confer the Holy Spirit upon those who receive them illicitly from such persons. They are called void and not true because they do not bestow what they promise and are believed to confer, and are therefore to be condemned, so that their being given or received by heretics is not approved but forbidden. For they are not defiled as regards themselves, even though they are said to be defiled by heretics. Hence Gregorius calls the communion of Arius an execration, and Innocentius calls the ordination of Bonosus a condemnation — not because they are such in themselves, but because they produce this effect in those who give or receive them ill. So too Ieronimus in \[his commentary on\] Hosea calls their sacrifices bread of mourning — not as regards themselves but as regards their effect, as he himself shows by adding: “Whosoever shall eat thereof shall be defiled, because they shall take it unto judgment for themselves.” Hence also Augustinus in book II against the letter of Parmenianum:

### C. XCVIII.
