Iudas sold the Redeemer of all, and soon hanged by a noose did not obtain that same grace of the Redeemer — and rightly so, because no one can retain what he has sold. Symon Magus also, when he wished to purchase the same grace of the Holy Spirit, heard from Petrus the sentence of his own condemnation: “Your money perish with you, because you thought to possess the gift of God with money” \[Acts 8:20\]. The Lord too, to show that those who sell and buy are without this grace, through grace dismissed all sinners in the temple; he singled out the sellers and buyers, rebuked them, and drove them out of the temple with a whip — in which act the sellers and buyers are established to be destitute and empty of the grace of the Holy Spirit. Those whom Christ drove out of the temple — can the Spirit of Christ make them his temple?

### C. XII. Simoniacs cannot be priests.

Likewise, Gregorius.

Those who sell or buy sacred orders cannot be priests. Hence it is written: “Anathema to the giver, anathema to the receiver — this is the simoniacal heresy.” How, therefore, if they are under anathema and are not holy, can they sanctify others? And since they are not in the body of Christ, how can they give or receive the body of Christ? One who is accursed — how can he bless?

### \[C. XIII.\]

The same Gregorius to Siagrius and other Bishops.

Since it is clear that this heresy crept in before all others with a pestilential root, and was condemned by apostolic detestation in its very origin, why is it not guarded against? Why is it not considered that the blessing of him who is promoted so as to become a heretic is turned into a curse?

### C. XIV. He who ordains for a price confers leprosy, not office.

Likewise, Ambrosius, on Ministries \[De officiis\].

When he was ordained bishop, what he gave was gold, but what he lost was his soul; when he ordained another, what he received was gold, but what he gave was leprosy.

### \[C. XV.\]

Likewise, in the same.

You did not receive grace when you were ordained, because you did not merit it freely.
