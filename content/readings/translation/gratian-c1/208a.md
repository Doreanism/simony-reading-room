“O how many provostships they share out! Primianus holds one provostship at Carthage, Rogatus holds another in Mauretania, and this or that person holds another in Numidia — persons so numerous that we cannot even name them all. And so someone goes about to buy a dove; each one praises his own provost for what he sells, etc.” Behold, the venerable Augustinus, in treating of the trafficking of a provostship, understands under the name of the dove the selling or buying of the Holy Spirit — as indeed do all the commentators on this chapter of the Gospel. §. 4. It must be considered, moreover, with what penalty those are to be punished who sell or buy things already offered to God and his Church, if those who sold or bought things to be offered to God were driven out of the temple of God with whips. If concerning sellers and buyers of things to be offered it was said: “You have made my Father’s house a house of commerce and a den of thieves” — what will be said concerning the sellers and buyers of things already offered to the Church? §. 5. And lest any madman object that the Lord took such severe vengeance for this reason — that those things were being sold in the temple of God, whereas ecclesiastical properties are now disposed of outside the temple — attend to Augustinus above, who does not limit the place of the selling or buying of provostships, but only says indefinitely: “Someone goes about to buy a dove; each one praises his own provost for what he sells” — without adding, “in the temple” or “outside the temple.” Let these arguments suffice against the sellers and buyers of sacred things.

### C. IX. Ministers and servants of the Church shall be ordained by bishops without any venality.

Item, Alexander II, Bishop, servant of the servants of God, to the clergy and people of the Church of Lucca, in perpetuity.

We have learned that this detestable evil has grown up in your church over many years: that no cleric, however devout, however endowed with learning and character, was ever granted an ecclesiastical benefice, unless it were one who had striven to buy it with the profane gift of money. And so the Church and its properties were made as venal as some common earthly merchandise put out for sale by traders. How detestable this evil is, how contrary to God and the saints, is taught by the sacred canons and is manifest to nearly everyone. This judgment is found in chapter 2 of the Council of Chalcedon. §. 1. For the Council of Chalcedon — one of the principal councils — condemns with equal penalty both those who acquire the benefice of the Church through the mediation of money and those who are known to traffic in the sacred laying on of hands (through which the Holy Spirit is conferred). For by inexpugnable authority it orders both groups to be expelled: the former from the benefice, the latter from holy orders. Hence the Redeemer of the human race, casting out all who bought and sold from the temple, overturned the seats of those who sold doves, poured out the coins of the money-changers, and declared by the authority of his command: “Do not make my Father’s house a house of commerce.” §. 2.
