The Apostle, however, who so preached the Gospel that he provided necessities for himself by his own hands, speaks of himself with confidence: “But I have used none of these things.” And why he said this he explained in what follows, saying: “For it is better for me to die than that any man should make my glory void.” He says his glory is made void if he had wished to receive from those to whom he preached the provision of temporal life. For he did not wish to receive the fruit of his labor in the present, but in the future.

### C. X. Priests shall distribute ecclesiastical stipends to clerics according to the merit of their labor.

Item from the Council of Agde.

All clerics who faithfully and diligently serve the Church shall receive, through the ordinance of the canons, the stipends due to their holy labors, according to the merit of their service, from the priests.

Gratianus. By the authorities of many it is more clearly than light established that money may not be demanded from those about to enter a monastery, lest both the one who demands and the one who pays incur the crime of simony.

## QUESTIO III.

GRATIANUS.

But it is further objected that those who give money upon entering a monastery are not purchasing religious profession but rather a share in the stipends of the Church. They are therefore buying temporal things, not spiritual, and are in no way to be considered simoniacs. §. 1. To this it is replied thus: Not only those who receive spiritual things for a price, but also those who receive temporal things annexed to them, are judged simoniacs. Hence Malachias, speaking in the person of the Lord, says: “Who is there among you that will shut the doors and kindle mine altar for nothing? I have no pleasure in you, saith the Lord of hosts, and I will not accept an offering at your hand.” To shut the doors is not a function of sacred office, but is merely attached to it.

Item, Gregorius writes to all the Bishops of Spain:

### C. I. Concerning those who dispense sacred orders through the acceptance of gifts.

We have heard, most beloved, what we were bound to hear but truly did not wish to hear: that there are certain dispensers of sacred orders who, through the giving or receiving of gifts, usurp for themselves the churches of God or their benefices. We greatly wonder that you did not sharply correct this crime long ago; for it is just that both should be rebuked and removed from the threshold of the holy Church — both the one who lavishes gifts in order to obtain a church, and the one who presumes to accept anything in order that a person may have access to a church. The one, indeed, presumes to buy the gift of God; the other, to sell it. Nor would I call this deed far removed from sacrilege, since that which ought to have been done freely and by the holy deliberation of reason is instead transacted under a monetary agreement. Wherefore, most dear brothers, diligently refute what has been done in such a manner, forbid what has not yet been done, and do not hesitate to strike with the worthy bond of anathema those who resist your commands.
