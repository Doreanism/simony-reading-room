III. Part. Gratianus. From this authority it is gathered that ignorance excuses such persons from the guilt of the crime, as was said above — but not from the fruit of the purchase. For they are not permitted to remain in the churches which their parents’ money obtained, except out of mercy, and only after they have relinquished those churches entirely. By this it is clear that, even though the sin of simony was committed without their knowledge, they cannot nonetheless retain the fruits of their father’s purchase.

That they are excused from the crime is clear from the fact that they are not removed from holy orders on this account. For if they were participants in that crime, they could not ascend to holy orders. The same is to be understood of one who obtained the dignity of the priesthood by means of money — money given not to the bishop, but to some one of the bishop’s leading men — with both parties, namely the ordainer and the ordained, being ignorant of this.

Urbanus II writes to Albertus, Bishop of Meaux:

### C. III. Concerning a certain man for whom, through his parents’ negligence, money was given not to the bishop but to one of his leading men.

The bearer of this letter, as you have informed us, obtained the dignity of the priesthood through his parents’ negligence, by money given not to the bishop but to a certain one of his leading men. Although the holy canons testify that he ought to be deposed, yet because he committed this offense unknowingly and under compulsion, and because he himself voluntarily petitioned to be deposed from that same order, we advise your fraternity — in consideration of discretion, which is the mother of all virtues — to have mercy rather than enforce canonical rigor, and to allow him to remain in that same order; yet in such a way that, if the church he serves does not suffer a shortage of priests, he shall remain suspended from the priestly office. If, however, the needs of the church require him to take up the care of governance, let him be permitted, with the concession of his bishop and the obedience of his brothers, to exercise the priestly office.

## QUESTIO VI.

GRATIANUS.

What is to be done with those who have been unknowingly ordained by simoniacs — which is the sixth question posed — is to be sought above, in the chapter of Urbanus that begins: “If any persons have been ordained by simoniacs without themselves committing simony.”

Now the question concerns those who do not offer money for the ordination itself, but for the election: whether to have the decree of election drawn up, or to have it confirmed by subscription, or to prevent opposition to the election, they bestow gifts on certain persons — is their election to be judged defective? For those who give gifts not for the consecration but for the election do not seem to be purchasing the gift of the Holy Spirit, since election does not make anyone a participant in any spiritual grace.
