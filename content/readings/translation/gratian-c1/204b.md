Likewise Bonifacius, Bishop of the city of Rome, writes to all the monks of the Caralitan church:

### C. II. Nothing is to be demanded from those who come to conversion, nor is anyone to be invited to conversion for a price.

How great is the compassion with which the Roman church, in pious devotion of heart, sympathizes with your destitution, brothers most dear, may be unknown to you, but it is not unknown to him who knows all things. I call God to witness concerning what fitting answer I can give to your doubt; that very doubt also renders me anxious and unfit. For we have never read that the Lord’s disciples, or those converted through their ministry, urged anyone toward the service of God through the intervention of any gift — unless someone were to bring up, out of place, the matter of food for the poor, for whom provisions were denied to none, of whatever calling he might be. We know indeed that “every good gift and every perfect gift is from above,” from whom he receives the gift of good will who, by the holy judgment of his deliberation, freely resolves to serve God. It follows, therefore, that whoever receives a gift for some acceptance into the church is selling a gift of God which ought to have descended from the Father of lights; and he who bestows the gift buys it presumptuously. What consequence follows upon the sellers and buyers of divine gifts need not be stated as a conclusion. §. 1. Take care, therefore, brothers, how ambiguous is the counsel you have sought, which we find confirmed by no reasoning in accordance with the purpose of your will. Nevertheless, if the person you have found is so useful to your need, provided that every pact is absent and every agreement ceases, and no alienation of your church takes place, let him come, let him begin to serve God freely, and let him devoutly perform the service of his governance; and thereafter the Roman church permits you, by way of assistance as it were, to bestow some gifts of his church for the consolation of the brethren.
