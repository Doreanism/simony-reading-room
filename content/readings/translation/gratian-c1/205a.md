### C. IV. Nothing ought to be demanded from one who has been ordained, but neither ought voluntarily offered gifts to be refused.

Likewise Gregorius in the Register.

Just as it does not befit a bishop to sell the hand he lays on, so the minister or notary ought not to sell his voice or his pen at an ordination. §. 1. But if one who has been ordained, not by prior agreement, not under compulsion or solicitation, after receiving his letters and pallium, wishes to offer something to any member of the clergy solely out of gratitude, we by no means forbid this to be accepted, because his offering incurs no stain of fault, since it did not proceed from the petition of one seeking \[payment\].

Gratianus. By the authority of Bonifacius it is plainly shown that just as no one is to be invited to conversion for a price, so no one ought to be granted entry into a church through the intervention of money. By the authority of Gregorius, however, it is made clear that it is not lawful to demand money for entry into a church, but it is lawful to receive money offered spontaneously.

II. Part. There is also the objection of the Apostle to Timotheus.

### [C. V.]

“Honor widows who are truly widows. But those who are not truly widows — that is, those who can be supported by their own resources or those of their relatives — are not to be received, so that the church may be sufficient for those who are truly widows.”

### C. VI. Those who can be supported by their own means should not receive stipends from the church.

Likewise Ieronimus to Pope Damasus.

It is fitting that those clerics should be supported by the stipends of the church who lack the support of parents and relatives. But those who can be supported by their parents’ resources and wealth — if they receive what belongs to the poor, they commit sacrilege indeed, and through the misuse of such things they eat and drink judgment upon themselves.

### C. VII. Those for whom their own means suffice for sustenance are not to be supported by the church.

Likewise Prosper in the book on the contemplative life.

The shepherd of the church ought not to distribute anything to those for whom their own means suffice, since to give to those who have is simply to lose it. Nor do those who, though possessing their own means, wish to have something given to them, receive without great sin that from which the poor man was going to live. Of clerics indeed the Holy Spirit says: “They eat the sins of my people.” But just as those who have nothing of their own receive not sins but sustenance, which they seem to lack, so those who are owners receive not sustenance, of which they have abundance, but the sins of others.
