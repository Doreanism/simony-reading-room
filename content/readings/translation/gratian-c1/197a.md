Further, the following text of Augustinus is cited against heretics even when condemned:

### C. XCVII.

One who departs from the Church loses neither baptism nor the right to confer it.

That some say a person who departs from the Church does not lose the baptism he received, but does lose the right to confer it that he received, is shown in many ways to be said in vain and without foundation. §. 1. First, because no reason is given why one who cannot lose baptism itself should be able to lose the right to confer it. For each is a sacrament, and each is given to a person by a certain consecration — the one when he is baptized, the other when he is ordained; hence neither may be repeated by a Catholic. §. 2. For if at any time those coming from that party, even their superiors, were received back for the sake of peace — their schismatic error having been corrected — even if it seemed necessary for them to hold the same offices they had held, they were not to be ordained again; rather, just as baptism remained intact in them, so too did ordination, because the fault lay in the separation (which the unity of peace has corrected), not in the sacraments, which are what they are wherever they are. And although it may seem expedient to the Church that their superiors, when coming to Catholic fellowship, not administer their offices there, nevertheless the very sacraments of ordination are not taken from them, but remain upon them. Therefore hands are not laid upon them again, lest the wrong be done not to the person but to the sacrament itself. §. 3. Just as in baptism there exists something that can be given through them, so in ordination there exists the right to confer; each is held to their own destruction so long as they lack the charity of unity; yet it is one thing not to have, another to have destructively, and another to have beneficially. Whatever is not had must be given when it needs to be given; but what is held destructively must, through correction, have the destruction driven out, so that it may be held beneficially. Now if a layman, compelled by necessity, baptizes someone in danger — having learned how baptism is to be conferred when he himself received it — I do not know whether anyone would say it ought devoutly to be repeated. For if it is done without any compelling necessity, it is an usurpation of another’s gift. But if necessity presses, it is either no sin at all or a venial one. But if it is usurped without necessity, and given by anyone to anyone, what has been given cannot be said not to have been given, though it may rightly be said to have been given illicitly.
