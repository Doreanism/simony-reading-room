### C. CXXI. By the intercession of the bishop, the Pope ordained a certain acolyte.

I have made the bearer of this letter an acolyte through the intercession of your holiness, and have sent him to you in your service, so that, if he shall serve still more in the winning of souls, he may be able to advance further.

### C. CXXII. Whoever has received holy orders in the Roman church may not depart from it thereafter.

The same \[Gregory\] to Helias, priest and abbot.

You instructed us that we should promote your son Epiphanius to holy orders and send him back to you. But in one of these requests we heeded you; in the other we could not. He has indeed been made a deacon; but whoever has once received holy orders in this church does not have permission to depart from it thereafter. If I could not see you \[in person\], I have this consolation on that account: that I take my rest in your son.

Gratianus. Ordinations made at the request of various persons are not therefore to be judged false; rather, only those are \[false\] which are made at the request of the person to be ordained or his friend, prompted not by spiritual but by carnal affection — requests which, had things been otherwise, would not have been made; ordinations which are extorted not by charity intervening, but by ambition petitioning. Moreover, the supreme Pontiff has the power not only to ordain clerics of any bishop in the Roman church, but also, when necessity demands it, to summon and invite monks and any clerics whatsoever. Hence Pope Nicolaus writes to Emperor Michael in the letter that begins “Proposueramus”:

### C. CXXIII. On the same.

Through the preeminent authority of the blessed apostles Petrus and Paulus, we have the right to summon to ourselves, when necessity requires it, not only monks but any clerics whatsoever from whatever diocese, and to invite them when ecclesiastical needs demand.

XV. Part. Gratianus. Therefore, whenever any gift whatsoever intervenes, an ordination is judged false. Moreover, just as holy orders are not to be conferred when payment intervenes, so neither are they to be restored \[for payment\]. For as blessed Ambrosius says, holy orders are neither to be purchased for a price nor redeemed for one. Hence also Gregorius VII says:

### C. CXXIV. When payment intervenes, the priestly office is not to be restored.

Let no bishop impose a burden or servile service upon his abbots or clerics contrary to ecclesiastical custom, nor restore an interdicted priestly office when payment intervenes. If he does so, let him incur the risk of losing his own office.

Gratianus. Those who accept money in order not to confer holy orders on those to be ordained, or in order not to give their assent to a canonical election, or who withhold a blessed stone or consecration from churches being built or consecrated, are proven guilty and infamous by many arguments for having received payment.
