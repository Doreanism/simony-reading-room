Just as Jacob attained the blessing through deception, as a crop sown from diseased seed is nonetheless reaped clean, as a fair offspring is born from an adulterous union — so too money acquired through simony has a happy outcome when it is distributed for the benefit of the poor. But none of these examples provides any defense for simonists. For Leo says to the bishops of the Mauri:

### C. XXV. What begins with an evil principle is not brought to a good end.

A position of authority that either sedition has extorted or ambition has seized, even if it gives no offense in conduct and action, is nonetheless pernicious by the very example of its vice. And it is difficult for things that have begun with an evil principle to be brought to a good end.

### C. XXVI. No good work is built upon a foundation where Christ is not.

Likewise, Gregorius to Anastasius, Bishop of Antioch.

Since Paulus says: “No one can lay any foundation other than the one that has been laid, which is Christ Jesus \[1 Corinthians 3\],” it follows that where Christ is not the foundation, there is no superstructure of good work.

### C. XXVII. Alms cannot be made from money acquired through simony.

The same \[Gregorius\] to Bishop Siagrius.

It is not to be considered an act of charity if what is acquired through illicit means is dispensed to the poor, because one who receives wrongly with the intention of distributing well is burdened rather than helped.

§. 1. From this it is also certain that even if monasteries, hospices, or anything else is built with money given for sacred ordinations, it does not profit one’s merit.

§. 2. Therefore, lest under the pretext of charity we should strive to receive anything along with sin, scripture forbids it, saying: “The offerings of the wicked are an abomination, those offered from crime \[Proverbs 21\].” For whatever is offered in sacrifice to God from crime does not appease the wrath of almighty God but provokes it.
