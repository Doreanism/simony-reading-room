### \[C. XXIV.\]

The same, in the same.

Divination is never taken in a good sense. They seemed to themselves indeed to be prophets, but because they accepted money, their prophecy became divination.

Gratian. Since, therefore, according to Hieronymus, the good prophets did not accept gifts as payment for prophecy but as offerings for the tabernacle, while the evil ones who did accept them had their prophecy — which appeared to be a gift of God — turned into divination, which is of the devil, what else can be concluded but that, as was said above, no spiritual grace cooperates with simoniacs?

For since in the sacraments it is written “neither he who plants nor he who waters is anything, but God who gives the growth” \[1 Corinthians 3:7\], who acts in them if the Lord, who gives the growth, does not shine in them but remains idle? Likewise, the argument that just as Christ when sold restored the ear of Malchus and performed miracles, so too the Holy Spirit can be sold and can operate its grace in that very selling — this analogy does not hold. For the Holy Spirit does not effect spiritually in the faithless traffickings of avarice what Christ performed bodily for a specific sacramental purpose and as an example of patience. For if the analogy holds in every respect, since the Spirit is believed capable of being sold like Christ, let it also be believed that the Holy Spirit can be crucified — which is impossible. It may also be understood, without departing from the analogy, that just as Christ, when sold, conferred the gifts of his grace not upon the seller or the buyer but upon those who were present without knowing \[what was happening\], and for whom he also prayed upon the cross, so too the Holy Spirit is shown to bestow the effect of its grace not upon the seller or buyer but upon those who receive the Lord’s sacraments from their hands, led by ignorance.
