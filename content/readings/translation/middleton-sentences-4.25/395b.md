This being understood, it must be known that some are notorious according to the canons who are not notorious according to the civil laws, and some are notorious according to the civil laws who are not notorious according to the canons — C. 6 q. 1, “Omnes vero,” in the gloss — but that for the most part all who are notorious according to the civil laws are notorious according to the canons. Hence in the aforesaid chapter the text says: “We declare all those to be notorious whom the laws of the world call notorious.” I say therefore that those who are notorious by civil law in this last sense — whether the infamy is inflicted by sentence or contracted by the very fact — unless they have been fully restored \[*restituti in integrum*\] by one who has the power to do so, are by law itself deprived of legitimate acts; and thus they are to be excluded not only from reception of orders, but from accusation and from testifying, even after completed penance for the crime on account of which they became notorious. The reason for this is that those things on account of which both civil and canon law concur in judging someone to be notorious render him both disgraceful and base in the estimation of men, even if he has done penance; and accordingly it is said in Extra, de excessibus prælatorum, “Inter dilectos” \[*Among the beloved*\]: “The gates of dignity ought not to stand open to base and unworthy persons.” Sometimes, however, on account of the enormity of the crime — as in the crime of simony and other excepted crimes — criminal witnesses are received in equity when the matter is pursued civilly; they are not, however, received simply, but only along with other supporting evidence and when the person against whom they are adduced is of ill repute. Extra, de symonia, “Per tuas” \[*Through your*\].

To the first objection in opposition, it must be said that it proceeds from those who are notorious by civil law only.

To the second objection, it must be said that the minor premise is false, as is clear from what has been said in the body of the question.

To the first \[objection\] on the other side \[*ad partem aliam*\], it must be said that that gloss is not to be understood as meaning that the Pope cannot restore an infamous cleric to good repute; for the contrary is expressly stated at C. 2 q. 3, “Euphemium,” § “Hinc colligitur” \[*Hence it is gathered*\]: for there it is said that from this it is gathered that the infamy of clerics can be abolished by the Roman Pontiff.

It may also be said — as some hold, and probably — that he can abolish the infamy of laymen even when it has been inflicted by a civil judge. Hence that statement, “In other respects we can save them through penance, but we cannot abolish the infamy,” is to be expounded thus: “We cannot \[abolish it\], namely, through penance; yet we can through full restoration \[*per restitutionem in integrum*\].” And this interpretation is touched upon by the gloss there. That a \[secular\] prince can remove infamy of this kind \[inflicted on a layman\] by full restoration is found at the same question, “Si quem penituerit,” § “Notandum quoque,” near the end; and there also the form of restoration is found, which is as follows: “I restore you in full to your honors, your rank, and all else.”
