Furthermore, Decree, Distinction 56, at the beginning: *The sons of priests are not to be admitted to the sacred offices.*

Furthermore, Extra, *de filiis presbyterorum non ordinandis* \[Decretals, on the sons of priests not to be ordained\], chapter 1: *The sons of priests and others born of fornication are prohibited from being promoted to holy orders.*

ON THE OTHER HAND, a son, with respect to those things that concern the soul — since he does not have his soul from his carnal father — ought not to bear the iniquity of his father. Hence Ezekiel 18: *The son shall not bear the iniquity of his father.* Therefore the fornication of father and mother does not impede, in the offspring of fornicators, the reception of orders.

Furthermore, Decree, Distinction 56: *It is shown to be altogether perverse and contrary to ecclesiastical censure that anyone should be deprived of his own lawful advantages on account of the pleasures of others.* But if those born of fornication could not receive orders, they would be deprived of their own advantages on account of the illicit pleasures of their parents; therefore it seems that defect of birth does not impede the reception of orders.

Furthermore, one’s own fault ought to harm one more than another’s; but a fornicator, after performing penance, can lawfully be promoted; therefore much more can one who was born of fornication and is not himself a fornicator.

### CONCLUSION.

Defect of birth does indeed impede the reception of orders by right; but one illegitimately born who is ordained in fact truly receives the sacrament.

I REPLY that defect of birth does not impede the reception of any order as a matter of fact, for otherwise those dispensed would not be permitted to minister unless they were ordained again; yet it does impede the reception of orders by right. For the laws prohibit such persons from being ordained: first, on account of reverence for the sacrament; second, because the ordained person is placed in a position of eminence, which eminence does not befit the illegitimately born, since their reputation is obscured in the sight of men by reason of the baseness of their origin; third, on account of detestation of the parents’ crime; fourth, because such persons more than others tend, for the most part, to be ill-bred, since they are not instructed by their parents as carefully as those legitimately born. By dispensation, however — unless they have been imitators of their father’s incontinence — they may lawfully be promoted; and the dispensation is the more difficult to obtain, the more shameful their origin. For those to be able to hold dignities, personates, and other benefices having the cure of souls, a dispensation is required from the apostolic see: Extra, *de filiis presbyterorum non ordinandis* \[Decretals, on the sons of priests not to be ordained\].
