Furthermore, if a monk asks a priest to baptize a child at the point of death when there is no one else who can baptize him, and the priest refuses to baptize him except for money, then either the monk will allow the child to be damned — which is in no way to be permitted if help can be given — or he will purchase the sacrament of baptism from the priest. In this case, therefore, the sacrament of baptism can lawfully be purchased.

On the contrary, in Prima, q. 1, “Baptizandis,” etc., the opposite is held.

Furthermore, Extra, de simonia, “Si quis”: “If anyone ordains, or presents for ordination, receiving from him any promise or oath that he shall not be troubled regarding his provision — let the ordainer know himself suspended from the conferral \[of orders\], the presenter from the execution of orders for three years, and the ordained from the order thus received until he shall have merited to obtain a dispensation from the Apostolic See”; therefore, with much greater reason it is unlawful to buy or sell ordination or any other sacrament.

### Conclusion

By selling or buying the sacraments, simony is entirely committed.

I reply that in the buying or selling of the sacraments of the Church, simony is committed; and the reason for this is threefold.

The first is that it is contrary to the nature of grace that grace itself be sold or bought. Accordingly, it belongs to the very nature of grace that it be given freely; but the sacraments are instruments disposing \[souls\] toward grace, and therefore whoever sells or buys them does an injury to them.

The second reason is that the dispositive cause whose effect cannot be measured by any temporal thing exceeds the price of any temporal thing; and therefore when money is given for the sacraments, something beyond price is set under a temporal price. For the sacraments are instrumental causes disposing \[souls\] toward grace, which is beyond all price.

The third reason is that human beings are not lords of the sacraments but ministers; yet a thing ought not to be purchased except from its lord, nor ought a thing to be sold except by the lord or by a minister with the lord’s authority. God, however, did not give human beings the power of selling the sacraments, although he gave the ministers of the Church the power of administering them. Therefore whoever purchases a sacrament purchases a thing from one who is not its lord and who cannot sell it, and therefore such a purchase or sale is deservedly unlawful and execrable.
