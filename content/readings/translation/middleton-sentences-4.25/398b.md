## On the Letter of the Master \[of the Sentences\]

A bishop who has been degraded does not have the power to confer holy orders; what is to be held concerning this matter has been stated in the Questions. “Their consecration is entirely null”: this is said of those who knowingly and voluntarily made themselves ordinary \[ordinators\] to simoniacs, whose ordination is null as regards exercise, because by custom no dispensation is granted in such cases — especially if in addition to this there is simony on the part of the one who is ordained.

Note the verses in which the cases are set out in which by custom no dispensation is granted.

Whether order is given twice, or baptism is repeated; or whether it is granted so that simony accomplishes both; or whether order is sought so that the state of the faith be overthrown — the hope of dispensation is utterly void. Whoever sells either of these, without which the other is not had, leaves neither unsold.

## Objection

as was seen in the Questions, the right of patronage ought not to be sold, and yet it passes when a village or a castle is sold, unless it is expressly excepted.

## Reply

the word of the Master \[of the Sentences\] is not to be understood as referring to the sale of temporal things along with which something annexed to spiritual matters passes — such as the right of patronage — but to the sale of temporal things that are ordered toward spiritual things and that follow upon them. This is clear because the word of the Master is taken from Decree, 1 q. 3, “Si quis obiecerit,” where Pope Paschal \[II\] speaks against those who purchase the revenues of a church that by right follow upon or are owed to whoever is ordained as prelate of that church, and the right to receive those revenues cannot by right devolve upon anyone other than the prelate. Other expositions of the said word you will find in the gloss on the cited decree.

Note here that the right of patronage is transferred by sale, as was established above in the Questions, and by succession: Extra, de iure patronatus \[on the right of patronage\], c. 1 and c. “In quibusdam”; and by exchange: Decree, 16 q. last, “Nemini regum”; and by donation made with the consent of the bishop: Extra, de iure patronatus, “Nullus.” Hence the verse: *Heir, exchanged property, donation, and sale cause the right of patronage to pass.* “Simoniacs who are condemned are also to be deposed,” etc.: Those who efficaciously and faithfully revoke the agreement while the matter is still intact are not subject to this penalty; because even though such persons have made an unlawful pact and have sinned in this, yet the effect of the pact was not carried out.
