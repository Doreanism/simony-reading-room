To the seventh it must be said that it is in no way lawful to preach for money such that the money is the price of the preaching; it is lawful, however, to preach for money such that money is the thing preached about, provided the end of the preaching is not placed in this — as when a man preaches to the wealthy so that they may give money to the poor or to some pious work.

To the eighth it must be said that prayer is simply supernatural, both in its essence and in its cause; hence nothing may in any way be given for it by way of pact. Those who give money to the poor are not buying prayer, but are drawing the hearts of the poor to pray for them and making the poor their debtors. As for clerics who recite psalters for the dead: if they receive money for their labor, they do not sin, provided they are in need and are not otherwise already bound to do this.

To the ninth it must be said that such things could lawfully be brought to the prophets, not as the price of prophecy, but for their sustentation, so that they might be more freely available for spiritual things; yet it was not lawful for any pact to intervene.

## To the second part

TO THE SECOND, regarding the second part, it must be said that although it is unlawful to exact something for granting a license to teach, as the legal authorities cited clearly demonstrate, strictly speaking it is not simony. Hence the gloss there does not say that it is simoniacal, but quasi-simoniacal; and the lord Hostiensis \[Henricus de Segusio\] says in his Summa that it is not simoniacal but the crime of extortion. The crime of extortion occurs when someone, under the pretext of his office, extorts from an unwilling party something he is not owed. 33 quaestio 1, Militare \[Decretum C.33 q.1\]. When it is therefore called simoniacal, the name of simony is extended beyond simony in the strict sense.

## Question III

### Whether simony is committed in the buying or selling of things annexed to spiritual matters.

### Argument I.

IT SEEMS that it is not: for the sacred vessels of the church are annexed to spiritual things, and yet for the liberation of captives and the relief of the wretched poor they may and ought to be sold. 12 quaestio 2, Aurum \[Decretum C.12 q.2\].

### Second.

Item, the right of patronage is annexed to spiritual things, and yet it can be sold together with a village or castle, for it passes with such a sale unless it is expressly excepted. Extra de iure patronatus, Ex literis and Cum saeculum \[X.3.38.8, X.3.38.21\].
