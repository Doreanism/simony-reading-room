But if he does receive a salary from the church, he incurs the vice of simony, or something quasi-simoniacal, if he exacts a price for his teaching from clerics of the same church and from the poor wherever they may be. Extra de magistris, ne aliquid exigant, Quia nonnullus \[X.5.5.4\], in the text and in the gloss.

Some say, however, that he is not forbidden to exact a fee from the wealthy, as the gloss there seems to indicate — which I think must be understood as referring not to wealthy persons of the same church but of another; and even from those he ought not to exact a fee unless he has been made poor by legitimate cause or is bound by debts. Moreover, the aforesaid sciences \[i.e., the philosophical sciences\] cannot be sold either by right or in fact. Hence Proverbs 17: “What does it profit a fool to have riches, since he cannot buy wisdom?”

To the first objection it must be said that the bishop ought not to exact a procuration for the consecration of a church as its price, but as something owed for his labor according to the determination of the church.

To the second it must be said that for the celebrating of an anniversary it is not lawful to make a pact about money to be given; however, if money is given to the church out of devotion, the church is bound to celebrate \[Mass\] for those on whose behalf it is given, according to how it sees itself more obligated in view of the benefit received.

To the third it must be said that for correction or visitation it is not lawful to receive money as the price of those acts, but only as something owed for sustentation by the ordinance of the church.

To the fourth, the solution follows from what has been said in the question.

To the fifth it must be said that it is not lawful for a judge to sell judgment, since he is bound to this by his office; nor \[may he sell\] testimony, since anyone who is questioned in the proper place and time is bound to bear witness to the truth. Hence those who sell such things sin gravely, and if these things have a spiritual bearing they are simoniacs. An advocate, however, may sell his advocacy, and a legal expert his counsel, when they do not receive a salary from a judge or court that would oblige them to these services without additional payment. Nor can it be said that such persons sell justice or truth, but rather their labor and diligence.

To the sixth it must be said that when the possessions of a religious house do not suffice to sustain more than those already there, it is lawful for one wishing to serve God with them to make known, without any pact, that unless he were to bring something to live on, both he and the others would suffer want, by which they might in some way be impeded from the divine office. But if the monastery is well-off, it is not lawful to say this to one who wishes to enter.
