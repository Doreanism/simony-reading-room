A cleric who serves a bishop in hope of future reward does not commit simony if he serves without any pact or condition, provided that this is not his principal intention, and that the bishop does not directly assign him a prebend in return for that service \[Extra, de simonia, *Cum essent*, in the gloss\]. But if he serves principally with the intention of obtaining some ecclesiastical benefit, yet without any pact whatsoever intervening — although he sins gravely and incurs mental simony — he does not incur that simony which obliges him to resign an ecclesiastical benefice obtained simoniacally, as may be proved by analogy from what is found \[Extra, de simonia, *Mandato vestro*\].

## Article IV.

### The Fourth Principal Question Is Raised Next

The fourth principal question is raised next. And in this connection three things are asked.

First, whether the female sex impedes the reception of orders.

Second, whether infancy of age \[does so\].

Third, whether defect of birth \[does so\].

## Question I.

### Whether the Female Sex Impedes the Reception of Orders

It seems that it does not. It is Christ who principally ordains; but as it is said in Galatians 3: in Christ Jesus there is neither male nor female — therefore a woman can be ordained just as a man can.

Again, at Decree, d. 32, *Presbyter* and the chapter *Mulieres*, mention is made of a female presbyter (*presbytera*); and at 27, q. 1, it is said that a deaconess (*diaconissa*) ought not to be ordained before the age of forty — therefore women can receive the orders of deacon and presbyter.

Again, women can have the act of prophesying. Thus Luke 2: Anna was a prophetess — therefore, by analogy, women can be ordained.

On the contrary, at Decree, d. 23, *Sacratas*: it has been reported to the Apostolic See that women consecrated to God, or nuns, handle the sacred vessels and the sacred altar-cloths and carry incense around the altars — all of which, no man of right judgment can doubt, are full of reproach and censure.
