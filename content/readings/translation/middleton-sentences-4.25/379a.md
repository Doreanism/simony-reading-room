What Augustinus says, they understand as spoken of heretics who have been separated from the truth of the faith and from the unity of doctrine not by the sentence of the church but by the perversity of their own opinion; such men, though they are of that character, nonetheless retain the right to ordain and to consecrate. And those who are ordained by them before open separation — even if they have openly departed with them and have been condemned by the sentence of the church — need not be ordained again upon returning. And wherever it is read that those ordained by heretics may minister with their orders preserved, or that they are not to be ordained again, they say this is to be understood of persons of this kind. For after heretics have been cut off and condemned by the judgment of the church, they hold that the right to ordain and consecrate has been taken from them, just as from those degraded or excommunicated.

Others, however, say that sacraments celebrated by heretics and those who have been cut off, according to the form of the church, are true and valid, because those who depart from the church have not lost the right to give and to consecrate. Those who are thus ordained by heretics are not to be ordained again when they return. But those things done by heretics otherwise than in the church are false and empty, and those who appear to be ordained by such persons receive not a gift but a wound. According to this distinction, the doctors speak variously about these matters.

Certain others say that the same sacraments can be celebrated by heretics who have been cut off as by Catholics — provided that the form of the church is preserved by them — and that those celebrated by them are true and valid as regards themselves; but as regards their effect, they are false and empty, and are rendered void and false in those who handle them badly and in those who receive them badly, because they do not confer what they promise and are believed to convey. They are also said to be condemned because, for those who give or receive them illicitly, they are unto judgment. They are also said to be polluted — not as regards themselves, but on account of the unworthy handling by heretics. Hence Gregorius calls the communion of Arrius an execration, and Innocentius calls the ordination of Bonosus a condemnation: not because they are such in themselves, but because they make those who give or receive them badly into such persons. So too Hieronymus calls their sacrifices “bread of mourning” — not as regards themselves, but as regards their effect.

Some others hold that those heretics who were ordained in the church retain the right to ordain and to consecrate even after they have separated. Those, however, who have been ordained and anointed by them while in schism or heresy lack that right. And therefore when such \[latter ones\] wish to ordain, they inflict a wound rather than confer grace.

## On Simony: whence the term derives, and what it is.

Concerning simoniacs, there is no doubt that they are heretics; yet before the sentence of degradation, they both ordain and consecrate. And although “simoniacs” properly refers to those who, in the manner of Simon Magus, wish to purchase priceless grace with money — while those who receive payment for sacred ministry are to be called “Gehazites” in the manner of Gehazi — nonetheless all, both givers and receivers, are called simoniacs, and both are struck by the same sentence.
