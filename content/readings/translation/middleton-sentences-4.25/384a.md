## Article III

Next we inquire concerning the third \[article\]. First, whether simony is committed in the buying or selling of the sacraments. Second, whether in the buying or selling of any spiritual things whatsoever. Third, whether in the buying or selling of things annexed to spiritual matters.

### Question I

Whether simony is committed in the buying or selling of the sacraments.

It seems not: for although temporal things cannot be the price of spiritual things, one spiritual thing can be the price of another; therefore it seems that it is not unlawful to purchase one sacrament with another.

Furthermore, the Mass consists principally in the most exalted sacrament of the Eucharist; yet it is lawful to give something temporal for the celebration of Mass. For as is found in Extra, de præbendis, “Significatum est”: certain canons gave a priest a prebend in their church so that he might celebrate Mass of the Blessed Virgin daily, and the Pope confirmed that arrangement.

Furthermore, election to a bishopric is a spiritual matter, and the episcopate itself is something sacramental; yet as is found in Extra, de electione, “Ut præteritæ,” the Pope declares: “We ordain that the necessary expenses which the chapter of Constantinople, or its representatives, shall have incurred for the business of an election during a vacancy, shall be borne entirely by the goods of the patriarchate, or shall be reimbursed from them.”

Furthermore, matrimony is a sacrament; yet money is lawfully given for marriage by express agreement.

Furthermore, sacramental absolution is something sacramental, yet officials commonly receive money in order to absolve from excommunication.

Furthermore, the episcopate is something sacramental; yet it is lawful for one elected to a bishopric to give money to his adversary so that he may desist from opposing his election.
