## Reply to the First Argument

To the first argument it must be said that the apostle’s authority \[1 Corinthians 7:21–22\] is to be understood with respect to merit, not with respect to office.

To the second it must be said that the cases are not alike, because in conferring other sacraments upon slaves no prejudice is done to their lords, since they can preserve in themselves the dignity and reverence of those sacraments and still serve their lords.

To the third it must be said that the cases are not alike, because the servitude of a man cannot be laid aside by the slave at his own will, whereas the slave of the devil can become free whenever he wishes, with God’s help. It can also be said that a cleric, so long as he is in mortal sin, ought not to be ordained; and one who knowingly ordains such a person sins — except in a case where his sin is hidden and the bishop is bound to ordain him.

The arguments on the other side proceed from the reception of orders as a matter of right.

## Question II.

Whether a notable defect of a bodily member impedes the reception of orders.

And it seems that it does not: affliction should not be heaped upon one already afflicted; therefore, on account of a defect of a member — which is itself a kind of affliction — a man ought not to be impeded from the reception of orders.

Again, for the reception of orders integrity of discernment is more required than integrity of body; but some can be ordained before the age of discernment; therefore they are not impeded from the reception of orders on account of a notable defect of a member.

On the contrary, in *Extra*, “De corpore vitiatis,” *Exposuisti*, it is said of one whose hand has been mutilated that he cannot be promoted to holy orders.

## Conclusion

One who is notably defective in body is judged unfit for promotion to orders, out of reverence for the sacrament.
