## Question II.

*Whether infancy of age impedes the reception of orders.*

It seems that it does. Decree, d. 77: a subdeacon of fewer than twenty years shall not be ordained; and in the following chapter, *Placuit*, it is said that deacons shall not be ordained before the age of twenty-five, nor shall virgins be consecrated, nor shall lectors greet the people.

Again, according to the Philosopher in the book *On Sleep and Waking*: the act belongs to the same \[subject\] as the potency — but the act of orders cannot belong to infants; therefore neither can the power.

Again, continence is annexed to sacred orders; but infants cannot be obligated to continence, because no one is obligated to this except voluntarily — therefore they cannot receive orders.

Again, just as a form is not received except in disposed matter, so too orders are not received except in a disposed soul; but the disposition in the soul for receiving orders is consent — therefore infants cannot receive orders, since they cannot consent.

On the contrary: one ordained in minor orders below the age of discretion is not afterwards re-ordained \[Extra, de clerico per sal. promoto\] — which would not be true unless he had received a true order.

Again, boys can receive other sacraments that impress a character, such as baptism and confirmation — therefore, by analogy, \[they can receive\] this one as well, since it imprints a character.

### Conclusion.

*Infancy of age, or defect of the use of reason, impedes neither the reception of minor nor of major orders.*
