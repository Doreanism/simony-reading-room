I answer that among bodily defects some are so notable that they impede the execution of office — for example, the cutting off of the thumb and the like — and these prohibit the reception of orders as a matter of right. Others notably obscure the personal dignity of the candidate — for example, the cutting off of the nose and the like — and such defects likewise impede the reception of orders as a matter of right, for the canons prohibit such persons from being ordained on account of the reverence and dignity owed to the sacrament. Hence Leviticus 21 \[:17–18\] says: “A man who has a blemish shall not offer the bread of his God, nor shall he approach his ministry” — in which it was prefigured that in those to be promoted to the priesthood of the new law, attention must be paid not only that they be free from blemish of soul, but also that they be free from notable blemish of body, which could detract from the reverence owed to orders. Hence one who has had an eye gouged out, whether through his own fault or not, is prohibited from ordination \[*Decretum*, dist. 55, *Si evangelica*\]. Defects of members that do not produce notable deformity of the face or hands, if not brought about through one’s own fault, induce no irregularity. Hence \[*Decretum*\] same dist., *Eunuchus*: a eunuch, if he has been made such by the treachery of men, or if in persecution his virile members were amputated, or if he was born so, and is worthy, may be made a bishop; and in the following chapter, *Si quis*: if anyone has had natural members excised by physicians on account of illness. Similarly, those castrated by barbarians or by their lords who are found worthy in character — the canon permits these to be promoted to the clergy. But if one mutilates himself voluntarily without just cause, he is to be repelled from the reception of orders. Hence those who cut off their virile members to repress temptations of the flesh are irregular \[same dist., *Si quis absciderit*\], for such a person has no just cause for his castration, since carnal vice is to be resisted by virtue \[same dist., *Hi qui se*\]. In the same distinction it is also said: One who willfully cuts off any part of a finger — the canons do not admit him to the clergy. Thus it is clear that there are many bodily defects which, if they arise through the fault of the person, induce irregularity, and if they arise without his fault do not render him irregular; and many which render the person irregular regardless of the cause from which they arise. Yet none of the aforesaid defects impedes the reception of orders as a matter of fact: for if the rites that ought to be performed for one being ordained are performed in respect of an irregular person, he truly receives the order, which just as he ought not to have received, so also he ought not to exercise once received.
