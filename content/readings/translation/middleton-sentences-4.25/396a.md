### QUESTION II.

*Whether those infamous by infamy of canon law ought to be barred from the reception of orders even after completing penance.*

AND IT SEEMS that they should not: for in the gloss on *Extra, de testibus, Non debet* \[X 2.20.2\], it is said that testimony in criminal matters, accusation, and the reception of orders proceed in step; but infamy of canon law is abolished by penance; therefore such persons, after completing penance, are not to be barred from the reception of orders.

Likewise, at *C.6 q.1, Illi qui* \[Decretum C.6 q.1 c.22\], it is said of such persons that they are marked with infamy before they are healed by priestly authority; therefore it appears that after penance they cease to be infamous, and if so, they are not to be barred from the reception of orders.

ON THE OTHER SIDE: one who is infamous for perjury is infamous by infamy of canon law, and yet when he has made amends for such a crime he is not admitted to give testimony, neither in a criminal cause nor in a civil cause — *Extra, de testibus, Testimonium* \[X 2.20.37\], in the text and in the gloss — therefore neither is he to be admitted to the reception of orders, since, as the gloss on that same chapter states: the reputation of one to be ordained must be without any blemish.

Likewise, one infamous by infamy of canon law is not, even after amendment, received as a witness in a criminal cause; but, as was said above, testimony in a criminal matter, accusation, and the reception of orders proceed in step; therefore one infamous by infamy of canon law is to be barred from the reception of orders even after completing penance.

### CONCLUSION

*Indeed, if this infamy was imposed by a judge through a definitive sentence and has not yet been abolished by one having authority to do so, the case is different from one who incurred infamy by operation of law alone through a mortal sin that either does not entail deposition, or, if it does, is occult.*

I RESPOND that those infamous by infamy of canon law who are nonetheless not infamous by infamy of civil law either contracted that infamy by operation of law, or had it imposed upon them by a definitive sentence of a judge. If in the second manner, I do not believe that the infamy is abolished by penance in such a way that they may be ordained as a matter of right; rather it must be abolished by full restoration \[*restitutio in integrum*\] by one who has the power to do so.
