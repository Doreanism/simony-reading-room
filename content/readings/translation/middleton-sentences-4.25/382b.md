### Reply to the second

To the second it must also be said that by resignation a bishop cannot deprive himself of all \[dignities\] of which he could be deprived by degradation. For although ordinary or delegated jurisdiction can be conferred by will and word alone, the consecration of episcopal dignity is not conferred by will and word alone — rather, an act is required, *extra de conse. ecclesiæ. A qua in gl.* \[*Decretals*, on the consecration of a church, *A qua*, in the gloss\]. I say by analogy: just as bishops can be deprived of things pertaining to jurisdiction by will and word alone, yet they cannot be deprived of episcopal dignity by will and word alone.

## Article II

Next we inquire concerning the second principal matter. And regarding this, three questions are posed. First, whether simony is a deliberate will to sell or buy something spiritual or something annexed to the spiritual.

Second, whether simony is fittingly named.

Third, whether simoniacs are irregular. As to whether they are heretics, this was examined above, at Distinction 3.

### Question I.

*Whether simony is a deliberate will to buy or sell something spiritual or something annexed to the spiritual.*

It seems not: because a deliberate will with respect to evil is called malice, but a person does not always commit simony out of malice — sometimes it is out of weakness or ignorance. Therefore “deliberate will” is poorly placed in the definition of simony.

Again, spiritual things cannot be possessed by money. Hence, as is found in Acts 8, when Simon Magus wished to buy the gift of the Holy Spirit, Peter said to him: “Thy money perish with thee, because thou hast thought that the gift of God may be purchased with money.” Therefore, since, as found in *Ethics* III, ch. 5, choice is not of impossibilities, and deliberate will involves choice, it is poorly said that simony is a deliberate will to sell or buy spiritual things.

On the contrary, *prima quaestio prima, Qui studet* \[*Decretum*, C. 1 q. 1, *Qui studet*\]: the aforesaid definition is implicitly contained therein.

### Conclusion.

*This description of simony is indeed fitting.*

I reply that the aforesaid description of simony is apt: it contains the principal act in the phrase “deliberate will,” that is, an act of the will carried through to effect according to the gloss there; and \[it contains\] the exterior act, namely the selling and buying; and the object, which is the spiritual or the annexed to the spiritual.
