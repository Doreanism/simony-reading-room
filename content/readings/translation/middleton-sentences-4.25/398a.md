Moreover, sometimes persons are stigmatized with infamy of deed through no fault of their own; yet because such infamy creates some presumption against the defamed person, legitimate acts are therefore forbidden to them until they have cleared their infamy by canonical purgation. For that infamy of deed which is more properly called defamation — and by which a person is more properly said to be defamed rather than infamous — is abolished by purgation to the extent that law requires. Decree, 15 q. 5, “De crimine,” and Extra, de purgatione canonica, “Ex tuarum.” Hence, after such purgation, they are not to be barred from receiving orders, although frequently, even after canonical purgation, their reputation remains tarnished in the opinion or estimation of men.

## The First Two Arguments for the First Position

do not conclude more than this: that legitimate acts are not forbidden by law itself to those who bear only infamy of deed — and this has been conceded. Hence, if such persons were ordained, they would not be suspended by law itself from the exercise of their orders; yet in terms of practical rule they would not have much force, since many things impede a candidate for promotion that do not depose one already promoted.

## To the Third

When it is said that to remove infamy of deed belongs to God alone, etc., this is to be understood with respect to men’s opinion or estimation, because as far as the law is concerned it is removed by canonical purgation, so that on account of the said infamy the person is not to be barred from any legitimate act.

## The Arguments for the Second Position

Those who are accused of a crime while their accusation is pending, and those who have been condemned for a crime and have appealed against an unjust sentence while their appeal is pending, are not to be barred from accusation or testimony in a civil or criminal case when the matter is being handled civilly, if they have made amends for the crime of which they were accused: Extra, de testibus \[on witnesses\], “Non debet,” in the text, but more explicitly in the gloss. Likewise, those whose reputation has been burdened because they were at some point repelled from giving testimony by means of an exception are not to be barred from accusation or testimony in a civil or criminal case when the matter is being handled civilly, if they have made amends for the crime. Supra, eo. tit., “Testimonium,” in the text and in the gloss.
