## Question III.

Whether homicide impedes the reception of orders.

And it seems that it does not: Exodus 32 \[:29\] says that Moses said to the Levites: “You have consecrated your hands today to the Lord, each man in his son and in his brother, so that a blessing may be given to you.” But as the Master says in the preceding distinction, chapter 7 \[*Sentences* IV, dist. 24\], the order of the diaconate took its name from the tribe of Levi; therefore it seems that deacons may lawfully kill men for just cause, as it was lawful for the Levites.

Again, in *Decretum*, dist. 50, *Quia te*, it is said of one who, captured by Saracens, appeared to have killed men — that because he is recognized to have done this not of his own will, he shall in no way be judged canonically on that account; therefore not every homicide impedes the reception of orders.

Again, no penalty is owed for the act of virtue; but sometimes a man in killing another performs an act of the virtue of justice, as when a judge hangs a thief; therefore for such a homicide he does not incur the penalty of irregularity.

Again, for that which a man is bound to do by necessity of salvation, he ought in no way to be repelled from the reception of orders; but a man placed in a situation of necessity — that he must either kill or be killed — is bound by necessity to kill, if he can, because the precept of charity is to love oneself more than one’s neighbor; therefore such a homicide does not impede the reception of orders.

Again, a man who brings a criminal charge against a thief before a secular judge — not criminally, however, but civilly — does not become irregular if the secular judge, on account of such a complaint, proceeds to a harsher penalty, such as the death penalty \[*Extra*, “De homicidio,” *Postulasti*, in the text and in the gloss\]; yet it is through the one who lodged the complaint that the man is killed; therefore not every homicide impedes the reception of orders.

Again, a man sometimes kills a man by accident; but accidental homicide does not render a man irregular, because, as is said in *Extra*, “De homicidio,” last chapter \[*Sicut*\]: one who perhaps did not foresee all the unforeseen accidents that could not have been anticipated ought not, on account of a homicide thus accidentally committed, to be impeded with respect to office or benefice.

Again, nothing is imputed to those lacking the use of free will for what they do, according to Bernard in *De libero arbitrio* before the middle \[of that work\]; therefore if a child, or a man in a fit of madness, or one sleeping, or one drunk kills a man, they are not for this reason irregular.

On the contrary, *Decretum*, C. 15, q. 1, *Si quis infaniens*: “Those things that are done in a state of alienated mind, even if they are not imputed as a penalty, nevertheless impede the execution of sacred office”; such persons therefore are irregular, and much more so any other homicides.
