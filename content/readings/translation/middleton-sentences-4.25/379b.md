## On those who say they are purchasing material things, not spiritual things.

If any should object that they are purchasing not the consecrations themselves but the physical things from which the consecrations proceed, they are proved to be utterly foolish. For whoever sells either of the two, without which the other cannot be had, does not cease from selling both.

## The classification of simoniacs.

The heresy of simony is divided into three kinds. For some are ordained simoniacally by simoniacs, others simoniacally by non-simoniacs, and others non-simoniacally by simoniacs. Hence Pope Nicolaus \[II\]: We establish a decree concerning the threefold simoniacal heresy, that is, concerning simoniacs who simoniacally ordain or are ordained, concerning simoniacs who are ordained simoniacally by non-simoniacs, and concerning simoniacs who are ordained non-simoniacally by simoniacs. Simoniacs who are ordained simoniacally, or who ordain simoniacally, shall be deposed from their proper rank according to the canons. Simoniacs who are also ordained simoniacally by non-simoniacs shall likewise be removed from office. Those simoniacs, however, who are ordained non-simoniacally by simoniacs, we mercifully permit to remain in office by the laying on of hands, in view of the necessity of the times. This is to be understood of those who are ordained by simoniacs in ignorance of their being simoniacs. What makes them simoniacs is not the guilt of the crime but the ordination \[performed\] by a simoniac.

## Pope Alexander on those who are ordained by force by simoniacs or heretics.

Likewise, when Pope Alexander \[II\] decrees that simoniacs are altogether to be condemned and deposed, it is to be understood as implying: unless someone has been drawn in by violence. For concerning those ordained by force even by any kind of heretics, Innocentius \[I\] says that they may have some ground for excuse, if they depart from them immediately and renounce their wicked assembly.

## On the age of those to be ordained, thus decreed by Pope Nicolaus.

The sacred canons, he says, have ordained that a subdeacon is not to be ordained before the age of fourteen, nor a deacon before twenty-five, nor a presbyter before thirty. Thereafter, if he shall be worthy, he may be elected to the episcopate, which we likewise command to be observed in the same manner. Item, Fabianus \[I\]: If anyone has not completed thirty years of age, he is by no means to be ordained a presbyter, even if he is very worthy — for the Lord himself was baptized at thirty years of age, and thus began to teach.
