Hence Augustinus writes: What certain people say concerning heretics who have even been condemned — that one who departs from the church does not lose the baptism he received, but loses the right to give what he has received — is shown to be vainly said in many ways. First, because no reason is shown why the one who cannot lose that baptism can lose the right to give it. For each is a sacrament, and each is conferred upon a person by a kind of consecration: the one when he is baptized, the other when he is ordained. Therefore it is not lawful for either to be repeated in the Catholic Church. For if at any time those coming over from the schismatic party, even their superiors, have been received for the sake of the good of peace after the error of schism was corrected, even if it seemed necessary for them to exercise the same offices they had previously held, they were not ordained again. But just as baptism remained in them, so also their ordination remained intact, because the fault had been in the separation — which is corrected in the unity of peace — and not in the sacraments, which, wherever they are, are true in themselves. And even when it seems expedient to the church that their superiors coming to Catholic fellowship should not exercise their honors there, yet the sacraments of ordination themselves are not taken away from them but remain upon them. And therefore hands are not laid upon them again: lest the injury be done not to the man, but to the sacrament itself. Now just as in baptism there is a right that can be conferred through them, so in ordination there is a right of conferring: each, to be sure, held to their own perdition. But it is one thing not to have \[something\], another to have it perniciously, and another to have it salutarily. The same doctor writes: Concerning those who have been separated from the unity of the church, there is no question but that they both have and can give \[the sacraments\], yet they have them perniciously and give them perniciously, because they are outside the bond of peace. Injury is to be done to neither sacrament. Just as one who departs from unity does not rightly have \[baptism\], yet nonetheless has it, and therefore it is not given again to one returning: so also one who departs from unity does not rightly give \[orders\], and yet gives, and therefore what is received from him is not repeated for one coming to unity. The same: It is one thing not to have something, another to have it unlawfully or to usurp it illicitly. Therefore the sacraments of Christ and the church are not \[rendered\] non-existent because not only heretics but all the impious use them illicitly; rather, those persons are to be corrected and punished, while the sacraments are to be acknowledged and venerated. Item, Gregorius \[I\]: What you say, that one who has been ordained should be ordained again, is quite ridiculous; for just as one who has once been baptized should not be baptized again, so one who has once been consecrated cannot validly be consecrated again in the same order. By these and other authorities it appears to be asserted that in all the impious — even in heretics who have been cut off and condemned — the sacraments of Christ remain with the right of conferring them. For they can confer them, but perniciously; and those upon whom they have conferred them are not to be ordained again. These assertions appear to stand in direct opposition to the foregoing.

## Resolution of the aforesaid contradiction of authorities.

Certain doctors resolve this matter as follows: they say that heretics who depart from the church after having received sacerdotal or episcopal anointing retain indeed the right to confer baptism, but do not have the capacity to confer holy orders or to consecrate the Lord’s body, once they have been cut off and condemned by the church.
