Hence the Apostle, Romans 11: “If \[it is\] by God’s grace, then not by works; otherwise grace is no longer grace.” But although this solution contains truth, it does not answer the argument, because one sacrament could be the price of the same kind of sacrament if they were saleable things. Hence, were there no other obstacle, two adults to be baptized could make this agreement between themselves: “I promise you that I will baptize you on this condition, and afterward you will baptize me” — yet it would not be lawful to do so, because in such matters every compact or agreement ought to cease. Extra, de simonia, “Tua nos.”

It must be said, therefore, in answer to the argument that, although giving something spiritual for a sacrament by agreement is not unlawful for as many reasons as it is unlawful to give money by agreement for such things, it is nonetheless unlawful for two reasons: both because a thing is being purchased from one who is not its lord, and because it is contrary to the nature of grace that it be sold for any price whatsoever. However, to say “I will pray for you” and “I ask that you pray for me,” and the like, is in no way blameworthy; for this is not selling or buying a spiritual thing for a spiritual thing, since here there is no compact.

To the second objection it must be said that it is not lawful to give anything by agreement as the price of a Mass; if, however, a priest does not have the resources for which he would be bound to celebrate Mass by reason of his office, and has none from any other source, he may accept money for his labor toward the sustenance of his life; nor does the decretal cited \[above\] count against the foregoing, because there was no compact there, but rather that office of singing such a Mass was annexed to the prebend.

To the third objection it must be said that it is one thing to receive the necessary expenses for the labor involved in conducting the business of an election, and another to receive money for the election itself. The former is lawful, especially when it is received not by compact but by the authority of law, which has ordained that it may be received.

To the fourth objection it must be said that matrimony is not only a sacrament but also a function of nature. Hence insofar as it is a sacrament, consisting in the administration of the ministers of the Church, it does not fall under sale. Therefore whoever receives money exacted for the blessing of the nuptials commits simony. But for matrimony insofar as it is a function of nature, it is lawful to give and receive a price and to introduce a condition regarding a temporal thing to be received or given, just as for other civil and bodily functions.

To the fifth objection it must be said that it is not lawful to receive money for the act of absolution as the price of absolution; but payment of money can be enjoined upon the one who is absolved as a penalty for sin. The one who absolves must, however, take care lest he be more intent in this on serving avarice than on correction.
