It can also be said that the perfection of sin lies in choice, even though a person is sometimes drawn toward it by ignorance or weakness; therefore simony is said to be a deliberate will, so that it is understood to be a will that is not inadvertent but one that chooses.

To the second it must be said that a spiritual thing is called such in two ways. In one way, essentially — such as grace and the virtues — and with respect to such things there can be no contract of sale or purchase. In another way, causally or dispositively — and in this sense the sacraments are spiritual, insofar as they are an instrumentally disposing cause toward grace and the virtues. And these, insofar as they are certain corporeal things, can be sold in fact, even though they cannot be sold in law; and it is in this sense that “spiritual things” are understood in the definition of simony.

## Question II

Whether simony is fittingly named.

It seems not: because, as is found in Acts 8, Simon Magus did not wish to sell spiritual things but to buy them. But as is found in 4 Kings \[2 Kings\] ch. 5, Gehazi sold spiritual things to Naaman the Syrian. Therefore it ought to be called “Gehazism” no less than “simony.”

On the contrary, simony is commonly named as such in law.

### Conclusion

This name rightfully claims the thing for itself.

I reply that it is more fittingly named simony than Gehazism, because the vice of buying and selling, as regards the will, was present in Simon Magus: for he wished to buy the gift of the Holy Spirit with money in order afterward to sell it to others. If, however, we wish to speak precisely and by proper attribution, we can call those who sell spiritual things “Gehazites,” and those who buy them “simoniacs.” And so it is clear what must be said to the arguments.

## Question III

Whether simoniacs are irregular.

It seems not. Giving money for the sacraments without a pact, and after the reception of the sacrament, does not make a person irregular; but one who does this is a simoniac. For Gehazi received money from Naaman without any pact and after he had been healed; therefore not every simoniac is irregular.

Again, as has been established in Book II, invincible ignorance entirely excuses. But it can happen that someone is ordained or obtains an ecclesiastical benefice through simony while he is invincibly ignorant of the fact — as when someone secretly gives money to the one ordaining or to the prelate, so that he may confer an order or benefice on another person who is unaware of it. Therefore such a person does not become irregular.
