### Ad primum in oppositum

TO THE FIRST objection on the other side, it must be said that the canonical infamy of which it is said at *C.6 q.1, Omnes vero* \[Decretum C.6 q.1 c.21\], in the gloss, and in the following chapter in the text and gloss, is abolished by penance — this is incurred through some mortal sin of which the sinner has not been condemned by sentence, and which does not entail deposition, or, if it does entail deposition, is nonetheless occult, so that it cannot be proved against the sinner, and no infamy of fact \[*infamia facti*\] has arisen against the sinner on account of it; and such a criminal person is restored to lawful acts by penance. This you have expressly at *Extra, de temporibus ordinandorum*, the final chapter \[X 1.11.12\].

From what has been said, it is clear what must be said in response to the second.

### Ad primum ad partem secundam

TO THE FIRST objection on the second side, it must be said that one who has made amends for a crime, if infamy still attends him after penance, is not to be received as a witness, neither in a criminal nor in a civil cause. But one who deliberately and with deliberation commits perjury is infamous by infamy of law — *Extra, de testibus, Testimonium* \[X 2.20.37\], in the gloss; *C. de transactionibus, Si quis maior* \[Cod. 2.4.38\]; and *C.6 q.1, Infames* \[Decretum C.6 q.1 c.6\] — and this mark of infamy is not abolished by penance. This is reasonable, because, as the Apostle says at Hebrews 6, the oath is the end of all human controversy and confirmation thereof \[Heb. 6:16\]; therefore the doors of dignity must be closed to the perjurer until he has been fully restored by one who has the power to do so.

And furthermore, concerning one who has once been convicted of knowingly committing perjury, it is presumed that he would easily perjure himself on another occasion — both because of the slipperiness of the tongue, and because he is presumed to be inconstant in confessing the truth.

## Quaestio III

Whether one infamous by infamy of fact ought to be barred from the reception of orders.

AND IT SEEMS that he should not: because that which does not bar the exercise of an office does not seem to bar the reception of orders; but infamy of fact does not bar the exercise of the office of prelacy — which I prove as follows: as is said in the gloss on *Extra, de testibus, Non debet* \[X 2.20.2\], when someone is accused of a crime, his reputation is at once impaired, and yet, as the same gloss goes on to say, while the accusation is pending he remains in his office, and in the meantime his subjects are bound to obey him.
