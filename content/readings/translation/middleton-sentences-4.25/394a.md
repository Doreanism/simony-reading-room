Homicide that is partly voluntary and partly involuntary occurs when someone, placed in the necessity of being killed or killing, kills: for he does not absolutely will to kill, yet he wills to kill lest he be killed. Such a person is irregular. Decree, dist. 50, “De his clericis pro quibus” \[Concerning those clerics on whose behalf\], and “De his clericis qui in obsidionis” \[Concerning those clerics who in a siege\]; yet dispensation should readily be granted in such cases. Likewise, those who kill a man not by intention but by accident, if they did not exercise due diligence, commit homicide that is partly involuntary — insofar as they voluntarily omitted to exercise due diligence — and partly non-voluntary, insofar as they did not absolutely will to kill; and such a person is irregular. Decree, dist. 50, “Si quis voluntate,” § “Si autem,” and “Eos vero,” and “Clerico.” Nor is only the one who kills to be counted irregular, but also one who mutilates a man in a member. Dist. 55, last chapter \[*ultimum capitulum*\]. For such a person is an enemy of the condition of God, same distinction, “Si quis absciderit.” Yet this irregularity is not, all else being equal, as grave as that contracted through homicide.

## To the First \[Objection\]

To the first objection in opposition, it must be said that the case of clerics under the old law and the new is not analogous: for the old law inflicted the penalty of blood, because it was a law of fear; not so the new law, because it is a law of love.

## To the Second \[Objection\]

To the second objection, it must be said that, according to the gloss there, that text is to be understood according to canonical rigor, because such a person is mercifully tolerated — by which is indicated that he stands in need of dispensation, though dispensation should readily be granted to him.

Or it may be said that he had acted out of unavoidable necessity and with the restraint of blameless self-defense \[*cum moderamine inculpatae tutelae*\]; and one who kills in this manner incurs neither sin nor irregularity.

## To the Third \[Objection\]

To the third objection, it must be said that this penalty — namely, irregularity — can be incurred even through a virtuous act, because through a virtuous act a man may perform a deed that represents cruelty, and on account of that representation he is rendered more unfit to represent the gentleness of Christ.

## To the Fourth \[Objection\]

To the fourth objection, it must be said that in the aforesaid case a man is not bound of necessity to kill, though he may; he is bound to defend himself with the restraint of blameless self-defense, and if death of the other follows from this, it is not imputed to him as sin, nor as the penalty of irregularity. If, however, he strikes in order to kill and death follows — even though he could not have freed himself otherwise — he is irregular, but dispensation should readily be granted to him.
