## Article VI.

Next, inquiry is made concerning the sixth principal \[question\]. And on this point three things are asked: First, whether those notorious \[*infames*\] by civil law should be excluded from reception of orders even after completed penance. Second, whether those notorious by canon law. Third, whether those notorious by fact.

### Question I

*Whether those notorious by civil law should be excluded from reception of orders.*

It seems not: for it does not pertain to the temporal judge to impose an ecclesiastical penalty; but to be excluded from reception of orders is an ecclesiastical penalty: therefore no one should be excluded from reception of orders on account of infamy under civil law.

Likewise, one condemned for theft is notorious under civil law. Hence it is said in the *Digest*, de furtis \[on thefts\]: “The provincial governor cannot bring it about that infamy does not follow one condemned for theft”; but if such a person amends his life and becomes a good man, there seems to be no reason why he should be excluded from reception of orders.

On the other hand, Extra, de testibus, “Testimonium” \[*Testimony*\], in the gloss, it is said of those condemned for theft and the like: that although we can save their souls through penance, we cannot abolish the infamy; and thus penance does not benefit them in this respect, because legitimate acts are forbidden to the notorious.

Likewise, at C. 2 q. 3, “Euphemium,” § “Hic autem colligitur” \[*Here, moreover, it is gathered*\], it is said that the words of Gelasius and Stephen — “although we can save souls through penance, we cannot abolish infamy” — and \[the statement\] that we receive them under the mark of perpetual infamy for penance, are to be understood of those upon whom infamy is inflicted by a civil judge. From this I argue as before.

### Conclusion

*All those who are notorious by civil law and canon law together are absolutely to be excluded from reception of orders.*
