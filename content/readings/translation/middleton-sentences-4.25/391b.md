ON THE OTHER HAND, Decree, Distinction 54, *Nulli*: *Let no one of servile condition be promoted to holy orders unless he has first obtained lawful freedom from his own masters.*

Furthermore, Extra, *de servis non ordinandis* \[Decretals, on slaves not to be ordained\], *De servorum*: *It has been decreed regarding the ordination of slaves that no bishop shall henceforth presume to promote them to holy orders.*

Furthermore, Decree, Distinction 54, *Si quis*: *If anyone is bound by servile tribute or by any condition or patronage of any lord, he is not to be ordained as a cleric unless he shall have been of proven life and the consent of the patron has been obtained.* But no one can receive any order without being a cleric; therefore servitude impedes the reception of any order whatsoever.

### Conclusion

*Servitude does not in fact remove the reception of orders, but is solely the cause why holy orders cannot by law be received.*

I REPLY that servitude does not impede the reception of any order as a matter of fact; for if \[a slave\] is ordained, he truly receives orders. Yet it does impede the reception of any order by right. For the laws prohibit such persons from being ordained unless they have first been restored to freedom, or unless it is done with the consent of their masters; for if the masters consent, they thereby become free by that very fact, and consent is presumed unless they know of it and are able to object. Decree, Distinction 54, *Si servus sciente*. For in the reception of orders a man is bound to divine service, and because a slave does not have power over himself, since he belongs to his master — for as the Philosopher says in the first book of the *Politics*, chapter three: *A slave is an animate thing possessed* — he cannot give himself to ecclesiastical service, because no one can give what is not his own. This reason is touched upon implicitly in Decree, Distinction 54, chapter 1, where it is said: *He who is to be enlisted in the divine militia must be free from other obligations, so that he may not be drawn away from the Lord’s camps, to which his name is enrolled, by the bonds of any necessity.* But if a slave is ordained in fact without the knowledge of his master, and the ordainer and the presenter being ignorant that he is a slave: then, if he has been constituted only in minor orders, he is recalled into slavery and returned to his master. Decree, Distinction 54, *Nulli*, and chapter *Ex antiquis*. A deacon, however, shall provide a substitute; if he cannot provide one to his master, he shall be returned to his master.
