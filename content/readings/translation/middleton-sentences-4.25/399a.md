Nor does he incur irregularity as a simoniac who promises a certain cleric’s father that he will assist the father’s son, if the father desists from entering into some agreement that he had intended to enter into; nor is the cleric who obtained a benefice through such assistance bound to resign that benefice after it becomes clear to him that he obtained it in the aforementioned manner — for the said agreement, by which his helper made \[a deal\] with the cleric’s father, does not appear to have been unlawful, since even a prelate is permitted to say to someone: “I promise you: I will provide you with a suitable benefice, if by declining from evil works and doing good works you render yourself worthy of an ecclesiastical benefice.” For such a condition is to the honor and reverence of God, and by such a condition the one about to receive the benefice is not bound to anything to which he would not otherwise be bound; hence by the said promise the prelate does not so much bind the one about to receive the benefice as he moves him to do what he is already bound to do by divine law. “Let them fall from their own rank”: this is said of simoniacs who were simoniacally ordained, or of ordainers \[who ordained simoniacally\], who according to the canons fall from their own rank — that is, as regards exercise — even though in reality they retain the character and the rank. “A subdeacon is not to be ordained before \[the age of\] fourteen”: this is stated according to the ancient law, for now he ought not to be ordained before twenty: Decree, dist. 78, “Subdiaconus.” “Nor a priest before thirty”: this rule is not now binding, for a priest may be ordained at twenty-five: Extra, de electione \[on election\], “Cum in cunctis,” § “Inferiora.” Bishops, however, are not to be promoted unless they have completed their thirtieth year, as is stated in the said decree.
