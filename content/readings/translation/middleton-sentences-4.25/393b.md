### Conclusion.

It is entirely fitting that voluntary homicide, and the mutilation of a member, should impede the reception of orders.

I answer that homicide is of four kinds: simply voluntary, simply non-voluntary, simply involuntary, and partly voluntary and partly involuntary.

Simply voluntary homicide impedes the reception of any order as a matter of right. The canons prohibit such persons from being promoted, and those already promoted from ministering in their received orders \[*Decretum*, dist. 50, *Miror*, and the following chapter\]. Three reasons of fittingness can be assigned for this prohibition. The first is that God did not will that David, who had shed much blood, should build him a house, but rather Solomon his son, as is recorded in 2 Samuel 7 \[:5–13\], where the gloss says: “A man of blood is prohibited from building the temple of God.” The second is that a priest represents Christ — who was of supreme gentleness and still is — and the office of the Mass calls to mind his meekness, which was most manifest in this: that he willed to die for our redemption. Homicide, however, is a representation of cruelty; hence a priest, insofar as he is a homicide, represents the killers of Christ more than Christ himself. The third reason is on account of the detestation of the shedding of blood and its horror — on account of which the Lord forbade Noah and his sons to eat flesh with blood, as is recorded in Genesis 9 \[:4\].

Non-voluntary homicide occurs when one lacking the use of reason kills a man, for in such a case there is neither will nor properly speaking unwillingness; and some do not regard such a homicide as irregular, if the person did not fall into the defect of the use of reason through his own fault, which they prove from what is said in *Decretum*, C. 15, q. 1, *Aliquos* — where it is said of one lacking the use of reason who kills a man: “How is one held as guilty who does not know what he has done?” — and they interpret *Si quis infaniens* \[of the same cause and question\] as being about one who has been deprived of the use of reason through his own fault. Others, however, say that such persons are irregular, even though it is not imputed to them as fault or any other penalty, as regards irregularity.

Involuntary homicide occurs when one, intending not to kill, is compelled to do so — as if his hand is taken by force with a sword and a man moves his arm by violence; or if, against one who wishes to kill him, he turns his sword lest that man strike him with a knife, and the other, driven by anger, rushes upon his sword and is struck by it. Such homicide does not induce irregularity, because properly speaking such a person is not a homicide, since in the killing he is not an acting but a passive cause: it is the one who moved the arm by force who kills, and the one who rushes upon the sword kills himself.
