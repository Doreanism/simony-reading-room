Item, preaching is a spiritual good, but temporal stipends may be received for it. Hence the Apostle, 1 Corinthians 9: “If we have sown spiritual things among you, is it a great matter if we reap your carnal things?”

Item, prayer is in the highest degree spiritual, and yet men give alms to the poor who pray for them.

Item, prophecy is a spiritual thing, and yet gifts were brought to the prophets. Hence, as is recorded in 1 Kings 14, the wife of Jeroboam brought ten loaves and a cake and a vessel of honey to Ahijah the prophet when she came to seek an answer of the Lord from him.

ON THE OTHER SIDE: Prima quaestio prima \[Decretum C.1 q.1\]: “Whatever is bestowed by the consolation of invisible grace must never be sold for gain or any kind of reward,” as the Lord says: “Freely you have received, freely give.”

Item, Extra de magistris, ne aliquid exigant, first chapter \[X.5.5.1\]: “Let no one exact a price for the license to teach, nor seek anything under the pretext of any custom from those whom he teaches; and whoever presumes to act against this shall be deprived of any ecclesiastical benefice.” In the chapter immediately following, it is also said: “Forbid that anything be exacted or even promised for the license to teach.” Since this license is something spiritual only in relation to the human spirit, it is all the more unlawful to buy or sell any other spiritual things whatsoever.

## CONCLUSION

One who buys or sells spiritual things ordered to the Holy Spirit without doubt commits simony.

I ANSWER that something may be called spiritual in two ways: either in relation to the Holy Spirit, or in relation to the human spirit. In the first way, some things are spiritual by essence, such as grace and virtues, and these can be sold neither by right nor in fact. Some are spiritual by instrumental cause, such as the sacraments, and these can be sold in fact but not by right, as was established in the preceding question. Some are spiritual quasi-effectively, such as things that befit someone by reason of some spiritual gift or office; these likewise cannot be sold by right, although something may sometimes be received for the labor involved, toward sustaining life.
