## QUESTION III.

*”That certain \[persons\].” §. “It is objected, moreover.” A degraded bishop does not have the power of conferring holy orders; the faculty of baptizing, however, he has not lost.*

Likewise, a prince justly deposed is not a prince: therefore, by analogy, neither is a bishop justly degraded a bishop. But one who is not a bishop cannot confer orders: therefore neither can a degraded bishop confer orders.

Likewise, he cannot confer true tonsure, because one tonsured by him ought to be tonsured again: therefore, by analogy, neither can he confer true orders.

On the other hand, as was shown above at dist. 13, a degraded priest can consecrate the body of Christ: therefore, by analogy, a degraded bishop can confer orders.

Likewise, heretics are by the law itself deprived of all dignity: since, therefore, heretical bishops can confer orders in fact, though not by right, as was established in the preceding question, a degraded bishop can confer orders in fact, though not by right.

Likewise, that power is founded upon something that cannot be taken away by a human being; for it is founded upon the fact of consecration having taken place, and upon the decree of divine sentence, which decree is that one who has been so consecrated shall have such a power.

## CONCLUSION.

*Although a degraded bishop cannot by right confer holy orders, yet if he ordains, he in fact confers true orders, even though he sins mortally in conferring them.*

To this question some have said that, although suspended, excommunicated, and heretical bishops can confer orders in fact, degraded bishops nevertheless cannot do so, because degraded persons are not merely suspended from the execution of their office, but have also been stripped of episcopal dignity — whence they no longer remain bishops. For although the power of a priest to confect the true body of Christ cannot be taken from him by a human being — since Christ granted such power to none except priests, and it was fitting to do so given the great dignity of his own body — yet because episcopal dignity regards the mystical body of Christ, over which he granted power to his vicar, the episcopal dignity can be wholly taken from a bishop by the authority of the vicar of Christ.

But against this position one can argue as follows. An altar, a chalice, and similar things, once consecrated, can never lose their consecration unless they are destroyed: therefore, all the more so can a bishop not lose his consecration, since the consecration of a bishop comes principally from God, even though it is administered through a human being.
