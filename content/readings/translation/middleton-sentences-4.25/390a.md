\[the ordainer\] sins mortally in conferring \[orders on one of such young age\].

I REPLY that infantile age impedes the use of reason, and therefore impedes all those sacraments which of necessity require an act of reason in the one receiving the sacrament — such as penance and matrimony. The sacrament of orders, however, does not require, in order to be received, an act of reason in the recipient, because in the conferral of this sacrament there is received in the recipient a certain infused power which can, in the order of nature and of time, precede the act; and therefore, although an infant cannot receive the sacraments of penance and matrimony, he can nonetheless in fact receive the sacrament of orders.

In the minor orders, the use of discretion is required in the recipient not of necessity of precept, but of propriety, on account of reverence for the sacred. But for the reception of the major orders, the use of reason is required not only of propriety but also of necessity of precept: both because of the vow of continence annexed thereto, and because so great a power is committed to them that it ought not to be given except to one who receives it with reverence and devotion.

TO THE FIRST objection in opposition it must be said that not everything which is of the necessity of precept is of the necessity of the sacrament.

To the second it must be said that although the power and the act belong to the same subject, yet the power can precede the act — not only in the order of nature but also in time.

To the third it must be said that continence, according to some, is not annexed to holy orders except in the case of those who receive them voluntarily, though some hold the contrary.

To the fourth it must be said that for a form to be received in matter it suffices on the part of the matter that there be nothing in it repugnant to the form to be introduced. In children, however, the soul has nothing repugnant to the reception of the character of orders. Yet it seems to many, and probably, that in adults an explicit or implicit intention to receive — whether particular or general — is required; and although for the reception of an elementary form it suffices on the part of the matter that there be nothing in it repugnant to that form, yet this is not true of every other form: for the reception of an intellective form requires in the matter some positive disposition, as was shown in Book II.
