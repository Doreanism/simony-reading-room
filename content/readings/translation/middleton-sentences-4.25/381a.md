Likewise, it is greater to confer the sacrament of orders than to receive it: but heretics cannot receive the sacrament of orders: therefore neither can they confer it.

On the other hand, Augustinus, in the second book against the letter of Parmenianus, past the middle, says of heretics that just as they have in baptism what can be given through them, so also they have in ordination the right of giving both \[baptism and orders\] — to their own destruction, so long as they do not have the charity of unity.

Likewise, a heretical priest can consecrate the body of Christ: therefore, by analogy, a heretical bishop can confer orders.

## CONCLUSION.

*A heretical bishop can in fact confer holy orders, provided he ordains with due intention in the form of the Church, even though he sins in ordaining.*

I respond that heretical bishops were either ordained and consecrated in the form of the Church, or not. If not, they are not true bishops, and consequently they cannot confer orders. If they were, they are true bishops, but they do not have the execution of their office, because, as was established above, all heretics are by the law itself excommunicated with major excommunication, and all those so excommunicated are suspended from all lawful acts — and therefore heretical bishops cannot confer orders by execution of orders, because they do not have it; for what someone does not have, he cannot give — Prima, q. 7, “Diambertum,” and Extra, de ordinatis ab episcopo qui renunciavit \[on those ordained by a bishop who has resigned\], c. 1, in the gloss.

Nevertheless they do confer true orders if, with due intention, they observe the form of the Church. Decree, dist. 19, “Secundum.” But if they do not observe the form of the Church with due intention, they do not confer true orders. However, however much they observe the form of the Church in ordaining, they sin mortally, because they are by the law itself suspended from the execution of their office.

To the first objection it must be said that, although heretical bishops are not joined to God through faith and charity — which union regards the bishop insofar as he is a singular person — yet they are joined to God in a qualified sense, namely insofar as they are moved by God in observing the form of the Church in ordaining, because in this respect they are ministers of the Church.

To the second it must be said that such consecration is said to be null with respect to execution.

To the third it must be said that, just as heretics can in fact, but not by right, confer true orders, so also they can in fact receive them. Hence, although a heretic, if he is ordained in the form of the Church, receives true orders, yet in receiving them he sins mortally.
