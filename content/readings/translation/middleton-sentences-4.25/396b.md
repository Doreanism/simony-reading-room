That this distinction is reasonable appears from the fact that some degree of infamy according to the canons is incurred through every mortal sin — *C.6 q.1, Omnes vero* \[Decretum C.6 q.1 c.21\], in the gloss, and in the text of the following chapter — but not every mortal sin that a man commits after his promotion entails the deposition of one already promoted, as the lord Hostiensis holds in the *Summa*, under the title *de accusationibus* \[X 5.1\], where he explains what the effect of an accusation is. Nor does every mortal sin that according to the canons entails deposition necessarily give rise to civil infamy — as is evident if one carefully considers the crimes by which civil infamy is contracted, which are set out in *Dig. de his qui notantur infamia* \[Dig. 3.2\], and the crimes which according to the canons entail deposition, many of which the lord Hostiensis recounts in the aforementioned title. Some, therefore, say that those who incur infamy according to the canons by operation of law through one of those mortal sins none of which entails deposition are not, after completing true penance legitimately proved according to law, to be barred from the reception of orders, nor from accusation and testimony, whether in civil or in criminal matters, because they have become, as it were, new men.

Others, however, say that such persons are to be barred from the reception of orders, accusation, and testimony in a criminal cause — though not in a civil cause or in a criminal cause proceeded with civilly — and they say that the decretal *Testimonium* \[X 2.20.37\] must be understood to refer to such criminal persons, which they argue from the fact that after it says “if he has made amends for the crime,” it immediately adds “and infamy does not attend him.” These writers say that infamy which is wholly abolished by penance is that which is contracted through a secret crime only, about which there is no infamy of fact \[*infamia facti*\] and which cannot be proved. But this does not seem right to others, because from among the sins spoken of in that decretal *Testimonii* \[X 2.20.37\], perjury is excepted — which is such a crime as entails deposition. And furthermore, the legal authorities and the legal doctors say that the penance by which infamy is abolished — the infamy that the law says is abolished by penance — must be proved through a priest. But \[infamy contracted\] through a secret crime alone requires no such proof. Hence the phrase “and infamy does not attend him” must be understood, according to these writers, as referring to civil infamy or to infamy of fact \[*infamia facti*\].
