### Conclusion

Whoever obtains for himself, through simony, either the reception of orders or the acquisition of an ecclesiastical benefice, becomes irregular.

I reply that every simoniac — whether he is a simoniac on account of the simoniacal reception of an order, or on account of the acquisition of an ecclesiastical benefice — is irregular, because the laws decree that such persons cannot be promoted to orders without a dispensation. This I believe must be understood to apply when such simony was committed with his own knowledge; because if money was given for his ordination or for the conferring of an ecclesiastical benefice upon him while he was invincibly ignorant of it, then although immediately upon gaining knowledge of the matter he is obliged to renounce the benefice thus obtained and to desist from the administration of the order thus received — until a dispensation has been granted to him — he nonetheless does not incur irregularity for the acquisition of such a benefice. Yet he has incurred it in a qualified sense for the reception of such an order, insofar as he must not minister in it without a dispensation; he is not, however, simply irregular, because he can minister without any dispensation in orders legitimately received before \[the simoniacal one\], and by the very fact that a dispensation has been granted to him in the order he thus received, he can be promoted to higher orders without any further dispensation. It must be known, however, that simony in an order is more grave than in the acquisition of an ecclesiastical benefice. Hence it is not customary to dispense with those who have knowingly received an order simoniacally — especially if the simony was committed both on the part of the one ordained and on the part of the one ordaining or presenting. Only the Pope, or one to whom he has committed it, can dispense with any simoniacs whatsoever who have knowingly received simoniacally something spiritual or annexed to the spiritual — which can be proved by what is said at *prima quaestio 1, Erga* \[*Decretum*, C. 1 q. 1, *Erga*\], where the Pope himself utterly condemns them and decrees that they are to be deposed by apostolic authority, unless one was taken by force; and by *extra de simonia, Nobis fuit* \[*Decretals*, on simony, *Nobis fuit*\], in the text and in the gloss.

### Reply to the first

Gehazi, in receiving money from Naaman, incurred a threefold sin: because he lied, and he damaged his master’s reputation to the extent that was in his power, and because he directly and intentionally exacted money for a benefit of the Holy Spirit — albeit one already conferred. For it is one thing to give money freely for a benefit already received, not as a price but in place of giving thanks, which is lawful; and another thing to exact money as a price, which is what Gehazi did.
