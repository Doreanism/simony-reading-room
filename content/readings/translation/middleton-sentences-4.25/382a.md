But a degraded bishop is not stripped of episcopal dignity any more than this one is, even though he is suspended from the exercise of that dignity in a more disgraceful manner than the other. It therefore appears that a degraded bishop can confer a true order in fact, even though he ought not do so in law.

### Second opinion

Others, therefore, grant this conclusion and say that a bishop cannot be wholly stripped of his episcopal dignity by any human act. Hence degradation is not the removal of dignity but a perpetual suspension from the exercise of that dignity — *extra de conse. ecclesiæ, in gl.* \[*Decretals*, on the consecration of a church, in the gloss\] — for it is said there that visible exterior unction is a sign of interior unction in the heart, which is perpetual and cannot be lost, even though its exercise may sometimes be suspended.

### Reply to the first \[objection\] on behalf of the first opinion

Those who hold this second opinion, which seems to me more reasonable, can say in reply to the first objection that a degraded bishop is said not to have the power of conferring holy orders because he is entirely deprived of the effect of his power. The second point is clear from the following text, where according to the gloss there he confers the order but not the execution of the order.

### Reply to the second

To the second it must be said: just as all the Apostles — of whom bishops are the successors — received the dignity of the apostolate immediately from Christ, so too a bishop, with respect to episcopal dignity, has no power above him capable of removing it save the divine power alone, even though the exercise of that dignity depends on a superior jurisdictional power, namely the power of the Pope, which is superior to all other powers in jurisdiction, as has been said above. But earthly principate depends on the authority of the head of the church, and an inferior principate depends on a superior one; therefore a principate can be removed from an inferior by a superior prince, and from a superior prince by the Vicar of Christ, when there is just and reasonable cause requiring it.

### Reply to the third

To the third it must be said: one who has been tonsured by him \[a degraded bishop\] need not be tonsured again, but he ought nonetheless to be punished.

### On behalf of the second opinion

Those who hold the first opinion can say, in reply to the first objection on the opposite side, what has been said in the body of the question.

### Reply to the second

To the second it must be said that heretics are not by the law itself deprived of all dignity, except with respect to its exercise. Hence they ought rather to be said to be suspended from all dignity than deprived of it; they are, however, to be deprived by \[formal\] sentence.

### Reply to the third

To the third it must be said: just as it has been decreed by God that one upon whom such consecration has been performed shall have such power, so it has been decreed that one who is degraded shall lack that power.

### Reply to the objection

To the first objection raised in the body of the question against this opinion, it can be answered by denying the major premise. For the Pope could remove the consecration from an altar or chalice without destroying them.
