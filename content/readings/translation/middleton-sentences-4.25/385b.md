To the sixth objection it must be said that if the person elected has not acquired a right through that election, it is not lawful to give money to an adversary so that he may desist. If, however, a right has been acquired for him, it is lawful for him to give money to an adversary — not for the election, since he has already been duly elected, but for the redemption of his unjust harassment. Extra, de simonia, “Dilectus filius R.”

To the seventh objection it must be said that “to redeem” is there used by a kind of analogy, since it is not properly a redemption; yet it is called “redemption” because, just as a man by payment of a price draws back to himself an inheritance that had been alienated, so a man by giving alms to the poor for the love of God draws back to himself, in a dispositive manner, the divine friendship and the right of participating in eternal life, from which he had been alienated by his sins.

To the eighth objection it must be said that in that case it would not suffice to say that the monk should purchase water from the priest and baptize the child himself, because the monk could not immerse him. If, therefore, the case is posed in this way, it draws one apart: either the child must die without baptism, or money must be given to the priest for the sacrament. The monk ought rather to choose the former than the latter, because, according to the Apostle, Romans 3: “Evil must not be done that good may come of it.” Some, however, say that in this case money could be given, provided that the one giving it did not intend to give it for the sacrament, but to avoid the damnation of the child and to remove the madness of the priest. The case is different, however, with other sacraments, which can, without danger of damnation, be deferred for a reason.

## Question II

Whether simony is committed in the buying or selling of any spiritual things whatsoever.

It seems not: for the sake of the consecration of churches a bishop may demand procuration. Extra, de simonia, “Cum sit Romana”; yet the consecration of churches is a spiritual matter.

Furthermore, the whole of the ecclesiastical office is something spiritual; yet certain fixed revenues are frequently received for the celebration of an anniversary.

Furthermore, visitation and correction are spiritual matters; yet prelates receive procurations for their visitation.

Furthermore, teaching is something spiritual; yet masters of the arts receive money for their teaching.

Furthermore, judgment, counsel, and testimony are spiritual goods; yet money can be received for these.
