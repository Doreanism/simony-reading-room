### ARTICLE II.

### On the incapacity of certain ordainers.

Note. AS REGARDS the second article, it must be observed that a person can be incapable with respect to the performance of some act in two ways. In one way, because he cannot perform that act lawfully — and in this sense we are said to be able to do only what we can do justly. In another way, absolutely and simply, namely when he can accomplish nothing \[of effect\], even though he does everything that lies within him. In accordance with this, I set forth two conclusions.

Concl. 1.

The first is that a heretical bishop, or one otherwise subject to canonical penalty or ecclesiastical censure, is incapable of conferring the sacrament of orders in the first of the aforesaid ways.

For that which cannot be conferred without mortal sin on the part of both the conferrer and the one upon whom it is conferred, is not conferred justly or lawfully — and consequently the conferrer with respect to that conferral is said to be incapable in the first way aforesaid. But the sacrament of orders cannot be conferred by a heretical bishop, or one otherwise subject to canonical penalty — such as a bishop who is suspended, excommunicated, interdicted, irregular, deposed, or degraded — without mortal sin on the part of both conferrer and recipient; therefore, etc. The major premise is clear: for what is done justly and lawfully is done without sin; therefore, by contraposition, what is not done without sin is done neither lawfully nor justly. The minor premise is also clear: for one who rashly acts against the prohibition of holy mother Church sins mortally. But in the aforesaid cases, both the conferrer and the one who knowingly receives from him sin against the prohibition of the Church — for by the statutes of holy mother Church, each of the aforesaid persons is in canon law prohibited from exercising his office, and consequently from the conferral of sacraments, with the sole exception of the sacrament of baptism in a case of necessity; and consequently every faithful person is understood to be prohibited from receiving any sacrament from any of the aforesaid, except baptism alone in a case of necessity; therefore, etc.

2. Furthermore, no heretic or person subject to the aforesaid penalties can lawfully receive the sacrament of orders; therefore no heretic or person subject to the aforesaid penalties can confer the sacrament of orders. The antecedent is clear: for if it were lawful for any of the aforesaid to receive the sacrament of orders, this would seem most evident in the case of a suspended person, who has not yet been excommunicated or declared irregular. But this is not the case: for while suspended from an office already received, it is not lawful for him to advance to a higher grade, since one who is prohibited from executing a lower grade is thereby understood to be prohibited from receiving a higher grade.
