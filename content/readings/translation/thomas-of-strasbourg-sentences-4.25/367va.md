And because the will seems to be posited in the definition of simony in this second sense — since a will can be called deliberate \[*studiosa*\] when its earnestness or solicitude appears outwardly in word or sign — therefore, if in the argument the will is taken in the first sense alone, then the consequence must be denied, because simony in the forum of God can exist without the irregularity that is to be remitted in the external forum of the Church. But if it is taken in the second sense, as it ought indeed to be taken in the present context, since what is posited in the definition of simony is not mere will but deliberate will, then the antecedent is false, because, as has just been said, a deliberate will — inasmuch as its earnestness has been expressed and declared outwardly — does induce irregularity.

To the third objection it must be said that if the prayers are of such a character, or the favor of such a kind, that they induce simony, then such prayers or favor are equivalent to payment, and therefore in such prayers and favors, although they are not formally a buying and selling, they are so equivalently. Hence Gregorius rightly says that there is a threefold gift: the gift of the hand — for example, money or other temporal things; the gift of the tongue — for example, prayers or influence; and the gift of service — for example, service rendered to someone \[cf. Gregorius, *Moralia in Iob* 18.15\]. And in each of these simony is sometimes committed, and sometimes not. This can be seen first with regard to the gift of the hand: a person who accepts a temporal thing for a spiritual one either is bound to confer that spiritual thing — as a parish priest is bound to baptize the child of his parishioner — and then, if he is unwilling to confer that spiritual thing without a gift, he is truly a simoniac; or he is not so bound, and this in two further ways, because when such a person demands a gift he either intends thereby to put that spiritual thing up for a price, in which case he is also a simoniac, or he intends it as a stipend for his bodily labor, in which case he is not a simoniac. One can also make a distinction with regard to the gift of the tongue: either the one who by his prayers promotes another to an order or a benefice petitions knowingly for an unworthy person, moved solely by carnal attachment — for instance, because the person for whom he petitions is his kinsman or friend — in which case he commits simony; or he petitions for a worthy person, and then he does not commit simony, especially if his consideration is directed toward the merit of the one he is promoting.

A similar distinction can be made with regard to the gift of service: either the service is spiritual — for instance, because the person is a chaplain or instructor and master \[*magister*\], by reason of which a benefice is conferred on him — and then simony is not committed, because the spiritual thing is not given for a temporal but rather for a spiritual one; or the service is temporal, and this in two further ways: either the service is honorable, and then simony is not committed, especially if the person provided for is worthy and the consideration of the one making provision is directed to that end; or the service is dishonorable, and then simony is committed.

To the fourth objection it must be said that the right of patronage cannot in itself be sold without simony; however, the temporal lordship \[associated with a property\] can be sold, together with which the right of patronage may pass without simony — provided that the said temporal lordship is not sold at a higher price because the right of patronage passes with it, since if it were sold at a higher price on that account, such a contract would not be free from the vice of simony.

To the fifth objection it must be said that if a chalice is sold for the use of another church, it can be sold while remaining intact — provided, however, that in the sale no regard is had to the consecration, but only to the material and the craftsman’s work. For if it were sold at a higher price on account of the consecration, simony would be committed. But if it is sold to a secular person not for the use of another church, it must first be broken before it is sold, so that the consecration is removed, because it is not lawful for a consecrated chalice to be handled by an unconsecrated person.

Perhaps it will be said that at least in this case something annexed to a spiritual thing is being sold — since lordship is annexed to the right of patronage, and the chalice, while it remains intact, is annexed to its consecration — which seems to constitute simony according to the aforesaid definition.
