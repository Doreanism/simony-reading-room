### Resolution.

Although by common law only a bishop can confer the sacrament of order, and no mere presbyter can consecrate a bishop, yet although a heretical bishop, or one subject to canonical penalty or ecclesiastical censure, is rendered unfit to confer the sacrament of order licitly, nevertheless \[he remains capable of doing so\] in an absolute sense, with the form and intention of the Catholic Church duly maintained.

### Article I.

On the power of the ordainer.

WITH respect to the first \[point\] I set forth two conclusions.

The first is that by common law only a bishop can ordain, or confer the sacrament of order. For it requires no less power to dispose and ordain commanders than to enroll soldiers: but in the conferral of the sacrament of confirmation, soldiers of the Church militant are enrolled, and in the conferral of the sacrament of order, the commanders of those very faithful are disposed; since therefore only a bishop, by common law, can confer the sacrament of confirmation, as is clear from what was said in dist. 7, therefore episcopal power alone suffices by common law for the conferral of the sacrament of order.

Furthermore, the consecration of vessels pertaining to divine worship does not require greater power than the consecration of the ministers who use such consecrated vessels: but vessels, for example chalices, cannot by common law be consecrated except by a bishop; therefore neither can clerics and priests who use such vessels be ordained except by a bishop. And this seems to be the intention of Isidorus in his *Etymologiae*, where he says: the bishop is named the high priest and supreme pontiff; for he himself makes priests and Levites, and disposes all ecclesiastical persons.

Perhaps it will be said that when certain words are synonyms, then the thing signified by one is not of greater power than the thing signified by the other: but the word “bishop” and the word “presbyter” are synonymous terms. The major premise is clear, because synonyms are \[words\] whose names are many but whose thing is one. The minor is also clear in many places in the Apostle’s letters, where the Apostle himself seems to take the name “bishop” and the name “presbyter” as the same. Hence at the beginning of the letter to the Philippians he says: “Paul and Timothy, servants of Jesus Christ, to all the saints in Christ Jesus who are at Philippi, with the bishops and deacons: grace to you and peace,” etc. \[Philippians 1:1\]. Where it is plainly evident that by “bishops” he understands “presbyters,” because a single city does not have multiple bishops, unless by “bishops” we understand presbyters. But Philippi, to which the Apostle writes there, was one city. And the same is clear in Acts 20, where the Apostle, addressing the presbyters of the city of Ephesus, says: “Take heed to yourselves and to the flock, in which the Holy Spirit has placed you as bishops to rule the Church of God.”

Also conversely, by the name “presbyter” the Apostle expresses the office of bishop — 1 Timothy 4 \[v. 14\], saying: “Do not neglect the grace that was given to you through the laying on of hands of the presbyter,” that is, of the bishop, through whom Timothy was ordained.

On account of these and similar authorities, a certain opinion holds that these two names, namely “presbyter” and “bishop,” were once synonymous.
