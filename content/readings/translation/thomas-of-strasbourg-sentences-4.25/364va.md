The minor of the aforesaid objection must therefore be denied. In response to its proof it must be said that the terms “bishop” and “presbyter” can be considered in two ways. One way, with respect to their etymology; another way, with respect to their proper imposition and signification. In the first way, one of these terms can sometimes be taken for the other: because “bishop” means, as it were, one who oversees, as is clear from the text here; but “presbyter” means, as it were, one who provides a way \[*praebens iter*\]. Since therefore the same person who is a spiritual leader, providing a way to the people of God, ought to attend to that same people, and conversely, one whose task it is to attend to the people ought by word and example to provide a way to the people — for this reason the authorities of Scripture sometimes use one of these terms for the other, and vice versa. If \[we consider them\] in the second way, then the name “presbyter” is more universal and extends further than the name “bishop”: because the name “bishop” is not imposed to signify just any presbyter, but only one who is so placed in oversight that he has jurisdiction over all others, both clerics and laypeople, dwelling within his diocese. Also not only in such jurisdictional power, but also in the power of confirmation and the power of consecration, a bishop exceeds a simple presbyter: for he alone can by common law give the sacrament of confirmation, and ordain or consecrate clerics and priests.

But against this, and consequently against the principal conclusion, one can argue from the statements of certain doctors as follows. Whoever’s power extends to what is principal and to what is greater, that person’s power will be able to extend to what is accessory and to what is lesser: but the power of any presbyter extends sufficiently to the consecration of the sacrament of the altar, which is greater than the sacrament of order, and with respect to it the sacrament of order is accessory; therefore the power of any presbyter extends sufficiently to consecrating and conferring the sacrament of order.

2. Furthermore, spiritual power is not of lesser force than bodily power: but the vigor of bodily power over things corporeal is such that whatever is perfect in its species can generate something like itself, as is clear from Book 2 *On the Soul* \[of Aristotle\]; therefore one possessing the perfect power of a presbyter will be able to ordain another presbyter like himself.

3. Furthermore, a bishop can consecrate a bishop; therefore a presbyter can consecrate a presbyter, or at least a deacon, or subdeacon, or other lower clerics. The antecedent is clear; the consequence is proved as to its first part, because a presbyter stands in the same relation to a presbyter as a bishop stands to a bishop. And as to its second part: because the power of a presbyter exceeds the power of a deacon more than the power of one bishop exceeds the power of another bishop, and consequently the act of hierarchical dignity, namely ordaining, belongs to a presbyter with respect to a deacon no less than it belongs to a bishop with respect to another bishop.

4. Furthermore, Cardinal presbyters \[*presbyteri Cardinales*\] can confer minor orders; therefore conferring the sacrament of order does not belong to bishops alone. The antecedent is commonly conceded. The consequence is also clear, because Cardinal presbyters are not bishops, just as Cardinal deacons are not presbyters.

To the first \[argument\] it can be said that the major premise is not true, especially when the greater and the lesser are not of the same nature, such that the greater does not include the lesser within itself; which is evident in natural things: because although it is greater to reason than to fly, it does not follow that a man can reason, therefore he can fly — and this for the very reason that reasoning and flying are not of the same nature, nor does reasoning include flying within itself.
