The first case occurs in four ways. First, on account of simony — for instance, when someone purchases an order or a benefice. In cases of simony of this kind, sometimes the Pope alone can dispense, as is clear from *Extra*, “De simonia,” c. “Nobis” \[X.5.3.20\]; and the same is clear from *Decretum* 1.q.1 \[C.1 q.1\].

The second crime by which irregularity is contracted is theft in the reception of an order — for example, when a bishop commands that those who have done such-and-such a thing are not to present themselves for ordination, and yet someone knowingly acts against such a prohibition and receives orders clandestinely: such a person becomes irregular, as is clear from *Extra*, “De eo qui furtive ordines suscipit” \[X.1.11\].

The third crime is in one who ministers in an order he has not received, especially if it is a sacred order and the ministration solemn. That such a person is irregular is clear from *Extra*, “De clerico non ordinato ministrante” \[X.3.43\].

The fourth crime by reason of which a man becomes irregular is conducting oneself contumaciously in failing to observe canonical penalties; and this is clear from *Extra*, “De clerico excommunicato vel deposito ministrante” \[X.3.44\]. And what is said there about excommunication is to be understood of major excommunication, because, as is likewise said there in c. “Licet,” although one who celebrates in minor excommunication sins gravely, he nonetheless incurs no taint of irregularity.

The second case occurs in three ways. For irregularity follows upon serfdom, even though serfdom is not itself a crime. Hence any bonded serf is prohibited by the law itself from receiving the sacrament of orders, unless he has been restored to freedom with the consent of his lord, as is clear from *Extra*, “De servis non ordinandis” \[X.1.18\]. Irregularity also follows upon illegitimate birth, even though such illegitimacy is not the fault of the one born in that condition; and this is clear from *Extra*, “De filiis presbyterorum non ordinandis” \[X.1.17\].

It follows also, third, upon bodily defect — either the loss of a notable member through grievous mutilation, or debilitation from a serious and loathsome disease. Such persons are irregular and ought not to receive orders, as is clear from *Extra*, “De corpore vitiatis” \[X.1.20\] and *Extra*, “De clerico debilitato.”

The third case occurs in two ways — namely, through homicide and bigamy — each of which is sometimes without sin and sometimes with sin. This is evident for homicide, because a just judge may sometimes lawfully put guilty men to death by true judgment and without sin; and nonetheless both the judge and the witness, and the notary who records the sentence, and the counsellor of the judge, are all irregular, as is clear from *Extra*, “De homicidio voluntario” \[X.5.12\]. The matter is also treated at *Decretum* dist. 50, c. “Miror,” and dist. 55, c. ult. \[D.50 c.25, D.55 c.ult.\].

That homicide is sometimes a sin — for instance, when a guilty person kills an innocent one — is self-evident, since it is against the commandment of God. That such a person is also irregular is clear from the same passages just cited. That bigamy induces irregularity is clear from *Extra*, “De bigamis” \[X.1.21\]. That it can exist without sin is also clear, because a man who marries a woman previously corrupted becomes a bigamist, though he may do so without sin, as will be shown below, in the treatment of matrimony. That bigamy may sometimes befall someone with sin is also evident: for suppose that a man marries a virgin wife and thereby does not contract bigamy; but if that woman subsequently commits adultery with another man to her husband’s knowledge, then in the first act of intercourse in which the legitimate husband cohabits with his now-adulterous wife, he contracts bigamy — and this is not usually without sin, especially if out of a desire to satisfy his lust he knowingly continues to harbor an adulteress without correcting or repudiating her.
