2. Furthermore, when the use of some office cannot belong to a person, then that office is committed to him in vain; but the use of no order can belong to one who lacks the use of reason; therefore order is conferred in vain and to no purpose upon one who lacks the use of reason.

Furthermore, one who does not intend to receive the sacrament of order does not receive that sacrament; but one who lacks the use of reason does not intend to receive the sacrament of order. The minor is clear, because intention presupposes the use of reason. The major is proved, because the intention to receive the sacrament in the one who receives it is required no less than the intention to give or confer it in the one who confers it. But in order for an ordainer to confer the sacrament of order, it is necessary that he intend to confer it; therefore, etc.

Furthermore, a definite time and age are determined with respect to the reception of orders in the book of Decrees, dist. 77; but this would not be the case if the use of reason were not necessary for the reception of orders.

But this opinion is not held in modern times, because it is common enough for minor orders to be conferred upon those who are under age and lack the use of reason.

Furthermore, in the Decretals, under the title *de clerico ordinato per saltum* \[on a cleric ordained by skipping a grade\], it is stated that a certain cleric had been promoted to minor orders while under the age of discretion, and yet afterward permission was granted to that cleric to minister in those orders without their repetition. This, however, the Pope would by no means have done if he had not judged that cleric to have received the minor orders before he had the use of reason — that is, when he was under age.

To the first supporting argument of this opinion, therefore, it must be said that the inference does not hold. Against the proof I deny the major, because the sacrament of baptism is more dignified and gracious than the sacrament of matrimony, and yet in the reception of baptism the use of reason is not required of the necessity of the sacrament — otherwise infants could not be truly baptized.

To the second it must be said that, although the major is true when the one upon whom an office is conferred is so incapable of having the use of that office that neither for the present can it belong to him, nor, when separated \[from his present condition\], will he ever in the future be able to exercise such an office, nevertheless it is false if there is a reasonable hope that at some future time he will be able to exercise such an office fittingly. But the present case is such that, although a boy who has not yet attained the age of discretion cannot exercise the office of minor orders, he will nevertheless be able to do so in the future.

To the third it must be said that the lack of intention by which someone is said not to intend the reception of order can come about in many ways. In one way through a positive act elicited, for instance when he intends not to receive the order and explicitly refuses such a reception. In another way, through inattention — for instance, because he is not actually thinking of this when he receives the order. In a third way, because he does not at that time have the use of reason by which he could intend such a reception. Now although the major is true in the first way, it is not true in the other two ways.

To the proof it must be said that, although the major may in some way be true of adults, it is nevertheless not true of infants; whence it is commonly enough said that in sacraments in which a character is imprinted, the intention of the recipient is not required of the necessity of the sacrament — as is apparent from the sacrament of baptism and confirmation, which for the most part are conferred upon infants who are under age.

It will perhaps be objected: if this were true, then an infant lacking the intention to receive the order of the priesthood could receive that order if ordained by a bishop; but the consequent is absurd. The inference is clear, because a character is imprinted in the conferral of a higher order no less than in the conferral of baptism, or confirmation, or any minor order.
