As for the first, it should be known that doctors quite commonly give this as the definition of simony: Simony is the deliberate will to buy or sell something spiritual or annexed to a spiritual thing. This description is reasonably well given. For just as virtuous habits are known — and consequently defined — by their acts and their objects, so too are vicious habits; and therefore in the present case simony is defined first by an interior act, when “deliberate will” is placed first; second by an exterior act, when “to sell or to buy” is added; and third by the object, when “something spiritual or annexed to a spiritual thing” is appended.

But against this definition certain objections are raised as follows. Simony takes its name from Simon Magus; but Simon does not seem to have wished to sell spiritual things — rather, he wished to buy them from the Apostles, as is clear from the Acts of the Apostles \[Acts 8:18–19\]. Therefore the will to sell is wrongly included in the definition of simony, which is named after that Simon.

Furthermore, the mere will to buy or sell a spiritual thing does not induce irregularity; therefore such a will is not simony. The antecedent is clear, because such a will can be expiated by penance without a suspension by a superior — which could not be done if it induced irregularity. The consequence is also clear, because, as has been said above, simony does induce irregularity.

Furthermore, one who obtains an order or benefice through prayers or favor commits simony; but such a person neither sells nor buys; therefore simony is not sufficiently defined by the will to buy or sell.

Furthermore, the right of patronage is something spiritual, and yet it can be sold without simony; therefore it is not well defined by the will to sell a spiritual thing. The consequence is clear. The antecedent is proved as to the first part: because the authority to confer an ecclesiastical benefice is a spiritual right, and the right of patronage is precisely such an authority. It is also clear as to the second part — namely, that it can be sold without simony — because we often see in practice that it is sold, and yet those who sell it are not on this account reproved.

Furthermore, a consecrated chalice can be sold without simony, and therefore simony is not well defined by the sale of something annexed to a spiritual thing. The antecedent is clear, because we read of Ambrosius that he had chalices sold for the ransom of captives and the support of the poor \[cf. Ambrosius, *De officiis* 2.28\] — which a man of such great holiness would by no means have done if simony were committed in the sale of a chalice. The consequence is also clear, because being consecrated is something spiritual, just as consecration itself is; but a consecrated chalice cannot be separated from its being consecrated.

### Solution

To the first objection it must be said that Simon wished to buy spiritual power from the Apostles precisely in order that he might afterwards sell it; and therefore he had not only the will to buy but also the will to sell. On this account, not only those who buy spiritual things are rightly called simoniacs, but also those who sell them. And accordingly it is clear that, although simony takes its name from Simon, it is nonetheless well defined by the will to buy and to sell the spiritual thing itself. If, however, someone wished to call only those who buy spiritual things simoniacs, he could name those who sell them Gehazites, after Gehazi, the servant of Elisha, who received gifts from Naaman the Syrian for the healing granted to him at the prayers of Elisha himself, as is clear from 2 Kings 5 \[2 Kgs 5:20–27\].

To the second objection it must be said that the will to buy or sell a spiritual thing can be understood in two ways: one way, precisely, without any exterior expression; another way, as it is expressed outwardly in some manner — for instance, because the will is so intent upon this that it is signaled outwardly by word, nod, or sign. In the first way it does not induce simony in the forum of the Church, although one who has such a will is truly a simoniac before God, that is, in the forum of God.
