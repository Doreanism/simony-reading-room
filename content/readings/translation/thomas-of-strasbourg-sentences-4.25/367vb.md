### The Second Inquiry of the Fourth Article.

As for the second question in this fourth article, it should be known that certain things are called spiritual in two ways: one way, because they are ordered and referred to what is spiritual in the sense of the rational soul — such as knowledge, counsel, and the like — and in the sale of such things simony is not committed, although in other respects one sometimes sins gravely in such transactions. Another way, because they are ordered toward spiritual grace; and this in two further ways, because either such things are spiritual by essence, as are the gifts of grace, and with respect to these simony cannot be committed, because they can be sold neither by right nor in fact; or they are spiritual causally, as are the sacraments, which are in some manner a cause of the spiritual grace conferred upon recipients in their administration. And with respect to these, simony can be committed, because, although not by right, they can nonetheless be sold in fact.

1 Perhaps you will say that matrimony is a true sacrament, and yet in the marriage contract a stipulation is made concerning a temporal thing without simony — for if those contracting matrimony were thereby to commit simony, the Church would prohibit such stipulations.

2 Furthermore, the Mass is a thing of great spiritual value, and yet money is frequently given for a Mass to be celebrated.

3 Furthermore, a man does not seem to sin, nor consequently to commit simony, in preferring a spiritual thing to temporal things; but one who gives temporal things for spiritual things is preferring spiritual things to temporal ones. Therefore he does not seem to contract simony.

### Solution.

To the first objection it must be said that such a stipulation ought not to be made for the sacrament of matrimony itself, nor for the blessing of the contracting parties — for since those are spiritual things, if a temporal thing were demanded precisely for them, simony would be committed. But because the contracting parties, both for themselves and for the offspring they intend to generate, stand in need of temporal things, a stipulation concerning a temporal thing can therefore be made, with consideration directed toward that end, whenever the contraction of matrimony is at issue.

To the second objection it must be said that one who were simply and precisely to buy or sell a Mass would sin and commit simony; nonetheless, money or another temporal thing can be given without sin to one who celebrates Mass for his labors, because no one is obliged to serve at his own expense.

And in this sense the Apostle says that those who sow spiritual things are owed temporal things \[1 Cor 9:11\].

To the third objection I deny the minor, because one who judges that a spiritual thing can be matched to a temporal price does not prefer the spiritual; rather, he equates it to the temporal thing, since what is appraised is made equal to its price.

But what is to be thought of claustral religious — and especially of certain nuns in Germany — who quite openly demand temporal things from those who are to be received into their community, given that the religious state is a spiritual thing?

### Note.

To this, Thomas de Aquino replies in his commentary on book four of the *Sentences*, dist. 24 \[*In IV Sent.* d.24\], saying that when the possessions of a religious house are insufficient to support more persons than it currently has, then from one who desires to serve God in that place there can be required \[a contribution\] — not as a price for the religious life, but so that the monastery may have the means to provide for him — and in this way simony is not committed. But if he can be received without burden to the house, then it is simoniacal to exact anything for his reception.

To the principal argument, the answer is clear from what has been said at the beginning of this second inquiry.
