# Distinction XXV.

The division of this distinction resolved, in which the conferral and reception of orders is treated.

It is also customary to inquire. After the Master treated the difference and distinction of orders, he now in this 25th distinction treats of the conferral and reception of orders. And it is divided into two parts: for first he treats of the power of the one conferring. Second, of the fitness of the one receiving, at the words, *He says “sacred.”* The first \[part\] is divided into two: because first, he shows how heretics stand with respect to the conferral of orders. Second, how those stand with respect to the same who are made simoniacs by purchase or sale, at the words, *But concerning simoniacs.* The first \[of these\] is divided into three: because first he raises the question of whether heretics can confer the sacrament of order. Second, he draws arguments for both sides of the question from many authorities. Third, he responds to the question in accordance with the varied teaching of various opinions. The second \[begins\] at, *For a certain view seems.* The third at, *But these things thus.* The second of these divisions is divided into two parts: because first, he argues for the negative side. Second, for the affirmative side, at the words, *But others on the contrary.* The third part, in which he responds to the question, is divided into four parts, in accordance with the four opinions the Master adduces. The second \[begins\] at, *But others.* The third at, *But certain ones.* The fourth at, *But some.* There follows that part, *But concerning simoniacs.* And it is divided into two parts: for first he indicates the gravity of the sin of simony. Second, in accordance with the threefold species of simony, he shows the manifold penalty inflicted by law, at the words, *Simoniacally.* The first \[part\] is divided into three: for first he shows the deformity of simoniacal wickedness. Second he indicates that by reason of knowledge and ignorance there is great variety in simony. And third he removes the difficulty of a certain objection. The second \[begins\] at, *They differ, however.* The third at, *But if.* Concerning this distinction I inquire.

## Question I.

Whether an ordainer who demands money from those he ordains becomes a simoniac.

\[References: Thomas Aquinas, Commentary on Book 4 of the Sentences, dist. 25, q. 1, aa. 1–3; Second Commentary, dist. 25, q. 2, a. 1; same, Summa Theologiae I–II, q. 100, a. 6. Also Alexander of Hales, part 2, q. 169 throughout, and q. 120, member 4, a. 2, cases 2–3; part 3, q. 36, member 5, a. 4. Bonaventura, dist. 25, a. 1, qq. 1–4 throughout. Scotus, dist. 25, q. 2. Bacon, dist. 23, qq. 4–7. Durand, dist. 25, qq. 2–4. Biel, dist. 25, qq. 1–2.\]

WHETHER an ordainer who demands money from those he ordains becomes a simoniac. It seems that he does not, because those who sow spiritual things are owed temporal things by law \[*de iure*\]: but a bishop who confers orders sows spiritual things; therefore, etc. The major premise is clear from the Apostle, 1 Corinthians 9. The minor is also clear, because the sacrament of order is a spiritual thing.

On the contrary: whoever by pact \[*ex pacto*\] gives a spiritual thing in exchange for a temporal thing is a simoniac; such an ordainer gives a spiritual thing for a temporal thing by pact. The major premise is clear from the definition of simony, which will be set forth below. The minor is also clear, because an open exaction of money seems to be equivalent to a pact. Here we must first examine the power of the ordainer. Second, the unfitness of certain ordainers. Third, the qualifications of those to be ordained. And fourth, for the sake of the question under consideration, certain things about simoniacal wickedness must be examined.
