To the fourth objection it must be said that, even granting the entire argument, nothing is thereby gained against the conclusion. For just as one who is cut off from the church is by right excluded from participation in the sacraments — yet if he thrusts himself beyond this into communion with the faithful, he truly receives the sacrament, with the sole exception of the sacrament of penance — so too he is by right excluded from the conferral of sacraments, though he truly confers the sacrament if, contrary to the prohibition of law, he in fact confers it.

### ARTICLE III.

### On the quality of those to be ordained.

As for the third article, in which the question concerns the quality or condition of those to be ordained, it should be known that we can speak in many ways about the things required on the part of the ordinand himself, for some things are required not of necessity but only of propriety or fittingness.

Other things, however, are required of necessity, and this in two ways: for some things are required of the necessity of the sacrament — that is, things without which the ordinand does not receive the sacrament of order, even if all that the ordainer ought to do with respect to the ordinand is done outwardly. Other things are required of the necessity of precept, even though they are not required of the necessity of the sacrament — namely, such things as are prescribed to be observed, either affirmatively or negatively, by law on the part of the ordinand himself. In accordance with this I set forth three conclusions.

The first is that, if the ordinand has attained the age of discretion and has the use of reason at the reception of minor orders, this is required not of the necessity but of the propriety of the sacrament; for that which is fitting and appropriate to observe with respect to any ordinand whatsoever seems to be required also in the case of one receiving minor orders — if not of necessity, at least of propriety. But it is fitting and appropriate that whoever receives the sacrament of order should know and discern the office into which he is placed by the conferral of this sacrament; and this cannot come about unless the ordinand has attained the age of discretion and has the use of reason in some degree. Therefore, etc.

Against this, however, there is a certain ancient opinion which holds that the use of reason is required of the necessity of the sacrament in the reception of any order whatsoever.

\[In support of this:] 1. The use of reason is required of the necessity of the sacrament in the sacrament of matrimony; therefore also in the sacrament of order. The antecedent is clear, because matrimony requires mutual consent, which cannot occur without the use of reason. The consequence is proved, because the more dignified a sacrament is, the more the use of reason is required for its reception. But the sacrament of order is more dignified than the sacrament of matrimony, because in the conferral of any order the one who is ordained is placed in some spiritual office, which does not occur in matrimony.
