### Solution

To the first argument of those \[who hold the opposite\] it must be said that in antiquity certain nuns were called deaconesses, not because they held the order of the diaconate, but because they were licensed to read the Homily at the morning office. A presbytera, moreover, among the Greeks is the wife of a priest; in the Roman Church, however, a presbytera used to be called an abbess, or any other respectable matron who provides a path of conduct and example for others. And so she is not called a presbytera from the order of the presbyterate, but only from the etymology of the name.

To the second it must be said that the power of judging is twofold: one in temporal matters, and the other in spiritual matters. The first is not incompatible with a woman, because many women have had great and extensive temporal power and still do in various parts of the world. Such also was the power of Deborah, from whom the argument proceeds. But spiritual judicial power belongs to no woman, and consequently neither does order, whose power is simply spiritual.

To the third it must be said that, although there is no distinction between man and woman with respect to the soul in those things that pertain to the merit of eternal life — since in this regard, whether one is a man or a woman, whoever loves God more and conducts himself more virtuously in this world will have a greater reward in eternal life; and in accordance with this the Apostle says: In Christ Jesus there is neither slave nor free, neither male nor female — there is nevertheless a distinction between man and woman with respect to the soul in those things that pertain to office, especially if such an office is one whose exercise the soul pursues by means of the body and by means of bodily operations that pass outwardly, as is the case with the office of any order. And therefore, although order is a certain spiritual power, yet by reason of the annexed office it is not communicable to a woman by divine ordinance.

To the fourth it must be said that the major is not true when speaking of an instrumental agent, and especially with respect to such an effect as is attained by the instrumental cause only according to the external disposition belonging to it by the ordinance of the principal agent, who freely and contingently induces such an effect. When these conditions hold, then notwithstanding the similarity of the patients, an action with respect to one of them can belong to the instrument beyond what belongs to it with respect to the other, according as the ordinance of the principal agent determines the instrument toward one of those patients and not the other. But as regards the conferral of the character or power of which we now speak, the ordaining bishop is an instrument of God, externally disposing — by divine ordinance — the male sex and not the female for the reception of the aforesaid power; therefore, etc.

### Conclusion 3

Conclusion 3 is that it is required of the necessity of precept, though not of the necessity of the sacrament, that the ordinand not be subject to any canonical penalty justly imposed upon him — for instance, that he not be suspended, excommunicated, irregular, interdicted, infamous, deposed, or degraded. All of these can be proved from various citations of law, which for the sake of brevity I here omit.

Regarding irregularity in particular, however, it should be noted that under this penalty a very great many people are comprehended, all of whom are excluded by the necessity of precept from the reception of this sacrament. For this reason it should be known that in general a person can incur irregularity in three ways: in one way through crimes; in another way without crimes; and in a third way through things that are in some manner indifferent — that is, because they are sometimes criminal and sometimes not criminal.
