And blessed Gregorius \[I\] touches upon the same reasoning, saying: “Just as one who has been baptized once ought not to be baptized again, so one who has been consecrated once in the same order cannot and should not be consecrated again.” And from these considerations it is also apparent that, among the four opinions which the Master \[Peter Lombard\] sets out at chapter 3 of this distinction, the third opinion is to be held — for it speaks consistently with the truth just stated.

To the 2nd \[objection\] it can be said that those authorities are to be understood in accordance with the truth I set forth in the first conclusion. For \[the heretics and those subject to canonical penalties\] cannot confer the sacrament of orders lawfully and justly, and with the salvation of the soul of the giver or receiver; and therefore their blessing is called a curse, because what is done with mortal sin is done with God’s curse; and the one ordained is said to receive nothing, because he receives nothing there that redounds to the salvation of his soul. It could also be said that those authorities are to be understood of those heretics who, in conferring sacraments, do not use the form that holy mother Church uses, or who do not intend to confer what the Church intends.

To the 3rd \[objection\] it must be said that the dependence of an inferior power upon a superior can be understood in two ways: in one way, as to coming-to-be and as to conservation; in another way, as to coming-to-be but not as to conservation. In the first way, the major premise is true, but not in the second way. Now the situation is such that while in conferring the sacrament of penance the sacerdotal power depends in the first way not only upon the power of God but also upon the power of the Church, yet in conferring the other sacraments it depends upon the power of the Church in the second way — but not in the first way.

For the understanding of this it must be observed that the power conferred upon some ecclesiastical person can stand in four relations: sometimes it rests on order alone, as the power of confecting the sacrament of the Eucharist; sometimes it rests on order together with an annexed indelible dignity, as the power of ordaining or confirming; sometimes it rests on jurisdiction alone, as the power of excommunicating; sometimes it rests simultaneously on order and jurisdiction, as the power of conferring the sacrament of penance. For as was established above at dist. 17, q. 2, art. 3, it does not suffice to possess order and the keys of the Church in order to be able to absolve someone in the forum of penance; it is further required that one has jurisdiction — either ordinary or delegated — over those one absolves. Since, therefore, the spiritual jurisdiction of any priest depends upon the supreme pontiff, in whom alone resides the fullness of jurisdiction both as to coming-to-be and as to conservation — for he can daily give and take away such jurisdiction as he sees fit for the governance of the state of the Church — therefore the power of excommunicating, which rests precisely on jurisdiction, depends upon the power of the Church or of the supreme pontiff not only as to coming-to-be but also as to conservation, since that which rests upon something is no less dependent than that upon which it rests. And by the same reasoning the power of absolving in the forum of penance, insofar as it rests upon jurisdiction, depends upon the power of the Church both as to coming-to-be and as to conservation. And hence it follows that when jurisdiction is withdrawn by the sentence of law or of a judge from a prelate or priest, they then cannot excommunicate or impose salutary penances; and if they attempt it in fact, what they do is void and null, because the power by which they ought to do these things is not preserved to them, but rather has been nullified by him upon whom the conservation of such power depends.
