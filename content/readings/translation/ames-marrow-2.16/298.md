Thus we are said to love God in men, and men in God — which is one reason for the phrase “beloved in the Lord.”

9. Hence nothing that is contrary to religion can properly be owed to any man. Acts 4:19 & 5:29: *Whether it is right in the sight of God to obey you rather than God, judge for yourselves: we must obey God rather than men.*

10. Hence also the truth of religion cannot stand alongside the neglect of justice and charity toward our neighbor. James 1:27: *Religious worship, pure and undefiled before God and the Father, is this: to visit orphans and widows in their affliction.* 1 John 4:20–21: *If anyone says, “I love God,” and hates his brother, he is a liar. This commandment we have from God: that whoever loves God must also love his brother.*

11. Hence, finally, religion is best proved and tested by justice, according to the frequent usage of Scripture — though this argument serves far more certainly for negation than for affirmation, if it is understood of the outward works and offices of justice. For such works of justice may sometimes be present where true religion is absent; but where true religion is present, they cannot be wholly absent.

12. By the same reasoning, unjust works argue more strongly that a man is ungodly than just works argue that he is godly: thus the works of the flesh are said to be manifest (Galatians 5:19), which is not affirmed of the fruits of the Spirit (verse 12).

13. The ordering of this charity is as follows: God is first and chiefly to be loved by charity, and so serves as, in a sense, the formal ground of charity toward our neighbor. Next after God we are bound to love ourselves — that is, with that charity which regards true blessedness. For in loving God himself with the love of union, we love ourselves immediately with that highest charity which regards our spiritual blessedness. We ought to love others, whom we would have share in the same good, as it were secondarily.
