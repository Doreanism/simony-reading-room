53. A scandal is said to be given in the following cases: either when some manifest sin is committed, or at least something is done that has every appearance of sin and becomes known to others; or when something is rashly done that is not required by God’s command and yet brings spiritual harm to others — and still more so when the very purpose of the action is to lead our neighbor into sin or to trouble him.

54. But if offense arises not from the nature of our action but from the sheer malice of others, it is called an offense taken — as in the case of the Pharisees — and this is not our sin but the sin of those who take offense. Matthew 15:12–14: *Knowest thou not that the Pharisees were offended at that saying? Let them alone: they be blind leaders of the blind.*

55. But although this offense taken cannot be avoided by us, an offense given both can and ought to be avoided. For God never lays upon his \[people\] a necessity of giving offense.

56. That scandal by which one is said (metaphorically) to offend himself — that is, to provide himself with an occasion of sinning — is by analogy referred to \[the category of\] offense given.

57. A real effecting or procuring of our neighbor’s good is when we ourselves perform something that by its own nature tends to our neighbor’s good, without any intervening action on his part. Hebrews 13:16: *To do good and to distribute forget not.*

58. But although all acts of justice ought to have charity joined to them, there are some in which justice shines forth more prominently, and others in which charity more clearly governs.

59. From this arises the distinction by which some duties are said to belong to justice strictly taken, and others to charity — of which difference and formal distribution Christ himself is the author. Luke 11:42: *Ye pass by judgment and the love of God.*

60. Those are acts of justice which involve the consideration of a debt and equality in relation to others.

61. Those are acts of charity whereby the good of another is regarded beyond our debt.

62. The duties of justice take precedence, and bind more strictly, than those which belong to charity.
