# Chapter XVI.

Of Justice and Charity toward Our Neighbor.

*So much for Religion. Justice follows, which is contained in the Second Table.*

1. Justice is a virtue by which we are inclined to perform our duty owed to our neighbor. Thus the duty of children toward their parents is said to be just, Ephesians 6:1. And the duty of masters toward their servants is called right and equitable, Colossians 4:1. All that we owe to our neighbor is fulfilled in living justly.

2. But justice in this context is not taken in its most general sense, as encompassing every duty toward another — for in that sense it would include even religion itself. For the justice called general is nothing other than virtue in general, as was explained earlier when we showed that justice is chief among the general dispositions of virtue. Nor should it be understood in its most narrow sense, concerned only with the quantity of what is deserved or received — for in that case it would cover only a few duties of the Second Table, namely those by which like is returned for like. Rather, justice is here used in a certain middle sense, by which it sets forth the mutual duty between those bound by the same right — and in this sense it contains all the force of the Second Table.

3. Its object is our neighbor, that is, every person — and angel as well — who is or may be with us a partaker of the same end and blessedness, Luke 10:36,37.
