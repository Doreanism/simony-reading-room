As it is, however, since they are nowhere regularly or legitimately settled, they wander here and there carrying about their offices for hire, which they perform nowhere except under a money agreement. Further chapters must be brought forward to demonstrate that such offices are void and invalid, against manifest simoniacs. The Council of Piacenza, held under the presidency of Pope Urbanus, says: “Preserving the statutes of the holy fathers, let the ordination of simoniacs be held invalid.” In that same synod against simoniacs it was also said: “We confirm with apostolic authority what the holy fathers have decreed concerning simony. Whatever, therefore, has been acquired in holy orders or in churches by simony — that is, by a price given or a reward promised, by service or by any stratagem of this sort — shall never obtain any validity. If, however, some have been ordained, and they can prove that when they were ordained they did not know \[that simony was involved\], and if they were at that time regarded as Catholics, we mercifully tolerate their ordinations in the Church; provided, however, that a praiseworthy life commends them. Those, however, who knowingly suffered themselves to be consecrated — or rather execrated — we decree their ordination to be altogether invalid.”

We could gather many chapters consonant with these; but we believe the foregoing sufficient for an intelligent reader to confirm our thesis, by which we hold that all the offices of manifest simoniacs are voided and condemned as utterly invalid in the chapters cited above, just as we assert that the offices of the Donatists and other condemned and excluded persons are altogether invalid, even though they possess the same sacraments as we do.

Here I know that many will object, saying it cannot be that where the integrity of the sacraments is present, anything in the Mass or in other offices can be called invalid. Let us therefore inquire what the word “invalid” means, and what this other word “intact” means, since to some these two words seem to signify things that are, as it were, contrary to one another — things that cannot be predicated simultaneously of one and the same subject, just as “white” and “black,” “sweet” and “bitter,” and other names of contrary things do not simultaneously apply to one and the same subject. We, however, predicate both of one and the same subject — namely, of one and the same sacrament of an excommunicated or condemned heretic — affirming both that it is intact and yet invalid. For, as Augustinus affirms when he argues about Hagar and Ishmael, the same sacrament is known to be equally intact in both heretic and Catholic, if it has been celebrated according to ecclesiastical rite in both cases — the word joining the element — just as, in the case of Ishmael and Isaac born alike from Abraham’s seed, the nobility of their birth was equal as regards their father’s seed. Let us see, therefore, whether a sacrament that is intact in both cases is also ratified in both cases. To help us see this, it is fitting to set down something like a definition of each of the two words, “ratified” and “intact.” We call something “intact” \[integrum\] when it lacks none of its principal constitutive parts. For example, when a house lacks neither roof nor walls nor foundation, it is called intact; but if any of these is missing, it will not be called an intact house — indeed, it will not be called a house at all.
