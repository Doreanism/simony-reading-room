The seventh universal synod, held by the five patriarchs with three hundred and fifty fathers, in the letter it sent to Pope Adrianus, said among other things: “The divine apostle Petrus, together with the others, and your holiness, deposes those who have laid hands on men for money and who continue to do so, as Simon Magus. For more tolerable is the impiety of Macedonius and those around him who attacked the Holy Spirit than the impiety of those who, with some transaction, come and sell the Holy Spirit — the Spirit of God the Father and the Son — in a raving frenzy. For they are also thus constrained to maintain that they cast out that same Holy Spirit \[from his rightful place\]. For every master sells what he has and what he has bought — whether a slave or any of his possessions. Likewise, one who buys something, wishing to be master of what he has purchased, acquires it through the payment of money: and so those who carry out this transaction disparage the Holy Spirit, sinning equally with those who blasphemed by saying that Christ cast out demons in Beelzebub; and beyond doubt the grace of the Holy Spirit — that is, the holiness of the priesthood — is not in them. For Petrus said to Simon: ‘You have neither part nor lot in this matter.’ For both the twentieth and the twenty-eighth canons of the apostles, and their acts, and the third and fourth books of Kings, utterly cut off from the priesthood any who at any time give or receive money — whether before the laying on of hands or after. For ‘to receive’ is to receive at any time.” Likewise from the final letter of that same synod: “Whoever consecrates someone for money — that is, whoever is the consecrator — is estranged from the priesthood, as all of us who are nurtured on canonical discipline as children of the Church know.” Who would not be moved by the consonant authority of so many fathers sitting in one synod, proclaiming the very same thing that our lowly selves also proclaim? If anyone thinks it easy to contradict this, it remains for him to annihilate the authorities of so many fathers who agree with us and who affirm together with us that the simoniac surpasses the heretic Macedonius in his heresy.

But because many concede, concerning the laying on of hands, that if it is conferred for money both the giver and the receiver become heretics — yet concerning other ecclesiastical goods they do not concede the same — let them hear the following, from the letter of Pope Paschalis addressed to the Milanese: “If someone objects that it is not the consecrations themselves but the things that arise from consecration that are being sold, he seems to say something, yet understands nothing at all. For since the corporeal bishop or abbot of a church can no more profit in this life without corporeal and external goods than a soul can live temporally without a body: whoever sells one of these two, without which the other cannot be had, leaves both of them unsanctified. And yet the sacred canon of Chalcedon utterly destroys this objection, since it so strictly forbids the steward or administrator of a church, or any person subject to a rule, from being ordained for money, that it strikes down with the sword of anathema even the intermediaries of so great a crime.” What more need be said? If simoniacs and neophytes have been anathematized and excommunicated and separated from the Church as true heretics, who would not see that the masses and prayers of such priests provoke God to anger over the people, whom we believed to be propitiated by such men?
