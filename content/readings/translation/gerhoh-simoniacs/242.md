Who therefore, being righteous, would boast of a righteousness that is his own — a righteousness that neither human industry nor free will, but only the obedience of Christ, produces? That obedience, indeed, by which he went to the Father and we see him no more. Through that obedience, such great grace is conferred upon our free will in those who believe in Christ and love him for the sake of that very obedience, that within the free will there is also a free counsel for pursuing what is both discerned by free will as just and approved by free counsel as right — so that man’s very righteousness is rightly held to be derived from the aforesaid obedience of Christ. Wherefore the spirit of truth rightly rebukes the world concerning this righteousness not only in those who neglect it, but in those — and far more — who ascribe it to their own powers; for the former are inexcusable on account of free will, which, as you assert in a certain work of yours, is bound by no necessity to sin, and the latter are damnable on account of their craving for vainglory.

For after Christ went to the Father by the way of obedience, which is the source and efficient cause of our justification, whoever from that moment on, having heard of Christ’s victory, serves sin — that is like what it would have been if, after the giant Goliath was laid low and the entire Philistine army put to flight, the people of Israel had chosen rather to remain subject and enslaved to the Philistines than to enjoy the freedom won for them by the strong hand of David. And those who arrogate to themselves either Christ’s victory or any righteousness derived from that victory, or even the praise and glory due to that righteousness, are found to resemble proud Saul, who was overly envious of humble David and yearned to claim David’s glory for himself. For Saul was angry and said: “They have attributed thousands to me, and ten-thousands to David” \[1 Samuel 18:8\]. And now many are angry if the glory owed to God alone is not offered to them instead — because they receive glory from one another and neglect the glory that comes from God alone \[cf. John 5:44\]. These men, as I have said, the spirit of truth rebukes both in your words and in your writings, proving that man’s righteousness is to be attributed to divine grace alone. I now recall that saying of the Apostle which you urgently impressed upon me at the court of Bamberg: “To the King of ages, immortal, invisible, to God alone be honor and glory” \[1 Timothy 1:17\], and that angelic word: “Glory to God in the highest” \[Luke 2:14\]. These two testimonies, drawn from apostolic and angelic lips, you perhaps set before me so that I, a wretch in my pursuit of glory, should not lose the grace of peace invoked upon men of good will. I rejoice, most pious and reverend father, at your solicitude for me; and yet I give thanks to God that I am not conscious in myself of a craving for human and vain glory. I am not conscious of it in myself, I say — yet in this I am not thereby justified. For it is the Lord who judges me; and even this very gift is his in me, that, as it seems to me concerning myself, it matters but little to me to be judged by you or by any human tribunal — nor do I judge myself — because I await with trembling the divine judgment concerning all my deeds, words, and thoughts, my trembling increased by your fatherly admonition in which you said that each of us ought to consider what manner and how great a fruit would come from his labor in the word of God. You were able to say this with confidence, as one who is conscious of great fruits — fruits that, through your planting and watering while God gave the increase, have sprung up from your labor about the word of God.
