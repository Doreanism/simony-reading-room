But if the Holy Spirit, who in the sacraments flees the manifest undisciplined heretic, also flees the disciplined dissembler — that is, the hidden heretic, the wolf clothed in sheep’s wool — who would dare say to him: “Why do you act thus?” We believe nonetheless that the faithful of Christ who do not know these hidden heretics and communicate with their sacraments in the faith of Christ are not defrauded of the gift of Christ and the working of the Holy Spirit, who, as he blows where he wills, so also blows through whom he wills — even through persons like Caiaphas, the killer of Christ, who, though they deserve to be condemned, yet have, by the example of Caiaphas himself, the power to judge even Christ in his members, when, as I have said, they ought rather to be judged and condemned by the members of Christ. As long then as Christ tolerates such men among his members, such a one reigns as a Caiaphas-like high priest in the house of God; his familiarity is no more welcome to the true followers of Christ and the apostles than the familiarity of Caiaphas was welcome to the apostles themselves. But they endure the whitewashed wall unwillingly, until God shall strike it. Those, however, who prop up that same wall are far removed from the apostle Paul, who, standing before one such high priest and being struck at his command, said: “God shall strike you, you whitewashed wall” \[Acts 23:3\]. But let us leave this whitewashed wall, plastered over with a kind of cement of apparent justice, to be struck by God; and let us at least quietly withdraw from it as it is about to fall, as best we can, lest, if we strive to prop it up like posts, we be crushed when it begins to collapse. The posts supporting this wall are learned and powerful clerks, laymen who are enemies of religion, and all their friends of such a kind as was that friend of the high priest who, when he struck Christ, said: “Is that how you answer the high priest?” \[John 18:22\]. The arm of the Lord could indeed have struck back him who struck, and could have instantly overthrown not only him but also the whitewashed wall that so rigid a post was propping up — that is, the one falsely called the high priest — but \[Christ\] accepted the injury, preserved patience, and sought no clemency from so unjust a high priest toward himself. So then let us also, with his help, preserve patience and seek no clemency, good will, or munificence from unjust high priests, if we would rather be saved with Christ than be damned among the flatterers of impious high priests. These Caiaphas-like high priests, sitting in the seat of Moses, share this further kinship with that Caiaphas: just as he held Christ captive and put him to death — not only by his own tongue but also by the Roman hand — so these men hand Christ over as it were captive in the sacraments to those to whom he himself does not wish to be handed over, so that they become guilty of the body and blood of the Lord: not only when they eat and drink judgment to themselves, but also when they hand over the very body of the Lord to be eaten by those whom they ought rather to drive from the Church until they render fitting penance — which is the same as handing Christ over to the gentiles to be mocked, spat upon, and crucified, because, as the apostle witnesses, the guilt of the body and blood of the Lord is incurred when his body and blood are eaten unworthily. But those manifest heretics — severed and withered branches, useless ministers of the sacraments — should not even suppose that they possess Christ or his life-giving body in the way that Caiaphas had him among himself, even though, as has been shown above, they do have the sacrament of the Lord’s body.

Again, let us not console ourselves with the thought that if Christ is not present in their sacraments, we ourselves do not become guilty of the body and blood of the Lord by participating in those sacraments. For it is established that the participants are bound by an equal or even greater guilt: for upon the killers of Christ himself, persevering in their guilt, even that killing was gravely imputed; but the division of Christ’s body would have been imputed yet more gravely, had they torn him apart like butchers and rent him limb from limb and broken his bones.
