But those men cheer us greatly who are accustomed not only to hide with Elijah but also, like him, to manifest themselves in this — that they annihilate the sacrifices of false prophets and slay those same spirits with the sword of the word. But what shall I say of those who never hide with Elijah but always dwell in the cities with Isaiah, Zechariah, Jeremiah, and the other prophets, who had no disciples or very few — and yet those who resist wicked men to their face are rendered glorious before God by this alone: that having experienced mockeries and scourges, and moreover chains and prisons, they were stoned, they were sawn asunder, they were tempted, they were slain by the sword \[cf. Hebrews 11:36–37\]? My soul, thirsting to imitate such men, is nowhere more sweetly refreshed than when it looks upon the presiders of the apostolic see. For through them Simon Magus was crushed; through them, flocks of companions are driven away from ministry at the sacred altar — namely those who fornicate like Nicolaus, Ebion, and Paulus of Samosata, or who traffic in the house of God like Simon Magus himself and Judas, that most wicked merchant. To pass over the rest in which the prince of this world has now been judged, who would judge it fitting to call the impious Simon and his followers — so often already condemned — once more to judgment, as though still awaiting sentence, when the prince of this world has been so definitively judged in him that the holy Gregorius, writing to bishop Victor about those who tolerated Simon, declared: “Whoever has not burned intensely, as befits his office, to correct this crime” — namely the heresy of simony or of neophytism — “let him not doubt that he has his portion with him from whom this accursed sin first took its beginning.” We wish it noted here, however, that we do not say that the prince of this world is condemned in doubtful cases or concerning persons who are unknown — for the cases of such persons await the judgment of the final divine examination. We say that the prince of this world is condemned only in those cases and those persons concerning whom we find that the apostolic see has clearly passed judgment. For that see, employing careful circumscriptions, does not destroy the just with the wicked: therefore its presiders do not judge others together with Nicolaus or Simon Magus, nor name them Nicolaitans or simoniac heretics, except those who, hardened in the sect of their error, knowingly and openly disobedient to the synodal definitions of the holy fathers and the decretal statutes made by them, and recalcitrant against them with the obstinacy of apostates, resist in purpose and will. Against such persons the sentence of anathema has long since been passed, and their offices have been entirely forbidden, although the Church still permits communion with them in shared living, in greeting, and in other such matters — because if they were forbidden to us even in these things, we would have to leave this world, full as it is of Nicolaitans and simoniacs. For who would doubt that hired clerics and those who hire them are simoniac and heretical traffickers? Who would doubt that those ministers of the altar who fornicate, who usurp offices forbidden to them, and who pertinaciously defend such presumption against the teaching of the apostolic see, are heretics — inasmuch as they are at odds with and rebels against the Roman Church? But because it is exceedingly difficult to determine precisely what manner of persons the pertinacious defenders of such presumption and disobedience — as manifested in the usurpation of forbidden offices — are, such that they must be named heretics and publicly avoided, especially if their own bishops instruct that they are to be avoided, or else merely cautiously shunned rather than publicly avoided — especially since they do not defend or teach that what they do ought to be done, or because their own bishops still tolerate them though they have been forbidden by the Roman see — therefore we shall perhaps more correctly count such persons among the wicked and interdicted Catholics rather than among the excluded heretics.
