This judgment of the prince of this world, already accomplished, was well perceived by Roman diligence, which teaches that hired clerics are to be tolerated in no churches, since they are condemned in the wicked Balaam as the first hired man — marked under that same detestable name of Balaam in the Apocalypse of the Apostle John, in the letters of the Apostle Petrus, and in the letter of the Apostle Jude — indeed, anathematized many times over in Simon Magus. These hired men the free mother once judged as sons of the slave woman, saying to God the Father: “Cast out the slave woman and her son” — indeed, also the many sons of concubines, who, though they receive many gifts and revenues by God the Father’s forbearance, shall not be admitted to the inheritance promised to the sons of the free mother alone, even while they join field to field and house to house. But they shall be cast out from the fellowship of noble sons, whether these employers imitate King Balak or the hired men imitate Balaam the diviner, who “loved the wages of unrighteousness.” Because the ass, wishing to avoid the sword it sees menacing such men, speaks quite enough against their foolishness, let us set aside these Balakites and Balaamites as dry chaff reserved for eternal fire — content only to keep clear of them as men who walk disorderly — and not dispute at all about the sacraments celebrated by them. Since above we conceded that they can be counted among the wicked and interdicted Catholics (their wicked and obstinate defenders excepted), let us now turn our pen to those whom we do not hesitate to call heretics, and whom we rightly name simoniac heretics: even if they do not defend the trafficking that has been condemned once and indeed cut off many times — in the same way that Simon Magus did not defend it when, though he was “in the gall of bitterness and the bond of iniquity,” he nonetheless said to the apostles: “Pray for me to the Lord, that none of the things you have spoken may come upon me.” Although therefore those who have been accused of simony but are not manifestly stained with the leprosy either of a simoniacal act or of a simoniacal pact must be summoned to judgment — so that those who still have a voice of denial may have room for defense — yet those hired clerics and their employers are such manifest traffickers in the house of God that not only the act but also the simoniacal pact is so evident in their case that it ought to be established as certain that they have long since been condemned together with Simon Magus and in Simon Magus, and that they have no share or lot in the priesthood of Christ; as Petrus said to Simon Magus: “You have neither part nor lot in this matter, for your heart is not right before God.” For a like case is subject to a like sentence. Did not Petrus, in condemning that magician, set forth the very same cause that no one can now deny exists among hired clerics and their employers? “For your heart,” he says, “is not right before God.” So too now, if the hired clerics ask why they have been proscribed by the vicar of Petrus, let them take as said to themselves what was said to Simon Magus: “For your heart is not right before God” — not right in what respect? Namely, because “you thought you could possess the gift of God with money.”

It is pleasing, bringing the lamp of Scripture closer and letting that light which enlightens every man illuminate the matter, to examine why simoniacs have been condemned more severely than all other heretics.
