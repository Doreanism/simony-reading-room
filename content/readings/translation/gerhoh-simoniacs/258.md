Concerning sacraments celebrated outside the Church but nonetheless according to the ecclesiastical rite, four things have now been demonstrated: namely, that they are integral with respect to their principally constitutive parts; that they are dead, according to the testimony of Augustinus; that they are extinguished, according to the testimony of Pope Leo; and that they are null, according to the testimony of the Council of Piacenza. It is now our pleasure to distinguish between the sacraments themselves — which can be dead, extinguished, and null — and the efficacy of the sacraments, which can never be dead, extinguished, or null, but wherever it exists is living, radiant, and valid. For there is no small difference to be considered between these, and it will readily become apparent if the effects of the sacraments are first distinguished. The effects of the sacraments are not uniform: the term “effect of the sacraments” is used in a passive sense, to designate that by which the sacraments themselves are produced. It is also used in an active sense, to designate what the sacraments themselves produce. The former effect consists in this: that a sacrament is integrally celebrated according to the ecclesiastical rite — for if, in the Mass or in other divine offices, one proceeds according to a different rite than that which the Church holds as divinely transmitted, no sacrament is effected thereby, just as those heretics did who did not baptize in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit, and for that reason what they did was not counted as the sacrament of baptism. Those among them who came to their senses were baptized from scratch within the Catholic Church, as though never baptized; whereas those who had been baptized outside using the ecclesiastical rite, on coming to the Church, were confirmed through the laying on of hands, chrism, and unction, inasmuch as they possessed within themselves that effect of the sacrament whereby the sacrament could be effected even outside the Church. But that active effect which the sacraments produce and bring about exists only within the Catholic Church. The former effect is discerned by the eyes and by hearing, since the sacrament itself — that is, the sign of a sacred reality — is completed from audible words and visible species. The active effect of the sacraments, which the sacraments produce, is perceived only by the eyes of faith. Since a manifold confusion arises if the effects of the sacraments are named without qualification, I beg pardon for the fact that, constrained by necessity, I am calling them the passive and active effects of the sacraments, in a manner hitherto unusual. For the Apostle warns against only profane novelties of expression, not all novelties; and there is nothing profane in a novelty of expression that conveys no new truth about reality. It has always been, and always will be, permissible to set forth the truth of a matter using words adapted to the purpose in whatever way introduces no error — just as the holy fathers did at the Council of Nicaea, who under pressing necessity introduced into the creed the new word “homoousios,” by which they expressed no new reality. So too we, under pressing necessity, are compelled to distinguish by name those effects of the sacraments that are greatly distinct in reality, so that it may be more clearly apparent in what respect we say that heretics lack the effect of the sacraments. For just as the Apostles, when they saw someone casting out demons in the name of the Lord who was not following them, and forbade him, were rebuked by Christ for having forbidden, in respect of what he shared with them, one whom they should rather have invited to follow the one in whose name he worked signs and wonders — so too we could be reproached if we were to rebuke heretics in that respect in which they act together with us, and occasionally cast out demons, namely when they baptize either infants, or others who err, in which they themselves are excusably ignorant; or even those who, at the point of death and unable to obtain Catholic baptizers, receive through heretics what they would have preferred to receive through Catholics. For those so baptized, since they do not deny in their hearts the power of true religion, the sacrament of true religion itself is not to be believed to reside in them in vain.
