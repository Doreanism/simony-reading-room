And indeed this gift is so inestimable that whoever thinks it can be possessed, or bought, or put up for sale at a price is shown to be a son of either damnation or perdition — like that son of perdition to whom Petrus said: *”Your money perish with you in destruction.”* Not so severely, not with such vehemence, was that other son of perdition, the seller of Christ, condemned, that the price of Christ’s blood should perish together with the man himself. For once the trafficker himself was destroyed at some cost, great benefit was provided through that price of his blood: for according to the authority of prophetic Scripture the potter’s field was purchased with it as a burial place for foreigners. But to Simon it is said: *”Your money perish with you in destruction”* — from which we can discern that the crime of Simon is greater than the crime of Judas. For Judas indeed sold Christ, whom, being mortal, he did not understand to be God co-eternal — which we easily perceive from this, that on the very night Christ was betrayed, the disciples are read to have been rebuked with the words: *”So long a time I have been with you, and you have not known me?”* If this could be said to the good disciples, how much more to that one whose avarice — which blinds even wise men’s eyes — blinded him to knowing Christ as God? For in compensation, as it seemed to him, for the ointment that had been lost, which he himself had said could have been sold for three hundred denarii, he was, stirred by avarice, content to acquire a tenth of that sum, namely thirty denarii, from selling Christ — for thirty denarii are a tithe of three hundred. How blind a seller, who for so paltry a price sold one of whom it is read in the Song of Songs: *”His head is finest gold, and his hands are rounded gold, set with jewels”* — far more precious than thirty pieces of silver — indeed, who is that Wisdom of whom it is said: *”She is more precious than all riches, and nothing you desire can compare with her”*! Nevertheless, just as it is said to the rulers of this world that, had they known, they would never have crucified the Lord of glory, so it can be said of him that had he perceived Christ’s golden head, his golden hands, and all else desirable in him, hidden beneath the mortality of flesh, he would never have sold the Lord of glory for so vile a price. Yet through what we say Judas the worst of traffickers is by no means excused — for even if he could know nothing else of Christ than that he was an innocent man and a doer of good works, he deserved, by selling such a man to death for thirty denarii, to inherit the thirty curses foretold against him by the Psalmist, of which the first is: *”Set a sinner over him”* and the last: *”Let them be covered as with a garment with their confusion.”* Nevertheless, both Judas and all the heretics who sinned against the person of the Son are judged more leniently than those who sinned against the person of the Holy Spirit. For example, Arrius sinned more tolerably in his blasphemy by calling the Son a creature than did the impious Macedonius by calling the Holy Spirit a creature. For in order to say that Christ was a creature, Arrius could at first have been led by Christ’s own words spoken of himself according to his humanity and misunderstood by Arrius himself — for example when Christ says, *”The Father is greater than I”* — but when he obstinately resisted the judgment of the three hundred and eighteen fathers, consonant in their declaration of the consubstantiality of Father and Son, pronounced in one Spirit and confirmed by the indubitable authority of the Holy Spirit, and thereby judged himself unworthy of eternal life, then Arrius sinned not only against the Son but also against the Holy Spirit. For the indubitable authority of the Holy Spirit is the unanimous judgment of the orthodox fathers, all saying the same thing and thinking the same thing on some point; and whoever, scorning this, is driven by a discordant spirit in such a way that he never returns from dispersion to the unity which the Holy Spirit works — that man, as the blessed Augustinus witnesses, speaks a word against the Holy Spirit, to be forgiven neither in this age nor in the age to come.
