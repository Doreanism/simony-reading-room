Many people, however, suppose that the integrity and form of the sacraments can be verified both outside and inside the Church, while the efficacy of those same sacraments operates faithfully both within and without. This was never his view, for he proves that the sacraments of heretics, however integral they may be, are nonetheless dead and entirely stripped of all virtue. Nor are these two things contradictory — that something may be integral and yet dead — for we have seen a human body that is whole and yet lifeless. It frequently happens that a branch remains intact and yet contains no vital sap. A limb may preserve the form of a limb while no longer performing its function. He is therefore not contradicting himself when he defends the sacraments of heretics as integral and never to be repeated, while at the same time proving them to be dead. For in his sermon on blasphemy against the Holy Spirit he says: “The visible form of a branch can exist even apart from the vine, but the invisible life of the root cannot exist except in the vine. Accordingly, the bodily sacraments which those who are cut off from the unity of Christ’s body both carry and celebrate can possess the outward form of true religion. The invisible and spiritual power of true religion cannot reside in them” — just as sensation does not follow a severed limb. Is not Augustinus, in saying this, proving that the sacraments of heretics are dead, even as he teaches that they are, with respect to their outward form, integral — by reason of which integral form the Roman authority teaches that they are not to be repeated for those returning to the Church, but are to be confirmed by the invocation of the Holy Spirit alone through the laying on of hands? Hence Pope Leo, writing to Nicetas, patriarch of Aquileia, says the following: “Those who received baptism from heretics, not having been baptized before, are to be confirmed solely by the invocation of the Holy Spirit through the laying on of hands, since they received the washing of baptism without the power of sanctification. And this rule we proclaim to be observed in all churches: that the bath, once administered, is not to be regarded as having been given in vain, since the Apostle says, ‘One God, one faith, one baptism,’ whose cleansing is not to be violated by repetition. But, as we have said, sanctification is to be sought by the invocation of the Holy Spirit alone, so that what he received from a heretic, he may obtain from Catholic priests.”

Let heretics rejoice and glory in the integrity of their sacraments — provided only that it is established on our part that those same sacraments are utterly dead, utterly extinguished. Hence the same Pope Leo, speaking to the Emperor Leo concerning the excommunicated Alexandrians, who nevertheless celebrated the sacraments according to the ecclesiastical rite, says the following: “It is plain to whom your piety ought to come to the aid and whom it ought to oppose, lest the Alexandrian church, which has always been a house of prayer, should now become a den of robbers. For it is manifest that through the most cruel and monstrous frenzy the entire holy light of the sacraments has been extinguished there; the offering of sacrifice has been intercepted, the sanctification of chrism has ceased, and all the mysteries have withdrawn themselves from those parricial hands.” See, we have proved that the sacraments of heretics are dead and extinguished. Let us now affirm that those same sacraments are null, unless they have been confirmed by the Church. Let us now agree with the Council of Piacenza, which affirms that whatever has been acquired through holy orders or in ecclesiastical affairs by gift or promise of money, by servile compliance, or by persuasive speech, is wholly null and void and of no force. That synodal decree strikes also those who, having lost their offices through some crime, sought to recover them and reacquired them through gifts or promises of money, through undue compliance, or through persuasive speech.
