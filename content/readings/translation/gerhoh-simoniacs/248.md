But Simon Magus, or any other simoniac, is convicted of being a blasphemer against the Holy Spirit at the very inception of the simoniacal traffic, in which the gift of God is thought to be possessable with money. For that the Holy Spirit is called and is the gift of God is so evident that it requires no proof. Hence that which Christ said to the Samaritan woman — “If you knew the gift of God, and who it is that is speaking to you, you would have asked him, and he would have given you living water” — we find expounded by great doctors in such a way that by “the gift of God” of which Christ speaks we understand nothing other than the Holy Spirit. This being so, whoever thinks that the gift of God can be possessed with money reduces the Holy Spirit — who, equally with the Father and the Son, is the creator of all things — to a price, which is certainly a price created by the Holy Spirit the creator. When therefore he compares or subordinates the creator to a vile creature, he offends far more gravely than Arrius, since Arrius indeed asserted that the Son of God was a creature, but nonetheless one of the highest excellence, to whom he did not allow even angelic dignity to be considered equal. The simoniac, however, believing and thinking that the gift of God — that is, the Holy Spirit — can as if it were his own property be bought from him with money by a man (when everything a man possesses as his own property is lower and inferior to the man himself), places the Holy Spirit who is the gift of God — indeed who is God — beneath man and equates an inestimable grace with a price, and compares with anything the incomparable, if we consider all creatures; by none of which can the creator Spirit be valued or compared — any more than the Father or the Son, of both of whom this one Spirit is equally the Spirit, equally free as the Father and the Son. Indeed, while the Son, “taking the form of a servant,” was for thirty-three years a servant, made a little lower even than the angels who served him, the Holy Spirit is of such great freedom that he even makes free the servants whom he fills; hence that word of the Apostle: “Where the Spirit of the Lord is, there is liberty.” Likewise: “But if you are led by the Spirit, you are not under the law.” For the yoke of the law, which works wrath, and the yoke of sin, which weighs heavily, is removed only by this Spirit when he comes, according to that word of Isaiah: “The yoke will rot from before the oil.” Does not a man therefore commit a great and wicked sacrilege when he sells or buys this utterly free Spirit — this Spirit who is the author of all angelic or human liberty and liberation — as if he were his own slave? Indeed, since he cannot in fact do this, he thinks that he can buy or sell one who does not come to men through any purchase, but rather, being free, “breathes where he wills,” and divides his gifts “to each one individually as he wills” — not as he is purchased at a price, not as ambition demands, but, as I said, “as he wills.” Wicked Macedonius indeed sinned against this Holy Spirit by asserting that he was the servant of Father and Son, though to be set above all the angels; but the simoniac sins far more gravely, thinking that this most free Spirit can be sold by him as his own slave. And whoever strives to buy him both loses the price and does not acquire what he has priced, but the entire leprosy of the seller migrates in a wondrous way — while not departing from the seller himself — to the buyer, so that both buyer and seller may perish among the members of that one who is “exalted above everything that is called God.” For the simoniacal seller and buyer is exalted above the Holy Spirit, who is said and is, with the Father and the Son, one and supreme God; and in that presumption the Holy Spirit’s divinity is denied, since the Holy Spirit is placed beneath a vile creature, as has been shown — which is proved to be the most manifest crime and blasphemy against the Holy Spirit, and is more wicked than the crime of Macedonius and of those who with him blasphemed the Holy Spirit by calling him the servant of Father and Son.
