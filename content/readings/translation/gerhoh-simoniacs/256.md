For it is not more holy because of these additions than it was before, when it was consecrated by the Lord’s words alone and by the Lord’s Prayer alone; but it was above all fitting that faith, which at that time was still unpolished and, as a certain man both learned and faithful says, “turbid with rustic cultivation, with bare shoulders, uncut hair, and exposed arms,” should, where it could be adorned, be gilded above all in this part as if at its very crown, and be fitly decorated with little golden chains wrought with silver. Therefore, although, as I have said, the integrity of the sacrament established by Christ is neither increased by such additions nor diminished by their omission, yet if anyone rashly were to pluck away what the holy fathers have added to the Lord’s words, he would have to be likened to the soldiers who tore apart and divided the garments of Christ. But those Lord’s words fashioned from on high throughout, where Christ is presented speaking in his own voice and saying: “Take and eat of this, all of you; for this is my body. Take and drink of this, all of you; for this is the cup of my blood,” and so on down to “Do this in memory of me” — those words, I say, poured over the bread and wine, by the joining of which to the element of bread and wine mixed with water the sacrament is made, represent the likeness of that seamless tunic woven from on high throughout, which even Christ’s crucifiers themselves were not permitted to rend. This is the word that abbreviates and consummates all the integrity that exists in the sacrament of bread and wine, if it has been applied to the same element to which Christ applied it — that is, according to the ecclesiastical rite which we know was handed down by Christ himself. But if someone baptizing or someone offering sacrifice were to abandon the tradition of Christ, so as to employ other species of elements than those which Christ instituted in performing the sacraments, or to pass over and despise the very words of Christ himself, such a person does not perfect the integrity of the sacrament; indeed, what he performs no more bears the name of a sacrament than a thing can bear the name of a house when it is established to lack either roof or walls or foundation. A thing could still be a house if it lacked painted walls, or nail-fastened tiles, or other such things; and a statement could still stand if it happened to lack all the parts of speech except noun and verb — but with noun and verb removed, there would be nothing left from which a statement could be constituted. So too in the celebration of the offices of baptism or Mass, certain things are sometimes omitted that are known to have been added by the holy fathers — as when we baptize an infant in peril of death and recite there only the words of the one triune name, or when we sometimes omit the Gloria in excelsis and the Credo in unum — and yet we perfect the integrity of the sacraments. But if in baptizing we were to refuse to name even one person of the three, or if in offering sacrifice, reciting all that the holy fathers have added, we were not to introduce Christ speaking at his Supper — what we perform cannot be called a sacrament. Hence the holy fathers prohibit the rebaptism of those baptized by heretics in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit, recognizing in them the character of Christ, that is, the integrity of the sacrament. But those baptized by heretics who did not baptize in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit they judged should not so much be rebaptized as baptized from scratch, because the sacrament of baptism is not recognized where the invocation of the triune name over the element of visible water does not take place according to the rite handed down by God. The Donatists, however, and many other heretics celebrate the divine sacraments according to ecclesiastical rite; and therefore the blessed Augustinus argues forcefully in certain of his writings against those who rebaptize those baptized by such persons and against those who reordain those ordained by such persons, defending the integrity of sacraments celebrated, albeit by heretics — the integrity, I say, not the utility of such sacraments.
