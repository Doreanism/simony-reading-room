EXPLICIT OPUSCULUM AD ABBATEM.

For bishops have tithes and estates and villages, just as the Levites of old had tithes and cities with their suburbs for the pure fulfillment of the altar’s ministry — yet it is remarkable what we must say: bishops feed themselves from episcopal resources for secular courts and the business of this world; and from elsewhere they demand sustenance for the actual episcopal ministries themselves. Consecrating churches, ordaining clergy, confirming Christians — these belong to the episcopal office; tithes, first-fruits, and other ecclesiastical revenues are meant to support the bishop in this office, and therefore they are properly to be required for the use of the bishop and the clergy who assist him. If anything beyond this is demanded in the administration of spiritual offices, the leprosy of Gehazi is contracted, and in such administration one serves not God but Mammon. Hence the most just censure of Pope Honorius is to be praised, who deposed Otto, the so-called Bishop of Halberstadt, and declared him a simoniac by his letters, because he was convicted of having demanded money at the consecration of a certain church under the pretext of service. Therefore, O all you to whom the Bridegroom says: “Catch for us the little foxes that destroy the vineyards” — catch this fox, ancient in time but ever new through the constant renewal of its frauds. Separate yourselves from oratories that, through an open agreement of payment, through the exaction of undue service, have been not so much consecrated as desecrated. For if you count such churches as truly consecrated, you will be disobedient to the Roman See. For Pope Alexander II teaches — as we have noted above — that churches consecrated through an agreement of payment are not so much consecrated as desecrated, and are in no way to be considered churches. The same, celebrating a synod of more than one hundred bishops in the Constantinian church, struck this fox of which we speak with three blows. For among the other chapters of that council it is read: That churches and altars are neither to be sold nor bought; and whoever henceforth sells or buys shall be subject to anathema. Item: That churches consecrated for a price or by simoniacs are to be consecrated anew. Item: That priests ordained knowingly by simoniacs from the time of Pope Nicholas up to now and henceforth shall know themselves to be deposed. Struck by such blows, the fox might have seemed utterly destroyed, were it not perhaps that very beast of which is read in the Apocalypse: “Which had the wound of the sword and yet lived.” The Arian pestilence or any other heresy is then extinguished by the wound of the sword when it is condemned and anathematized by the judgment of a synod. Simony, however, although struck more than once by the sword of anathema, yet still lives, because the mortal wound seems to have been healed by the fact that even certain religious men receive the sacraments of manifest simoniacs, although these have been many times forbidden by the Roman pontiffs as if by the very mouth of Petrus himself.
