But if a branch is cut from the vine, it can no longer bear good fruit; it neither lives nor has any use except to be thrown into the fire and burned. So then, you heretics and schismatics, cut off like branches from the body of the Church, cut away by the weeding-hoes of your invented growths — do not suppose that you can bear living fruit. Likewise, you presumptuous ones and overconfident champions of free will, who think you have no need of the aiding and merciful grace of God, as if by your own will’s swiftness you could seize the fruit of eternal life: know that you are to be taken up by the terrible hand of the vinedresser and cast into the fire, unless you confess that all beauty and all loveliness of fruit proceeds not from yourselves but from the innermost root of the vine. For our faithful and truthful Witness says: “He who abides in me and I in him, he bears much fruit, for without me you can do nothing” \[John 15:5\]. Plainly nothing — for sin is nothing, and nothing is what we can do on our own apart from him. For sin is no corruption of substance. Likewise, a lie is to be reckoned as nothing. A lie is that by which something is signified to be what it is not, or not to be what it is. When we hear someone lying, we customarily say: “You are saying nothing,” which is the same as saying: “What you say does not exist; it is not so in the reality of things as your words signify.” And therefore a lie is rightly called nothing, because it has nowhere to subsist when the signified thing is removed or denied. So, after Christ was born — of whose future birth circumcision was the sign — as the apostle affirms: “Circumcision is nothing” \[1 Corinthians 7:19\], that is, it is a false sign, because whatever it signified as yet to come is now past, now that Christ has been born of the seed of Abraham; it was for that reason placed in that part of the body through which seed is transmitted, so that there would be no doubt concerning the seed of Abraham from which the one promised to Abraham for the blessing of all nations was to be born. Once he was born, whoever circumcises himself does nothing, because, as the apostle testifies: “Circumcision is nothing, and uncircumcision is nothing, but the keeping of the commandments of God” \[1 Corinthians 7:19\]. In the same way, whoever celebrates the sacraments of the Church given \[for use\] within the Church, outside the Church, does nothing — because there he applies the sign of a sacred thing where the sacred thing itself cannot exist. He offers the sign of sanctification where nothing is sanctified, and therefore that sign or sacrament is nothing. Truly therefore, “without me you can do nothing.” For this is the testimony of Truth, which Truth itself, without itself, has spoken: “For without me you can do nothing.” This is so true that the sacrament itself — that is, the sign of the sacred thing — whose outward form the severed branches display, is nothing: a form lacking all fruit, all power, all the greenness and sap of the true vine, and therefore in every respect like the circumcision of Jews who blaspheme Christ. For just as the Jews blaspheme Christ yet carry the sign that points to his nativity, so heretics — and above all, as has been shown, simoniacs — blaspheme the Holy Spirit, yet celebrate the signs that signify the operation and power of the Holy Spirit.

We wish the reader to bear in mind that, as we determined above, we are treating only of manifest and undoubted heretics who have been condemned by synodal decree; and we assert only those simoniacs to be guilty before a council, condemned as such in the holy councils, who have been shown to be in the gall of bitterness and the bond of iniquity by the fact that their simony is clearly established, and their office accordingly interdicted by the Roman See. For when simoniacs or other heretics are so hidden that they are regarded as catholics by catholics, we do not judge them guilty before a council — as one who says “Raca” to his brother is guilty before the council — but we consider them guilty before a tribunal, as we say that one who is angry with his brother is guilty before a tribunal: namely not a human tribunal but a divine one, before which belong all things done in secret. What truth may hold regarding the sacraments of hidden heretics we think must be left to the judgment of that One who, if he even works some power of salvation through such persons not yet openly excluded, thereby commends his great mercy.
