compelled by necessity I exceeded the bounds of brevity, and I have laid out before you, as before a spiritual father, my thinking — that which offends secular clerics and especially hired clergy — so that where you approve of what I hold, you may show yourself to me as defender and helper, and where you judge anything among them deserving of rebuke, as my corrector; adjured and besought by him from whom comes the ability to do what I ask of you. For if in our words and writings there are discerned those tokens by which Thamar is read to have been defended from burning — namely the ring, the staff, and the bracelet, by which are understood faith, hope, and charity — I beseech you, do not believe that my soul has played the harlot with the father of lies, but rather confess that she has been made pregnant by the seed of Judah, that is of Christ, so that after the example of Thamar she might bring forth, as it were, a son who would be called Phares, that is “division” — for God is my witness that with no grasping after my own glory, but with zeal for truth alone, either do I divide myself from the sacraments of heretics, or I demonstrate by words and writings that there is a great division and distinction between their sacraments and those of catholics, without prejudice to better \[arguments\], as I have said. Not long ago, compelled by necessity, I came to the lord Pope Innocentius at his own summons; when he examined me and asked by what faith I live, in what hope I rejoice, and by what love toward God and the holy Church I am moved — thanks to Christ’s gift — it was as though he had heard from my own lips about the man from whom these things originate; he received me with gracious favor, and by his letters commended my humble person to my lord the Archbishop of Salzburg for his protection — whom I desired above all others to be appointed and to serve as my defender, because he himself, no less than I, and indeed by reason of the greatness of his authority far more, that distinguished archbishop than I, a lowly cleric, strives to obtain this: that the prince of this world should already be judged in such a way, not only in himself but also in the members separated from the Church, that he is to be utterly despised, even if through empty sacraments he is found transformed into an angel of light. Hence, enkindled by zeal for justice, he does not permit in his diocese either hired clergy or manifestly incestuous persons to minister at the altar. For since he has a broad and wide diocese extending for a journey of ten days or more, you could not find within so large a territory so much as one hired cleric or one cleric openly guilty of incest. He also expelled irregular canons from their cloisters as true Canaanites unworthy of the holy land, and in their place introduced regular canons as true Israelites. Though we approve his action — comparing the irregular to Canaanites and the regular to Israelites — we nonetheless know that among the irregular there are some who will be saved, and among the regular some who will be damned, just as in Babylon some have lived praiseworthily and in Jerusalem some have perished. But because along the rivers of Babylon there is no safe seat, and on the banks of those same rivers — as exceedingly muddy — there is no sure footing, the good behavior of good men living there in no way commends Babylon itself, just as the dwelling of the wicked does not condemn Jerusalem, so that of these two cities it may be said of one: “Go out from her”; and of the other: “Thither the tribes went up, the tribes of the Lord, to give thanks to the name of the Lord.” And therefore it is not without reason that we rejoice wherever an irregular congregation, as if a Babylon, is destroyed, and we see Jerusalem built up in the restoration of common life from what are, so to speak, its finer stones. We rejoice, I say, and we ask that our joy may be made full through Christ our Lord. Amen.
