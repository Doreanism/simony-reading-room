## Sacraments and Sacrifice

These sayings of the Fathers are so patent and manifest that they plainly convict simoniacs and other heretics of having no priesthood and no sacrifice — or rather, they prove they have no true sacrifice, even though they possess a true and complete sacrament when sacrificing according to the ecclesiastical rite. For “sacrifice” and “sacrament” are two distinct terms, and what they signify differs greatly: the life-giving body of Christ is properly called a sacrifice, whereas it is not properly called a sacrament except insofar as it presents a species to the senses. That species may not incongruously be called a sacrament, inasmuch as it is the sign of the one body that is the Church — composed of many persons as from many grains into one unity, and made one body as of one loaf. This union is not signified to us by the existence of Christ’s body, which is far too remote from our senses, but by the species and form that is seen and sensed. For since a sacrament is a visible sign of invisible grace, I do not venture to call the secret body of Christ — hidden from our sight — a sacrament, because it does not signify a sacred thing but is the sacred thing, unless perhaps it is called a sacrament by its effect, because it hallows the one who partakes worthily, as an ornament is so called because it adorns. Hence I am compelled to wonder at the opinion of certain masters who affirm that by both the flesh and blood of Christ — each invisible and intelligible — the visible and tangible body of Christ is signified, since invisible things are customarily signified by visible ones, not visible things by invisible ones; as the authority of blessed Augustinus also affirms: “A sign is something that, beyond the species which it presents to the senses, causes something else to come to mind.” Therefore heretics, schismatics, and all those cut off by the gospel’s pruning-hook may be able to possess the identity of the sacrament and the sign, but it does not follow from this that they likewise possess with us the identity of the sacrifice, for whom that lamb is the unique sacrifice — the lamb who, as the most evident truth of the authorities cited above demonstrates, is eaten in one house alone.

As for those authorities that may seem in some places to point in the opposite direction — affirming that the same things exist, after the manner of the four rivers of Paradise, both inside and outside Paradise — their diversity does not trouble us at all; for we do not deny that the integrity of the sacraments can exist outside the Church: what we deny, following the assertion of the authorities cited above, is the truth of the sacrifice. Nor do those other authorities, rightly considered, stand in opposition to these, for they affirm not that a holy sacrifice but an integral sacrament, not the Lord’s body but the sacrament of the Lord’s body, can exist outside the Church. In determining this matter from these diverse — not contrary — authorities, it must be carefully noted that a great difference is found among sacraments and sacraments.
