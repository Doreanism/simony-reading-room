Likewise we not improperly call something “ratified” when we mean to signify that it can never lose the power it contains — as, for example, a proposition or statement that contains or signifies a truth that is immutable, either affirming that what cannot not be is, or the converse, can be called a “ratified” statement; and it can fittingly be called an utterance of reason \[oris ratio\], since “reason” \[ratio\] is derived from “ratified” \[ratum\]. Though even a false statement is called a statement, it does not produce a ratified — that is, firm or true — understanding, and therefore, just as a false statement can rightly be called false, so too an invalid statement can rightly be called invalid, since it lacks a ratified and true understanding — because the state of affairs is otherwise than the statement signifies it to be. Let this be an example of what we are saying. If someone proposes “God is truthful”: this proposition is so true and so ratified that everyone who has received his testimony can subscribe and sign to it, because God is truthful. For what truthful men say, they mean to be true; what they not only say but also write, they indicate more strongly and more durably to be true; and what they have said, written, and sealed with some inviolable seal, they mean to remain true under that seal itself. Hence John, speaking of Christ, says: “He who has received his testimony has set his seal that God is truthful.” In what is he truthful, unless because, just as he spoke to our fathers Abraham and to his seed forever, so he did, and does, and shall do forever? In what is he truthful, unless because, sending his Son to be incarnate, he visited and made redemption of his people, and raised up a horn of salvation for us in the house of David his servant — in no other manner, at no other time, from no other tribe, in no other village, than through a virgin in the fullness of time, from the tribe of Judah, in Bethlehem the village of David, as he spoke through the mouth of his holy prophets, who have been from of old? And so, having received the testimony of Christ, we rightly believe, rightly write in our heart what we believe, and seal what is written — to wit, that God is truthful. For this proposition is not only intact but also ratified. It is certainly intact, because no principal constitutive part is missing from this statement; for just as the principal constitutive parts of a house are roof, walls, and foundation — if any one of these is missing, the house is not intact, indeed it will not be a house at all — so the principal constitutive parts of a statement or proposition are noun and verb, and if either of these is missing, the proposition or statement will lack integrity. In treating of that kind of statement which dialecticians call enunciative, we take “noun” and “verb” broadly, including pronouns with nouns and participles with verbs — so that, in so weighty a matter, with so slight a determination, as it were with a small fan we may drive away the persistent flies of sophisms. An intact statement, then, will not be one lacking a noun or verb — indeed, one that lacks a principal and principally constitutive part of a statement should not even be called a statement at all. But this statement “God is truthful” is entirely intact, because it lacks none of its principally constitutive parts, even though it lacks the secondary parts of a statement — conjunction, preposition, and the rest — whose absence or removal does not diminish the integrity of the statement, that is, does not destroy the statement as a statement, any more than the removal of pegs or tiles destroys a house. Pardon me, reader, for lingering so long over such trifles — but what we have said, once noted, is of value to us for demonstrating the integrity of any sacrament. Let us say again: “God is truthful” is an intact statement — intact, I say, lacking none of its principally constitutive parts. But if it were only intact and not also ratified, it would by no means need to be sealed according to the counsel of John the Baptist, but would need to be blown away, however intact it might be. But this statement is so true and so ratified that it would be more possible for heaven and earth to be reduced to nothing than for this statement to be made invalid; for what it signifies is so true, exists so unchangeably, that it can never not be.
