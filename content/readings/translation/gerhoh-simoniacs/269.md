## Cap. 1

Compelled by necessity, I came to the lord Pope Innocentius at his own summons. When he examined me and asked by what faith I live, in what hope I rejoice, and by what love toward God and the holy Church I am moved — thanks to Christ’s gift — it was as though he had heard from my own lips about the man from whom these things originate; he received me with gracious favor, confirmed the little work I had then composed on this very question, and by his letters commended my humble person to my lord the Archbishop of Salzburg for his protection. I desired him above all others to be appointed and to serve as my defender, because he himself — no less than I, and indeed, by reason of the greatness of his authority, far more so, that distinguished archbishop than I, a lowly cleric — strives to obtain this: that the prince of this world should be judged in such a way, not only in himself but also in the members separated from the Church, that he is to be utterly despised, even if through empty sacraments he is found transformed into an angel of light.

I have played the fool in speaking of things that greatly surpass me, but because under compulsion I have said what I have said, I believe and hope that my imperfection can be perfected by that incarnate Wisdom of God herself; compelled by her adversaries, I became a fool, as I said — not only by scrutinizing things higher than myself, but also by writing to a man of the greatest wisdom when I am most foolish. I am not unaware of who speaks to whom; but following the example of Abraham, who said: “I will speak to my lord, though I am dust and ashes,” I too — though I am obscure by name among detractors — address you, holy man, Abbot of Clairvaux; you, I say, illustrious in name and in life. Once I had begun to speak, compelled by necessity I exceeded the bounds of brevity, and I have laid out before you, as before a spiritual father, my thinking — that which has caused offense — so that where you approve of what I hold, you may be my defender and helper, and where you judge anything among them deserving of rebuke, you may be my corrector.

## Cap. 2

Who is he \[i.e., Simon the fox\], and setting aside his own skin, then appears in such a form as can scarcely be recognized, when he reproaches the fault of another, and what he is doing does not appear at all — as though an innocent sheep? For when all simoniac prelates do not openly receive gifts for this cause, they cloak that very simoniac avarice under the name of an offering — quite improperly, to be sure, since an offering is one thing and an exaction another; what is given freely is one thing, and what is extorted and taken away even from the unwilling is quite another. The blessed Benedictus did not command this in his Rule; on the contrary, he forbade it, teaching that the poor who offer their sons should make a simple petition, while from wealthy parents he does not permit anything to be demanded; yet if they offer something of their own will, he allows it to be received — and what is received in this way may be called an offering, not an exaction. Christ too, when urging a certain rich man toward perfect conversion, did not say: “Go, sell what you have, and bring it to me”; but rather: “Give to the poor, and come, follow me.” The Apostle Petrus likewise, presiding over the common life in Jerusalem, demanded from no one what was placed at his feet as a voluntary offering. He did not demand that Ananias and Sapphira give their goods to Christ; but when they had already dedicated them to Christ, he claimed them, and executed fitting punishment on those who defrauded \[the community\]. By this example, great severity is owed to those who defraud tithes or other offerings already destined for the use of the common life, and bishops and other ministers of the altar ought not to be blamed when they demand the stipends owed to them, or sternly punish those who defraud such things. But what they demand in \[the administration of\] their divine ministries that is not owed — what is that but the most open simony, clothed in a new cloak so as to be unrecognizable, while Simon Magus seeks to be seen as Simon Peter?
