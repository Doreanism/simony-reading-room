Hence when all things had been accomplished that were written about his passion, \[they did to him\] all that was in their power. But had they not been divinely restrained, they would gladly have broken everything with their associates, and perhaps would have torn and divided him in pieces, as we read of those who were torn apart and rent limb from limb in the schisms they form. But holy Scripture, which foretold Christ’s killing, nowhere foretold — indeed, in every way forbade — the division of Christ himself; it also forbade the breaking of his legs, the Lord saying through Moses concerning the paschal lamb as a type of Christ: “It shall be eaten in one house” \[Exodus 12:46\] — which amounts to the same thing: “That one lamb shall not be divided among different houses, but shall be eaten in one house.”

And in that communion of his let there be this \[consequence\]. The fault therefore of the wicked who receive the body and blood of the Lord unworthily in the Church is to be reckoned, by the apostle’s judgment, as a kind of killing of Christ, since he asserts that the one who eats and drinks unworthily is guilty of the body and blood of the Lord \[1 Corinthians 11:27\]. And this fault is indeed great, but not out of keeping with the Scriptures. A heretic or schismatic, however, is charged not so much with the killing of Christ as with the division of Christ, because, contrary to all the authority of sacred Scripture, he thinks that Christ, the common Lord, can be eaten in diverse and multiple houses, when the law says: “It shall be eaten in one house.” Although Christ is never actually divided, yet those who contentiously affirm that the paschal lamb is eaten both outside and inside the Church become guilty of this division in their own thinking. The heretic or schismatic is, moreover, worse, more detestable, and more execrable than even the Jews and soldiers who killed Christ, because what those could not do, these attempt to do — but in vain, for holy Scripture has not granted that Christ be divided and eaten both outside and inside the one house of the Church, as the following testimonies of the Fathers most evidently prove. Cyprianus in his letter to Cornelius says among other things: “Someone thinks that things of the highest and greatest importance come about without the knowledge or permission of God, and that priests — that is, the dispensers of God — are ordained without his sanction. This is to have no faith in those whom we see; this is to give God no honor. But those priests who are made outside the Church are not ordained by his sanction, who act contrary to the dispensation and tradition of the Gospel, as the Lord himself says through the twelve prophets: ‘They have set up a king for themselves, but not through me’ \[Hosea 8:4\]. And again: ‘Their sacrifices are like the bread of mourning; all who eat of them shall be defiled’ \[Hosea 9:4\]. And through Isaiah the Holy Spirit cries and says: ‘Woe to you, rebellious children, says the Lord: you have taken counsel, but not through me, and have made a covenant, but not through my spirit, in order to add sin upon sin’ \[Isaiah 30:1\].” The same author, to Magnus the presbyter: “The Church is one, which cannot be divided entirely and indivisibly. For if \[the Church\] is with Novatian, it was not with Cornelius; but if it was with Cornelius, Novatian is not in the Church and cannot be counted as a bishop.” Hieronymus on the Twelve Prophets: “God hates the sacrifices of heretics and casts them away from himself; and as often as they have gathered in the name of the Lord, he will abominate their fragrance and close his ears. But ‘to hate,’ ‘to cast away,’ and ‘not to smell’ are spoken after the manner of human speech, so that we may understand the disposition of God in our own terms.” The same author, speaking of the paschal lamb, says among other things: “Let us not suppose that this lamb can be eaten everywhere.
