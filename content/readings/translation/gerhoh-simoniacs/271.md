\[Simony\] is forbidden. Simony plainly still lives — not, however, in such a state that those who see the death of Simon Magus walking through the lands regard it as alive; rather, for just cause we yield and concede that Simon Magus still lives in some spiritual power. Yet \[in truth\] he is already dead, and dies daily, and is reckoned as a rotting corpse by the elect who belong to Christ and who flee his contact, in accordance with what the Law forbade: that no one should touch a carcass. And whoever had touched one, the Law judged unclean. Note well that the Law absolutely forbids touching or eating a carcass. It does allow, under urgent necessity, that a carcass be carried, saying thus: “And if it be necessary to carry any of these dead things, he shall wash his garments, and be unclean until evening.” In view of this, we yield indeed that under urgent necessity a carcass must be carried; but we never concede that it is to be touched or eaten. For example: when we deal with a simoniac who, though some contend he is alive, we do not doubt is dead — we never concede that such a dead man is to be touched, because one must not consent to his sin. Still less do we agree that such a dead man is to be eaten, because his deeds are not to be imitated. Nevertheless we confess that, under urgent necessity, this dead man is sometimes to be carried, until a pit is dug for the sinner into which he is cast and carried no further. The pit will then be ready for the stinking corpse when, by God’s action either directly or through legitimate judges, he is cast out of the Church into the grave — either of infernal punishment, or, what is more to be desired, of penitential punishment. Where there is no opportunity to execute judgments against the false \[ones\], it is necessary that the carcasses be carried; but not without tearful compunction, by which we ought to wash our garments — that is, so to form and purify our outward conduct that we may not appear to tolerate sinners who have been excommunicated in councils more out of will than out of necessity. We wash our garments also when we pour out tears and prayers for them, lest perchance, as the Apostle says, God may give them over to a reprobate mind; but that they may recover themselves from the snares of the devil, by whom they are held captive at his will. Thus King David carried Saul — divinely deposed from the kingdom, yet still powerful by human support. The living king in God carried the king dead to God, but nevertheless did not, as it were, touch him: for he did not approve his deeds; rather, lest he be forced to touch him, he fled from him as a fugitive into the forests and at last into exile. David also washed his garments, in that he did not exult over the fall of his enemy, but mourned with great mourning over Saul and Jonathan his son. The Apostle Paulus likewise carried the high priest Ananias, but by no means approved his priesthood — to whom he foretold his destruction, saying: “God shall strike you, you whited wall.” Paulus also washed his garments, having great sadness for his brothers. And so, instructed by the examples of King David and the Apostle Paulus, let us carry the dead wherever it is necessary, yet as far as we can, decline their contact.

I have played the fool in treating matters above me; but because under compulsion I have said what I have said, I believe and hope that my imperfection will be perfected by that incarnate Wisdom of God herself; compelled by her adversaries, I became a fool — not only, as I said, by treating things beyond me, but also by writing to a man of the greatest wisdom when I am most foolish. I am not unaware of who speaks to whom; but following the example of Abraham, who said: “I speak to the Lord, though I am dust and ashes,” I too — though I am obscure to others in birth, in manner of life, and in very name — address you, holy man, Abbot of Clairvaux; you, I say, illustrious in name and in life.
