Let another propose: “The devil is true.” This too, as an utterance constituted entirely from a noun and a verb, is intact in the manner just described; but it is by no means true, since it was not fashioned from truth. It is an imitation, and falsehood said to be true; but in seeing through to the truth, everything in him is false. We therefore say confidently that this proposition is, precisely because of this, invalid and false by its very name — impious and alien to truth, endorsed by some such notary of whom the devil bears witness that the world lies under his power.

To return, then, to the matter under discussion: let us cast into this word “sacrament” the concept of a complete whole. For it has its constitutive parts. For example, the word joining the element makes the sacrament. For example: when a man is baptized in these words — “I baptize you in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit” — this word, joining the element of water, constitutes the sacrament, made perfect from the visible water as one of its principally constitutive parts. For the prayers that precede or follow those words already mentioned, and the other things that are customarily or habitually employed along with the water in baptism — such as oil and ashes mixed into the baptism — and other things of this sort, are not principal or constitutive parts of the baptismal sacrament. Rather, only this, as I have said: “I baptize you in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit,” rightly joining the element of water, constitutes the intact sacrament — whether celebrated in the Church or outside the Church, it is equally perfect. These words to be applied to the visible water were not handed down to the Church by an apostle or prophet, but were fashioned from on high, the weaver being the very wisdom of God — just as those words too, poured over the element of bread and wine to confect the body and blood of Christ, are fashioned from on high throughout. For from Christ alone both traditions had their origin: both that of perfecting baptism and that of confecting his body and blood — through those very words which first issued from Christ’s own mouth, either where he instituted baptism, saying “Go, teach all nations, baptizing them in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit,” or where he confected his body and blood from bread and wine, and commanded his disciples to do the same, saying “Do this in memory of me.” Whatever is added to this tradition — to this word brought to completion and abbreviated by Christ — whether in the celebration of baptism or of Mass, no more destroys the integrity of the sacraments if omitted, nor perfects them if added, than the remaining parts of a statement — aside from noun and verb — either destroy or perfect the statement by being removed or added.

For we are now compelled to use the likeness of such trifles as well. In former times, indeed, the solemn celebration of Masses was not adorned with such an elaborate outward display of splendor, nor was all this grace of religious observance brought to completion and polished by any one person. For the holy pontiffs, the brilliant lights of the Roman see, as they shone forth at different times, so too they gradually perfected, each by the brightness of his own zeal, the beauty of this saving office. And just as David and Solomon, above all, amplified with splendor the order of the old sacrifice handed down by the Lord through Moses — through the ministry of priests and levites, the great multitude of singers, the joyful dancing of the divine psalms, the illustrious glory of the temple or altar, and the abundance of sacred vessels — so the holy Roman Church, receiving the wondrous rite of the new sacrifice handed down by the Lord through the first apostles, embraced it with devout faith, preserved it with faithful care, adorned it with diligent appointment; bringing forth from its treasury new monuments of piety, it fashioned, as it were, from gold and precious stones a kind of diadem of religious office for the holy sacrifice — as one Roman pontiff established the antiphon at the Introit, another the Kyrie eleison, another the Gloria in excelsis, and yet another something else to be sung or said at Mass — not indeed to augment that unique sacrament, but to adorn the most divine worship of that same sacrament and to venerate the singular sacrifice
