Let us now return to what we had left aside, and distinguish still more fully the effects of the sacraments briefly distinguished above. That effect of the sacraments which we have called passive is always uniform, and both outside and inside the Church has a single institution and constitution with respect to the principally constitutive parts; for in the secondary and appendant parts the manner is sometimes varied and manifold, according to the usage of individual churches. But in the principal constitution of the sacraments the manner is always one and uniform in all churches, Greek and Latin alike. For in that the Greeks offer leavened bread, they do not omit the principal institution of Christ — which they would omit if they were to offer something other than bread, for example, cheese or something of that kind, which Christ did not offer. And therefore, although they would act more reasonably if, by offering unleavened bread, they were to agree with the holy Roman Church, yet the Church has long tolerated their leavened sacrifice, not with indifference, but with the earnest effort of many men over many years, that the Greeks should come to agreement with the Roman Church. Hence Pope Leo IX, the one hundred and forty-eighth \[in succession\] from the blessed Petrus, wrote a distinguished letter against Michael, patriarch of Constantinople, defending the Roman custom as sincere and more consonant with the institution of Christ. But the Greeks nevertheless persevere in their own custom, without departing from us in the principal constitution of the sacraments. Similarly, among the Latins themselves, while some differ in that, whereas almost every other church holds the Gregorian order, they still hold the Ambrosian institution, yet in the principal constitution of the sacraments all these churches agree with one another so fully that not even heretics dare to alter it; and if anyone were to alter it — by offering something different, or by baptizing in some other liquid, or by reciting something other than what the ecclesiastical tradition prescribes during the oblation or baptism — what he does is on no account to be counted as a sacrament. The sacraments are therefore inviolable, since wherever they are they remain the same; and once conferred, they can never be annulled. For when those who have been ordained or baptized are excommunicated, they are in no way deprived of the sacraments they received according to the ecclesiastical rite, but the sacraments of ordination or baptism remain in them, albeit dead, albeit extinguished, albeit null — that is, effective for no virtue or salvation. Such persons \[heretical baptizers\], in their baptizing, confer only the outward form of true religion which they themselves possess; but the power of that same true religion, which they do not possess, they will not be able to confer — especially upon those who consent to their errors and dissent from the Church, or in the very act of knowingly receiving the sacraments that are forbidden to them. Consequently, when those who have been baptized by such persons return to the Catholic Church, the washing of the flesh is not repeated for them; but, in order that what is lacking may be completed, they are incorporated into the Catholic Mother, so that through her reconciliation they may merit to receive the grace of the Holy Spirit. For in her alone the Holy Spirit is given, because God is not in the rending of minds, but in the unity and peace that is the bond of perfection. And though such persons were begotten in a less than legitimate bed, they are nevertheless counted as children: for even from every tribe of the sons of Israel those children were counted as known to God where the servants of our God are sealed on their foreheads — from the tribe of Gad and Asher, who were born of a handmaid, just as from the tribe of Judah and Joseph, who were born of lawful wives.
