Now those sacraments that concern rational creatures, when celebrated outside the Church, are void, empty, extinguished, and dead; they become valid, clear, and living when, with the rational creature — that is, the person who celebrated the sacraments outside the Church — doing penance, the Church’s confirmation follows. But those sacraments that concern inanimate things can in no way be confirmed, because those creatures cannot do penance. Hence we read at various times that personal sacraments conferred outside have either been confirmed by catholics upon the interposition of penance, or excused by the intervention of ignorance — as the sacraments of ordination and baptism celebrated by the condemned Acacius were excused by Pope Anastasius. Those who read his words carelessly think he holds an opinion contrary to the authorities cited above, but this will not appear so to one who reads attentively, if that reader is willing to attend to the difference among sacraments: namely between those that can in some way and at some time be excused, through the venial or invincible ignorance of those receiving these sacraments and seeking God’s grace alone in them, and those sacraments concerning inanimate things that, when exhibited outside, are nowhere read to have been confirmed inside or excused — but are in every way and everywhere rejected. Hence Pope Alexander says: “A church that has been consecrated — or rather, to speak more truly, execrated — by the arrangement of a price, shall by no means be regarded as a church.” What is said of a church may not inappropriately be said of all inanimate things, since neither wine nor bread nor oil execrated rather than consecrated by any heretic is to be called or held sacred. Behold, we have expressed our views about sacraments as best we can, without prejudice to better views and to those who express them better, to whom we are prepared to yield, once reason or authority is shown to us to which we ought not to object and on account of which we ought to change what we have said.

If, moreover, in our words and writings, as we recall having said before, those tokens are found by which Tamar is said to have been saved from burning — namely the ring, the staff, and the bracelet, by which faith, hope, and charity are understood — I beseech you, whoever reads in this way, do not believe my soul to have committed fornication with the father of lies, but rather declare that it has been made pregnant from the seed of Judah — that is, of Christ — so that, after the example of Tamar, it might bring forth, as it were, a son who would be called Perez, that is, division, because God is my witness that it is from no pursuit of personal glory, but from zeal for truth alone, that I either divide myself from the sacraments of heretics, or demonstrate in my words and writings that there is a great division and distinction between their sacraments and those of catholics, without prejudice, as I have said, to better views. Long ago —

Moreover, I wish still to say something about that most iniquitous plague, simony. Among all the little foxes that ruin the vineyards, no little fox is more wicked or more cunning than simony. For as a certain author says,

“By what knot shall I hold Proteus as he changes his aspect?”

so we may say: by what knot shall we hold simony — that most cunning little fox, manifold in face, thousand-shaped in habit, and almost always unlike not only the other little foxes but even unlike itself, to such a degree that, while it ruins the vineyards of Christ, it is sometimes seen in the very guise of the vinedresser? For whenever it begins to be recognized in any one of its skins, it immediately puts on another, and the vineyards it lays waste it sometimes pretends to be planting.
