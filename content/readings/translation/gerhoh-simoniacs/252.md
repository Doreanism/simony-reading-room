For, as the authority of Pope Pascalis cited above makes clear, whoever sells either of two things that are jointly bound together does not leave the other unsold. And whoever commits this crime secretly is guilty before the tribunal — that is, the divine tribunal. But one who gives an open and unmistakable sign of this crime in himself, as it were by saying “racha,” is guilty not only before the divine tribunal but also before the human council, since he is known to be subject to the penalties pronounced against simoniacs in any council of the holy fathers whatsoever. And if, when justly rebuked by anyone, he says to his rebuker “fool,” he shall be liable to the fire of Gehenna — and he is altogether like those who said to Christ when he rebuked them: “You have a demon.”

Now, setting aside for the divine tribunal those who are guilty before that tribunal because their crime is hidden, let us deal with those who are guilty before the human council, because their simony is in the open: namely, the manifest buyers and sellers of churches, prebends, and other ecclesiastical things as such. Among these we are compelled to number hired clerics and those who hire them, since both parties commit not a hidden but an open simony — the hired clerics selling, and those who hire them buying, the Mass or some other administration to be performed in the Holy Spirit. These we strike first as guilty before the council — or rather we demonstrate that they have already been struck — in the Council of Chalcedon, where, concerning such wandering clerics ordained to no fixed title, it was decreed and defined by the whole council that they could nowhere perform ministry, to the injury of their ordaining bishop. In harmony with this holy and great council, Leo Magnus says: “That consecration is to be held void which is founded neither on place nor fortified by authority.” The holy Pope Gregorius, approving that same council together with the other principal councils in his synodical letter, says: “I confess that I receive and venerate the four books of the holy Gospels and likewise the four councils — namely Nicene, in which the perverse dogma of Arius is destroyed; Constantinopolitan also, in which the error of Eunomius and Macedonius is refuted; the first Ephesine as well, in which the impiety of Nestorius is judged; and Chalcedonian, in which the wickedness of Eutyches and Dioscorus is condemned — with complete devotion I embrace them, with perfect approbation I keep them, because in them, as upon a squared stone, the structure of the holy faith rises up; and whatever the life and conduct of anyone may be, whoever does not hold their solidity, even if he appears to be a stone, lies outside the building. All persons whom the aforesaid venerable councils reject, I reject; whom they venerate, I embrace. For since they were established by universal consent, whoever presumes either to loose those whom they bind or to bind those whom they loose destroys them.”

Whoever therefore thinks otherwise, let him be anathema. And so we too, thinking in harmony with the holy Gregorius, cast out wandering clerics ordained to no fixed title who wander everywhere hawking their ministry for hire, as cast out in the Council of Chalcedon — lest we either incur the anathema pronounced by the holy Gregorius or lie outside the building according to his judgment. Some object that wandering clerics of this sort were assigned to certain titles when they were ordained, though they were not regularly bound to those titles; rather, they were merely named for those titles at the time of ordination, without having any post of divine ministry in those places, since they were neither placed over those titles or churches nor canonically or monastically bound to their superiors, but were attached to them in name only.
