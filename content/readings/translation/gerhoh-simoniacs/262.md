When therefore the sign of unity exists apart from the unity for the signifying of which it was instituted, the sacrament itself has indeed its own effect, which we called above the passive effect; but it does not have the effect that we may call active — for while the sacrament is itself effected or produced when the sign of a sacred reality is completed by the word’s accession to the element, yet because the sacred reality is absent from that place, the sacrament does not bring about its proper effects. For just as a body without a soul accomplishes nothing, so the sacrament of unity without unity does not bring about its effects. For just as the soul cooperates with the body in order for it to accomplish anything, so unity cooperates with the sacrament of unity in order for it to accomplish anything; and just as a body is alive when it contains within itself the life-giving spirit, and is dead when that spirit is withdrawn, so the sacrament of unity lives if unity itself subsists within it and it within unity, and when severed from unity it is utterly dead and powerless to produce the effects it ought to produce. For when unity cooperates, the sacraments accomplish something, because they work in creatures the threefold work of the Creator — that is, sanctification, transformation, and salvation. Sanctification even in inanimate things, as in chrism or in a man-made temple, which are sanctified through words that are themselves signs of sacred realities; transformation, as in bread and wine, which when sacrificed are changed into something else; and salvation, because the faithful who worthily partake of the sacraments and persevere in unity are saved through them. All of these things — sanctification, transformation, and salvation — one and the same Spirit works in and with the sacraments in the creatures themselves which are sanctified, transformed, and saved through our Lord Jesus Christ, to whom as to the vine the Catholic celebrants of the sacraments cling as branches. For as long as they agree with the teacher of truth through the unity of faith, preserving the bond of unity, all thinking the same in Christ, and each living from his Spirit, they possess the grace of inner vitality, and whatever sacrament they celebrate in the Church of Christ lives for the salvation of those who communicate with them. For Christ speaks in them, and whatever is done by them is rightly believed to be done by him and confirmed by his Spirit — even if they are found blameworthy in their conduct, so long as they remain within the bond of unity and cling to the one vine that is Christ. But truly, when those who dissent and divide themselves from the unity of the Church through heresies and schisms sever themselves from Christ, their every word and sacrament is dead; and whatever they appear to do is null; and whatever they are supposed to sanctify is polluted. Let them go then, and hasten to bear fruit — those seedlings of death and shoots consigned to the eternal flames: simoniacal hirers and hirelings and all other traffickers, driven out of the house of God by the Lord’s scourge; and all heretics and schismatics severed from unity and therefore utterly fruitless. For whatever is done among them, whatever is celebrated as though sacred or as a sacrament, is as truly devoid of the word of life and the spirit of truth as it is true that a branch, unless it remains in the vine, cannot bear fruit; as it is true that a hand severed from the body can neither live nor act. Therefore, since in all the deeds and words in which we receive the testimony of Christ it is necessary to note and mark this — that God is truthful — this testimony of his, as uttered by God, as uttered by the most truthful of witnesses, is to be carefully noted and marked with the seal of inviolable faith, where he says: “Abide in me, and I in you”; and he adds: “As the branch cannot bear fruit of itself unless it abides in the vine, so neither can you, unless you abide in me.” This true testimony of God, the truthful and most faithful witness, must be carefully marked and always carried in a mindful heart — not only against heretics and schismatics, but also against all the presumptuous.
