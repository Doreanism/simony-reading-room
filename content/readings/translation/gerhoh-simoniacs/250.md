Indeed, one must certainly believe that they are at least priests — since you thought the gift of God could be possessed with money; for \[Simon\] is condemned not for what he did but for what he believed he was doing, since to “think” is less than to “believe.” Therefore in what is added — “You have neither part nor lot in this matter” — it is plainly shown that nothing of holy ordination is received in such an advancement. Still some dare, even after these and other most powerful authorities, to defend hired clerics from the charge of heresy, saying that what has been said about simoniacs does not pertain to them, on the grounds that the hired contracts about which we are speaking do not appear to be expressly named in the aforementioned authorities. Let them hear the following, from the council of Pope Clement: “It has been commanded from ancient times, and we forbid under perpetual anathema, concerning the simoniacal heresy which has grown through folly, about which the holy synod has decreed: no one shall sell any church consecrations, archpresbyterships, commendations of altars, transfers of churches, abbacies, or provostships; and whoever shall have hired, or sold, or bought — let him be anathema. Amen.” And all responded three times: “Let it be, let it be.” Let the hired clerics hear this, lest they think it something new that we say hired arrangements are no less than purchases or sales of ecclesiastical goods pertaining to the anathematized traffic of simony. There are also those who strive to defend hired clerics and other such traffickers from the simoniacal heresy on the grounds that they sin out of desire for profit rather than being driven by an intent to corrupt — as if their perverse cupidity diminished rather than augmented the evil they do, when it is precisely through that cupidity that they are most culpable. For Gehazi was not thereby able to remove from himself the leprosy of Naaman the Syrian on the grounds that he had sinned more out of desire for garments and silver than with the aim of corrupting anyone. Pope Urbanus II plainly counters such men, speaking thus: “Whoever confers or receives ecclesiastical goods not for the purpose for which they were established but for private gain — whether through the gift of words, or of improper service, or of money — is a simoniac; since the principal intent of Simon was nothing but money and avarice, that is idolatry, as the Apostle says: ‘and covetousness, which is the service of idols.’” Against this authority of the Roman pontiff some make a foolish objection. They say: “If the conferral or obtaining of ecclesiastical goods through the gift of words, improper service, or money is simony, then the whole earth is already full of simoniacs, since ecclesiastical goods are sometimes granted even to stewards, other tenant farmers, and lay soldiers, with the intermediary commerce of speech, service, and gift.” Those who say this do not sufficiently distinguish between sacrilege and simony. For since an ecclesiastical benefice and office are joined together just as body and soul, whoever acquires one of them in such a way that the other is not obtained without it necessarily sins against the Holy Spirit — without whom it is established that no ecclesiastical office can be rightly administered. Ecclesiastical offices are those that go by these names: episcopate, presbyterate, diaconate, provostship, abbacy, clerical order, and the like — offices that cannot be rightly administered without the gift of the Holy Spirit. In order, therefore, to take one case, let us suppose regarding the episcopate alone that someone might obtain episcopal revenues through gift and so possess the villages and other goods that belong to episcopal administration, in such a way that he desires in no way whatsoever to be or be called a bishop — something that is known to have been done by certain associates of tyrants.
