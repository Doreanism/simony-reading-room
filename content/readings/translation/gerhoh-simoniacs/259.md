If therefore we tolerate those ordained by such persons when they are excused by the aforementioned reasons, far more should the Catholic Church peacefully acknowledge as her children those baptized by such persons and in no way consenting to their error — provided, however, that once they have learned of the error of their baptizers, they willingly renounce it and choose to follow Catholic truth and peace. We therefore do not forbid simoniacs and other heretics to perform signs in the name of the Lord — that is, to celebrate sacraments, which are signs of sacred realities — in the manner in which they perform them; for in performing them they do not act together with us, since in celebrating sacraments outside the Church they do not gather together with us. And since no benefit from such signs accrues to them, to those who share their depravity, or to those who are joined to them in their excommunication, they are barely and with difficulty admonished not to glory in signs, as the magicians of Pharaoh gloried, since the finger of God operates nowhere except in the Church of God, nor does he work anything through them in those they baptize or ordain, unless it is shown that the souls of these \[recipients\] are not cut off from the Catholic Church, and they are excused either by the ignorance of infancy, or by another invincible ignorance, or by the exigency of death — which excuse, however, is not received for any other sacrament in the same way it is for baptism. Hence Saint John of Alexandria says among other things: “Baptism may be received from any heretic or evildoer, provided that he has baptized in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit. For if someone is held back by illness and death draws near, and no priest or cleric is present to baptize him, he may even be baptized by such a person in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit; but penance is to be received from no one except a Catholic.” And a little later: “If no Catholic priest, whether virtuous or even wicked, is present, it is better to remain without visible communion and to be communicated invisibly by the Lord, than to communicate with a heretic and be separated from Christ. ‘There is no concord between Christ and Belial,’ says the Apostle; ‘no part for the faithful with the unfaithful.’ Every heretic is an unbeliever; but a simoniac is a heretic, and therefore an unbeliever. Although Catholics cannot possess the holy communion of Christ visibly and bodily on account of threatening heretics, yet while they remain united to Christ in mind and deed, they possess the holy communion of Christ invisibly — like the great many saints who, living in the desert far from all company, were seen by no one whether living or dying, yet whom we believe to have been so much the more united to God as they were separated from men.” Likewise a testimony from the same author: “Never at any time,” he says, “take part in the communion — or rather, the contamination — of heretics, even if throughout the whole of your life, owing to some compulsion or necessity, being unable to find the communion of the Catholic Church, you have remained without communion. For if, while legitimately possessing a bodily wife, when staying for a long time in some distant region without that woman, we are prohibited by God and by the laws from abandoning her and joining ourselves to another; and if we do so, we are punished — how much more, when we are joined to God through right faith and the Catholic Church, as the Apostle says, ‘I have betrothed you to one husband to present you as a chaste virgin to Christ,’ shall we become partakers of the fate that awaits heretics, if we adulterate the orthodox Church and the holy faith by communicating with heretics?
