Rather, if we assign fitting names to things, we call such a person not a simoniac but a sacrilegious thief, because by removing a sacred thing from sacred use, he renders ecclesiastical property secular, insofar as it lies within his power, stripping it of its ecclesiastical character. This sacrilege is to be charged to those laymen who render to such things an obedience owed to ecclesiastical administration, though their obedience is in fact unwarranted. In this sacrilege, laymen who occupy tithes are implicated, as are bishops who voluntarily cede those same tithes \[to laymen\]; yet by making such a concession of tithes to laymen, bishops do commit simony, because they attach no spiritual administration to those tithes when granting them — rather, as we believe we have sufficiently demonstrated above, they commit manifest sacrilege, inasmuch as they make ecclesiastical property, so far as in them lies, non-ecclesiastical. Pope Urbanus I, martyr, condemns such sacrilege in his decretals, saying among other things: These offerings are called such because they are offered to the Lord. They must not therefore be turned to any uses other than ecclesiastical ones and those benefiting our Christian brethren and the needy. If anyone, God forbid, acts otherwise, let him beware lest he incur the condemnation of Ananias and Sapphira and become guilty of sacrilege. We may rightly understand that those who render ecclesiastical property non-ecclesiastical, so far as in them lies, are here indicted for sacrilege. Pope Urbanus II also addressed such persons: The properties of individual parishes are under the authority of bishops, who stand and have stood in the place of the blessed apostle Peter and shall stand there in all times to come — from these resources bishops and their faithful stewards ought to supply the necessities of life to those living in common, as best they can, so that no one among them may be found in want. These same properties are called the offerings of the faithful, because they are offered to the Lord. They must not therefore be turned to any uses other than ecclesiastical ones and those benefiting our Christian brethren and the needy, for they are the vows of the faithful, the ransoms for sins, and things entrusted to the Lord for the purpose aforesaid. If anyone, God forbid, acts otherwise, let him beware lest he incur the condemnation of Ananias and Sapphira and become guilty of sacrilege, even as those did who defrauded the price of the aforesaid goods. Of these it is read in the aforesaid Acts of the Apostles: “A certain man named Ananias,” and so forth, down to “great fear came upon all who heard these things.” These things, brethren, are greatly to be avoided and feared, because the goods of the Church are not to be dispensed as if they were one’s own, but as things held in common and offered to the Lord — with the utmost reverence, faithfully, for no uses other than those aforesaid — lest those who divert them from the purpose for which they were entrusted incur the guilt of sacrilege and, what is worse, become anathema. Even if not in body, as Ananias and Sapphira were, who fell down dead, yet in soul — which is more important than the body — they shall fall dead and estranged from the fellowship of the faithful, and slide into the depths of the abyss. Thus far let it suffice us to have amplified, in the words of Pope Urbanus I, the crime of sacrilege committed in the alienation of ecclesiastical goods by bishops who, in the unlawful concession of tithes that they make to laymen, do not commit simony — since they do not attach any spiritual administration to those tithes — but manifest sacrilege, as we believe we have sufficiently demonstrated above, inasmuch as they make ecclesiastical property, so far as in them lies, non-ecclesiastical.
