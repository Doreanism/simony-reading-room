I say this not because truth holds you otherwise, but because it moves many greatly that we, who have been sent as scouts by one Lord, do not in all things say one and the same thing. Just as when Joshua and Caleb were encouraging the people of Israel to drive out the inhabitants of the promised land, the other scouts who had been with them said, “We are by no means able to go up, for they are stronger than we” — so now, while the pontiffs of the Roman see, Nicolaus II, Alexander II, Gregorius VII, and the other successors of their line, together with our own time’s pope Innocentius, are encouraging God’s people toward the extermination of simoniacs and others who, since both ecclesiastical offices and benefices have been forbidden to them, have had the promised land, as it were, adjudged against them — while these men, I say, are encouraging God’s people to the conquest of such enemies, there is no lack of learned readers of Scripture, like scouts of the promised land, who speak in harmony with those scouts who said: “We are by no means able to go up against this people, for they are stronger than we. We saw there certain monstrous beings, children of Anak, of gigantic stock, beside whom we were like locusts.” Against these, though the spirit that was in Joshua and Caleb cries out through the mouths of a few — “Do not fear the people of this land, for we can devour them like bread; their protection has left them, the Lord is with us, do not be afraid” — against these, I say, that spirit is crying out. Therefore, so that I may rather accord with the few in the truth than err with the many, I desire to be instructed by your fatherly wisdom whether, in the matters contained in the following tract, I am speaking words of truth and sobriety, or whether it seems otherwise to you — as some suppose, since at the court of Bamberg you did not stand firm either for me or for my adversaries. I wish, however, that this tract should reach you sealed with our seal and thus intact, so that it may be examined by you first, and if you judge it proper, by you alone as its reader. But if it shall seem good to the spirit of piety that dwells in your heart that it should come to the knowledge of many, then I ask that if anything in it is to be corrected, either you correct it or suggest to me what should be corrected. You will be able to send any written response to me through Lord Adam, which I beg you not to be slow to do, since I cherish your little works and am accustomed to give thanks to the spirit of truth that rebukes the world through you. From this act of thanksgiving, then, let the beginning of our little book take its start, with God guiding our spirit and our pen. Here ends the prologue.

## Here Begins the Tract on the Judgment Already Passed upon the Prince of This World.

I give thanks to the spirit of truth, who in both your spoken and written words rebukes the world concerning sin and concerning righteousness — concerning sin, indeed, because men do not believe in Christ, who though they confess him in words yet deny him in deeds; and concerning righteousness, which you so fully ascribe to divine grace that a man is no more permitted to boast of the righteousness divinely bestowed upon him than of that obedience of Christ upon which all our righteousness depends. And what is that obedience, except the one he himself designated when he said: “I go to the Father and you will see me no more”? For he said this on the night he was handed over to death, going through that very obedience of death to the Father, and leaving to his followers all the benefit of that obedience.
