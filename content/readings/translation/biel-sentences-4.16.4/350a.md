## To the second \[objection\]

To the second \[objection\]: the major \[premise\] is conceded, the minor is denied — when the minor asserts that all able-bodied persons are bound to labor, speaking of manual and bodily labor. And as for that text of Genesis \[“in the sweat of your face”\]: Alexander notes in question 109, article 1, that the precept concerning labor ordered to the seeking of sustenance — like 1 Thessalonians 4: “work with your hands, as we commanded you, so that you walk honorably before those outside and lack nothing from anyone” — is not solely a precept of positive law, but also of natural law. For those things are of natural law to which men are inclined by their natural constitution. Thus, by the very disposition of the body, man is naturally ordered to manual work, on account of which the blessed Job says in chapter 5: “man is born to labor and the bird to flight.”

But it should be known that precepts are twofold with respect to the deficiency they address: some address the deficiency of an individual person, whether spiritual — such as precepts concerning acts of virtue — or corporeal, as for example that of Genesis 2: “Of every tree of paradise you may eat.” Another kind is ordered to removing the deficiency of the species, such as Genesis 3: “Increase and multiply,” which commands the act of generation by which the human species is preserved. Between these two kinds of precept there is this difference: every individual is bound by a precept of the first kind of natural law; but a precept of the second kind, ordered to removing the deficiency of the species, does not bind each person individually, but rather the whole community of human beings is bound to ensure that some among them fulfill this duty.

Now the precept of manual labor is in a certain way contained under both of the aforementioned kinds: insofar as by labor one provides for the needs of others, it pertains to the second kind; insofar as one provides for one’s own needs, it pertains to the first kind, just as the precept of eating does. But a precept of either kind, ordered to removing a natural or bodily deficiency, does not oblige unless that deficiency requires it. Hence if there were someone who could live without food — as is reported of a certain hermit brother named Nicholas in Swabia — he would not be bound by the precept of eating. So too the precept of manual labor does not bind any individual, either insofar as it is ordered to removing the deficiency of others \[through common provision\] or insofar as it is ordered to removing one’s own personal deficiency, unless that deficiency requires it.
