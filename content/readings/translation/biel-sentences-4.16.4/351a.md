Arena-hunting is when someone fights against a toothed beast in the arena and hires out his labor for this purpose. This too is illicit, like the first type. One who practices it for pay sins mortally and becomes infamous \[cf. C.3 q.7 c. *Infamis*\]. Forest-hunting is that which is carried out in the woods and mountain thickets. This is licit, though it becomes illicit by reason of time, as during Lent, or by reason of person, since it is not licit for a cleric — at least that type involving dogs and clamor. To set out snares or nets without din or clamor is not illicit for a cleric. Regarding entertainers \[*histriones*\], a distinction is drawn. There are entertainers who, by some instrument or bodily gesture, intend to move men to vain laughter and evil, inept merriment. One who gives to them for this reason makes an offering to a demon, as the authority states, and sins. But there are others who are skilled in playing musical instruments — small bells or the zither — for the consolation of weak human nature, just as David played the zither before King Saul when he was troubled by an evil spirit and found relief. To give to such performers is not a sin, just as it is not \[a sin\] to play the zither for this purpose.

## Third Doubt: Whether almsgiving is more satisfactory than the other two parts of satisfaction, namely fasting and prayer.

It must be noted that satisfaction is ordered to several ends: to the appeasement of divine offense, to the remission of punishment, to the uprooting of the root of sins, to the restoration of what is unjustly possessed, and to other \[ends\]. The principal end to which satisfaction is ordered, however, is the appeasing of divine offense so that sin may not overcome \[us\] — as is clear from the strict definition of satisfaction in q. 2 of this distinction. On this account, that act or voluntary suffering is more perfect and more satisfactory by which divine offense is more fully appeased. And since nothing appeases God more than an act elicited from charity, the act elicited from greater charity is simply more satisfactory. Therefore the parts of satisfaction stand in relation to one another as exceeding and exceeded. As regards other ends, however — such as remission of punishment — fasting is preferred, since it is more afflictive of the body. As regards the avoidance of the cause of sins \[since the principal root of sin is pride, which prayer is ordered to address\], prayer itself takes precedence over the others. As regards the restoration of unjust possessions, almsgiving is more efficacious, since through it the debt of required restitution is discharged and disgraceful gains are compensated, as is excessive solicitude. And so whichever part of satisfaction extends to more of the aforementioned ends is more perfect than the others. And since almsgiving proceeds from charity first \[and especially\], which is specifically joined to love of God; since also it avails not only from the act of the giver of alms but also from the recipient, who by precept is bound by the law of gratitude to repay spiritual \[goods\] — it surpasses prayer and fasting. Also because through almsgiving avarice is uprooted, the root of sin, as it is written: “Those who wish to become rich fall into the snares of the devil” \[1 Timothy 6:9\], and it follows: “The root of all evils is the love of money” \[1 Timothy 6:10\], and Ecclesiasticus \[10:9–10\]: “Nothing is more wicked than a miser, nothing more iniquitous than to love money; for such a one has set his own soul for sale, since in his lifetime he has cast away what is within him.” All these things are not found thus in any one of the other parts of satisfaction, namely in prayer or fasting. And therefore in many passages of sacred scripture almsgiving is commended and satisfaction for sins is attributed to almsgiving.
