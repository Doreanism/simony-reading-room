And as for that saying to Adam, “in the sweat of your face”: it is said that those words of the Lord are rather a declaration of punishment than a precept imposing satisfaction for sin — whence he prefixes: “cursed is the ground on account of your work.” Similarly to the woman: “in pain you shall bring forth children.” It does not follow, therefore, that every man is bound to labor by necessity of precept. Otherwise it would follow that he is bound to agriculture, which the Lord specifically mentions there.

As for the saying of the Apostle \[about working with one’s hands\]: it is said that the Apostle commands manual labor for special reasons and for certain persons prone \[to these vices\] — for example, to prevent theft, to turn away the desire for others’ goods, to secure the necessities of life, to quiet anxious care, and to avoid idleness. But where these and similar reasons do not apply, the precept has no place.

## To the third \[objection\]

To the third \[objection\]: the poor who beg — those of the kind spoken of in the responsive proposition — do not despoil other poor persons, because they do not receive what is not lawful for them to receive. For they have the right to ask for necessities, and they are not bound to labor bodily, as has been said.

## As to the authority \[of Jerome\]

Jerome speaks of clerics who possess ecclesiastical goods that are owed to the poor — namely, those goods that remain beyond what is needed for an honest sustenance. If they divert these to other illicit uses, they despoil the poor and commit sacrilege. And he adds that manual labors are reckoned among the wealth of the able-bodied. This is true of those who are bound to work with their hands and are not prevented from doing so by superior goods. Alexander treats this matter at length there, in that proposition.

## Second proposition

The second proposition: one who gives alms to a pious petitioner is not required to inquire too carefully into the character of the one begging. This proposition reflects the mind of Christ, according to \[Chrysostom’s homily\] on the Epistle to the Hebrews, and is found at *Decretum* D. 42, c. *Quiescamus*. It says: “If one asks for food, do not scrutinize him in these matters; for Abraham showed himself hospitable toward all. If he had been a scrutinizer of those who fled to him, he would never have received the angels as guests — for perhaps he would not have thought them angels and would have turned them away with the rest. But because he received all, he received the angels too. For God will not render you a reward on the basis of the life of those you receive, but on the basis of your will and your great honor and goodness and mercy.” And this is what the word “pious” adds: for he gives piously who, moved by charity and mercy, gives to support the poor — not to perpetuate begging. Enough has been said about this in Conclusion 6.

## Second doubt

The second doubt concerns the saying of the blessed Augustinus, adduced in the proof of Conclusion 6, in which he censures donations made to actors and hunters alike. For the authority cited says: one who gives to a hunter loves in that man what makes him most wicked. The doubt, then, is whether one sins who gives something to hunters or actors.
