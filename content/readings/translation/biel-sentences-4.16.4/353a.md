And these authors further say that the remission of venial sin comes about through some act of displeasure at the venial sin, an act rendered more acceptable to God. This \[remission\] is either referred by the agent himself to that venial sin’s remission, or it is not so referred by the agent but is accepted by God in relation to it — as the works of one dying in charity with a venial sin not referred by that person toward the remission of the venial sin in which he dies, nor even at the death of the blessed one any meritorious act with respect to the remission of a venial sin, may nonetheless have their prior works accepted by God in relation to the remission of that venial sin at death. This can be understood as follows. Every meritorious work has a twofold effect: one with respect to the guilt to be remitted, another with respect to grace and glory to be conferred. For example: a satisfactory work by which the temporal punishment owed to venial sin — or to mortal sin deleted through penance with respect to guilt — is remitted, merits beyond this remission of punishment also a degree of grace and glory, and no less of either than if it were not a satisfactory work, as was shown above in Question 2, Distinction 1. For unless this were so, the Church would err in imposing a satisfaction on a penitent — since it would be better for the penitent to be absolved without any penance and then to defer the payment of punishment to purgatory. If indeed satisfaction can be made for another in this regard, \[and if\] enduring this punishment temporally would diminish essential misery, then it would be better to suffer this temporal punishment than to lack even the least degree of grace and, throughout all of eternal glory, than to be without a better degree of eternal glory without some better measure. This is the remission of temporal punishment. And on this matter blessed Bonaventura \[speaks\] at length above, Distinction 1, Question 2, in the sixth difficulty. For the present purpose: every meritorious act is remissive of some part of the temporal punishment owed to venial sin. Being accepted by God for this \[purpose\], whether the agent refers it to the discharge of punishment or not — the agent himself does not \[directly\] remit the punishment, due to an impediment; but once that impediment ceases, it has its effect. Sin that persists constitutes the impediment. For the punishment of sin cannot be discharged while the sin persists in act or in the will. Therefore if someone remains in venial sin until death, he impedes \[the remission\] until that death; because a cause that is impeded, while impeded, does not produce its effect, but once the impediment ceases, it does produce its effect. In the instant of death, once the impediment — namely the sin — ceases, then remission of the venial sin takes effect in virtue of the antecedent merits. If there were many \[merits\], much \[is remitted\]; if few, little. It is clear how the opinion of the Master \[of the Sentences\] may be understood to agree with what was said above in Distinction 14 on \[the view of\] Bartolinus.

## Notabile 3

Thirdly, it should be noted that penance is understood in two ways, as was said in Question 1, Distinction 14: first, as the sacrament — namely, the priestly absolution given by a confessor to the penitent; second, as the virtue or act of the virtue, namely displeasure, contrition, or preceding sorrow. Here it is taken in the second sense; what pertains to the sacrament of penance will be treated in the following Distinction. And in this second sense it can be taken in two ways: in one way, strictly, for the habit or act of detestation of sin, or for the disposition presupposed by such an act; in another way, much more broadly, for the cessation of the will from the act of delight in sin. Hence blessed Thomas, in Book IV, Distinction 16, Principal Question 2, Distinction 1, Question 1, Article 2, says: for the will to withdraw from what it previously willed is for it to be displeased by what it willed. This is the office of contrition. Such grace, or such displeasure, is \[even\] a little pain. And in this way one has formed penance.
