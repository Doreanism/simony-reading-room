## Articulus 2

Regarding the second article, the proper conclusion is as follows. Venial sins are not remitted in the proper sense through an act of delight residing in the will. This proposition is established from Article 1, Conclusion 1, Question 1, Distinction 1. It is proved by reason, because it is self-contradictory for a sin to be remitted while it remains in the will. If an ordered \[act\] pleases \[the will\], the sin remains in the will, because that very act of delight is the sin. While the sin remains, it is neither blotted out nor remitted.

## Conclusio 2

The second conclusion: for the remission of venial sin, penance taken in the strict sense is not required — neither actual nor habitual, neither sacramental. This is proved because one who remains in venial sin until death is not damned but will ultimately be saved; therefore the venial sin committed is remitted, since every debt of guilt is incompatible with beatitude — “for nothing unclean shall enter it,” Apocalypse 21 \[Rev. 21:27\]. Now it is remitted through the act of the one who sinned, because it \[remission\] comes from a determined merit of any kind, both of congruity and of condignity. Therefore this conclusion is true. It is remitted, moreover, through satisfaction in purgatory, by which the temporal punishment owed to a venial sin not remitted in the present life is discharged. Furthermore, an act performed with fervent charity consumes venial sin, just as fire does, even if in that very act one has no memory of the venial sin — according to the saying: “Charity covers a multitude of sins” \[1 Peter 4:8\]. And such an act is not an act or habit of penance, nor a sacrament.

## Conclusio 3

The third conclusion: taking penance in the second, broader sense, venial sins are not remitted in this life without penance. This conclusion follows from the first, because venial sin is not remitted while it is pleasing to the will. But not to take pleasure in a sin already committed is adequate penance for that sin, speaking broadly of penance, as was said in the article. Therefore the conclusion is true.

## Conclusio 4

The fourth conclusion: venial sin is remitted not only through an act of displeasure but through every meritorious act, especially when it is directed toward its remission. The conclusion is proved because every meritorious act is satisfactory with respect to temporal punishment, as was said in the first article. This \[satisfaction\] is applied to venial sin, in proportion to the degree of the venial sin. The reasoning holds because the punishment owed to a sin is never remitted while the sin itself persists. Therefore a good work that remits punishment remits the sin to which the punishment is owed, if it has not previously been remitted. Yet not every meritorious work remits the entire punishment owed to venial sin — just as not every satisfactory work for a remitted mortal sin, as enjoined, removes all its punishment. Rather, a certain proportion is required between the meritorious work and the punishment to be removed; this proportion, known in the divine acceptance, is determined by God alone. This is especially so when what such an act includes is displeasure at the sin to be blotted out. For in directing an act toward the remission of a venial sin, one wills the venial sin to be remitted, and consequently wills it not to persist — and this willing is displeasure at the venial sin. Among all other works, contrition or displeasure especially merits the remission of sin.

## Conclusio 5

The fifth conclusion: neither the virtue nor the sacrament of penance is required for the remission of venial sin. This is the view of Bonaventura. ## Articulus 3

The first question is: whether one venial sin can be remitted without another. The brief answer is yes, according to either of the opinions stated by Paludanus \[Petrus de Palude\] in Distinction 1 of the present question. For the punishment owed to one can be discharged while the punishment owed to another venial sin remains. The reasoning holds: because once the punishment is discharged, the liability \[*reatus*\] is discharged.
