And it was said pointedly that almsgiving binds the recipient by the law of gratitude — because the giver of alms is not obliged to bind the recipient by legal debt to repay spiritual goods, whether by contract or by agreement, since in that case he would commit simony by giving temporal things in exchange for spiritual ones. For almsgiving ought to be a free gift, not a loan \[cf. 1 Corinthians 13\]. \[That said,\] almsgiving can intend to move a poor recipient to a pious and charitable disposition by which he may be moved to pray — as \[Master\] Petrus \[de Palude\] says in the Parisian distinction 15, article \[on almsgiving\]. For almsgiving is given principally with a view to God, who counts what is given to any one of his poor ones as given to himself, who knows how to reward all things. The recompense is to be expected from him, not from the poor man. And on this basis the response to the doubt is to be varied according to the variation in comparing almsgiving to its diverse effects.

## Fourth Doubt: Whether spiritual alms are more excellent than corporal ones.

Briefly, by the authority of Thomas \[Aquinas\], Summa Theologiae II–II, q. 32, a. 3: Comparing spiritual almsgiving to corporal almsgiving simply, other things being equal — for example, when there is equal need and equal destitution on both sides — spiritual almsgiving is superior to corporal. First, because what is given, namely a spiritual good, is nobler than a corporal gift, as teaching is better than gold. Second, because the soul to which it is given is nobler than the body. Third, because the fruit of spiritual almsgiving is greater than that of corporal. For the fruit of spiritual almsgiving is amendment from sins or the perfection of virtue, which are nobler than any corporal gifts. But in the case where other things are not equal, it is otherwise — as the one dying of hunger must be fed before being instructed. Richard \[of Middleton\] posits a manifold distinction; but what has been said suffices. \[Alexander of Hales,\] speaking of the utility of almsgiving both as to the perfection and as to the utility of the giver: in this way corporal almsgiving can have a spiritual effect, since when it is given from charity of God and neighbor it merits an increase of grace and glory. Hence Ecclesiasticus 29 \[:11–12\]: “Lose your money for the sake of a brother; lay up your treasure according to the commandments of the Most High, and it will profit you more than gold.” Likewise, almsgiving moves the recipient to pray for the giver and to obtain for him grace and many spiritual gifts, by which he may advance toward the kingdom of heaven, according to Luke 16 \[:9\]: “Make friends for yourselves by means of unrighteous mammon, so that when you fail they may receive you into the eternal tabernacles.” And according to this, almsgiving — whether corporal or spiritual — is more excellent when it proceeds from greater charity and piety and is given to the more devout poor. And so much for this question.
