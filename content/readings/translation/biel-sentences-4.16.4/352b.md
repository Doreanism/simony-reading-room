[Charity coexists with venial sin] not only when it has not yet been blotted out after the act, but even when it is actually being committed, as is clear to all. A stronger proof: through charity one is worthy of eternal life. If together with this there stands a debt to eternal punishment, then that person is at once worthy of eternal life and of eternal punishment — which is impossible.

Furthermore, after the act of mortal sin nothing remains of the sin except the debt of eternal punishment, as was established above in Question 1, Distinction 14. If the debt of eternal punishment could coexist with grace, then an unremitted mortal sin could coexist with grace in the manner in which it persists after the act, and thus the same person would be simultaneously friend and enemy of God. Moreover, the essential punishment of the damned is not the punishment of sense but the punishment of loss \[*poena damni*\]. This necessarily accompanies any punishment whatsoever, because no punishment can coexist with blessedness; therefore the debt to any eternal punishment includes the debt to eternal loss — which is the essential punishment of damnation — and that does not correspond to venial sin. Furthermore, venial and mortal sin are proportionate in the character of their malice or offense, inasmuch as infinitely many venial sins would not equal one mortal sin in the character of offense, since not even all of them together would turn one away from the ultimate end as one mortal sin does. Therefore, the punishment corresponding to mortal sin by divine justice exceeds infinitely — that is, disproportionately — the punishment owed to venial sin. This excess is not proportionate according to intensity, since no punishment is intensively infinite; therefore it is according to extension or duration. Hence eternal punishment is not owed to venial sin. And this refutes what others say, namely that eternal punishment corresponds to venial sin by divine justice. Incidentally, however, when someone joins himself to charity — by which he is ordained to the kingdom — he owes temporal punishment. And so when someone is damned for mortal sin, his venial sins are also punished eternally in hell; nor would they be punished eternally by God unless eternal punishment were justly owed to them. That they are punished eternally is clear because otherwise there would be redemption in hell. And by contrast one would say that for venial sin, temporal punishment is owed of itself, but through its conjunction with mortal sin, eternal punishment in hell is owed incidentally. Against this he argues: God always punishes below what is deserved, and even if he always punished strictly to the measure deserved, it would be entirely unjust to inflict eternal punishment for something to which, in itself, only temporal punishment is owed. For whoever imposes something on another does not do so in order to exact it infinitely for a fault; therefore a punishment exceeding it infinitely does not justly correspond to it. From these considerations he concludes that for venial sin — whether punished in this life or the next, whether in hell or elsewhere — only temporal punishment is owed, neither in itself nor incidentally, because the offense is such that it is sufficiently punished by temporal punishment taken in itself. Nor is it incongruous that the temporal punishment owed to venial sin be determined \[and completed\] in hell. For example, if someone coming \[to purgatory\] in the first manner \[i.e., dying in grace\] relapses into mortal sin — having completed part of an imposed penance but not all — and is thereby damned, that venial sin, depending on how and by what it came about, requires a doubled penance to be rendered and discharged; he pays the punishment in hell, but only a temporal one, because from the moment a prior remission of sins converted the debt of eternal punishment into a debt of temporal punishment, he is never a debtor for those \[sins\] except for temporal punishment. And consequently, once the temporal punishment is paid, he will be free from these. Yet there seems to be redemption in hell for those sins for which he is damned, because for them the debt of eternal punishment was never converted into a debt of temporal punishment, so that debt always remains and will never be discharged. And this is how one should understand the statement that there is no redemption in hell. All these are \[the views of\] Scotus.
