## Doubt 3

The third doubt concerns why certain good works are specifically assigned as remedies for the remission of venial sin, when in fact a venial sin can be remitted through any meritorious work, as has been said above. \[The answer is found in\] a certain mnemonic verse in which these works are specifically assigned: *Giving, sprinkled \[with holy water\], eating, having confessed, giving, blessing. Saying the Lord’s Prayer, “Forgive us our debts.”* The expanded list is: Sprinkled with holy water. Eating the Eucharist. Receiving the sacraments or a general confession. Giving bodily alms. Receiving the episcopal blessing. Under “prayer” is included the striking of the breast. Under “communion of the Eucharist” is included the worthy reception of any of the sacraments. These works are assigned not because others are thereby excluded — for contrition, that is, displeasure at venial sin committed, avails far more than the rest — but they are said to be specifically assigned because these means stir the free will toward detestation of sin, through which \[sin\] is more effectively remitted; and also because in them the prayers and merits of the Church concur; and also because they are frequently directed, by the intention of the agent or of the Church, toward the remission of venial sin; and also because all other \[means\] are reducible to them. Thus any meritorious work has the power to remit venial sin. On this matter at length: Paludanus \[Petrus de Palude\], dist. 1 of the present question, art. 2. And on the value of holy water the same author speaks well, dist. 2, q. 1, art. 2, conclusion 2. To the same point: Hugo \[of Saint-Victor\] *On the Sacraments* bk. 2, pt. 9, at the beginning. Thomas \[Aquinas\], dist. 1, q. 2, art. 4. Alexander \[of Hales\], pt. 4, q. \[...\], member 1, art. 2, §1. Henricus \[of Gorichem\], dist. 21, art. 5, q. 1, at the end. The same, dist. 1, art. 1, q. 3. Also Augustinus *Enchiridion* ch. 70. Also *On Penance* dist. 3, *Water and salt*, etc.

## Doubt 4

The fourth doubt: whether a venial sin can become mortal, and conversely whether a mortal sin can become venial. For the solution, one must recall what was said in Book III, dist. 23, q. 1, and dist. 1, q. 1, doubt 1: that there is a twofold virtuous act, and likewise a twofold vicious act — namely, elicited and commanded. The elicited act is intrinsically and invariably virtuous, vicious, or indifferent by nature. Thus an elicited virtuous act cannot become vicious or naturally indifferent, nor can the reverse occur — because if such a virtuous act were to become vicious, something about the act would have to change, namely its object or circumstances.
