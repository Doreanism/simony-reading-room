# Distinction Sixteen, Question Four.

He \[Alexander of Hales\] cites James, the last chapter. Hence the whole Church is one commonwealth: whoever provides the necessities of Christian life is everywhere receiving in return what is necessary for himself. He proves the same point with examples from the blessed Alexius, Benedict, Dominic, and Francis, some of whom continued their lives in mendicancy and were holy and pleasing to the Lord, even though they were able-bodied. For the blessed Alexius, having spurned his patrimony and left his wife, went begging through the world; at his death the Lord wondrously revealed his holiness. Similarly, regarding Saint Benedict, Gregory says in the second book of the *Dialogues*, chapter one, that he lived for three years in a cave doing no work whatsoever, living on alms from a certain Roman monk. The cases of the others are well known. One might also adduce here the example of Christ, about whom various opinions are held among different authorities.

## He also proves \[his case\] by the authority of the Church itself

which, after Christ, has no greater authority, and which has approved the mendicant orders, in which able-bodied men receive alms — not only those who preach, but also the rest who recite the canonical hours and serve others in various capacities.

Note that it is lawful for able-bodied persons to receive alms, because the Church has granted and approved this — what would otherwise be illicit. And it approved it because it was lawful. Likewise, in Acts 6 the apostles said: “It is not right for us to forsake the word of God and serve tables” — provided, of course, that they are able-bodied. By the same token, it is not right to forsake spiritual things and serve the table, that is, to engage in bodily labor to the neglect of spiritual duties.

## Responses to the objections

By way of these considerations, responses are given to the objections set forth in the opposite direction at the beginning of the doubt. To the first objection, concerning the law forbidding able-bodied persons to beg: Alexander \[of Hales\] responds to this \[objection\] in part two, question 109, noting that this law was introduced against certain persons who, during a time of famine, usurped the alms of the poor by feigning idleness or incapacity for work. These were idle men who contributed nothing useful to the commonwealth — men who, though capable of working, occupied themselves with nothing profitable, seeking to abound through the alms of the poor, being prone to wrongdoing and plunder. Therefore, the rulers of the commonwealth justly barred such men from begging and punished them with the gravest penalties, namely perpetual serfdom if the man had been free. \[See the *Authentica*: “If he had been a slave, through his subjection he acquires for his lord at the fortress declared.”\] The case is otherwise with the religious poor, among whom there is no feigning of idleness, no love of sloth, and no danger of plunder.

Furthermore, Alexander says, this objection can easily be dismissed once one considers the respective purposes of the Gospel and the various aims of imperial law. For the aim of that law was to organize men usefully for the preservation, advancement, and stabilization of the commonwealth — whose preservation, advancement, and stabilization depends on the accumulation of wealth, the expansion of estates, and the cultivation of fields, which is accomplished by multiplying the powerful and rich and by reducing the needy and weak. By contrast, the aim of the Gospel is to order men toward the attainment and possession of the kingdom of God, which is better achieved through contempt of riches than through their accumulation and retention. This is why the Gospel pronounces blessed the poor in spirit — that is, the voluntarily poor. Hence, a gloss on imperial law makes no claim against it, since there is no contradiction between them on account of the different rationales behind the prohibitions and precepts; these operate differently.
