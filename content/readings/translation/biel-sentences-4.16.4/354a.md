## Doubt 2

The second doubt is whether a venial sin can be remitted while a mortal sin remains. An argument is raised that it cannot, because sin cannot be remitted except in a state of grace, and where mortal sin is present there can be no grace — therefore \[it cannot\]. The antecedent is proved from Proverbs 10: “Charity covers all offenses.” On the other side it is argued that the penalty can be discharged even while in mortal sin, and \[so too\] can venial sin — and this holds entirely — since the remission of venial sin is nothing other than

## Thomas’s Opinion

the remission of liability to penalty. On this doubt there are two opinions. One is that of the blessed Thomas and his followers: that it cannot \[be remitted\]. They strive to prove this as follows. Just as mortal sin takes away charity, so venial sin takes away the fervor of charity. Therefore, just as mortal sin is not forgiven unless charity is restored, so neither is venial sin forgiven unless the fervor of charity is restored. But the fervor of charity, being subsequent to charity itself, cannot be restored unless charity is first restored — and charity cannot be restored while mortal sin remains. Furthermore, while God remains angry because of the ongoing mortal sin, the sinner is his enemy. An enemy does not forgive an offense, whether small or great. But while mortal sin persists the sinner is God’s enemy — therefore \[venial sin cannot be remitted\]. Furthermore, venial sin is not forgiven unless some act more acceptable to God \[compensates for\] the venial sin that displeased him. But no act is acceptable to God unless the one acting is himself acceptable to God — for the Lord first looked to Abel, and only then to his gifts \[Genesis 4:4\]. But one who is in mortal sin is not acceptable to God — therefore \[no such act can avail\].

## Scotus’s Opinion

\[Scotus\] similarly argues by parity of reasoning on the other side. The second opinion holds that a venial sin can be forgiven while mortal sin remains, because the penalty owed for a venial sin can be discharged by one who is in mortal sin — whether in this life or in hell, as has been shown above. Once the penalty is discharged, the venial sin is discharged and forgiven, since when the act of sin has passed, nothing remains in the soul that would be opposed to the sin’s remission — beyond the liability to penalty (this qualification is added on account of vicious habits) — but once the penalty is paid the liability to penalty is removed, and so nothing of sin remains. To the first argument, concerning the fervor of charity: one must ask what is meant by “fervor.” If it means something belonging to charity itself — say, a certain intensity of charity — then it can be answered from what has been said that venial sin does not take away the intensity of fervor, since it does not diminish charity according to anyone. But if by “fervor” one means an act of charity: if one means an act actually present, then venial sin does not destroy fervor either, since it is not necessary that on account of an act of venial sin some other act currently present is destroyed.
