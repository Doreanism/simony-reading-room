A luxurious petition is a disorder, deviation, and departure from the order directing a human being toward salvation. Now this order instituted by God is twofold. The first is the necessary observance of the commandments, according to Matthew 19 \[:17\]: “If you wish to enter into life, keep the commandments.” The other is the compendious and useful — though not necessary — order for attaining salvation, such as the observance of the evangelical counsels. Departure from the first order, which is the transgression of the commandments, is always a mortal sin, because it brings perpetual death, deprives the soul of the life of grace in the present and the life of glory in the future. Departure from the second order — such as the observance of the counsels — through a perverse and disordered attachment to a creature, short of transgressing the commandments, is a kind of venial disorder. It is called a venial sin — a disorder in adhering to a creature that is less useful while forsaking what is more useful — since it does not corrupt rational choice (cf. Aristotle, *Ethics* I) and does not bring death, hence “venial.”

\[Venial sin,\] moreover, neither takes away grace nor diminishes it. Otherwise, since grace is finite, it could be entirely removed by the multiplication of venial sins — and thus venials would not be venial. It does, however, diminish the fervor of grace, that is, it impedes grace from its operation, as Augustinus says \[cf. *Confessions* X\]: “He loves you less who loves something together with you that he does not love on account of you.” He loves less — not by privation of the habit of charity, but in that, by turning to a creature through an act of love, he is impeded from actual purification by an actual act of charity toward God. For just as the intellect does not simultaneously understand many things through distinct acts, so neither does the appetite simultaneously tend toward diverse things through acts of love.

## From these \[observations\] further \[corollaries\] follow.

Mortal \[sin\] deprives \[one\] of glory and obliges \[one\] to gehenna. Mortal \[sin\] turns \[one\] away from the end. Venial \[sin\], however, does not turn \[one\] away but deflects and somewhat impedes progress toward the end. Likewise, mortal \[sin\] always has annexed to it contempt or interpreted contempt of God. Venial \[sin\], however, does not involve contempt of God (for no one despises what one loves), but involves a certain sloth or tepidity by which one adheres rather to a creature than hastens to adhere to God. Likewise, mortal \[sin\], if it is not blotted out in this life, remains forever inexpiable. For in the future \[life\] it avails nothing, where there is no place for penance. But venial \[sin\] is also remitted after this life, as will be said below, dist. 21. Indeed, to discern in particular regarding any inordinate act whether it is a mortal or venial sin is exceedingly difficult on account of the infinite circumstances that vary an act. Hence concerning this the venerable Gerson, chancellor of Paris, labored greatly in his book *De vita animae* \[“On the Life of the Soul”\], and offered many most useful rules for following this discernment, yet by his own testimony he did not arrive at sufficient certainty of discernment.
