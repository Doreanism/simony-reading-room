## Second Proposition

A venial sin that is a commanded act, remaining numerically the same, can become mortal, and vice versa. This is proved as follows: a person may enter a church out of a desire for carnal pleasure, and then — the motion continuing — may change his intention and enter in order to incite disordered love toward some person to whom he is inordinately attached. That continuing motion is numerically the same in the order of nature, being the same movement; in the first instance it is a venial sin, in the second it is mortal. The same can be illustrated with many other examples. A fuller treatment is to be found in the place cited, dist. 23, q. 3 of the \[Book III\] commentary.

## Doubt

But regarding this matter it is first asked whether the frequent repetition of a venial sin renders it mortal. This question arises on account of the words of Augustinus among those cited: “sins, however small, if recurring ones have been accumulated, will weigh upon us and overwhelm us just as some one great sin.” To this doubt the answer is that frequency alone, or the habit of sinning venially, never changes a venial sin into a mortal one. As Scotus argues, dist. 29, Book III, concerning the habit of swearing: if the first venial sin is venial, then any subsequent act of the same species is likewise venial, since the same moral reasoning applies to each act of the same species in the genus of morals. Nor does an interior habit render a sin grave, since there is no greater gravity in the habit than in the act from which it is generated — although an interior habit more strongly inclines toward the act, it does not incline toward an act of a different genus, nor toward a graver act, but toward an act similar to the one from which it was produced.
