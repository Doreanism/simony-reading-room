## Doubt 6

It is asked whether \[God’s being\] offended in a venial sin that pleases \[the sinner\] makes it mortal. As Augustinus testifies: “No sin is so venial that it does not become mortal while it pleases.” To this little question S. Bonaventura and others distinguish a threefold venial sin. One is venial by reason of surprise alone — that is, without advertence, it forestalls deliberation. This kind, just as it cannot be avoided, so likewise never becomes mortal; it remains long in that genus of surprise. It is otherwise if the act begun by surprise were to remain or be continued until full deliberation supervenes, because then it is no longer a matter of surprise. The second is that which occurs with thought or advertence, but without full consent — as in a thought about a woman, and in delight short of consent to that delight. The third is venial with reason and consent, but is venial because it is not against a precept — as an idle word or a jocular lie. And in reply to this, it is said: a venial sin of the first or second kind, if full consent supervenes, will become mortal. For it was venial on account of defect of consent or its imperfection; once consent supervenes, the venial character ceases, and it no longer lacks the character of mortal. And the answer notes specifically *full* consent, because, as Scotus says in dist. 39 of the third \[Book\], just as a meritorious act requires that it be fully human — that is, from full deliberation and consent — so no less does a demeritorious act require the same, because \[God\] is not more inclined toward condemnation than toward pardon. And when an act of the genus of mortal sin occurs without full deliberation, due to defect of perfect consent — and mortal sin further presupposes perfect deliberation — it is not counted as mortal. But it must nonetheless be observed that one who is habituated sometimes does not realize he has deliberated, because his deliberation is so brief — since he possesses great prudence which is prompt to deliberate in a time almost imperceptible. Such deliberation is sufficient for the character of merit and sin. To a venial sin of the third order the reply is that nothing must be placed in it as in a final end — as if someone were so seized by appetite for a jocular lie that he would even beg with entreaties to continue it if his lord were to forbid him: there he would place his rest in the jocular lie more than in God. And then contempt of God would always concur, to whom in that case he would prefer the jocular lie. And there is room for Augustinus’s words: “No sin is so venial that it cannot become mortal while it pleases” — not by any kind of pleasure, but \[that pleasure\] by which the will rests in it as in a final end. This happens when it pleases more than God — for instance, because one wills the act of that sort more than the friendship of God, which is the true and simply ultimate end.
