### Observation.

At the words *non facile credere*, etc.: a prelate or judge ought not to believe readily one who reports to the prejudice of another. This readiness to believe to another’s prejudice is therefore condemned. But where belief does not tend to the prejudice of the first person, such readiness is not then blameworthy but rather commendable goodness and a sign of innocence, as in \[the saying\]: *the innocent man believes every word* \[Proverbs 14:15\]. xii. q. iv.

Likewise, gather from this that when a judge proceeds by way of inquisition, the role of the accusing party is not assumed by the judge; rather, infamy takes the place of the accuser or denouncer.

### Note.

At the words *cum igitur*, first note that if in the denunciation some difficulty is found, the superior may set it aside and proceed from his own office by way of inquisition, and the denouncer is not punished for desisting in this manner or for not prosecuting the denunciation to its conclusion, according to Innocentius. Hostiensis holds the contrary view and says that this is a special prerogative of the pope; but the first opinion pleases me more, because when one acts for the public good \[and\] the other \[party\] is excommunicated, in the same way the contention between denouncers and the denounced ought not to be a cause impeding excommunication, except in one case dealt with here — yet in this matter one acts only for the benefit of correcting a person’s life. xxiii. q. v. *prodest.*

### Further note.

The suspicion of dilapidation is proved from conjectures alone, even for the purpose of suspending the prelate from his administration — which is well noted. And this is special in this crime on account of the imminent prejudice; otherwise, conjectures do not suffice to prove a crime, as at ii. q. viii. *sciant cuncti.*

And note well that once it is established as proved from the evidence, if a prelate is suspected of dilapidation, and it is established that the church is impoverished, in the course of the inquisition he can raise the objection that \[the accusers\] were enemies; and if it appears that his conduct was bound up with debts, he can excuse himself by alleging conspirators; and if he raises such an objection of a crime under the pretext of nobility — because, as the Doctors say, and rightly so, for the time being even a nobleman must make his expenditures according to the measure of the church’s revenues. I observe here that no one should ever spend everything he receives from revenues; rather, it is prudent counsel that one always set something aside each year for future necessities.

In the third part, the pope makes an interlocutory ruling. The second part begins at: *cum ergo ter.* The intervenors: thus the administration of a ward’s property is managed.
