### Note i.

From the text: from a confession made in the penitential forum, draw one general rule — that \[a person\] cannot be deposed or otherwise coerced from office against his will, even if the confession was made to the bishop or even to the pope, because each of them knows \[such matters\] as God, not as man. This accords with \[the chapter\] *Sacerdos*, *de officio ordinarii*, as noted in c. *Dilectus*, *de excessibus prelatorum*. For here the confession was made to the pope in the private forum. Therefore the pope says, as it were, that the transgression is secret — and this must also be understood to mean that the confession was made in the presence of the judge alone. For the judge is not believed against the one confessing if the latter denies it \[C.6, q.1, *Si tanti* and *Sacramento*\], as in c. *Cum satis*, and c. *Placuit*, *de octestato*, c. *Cum a nobis*.

### Note that simony is com-

mitted in the conferral of orders not only when money is given to the one conferring the order, but also when it is given to the minister or to the one assisting at the ordination — and \[see\] here, and c. *supra*, *de eo*.

### Note that by the words “advise and induce”

no power of strict compulsion is given. On this, however, say as noted in \[the chapter\] *Habitum*. He himself indeed concludes the contrary in c. *De sponsionibus*. And lastly, it is gathered that by entering religious life, a simoniac is not rehabilitated for exercising an order simoniacally received: that by entering religious life the defect is removed.

### Second, note there

\[that\] without sin on the part of the one entering, the contract \[is dissolved\], as in c. j., *de simoniacis*. Yet the contract is not dissolved by one’s own delict, as \[in\] *legi*, *non*, c., *de apostatis*.

### In gloss i., there: “the intention must be considered.”

Hostiensis says that intention alone does not induce conventional simony, but rather mental simony, which is abolished by penance alone without restitution, as in the penultimate chapter under the same title. And he says that the gloss had a good understanding of this. And indeed the point is well taken that by intention alone simony is not committed — as when something is given and received out of liberality, and thus c. *Si questiones* speaks. But it is otherwise where there is an actual effect of committing simony, for the blessing is something spiritual. He says more, pursuing four members at length. For either something temporal is conferred by agreement, and however small it may be, it induces simony — just as we say in theft and usury — and so the text speaks, and as I said in c. j., *de eo*.

Moreover, it is then called conventional simony, nor is the sin committed unless what was wrongfully received is restored, as in c. *De hoc*. And there intervenes a sin; but \[consider\] the depraved intent as to its coming about — either \[the candidate\] would not be advanced to higher orders because \[the ordainer\] would not have presented or ordained him unless he had first received something temporal: then mental simony is induced, which is abolished by penance alone, as in the penultimate chapter under the same title. It is otherwise in usury, where restitution is necessary, as in c. *Consuluit* and what is noted there, *de usura*. Or the mind was not depraved but something given out of liberality was received, and then simony is not induced, as in c. *Tua* under the same title. And the \[question\] is in doubt whether a depraved intent preceded, and this is properly the subject of c. *Si questiones*.

And for conjecturing intent, they weigh what is found there, and in the text by means of a brief distinction they bring many laws into harmony.

### In the gloss on the word “secret,” at the end.

From this gloss, draw two good limitations on c. *Ad aures*, alleged in gloss i. \[The rule\] applies entirely to regulars; but it is otherwise for secular clerics, since by the mere prohibition of a superior, without any apparent cause, they cannot be prevented from being promoted to orders. \[This is supported by\] c. *Ex tenore*, under the same title.

\[Panormitanus\] does not affirm the above-mentioned c. *Ad aures*. But it pleases me that it should not apply to seculars — by the argument in c. *Si tantum*, c. *Placuit*, C.6, q.1 — because that chapter *Ad aures* speaks properly of regulars, since they are more subject to their prelate than are secular clerics, by reason of the argument in c. *Si religiosus*, *de electione*, book 6, and in the final chapter, *de sepulturis*, in the same book, and C. \[...\], q.1, *Molo*, and c. *Non dicatis*.

The limitation is that although regulars can, by the mere prohibition of a prelate, be prevented from being promoted to certain orders, nevertheless they cannot be coerced from the exercise of an order already received by the mere prohibition of the prelate without any other apparent cause. For it is more shameful to be ejected \[from an office already held than never to be admitted\], etc., c. *de itt*.

### Second, gather that those who were simoniacally ordained but who

are not \[themselves\] simoniacs can receive a dispensation: since they are not simoniacs, they may by dispensation remain in their orders. And the truth is that \[even when there is\] simony, dispensation is possible — I shall speak of this more fully shortly, with the gloss.

### A person simoniacally ordained is by law itself suspended from the order.

\[See\] dist. 22, §. *Verum*. But this chapter is understood concerning coercion of fact — that is, that he be in fact restrained from celebrating and from the exercise of the order. It can also be understood concerning suspension imposed by human authority, because just as one who is excommunicated by law can also be excommunicated by human authority, as in c. *Quorundam*, *de Iudeis*, so the same must be said regarding the suspension of a simoniac.

### In the gloss,

at the end: this penance enjoined is rather admonitory and consolatory than salutary with respect to eternal life. And what this performance of penance effects and how many effects it has — see the gloss in c. *Quod quidam*, *de penitentia*, dist. \[...\].

Finally, \[the question is raised\] there whether this person under the agreement is bound to confess again, when he utterly rejects it from him. Abbas \[says\]: one who was knowingly ordained simoniacally \[must do so\].

## Capitulum XVII.

### §3. Simony is committed if something temporal is received for

the consecration of vestments, a chalice, and similar things. Whether a priest hiring out his services at some church commits simony.
