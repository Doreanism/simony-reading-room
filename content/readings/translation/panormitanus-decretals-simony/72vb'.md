## Capitulum XIII.

### From one zealous for obtaining spiritual things,

to exact homage is a matter involving suspension. In the first \[part of the text\]: the supplicant’s narration, petition, and undertaking. In the second: the granting of the petition with relaxation and the giving of letters, beginning at the word *quocirca*.

### The literal case.

The Archbishop of Canterbury induced a certain cleric to do him homage and fealty, on condition that he would grant the cleric an annual benefice. That cleric, seeing that such an obligation was illicit, earnestly petitioned the lord pope for absolution, declaring that he would accept nothing further from the said benefice. The pope, seeing that this petition was honorable and worthy of favor, absolved him from that fealty and \[declared\] that contract contrary to both divine and human laws, such that he should receive penance from the bishop or a discreet priest, lest he be tainted by any note of infamy. He grants him letters that he may show in testimony of his indulgence against those who defame him. Bernardus.

### Note first from the text

that homage and fealty are different things. Properly speaking, homage is when someone makes a promise to another on bended knee with hands placed between the other’s hands, such that he becomes that person’s man. Such a person is called a liege man, and homage is customarily rendered to the emperor or king, such that one recognizes no other superior. On this homage, see the Clementine *Pastoralis*, *de re iudicata*, and more fully in the *Speculum*, in the title *de feudis*, section 2. Fealty, on the other hand, is when someone promises another to keep faith with him. And this oath of fealty contains many provisions, on which see the text at C.22, q.5, *de forma*, and what is noted in *c. ego*, *de iureiurando*: this oath may be rendered to several persons.

### Secondly, note

that from the revenues of a certain dignity or church a fixed annual pension may be established for a spiritual benefice. And on this, the text of *c. conquerente*, *de clericis non residentibus*, provides good authority. In a second way this text may be understood as meaning that the cleric received a certain annual pension from an established benefice; but the first understanding seems better to fit the letter of the text, and it is the one Hostiensis approves.

### Thirdly, note

that one who alleges his own turpitude or sin is to be heard when he intends to desist from sin. Finally, gather that a grace granted by the Holy See must be established through letters, as is the practice of the Curia, and it could not be proved even by witnesses — on which see Archidiaconus in the proem to the Liber Sextus — unless the letter once obtained had been lost, as in *c. cum olim*, *de privilegiis*. But on this there is no place \[for the objection\] because there seems to be an objection from *c. cum clerici*, *de verborum significatione*. By way of solution: since it is clear that clerics receiving benefices from their superiors owe them an oath of fealty — and also an oath of homage — and on this last point, see the text in *c. quod sit grave*, *de excessibus praelatorum*. The doctors commonly say that an oath of fealty may properly be rendered to a superior upon receipt of an ecclesiastical benefice, but an oath of homage may not. And *c. quod sit grave* should be understood as concerning homage taken in a broad sense, so as to encompass fealty as well. Note this. And there can be a true reason for the distinction as to why homage ought not to be rendered for spiritual things but an oath of fealty may: because homage involves a temporal advantage for the one to whom it is rendered, for the one swearing becomes his subject and vassal and is bound to certain services that ought not to be rendered in exchange for obtaining spiritual things. And this is perhaps what the gloss here intended when it said that this was a gift of obsequious service — that is, a service-gift — because many services follow from it. This is not so with the oath of fealty, which exists peacefully alongside the prelate’s authority, because one merely undertakes to be faithful to him; and it is rendered to the dignity rather than to the person. To this end, see *c. venerabilem*, at the end, *de electione*. But you may ask: why did the pope absolve this man not only from homage but also from fealty? I think a twofold solution can be given. First, that a grace conceded by the Apostolic See cannot be proved except through letters — save where the document has been lost, as the Archidiaconus holds — and here the archbishop intended to bind this man completely so as to make him his vassal and subject; therefore the obligation is vitiated entirely, by the argument in *c. cum super abbatia*, *de officio delegati*. Secondly, it can be said that, on account of the unworthiness and sin of the archbishop — because he wished to receive homage and thus committed a simoniacal offense — the obligation is vitiated in its entirety so that no advantage follows from it. Thirdly, it can also be said that the oath of fealty is to be rendered to the bishop or to another who has episcopal rights, on account of the preeminence of the dignity; but the case appears otherwise with respect to other lesser prelates who have the collation of a specific benefice, for this is not established in law. Let them therefore possess only so much of episcopal right as has been granted or prescribed in writing, by the argument of *c. auditis* and *c. cum olim*, *de praescriptionibus*. And from these sources, note: an odious constitution ought in doubtful cases to be construed so as to be rather favorable — *c. cum ex iniuncto*, at the end, *de haereticis* — and by this the meaning of this chapter is clarified. In the gloss on the word *divinis*, at the end: Hostiensis says that the text used “divine” \[laws\] in the plural so as to encompass theological rules as well.
