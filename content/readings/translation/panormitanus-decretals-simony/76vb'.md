### Thirdly, gather that one received in a monastery through simony or simoniacal corruption, once removed from there, must be placed in a stricter monastery so that he may do penance for the offense committed.

And if no stricter monastery can be found, then, as the doctors say, he is to be placed in a cell of the same order by dispensation, after having renounced \[his former house\], as in the chapter *De regularibus* on this title. And by this the entire matter of the gloss remains clarified.

### And fourthly, gather that what is freely offered at the time of receiving some spiritual thing may licitly be accepted, if no prior assessment was made.

And from this I infer the following: suppose there is a statute in a church requiring that one newly received as a canon or as a monk must pay a certain sum. Someone who was received without any exaction nevertheless pays — is this licitly received? I say yes, the text can \[be so read\], because since an assessment preceded, that offering appears to have been made by virtue of that assessment \[q. I, *Ex multis*\].

And on the first gloss, where it says “money may be proved,” etc.: but note that what we say here and in the following at first glance appears to conflict with the text. Nor does the passage resolve \[the tension\]. In truth, strictly speaking the nuptial blessing is not a sacrament, but \[the gloss\] broadly sets forth various cases: from the one who is bound to offer \[spiritual services\] freely, nothing should be exacted — thus speaks this text. But in the cases posited in the gloss, the money is given by the one inviting rather than by the one invited. Hostiensis says here that in order to receive what is necessary \[for salvation\], we may give money, because without these things we cannot be saved; the giving of money is tolerated in order to attract someone to the faith and to salvation. But for receiving other voluntary sacraments, nothing should be given; hence one ought not to give money to someone to enter religious life, since one can be saved without entering a religious order, as in the final chapter *De summa trinitate et fide catholica*. Likewise, \[Hostiensis\] says that even for voluntary sacraments nothing should be given by agreement — a sufficiently notable statement. And limit this as in the chapter *Cum in ecclesia* on this title. However, one should not give money to an infidel by agreement so that he converts to the faith, but one may do so out of free liberality, in order to attract him to the faith.

In the gloss at the words “are accused of this”: this is the truer \[reading\], as is sufficiently proved. For someone could have been convicted through false proofs or unjustly condemned by a judge; \[such a person’s\] reception should not be held against another. In support of the gloss, the text helps where it says “if against them,” and the passage can comprehend both givers and receivers.

### In the gloss at the word “severity”:

it then asks what this deposition means in the monastic context. Innocentius and Hostiensis say that \[the deposed monk\] will not be summoned to chapter nor to other deliberations, nor will he receive his prebend or his meals with the others, but he will be in a workhouse — that is, in a confined place or prison. I hold that this teaching applies to a monk who is not a cleric, for upon such a person deposition from orders cannot fall, and deprivation presupposes the possession \[of orders\]. But in the case of a monk who is a cleric, deposition will operate just as it does for a secular cleric: he will be deposed from orders and will no longer have their exercise.

### In the gloss at the word “with love”:

at the end, the gloss seems to suggest that secular clerics convicted of simony committed even in benefices are deposed, even if the proceedings were conducted by way of inquisition. And the doctors, in confirmation of this view, adduce certain reasons of distinction between regulars and seculars. The first reason is that among regulars the judicial order that is called *usque quando* \[how long?\] is not observed, as in the chapter on accusations, with the heavier \[penalty\] rising in proportion to the quality of the benefice. The second reason is that a regular appears to have done this out of charitable intent for the salvation of his soul, not because he gave money in order to be simoniacally received: \[since\] both the received and the receiver are deposed, they could still serve God in a monastery. The third reason is that out of favor for religious life, through inquisition, one received \[simoniacally\] should be dealt with more leniently in a stricter monastery. Zenzilinus, combining these reasons, entangles this matter \[further\]. As I see it, in the chapter *Inquisitionis* and in the gloss, there is no real difference between a secular and a regular: for a secular convicted of simony committed in a benefice should likewise not be deposed when proceedings were conducted by way of inquisition; it would be otherwise if the simony had been committed in orders, as I fully discussed there. And this chapter proves the point well, without introducing any special rule for regulars. But what may perhaps be drawn from the text is that, since here proceedings were conducted by way of inquisition, the abbot and abbess are not removed from administration — although for a grave sin a secular is removed from administration. And regulars are more readily deposed from administration, as stated there. But regarding the present chapter, it can be said that they were dealt with more leniently because they were not exacting money for their own profit but for the monastery, and thus they do not appear to have sinned as gravely as another secular prelate who commits simony out of personal greed.

### In the gloss at the word “officials”:

where it says that all the members of the chapter are presumed to know. Hostiensis says that although other members of the chapter are presumed to know what is done by the chapter or \[its officers\], they are nevertheless not punished unless it is proved that they consented, for a delict requires a true will; it does not suffice to rely on the presumption of knowledge. With this view the doctors commonly concur here. But by this, the present question does not seem fully elucidated. Therefore I distinguish more fully as follows: if the delict was committed with the entire chapter convoked, then all who consented are to be punished — both those who expressly consented and those who remained silent, because they were bound expressly to resist the wrongdoing and to raise their voice in opposition, as Antonius notes.

If, however, some expressly spoke out against it in chapter, then those persons are not to be punished, as in the law \[ff. de magistratibus conveniendis\], where there is a good text, and in the passage *Romana*, section *In universitate* \[de sententia excommunicationis, lib. VI\], at the word “culpable” — for a penalty ought to bind its own authors. And \[likewise\] in the chapter at the end \[de his quae fiunt a maiore parte capituli\].
