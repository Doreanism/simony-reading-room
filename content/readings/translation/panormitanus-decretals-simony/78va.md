### On the three criminal readings.

Hostiensis says that the proceeding is to be by general decree and everyone should establish proof of title. On the criminal proceeding, see Roffredus in the rubric. It might be doubted whether such proof by oath suffices in a judicial proceeding, at the penultimate column, where, given the antiquity of the matter, if by common law the presumption were against the one holding the benefice — as one who possessed two incompatible benefices — extensively and usefully \[he discusses\] the sick person. \[He says\] he retained the dispensation and lost it; and because it would not suffice, he does well to make \[provision\] in the ordinary chapter. Palla. and *c. Contra morem* \[*c. Dixit*\]. I am sufficiently of the opinion that this question should be carefully considered, having regard to the length of time and the quality of the superior predecessor of the beneficed person who tolerated him — just as we say in analogous cases.

\[Regarding\] the burden of proof: as noted in *c. fin.* \[*de iureiurando*\], when scandal would greatly result and if the superior tolerated the beneficed person, then one should have recourse to proving that title even by oath, because he is deemed to have consented, as argued by the cited authorities in *c. Ex ore*. The glosses hold this in the cited passages. Conversely, the superior himself can take upon himself the burden of proving the defect of title, as argued in *c. Ordinato* \[*de praebendis*\]. This opinion of the doctors, I think, must be limited: unless by common law the presumption were against the possessor, in which case that tacit consent would not suffice — since one cannot consent \[to what is against the law\]. And through this \[is resolved\] Palla. \[on\] *c. Ordinarii*, which speaks indistinctly, as though the matter were recent, or \[whether\] the superior did not consent tacitly or expressly.

\[He is obliged\] to make full proof of his title, as argued in the cited *c.* 2, *Memini*, and as noted in Palla., in the gloss, and \[this is supported by\] what Joannes \[Andreae\] noted in *c. Super his*, after the bishops, *de accusationibus*.

So holds Baldus in \[the case of\] one who has a special interest. And if he is not seen to have consented to this person, he could press the matter, and the other would be compelled to show the titles of his benefice. This was the holding of the \[Holy\] See, consilium 98, and \[it is\] to be fully carried through as stated there. And these questions, being of daily practical occurrence, keep in mind.
