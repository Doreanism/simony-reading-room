The crime of simony is more serious — or simony is a graver offense — with respect to the tainting of sacramental character. I therefore think the matter should be stated as follows: either a person believes that the grace of God can be acquired with money, in which case, since such a person becomes a heretic, he sins more gravely, as I said in the preceding chapter; but where heresy is not at issue, homicide is the graver offense with respect to the one who commits it and the one against whom it is committed. Yet with respect to the tainting of sacramental character, simony in holy orders is a graver offense than poisoning.

A simoniac may be accused by persons of any criminal status whatsoever, even those otherwise disqualified — for instance, by a prostitute or by his own slave.

If persons of criminal status are called to perform some legal act contrary to the rules of the common law, a prostitute is not included unless special mention is made of her.

Cardinals who lease benefices entrusted to them under a fixed pension, receiving a fixed sum of money, do not commit simony if the sum is to be paid by the person being instituted \[in the benefice\].

A simoniac in orders, even a secret one, must be avoided: it is a sin to hear the divine office from him. A tolerated excommunicate need not be avoided in divine services, though in secret one may avoid him so that he may be shamed \[into repentance\]. A simoniac in orders is suspended both with respect to himself and with respect to others, and he cannot be restored except by the pope alone. Whether and when a simoniac in a benefice is suspended \[is discussed below\].

### The Literal Case

The question was raised whether, in a case involving the crime of simony, witnesses should be received from any person whatsoever for its proof. The ruling is that, on account of the enormity of the crime of simony, slaves are admitted against their masters, and persons of any criminal status are admitted to bring an accusation against simoniacs. Likewise, every sinner may celebrate Mass except a simoniac, whom anyone — even a prostitute — may accuse, so as to remove him from an order he has wrongly obtained.

Note the first of three cases in which a slave may appear in court, even to accuse his own master — and this one is specific to the crime of simony. Ordinarily, slaves do not have standing in court, on which see *Digest*, *de iudiciis* \[D.5.1\], *l. vir certis*; *ff.q.no.c.* Observe that under a general provision admitting persons of criminal status to the exercise of some legal act, a prostitute is not included unless specific provision is made for her — which is clearly implied by this text. For the text first stated that all persons are admitted, even criminals, and then added prostitutes separately. Although she herself is indeed a criminal, nevertheless because of her base condition she is generally excluded from lawful acts, and special provision is required concerning her capacity, by analogy with *l. quam Alexandrum*, *Codex, de adulteris* \[C.9.9\], and *l.q. adulter*. This text is relevant to statutes that speak of criminals: “if the accused has been defamed.” But limit this to a case where a cleric is accused by a lay criminal, for in that case it is required that the accused cleric be of ill repute. It is otherwise where a layman accuses a layman, or a cleric a cleric, on which I treated fully in the chapters cited above, in the gloss, and in *c. per inquisitionem* \[*Extra*, *de electione*\]; and see *c. de confessis* \[*Extra*\].

In the gloss: this first solution, although it may appear true at first glance, does not in fact hold. For a simoniac in orders is likewise cut off from the Church. Therefore this text would establish nothing peculiar regarding a simoniac. The subsequent solutions of the gloss could also be challenged. I therefore conclude briefly, following Innocentius \[IV\], that other sinners — even those cut off from the Church, if they are tolerated — we are not bound to avoid, even in divine services. For we may lawfully hear the divine office even from a tolerated excommunicate, and on this point see *c. cum non ab homine* \[*Extra*, *de sententia excommunicationis*\]. We may, however, avoid them in secret so that, being put to shame, they may do penance, by analogy with *c. vestra* \[*de cohabitatione clericorum et mulierum*\]. But a simoniac in orders, however secret, we are bound to avoid in divine services, as stated in the *Decretum* \[*dist.* 22\]. And so understand that a sinner — even one foreknown \[to be damned\] — if tolerated in the Church, can celebrate validly with respect to others, though he himself sins by celebrating. But a simoniac in orders, however tolerated, can neither celebrate Mass with respect to himself nor with respect to others. In the gloss at the words “unless he renounce the benefice,” etc.: I conclude more broadly, drawing on certain statements of Innocentius, that a person is either a simoniac in orders or a simoniac in a benefice. In the first case, he is suspended with respect to himself and with respect to others, nor may he renounce the benefice, nor may anyone dispense him except the pope alone \[*q.* 1, *c. gratiam*\]. If, however, he is a simoniac in a benefice, then if the simony is secret he is not suspended with respect to others but only with respect to himself. And if he renounces the benefice, he may exercise his orders after performing penance; but while retaining the benefice in which the simony was committed, he cannot do penance, as in *c. inquisitionis* in the beginning \[*Extra*, *de accusationibus*\]. If, however, the simony is manifest, then he is suspended also with respect to others; and even though he renounce the benefice, he still requires a dispensation, just as any notorious criminal does, as in *c. final.* \[*de temporibus ordinandorum*\]. But a dispensation may be granted in this case by the bishop, provided that bishop is not the one who committed the simony, as noted in *q.* 5, *c. presentium* \[*C.* 3\]. Likewise, a notorious simoniac may be dealt with by the pope, and in that case, if the person in the benefice is suspended with respect to others, this holds true insofar as the simony was committed with his knowledge. It is otherwise if he was ignorant of it — for example, if the simony was arranged by his parents or friends — in which case he is not suspended with respect to others, but he may not lawfully retain that benefice without a dispensation, as in the aforesaid *c. presentium*. And note what is stated in *c. sicut* and *c. nobis* on the same point.
