# Simony.

Simony holds the chief place among all crimes. Simony -- what it is and whence it arises: in how many ways it is contracted, who is admitted to accuse or to testify — see the cross-references.

## Capitulum I.

## Definition of simony.

Simony is the deliberate will or desire to buy or sell something spiritual, or something annexed to a spiritual thing. I said “will or desire,” because spiritual things cannot be sold: yet from the mere will to sell or buy, even without any external act, simony is committed. A bishop or his ministers commit simony by accepting anything temporal for conferring orders. Whether the bishop’s notary may lawfully accept anything temporal without the sin of simony on account of his writing up the names of those ordained. Simony is committed if anyone accepts anything temporal for spiritual things, even if it is given spontaneously. Whether the scribes of papal bulls in the Roman curia who receive payment incur simony.

## Whether simony applies in the Roman curia.

If the Roman curia observes a certain practice, we too should observe that custom, even if a contrary statute has been enacted among us.

## Whether the pope is implicated in the crime of simony.

An ordinary \[i.e., a bishop\] has the power of binding and loosing in habit \[i.e., as a potentiality\], but not in act. A priest can neither absolve nor bind the parishioner of another, nor anyone at all unless that person falls under his parish. If young men are excommunicated by a constitution of the bishop, they incur excommunication even if they associate with the bishop, although the bishop himself does not incur it, because he cannot be bound by his own constitution.

## On ordination.

For the conferral of an order, neither the ministers nor the notaries ought to exact anything temporal. And there are two points in the text, at the words “for ordination.” The literal case is as follows: It was proposed in a general synod whether anything could be accepted for ordination. And it was determined that no one should accept anything for ordination, nor should the person ordained give anything. From the text \[it follows that\] where the principal act must be gratuitous, all accessory and attendant acts must likewise be gratuitous. Accordingly, the prohibition extends not only to the bishop who confers the order — forbidding him to receive anything for the order — but also to all other ministers who are accessory to and assist the bishop in the celebration.
