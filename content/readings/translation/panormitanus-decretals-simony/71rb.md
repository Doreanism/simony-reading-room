I understand this \[to apply\] where the person had already been summoned to court, even if not by way of accusation. It is otherwise if he had come of his own accord. Hostiensis says here that even if someone confesses out of fear of proof, he should still be shown some leniency, though not entirely, and it will be a matter of judicial discretion how much of the penalty should be remitted — by analogy with *c. Si*, *de translatione*. But this statement of Hostiensis is refuted by no law; indeed, all the laws inflict equal punishment on those convicted and those who confess in court, as here and in *l. Si confessus*, *ff. de custodia reorum*, and *c.* 1, *de confessis*, with many similar \[authorities\]. And in support \[of the stricter view\] there is that notable statement of Innocentius in the aforesaid *c. Qualiter*, where he holds that one who confesses should be punished more severely. In the gloss at the word “deponendi” — where it says “if he proceeds by way of inquisition,” etc. — deposition, even from orders, can also be carried out without distinction: because \[the simoniac\] retained the order wrongfully received. But if someone were simoniacal with respect to a benefice, then it does not seem that deposition should be effected by way of inquisition — by analogy with *c. Insinuavit* 1 \[above\]. I have treated this fully in the aforesaid *c. Inquisitionis*. See the discussion there.

In the gloss at the word “regem” — where it says “does not confirm the election,” etc. — consider the advice of Rorius, *consilium* 7, at the beginning: if the necessary conditions are met, \[the patron\] does not confirm the election. But in truth this is false as a matter of law, if the patron does not allege some legitimate ground for objection — unless he held this by special right from the pope alone. On this, see the good basis in *c.* ... Perhaps this text had this privilege, as I hear the king of France and certain princes of the world have by privilege the power of collation. By custom, however, this could not be acquired by a layman, even if the custom were of such antiquity that its origin was beyond memory. In the final gloss on this chapter, distinguish more clearly according to Hostiensis and the doctors, following Antonius: either the inquiry concerns a secular church or a regular church. In the first case, either the one who sold \[the office\] for money has no means of support — in which case restitution of what was given must be made to him, by analogy with *c. Veniens* and other \[authorities\] — or he does have a livelihood; and then either all in the church were complicit in the offense or \[not\]. \[If all were complicit,\] the money should be applied to that church, since it cannot be applied to the criminals themselves, as they are expelled therefrom. If not all were complicit, then the money should be applied to the treasury of the prince — namely, the pope — according to Hostiensis, who will distribute it for pious purposes. For disgraceful profits are exacted by the treasury, as in *l. Fiscus*, *ff. de iure fisci*. But if only the prelate or a particular member of the church offends, then he alone is punished and the money is applied to the church to whose disgrace it was given, as here. In the second principal branch, concerning a regular church: either all in the church offended — in which case the money is given to the one who gave it to gain entry, according to the manner \[described\] below — or a particular member of the church offended. Then, lest his offense harm the church, he is bound to give the money to a second monastery as to its usufruct only, and upon the death of the one who entered, it will revert to the first monastery. And thus the end of the gloss can be understood as qualifying that chapter *Veniens*.

## Capitulum VIII.

### A Cleric at the Papal Curia

or in an ecclesiastical forum may serve as procurator for a layman; in a secular court, however, he may not, except for a single matter.

Those who receive anything by reason of a prelacy at the \[papal\] See are excommunicated by operation of law.

If a familiar of some prelate serves him for the purpose of obtaining a benefice, he commits simony.

A benefice conferred upon someone on account of temporal services received is simoniacal.

If a prelate who has someone in his service at a fixed salary then provides a benefice for him: whether he commits simony when \[the cleric\] retains such a benefice and especially \[whether\] his salary \[continues\].
