A simoniac is one who demands money in order to absolve an excommunicated person.

A debtor can be excommunicated at the petition of his creditor if he does not pay his debt.

An excommunicated person must be absolved even after he has made satisfaction, or for the cause on account of which he was excommunicated.

Prescription does not run against an excommunicated person, because he cannot interrupt it since he is unable to bring an action.

A statute providing that an excommunicated person shall pay a fixed sum for each month during which he remains excommunicated is valid, as is evident.

Simony is committed if anyone gives money to another so that he will not harass or vex him.

To obtain absolution of the excommunicated person, one should have recourse to the superior of the superior and compel him to accept the resignation. First the complaint is set out; second, the appointment of a judge, at the word *quoniam*.

### Literal Case.

A certain person was excommunicated on account of money he owed, because he did not pay it by the appointed term. Eventually he satisfied his creditors, but he was unable to obtain the benefit of absolution, since those who had excommunicated him demanded one hundred pounds for the absolution. He brought his complaint before the pope, who thereupon mandates certain persons that, if the aforesaid debtor has paid his debt, they should absolve him without charge; and if the excommunicators should decline to absolve the excommunicate, they are to proceed by inquiry.

### First point from the text.

A debtor can be excommunicated at the petition of his creditor for failure to satisfy his debt — on which point, see what is read and noted in *c. Odoardus, de solutionibus*. Second, it may be gathered by argument that an excommunicate person, by virtue of receiving \[absolution\], will recover his rights, provided he has made satisfaction; he needs absolution even after making satisfaction.

Note regarding suspension: whether it \[i.e., the need for formal absolution\] applies when a suspension has been promulgated — on which see Joannes \[de Lignano\] in *c. Cum hoc* and the *Glossa singularis* in *Clem., de decimis*.

### In whose hands the resignation of a benefice must be made.

The Gloss here states that the matter redounds to the vexation of the excommunicate. Joannes \[de Lignano\] here holds a third position: namely, that for absolution nothing temporal ought to be paid; rather, one should have recourse to the superior, by the argument in *c. Nullus, de iure patronatus*. Joannes Andreae agrees and says that before these persons suffer any prejudice from the delay of absolution — for instance, because one hopes to be elected, or must himself elect, or prescription is running against him unless he acts — then the opinion of the Gloss applies, so that it may be said that the vexation redounds to his detriment. But if no such peril is present, then the opinion of the doctors applies. This distinction is sufficiently probable and equitable.

### On the same Gloss, at that passage.

\[The Gloss states\] that the excommunicate must remain in place before he is absolved, etc. In support of this statement, compare the text in *c. Venerabilibus*, and *Sextus, de sententia excommunicationis*. But the question arises: what if a statute provides that an excommunicate shall pay one hundred pounds for each month during which he remains excommunicated — is this valid? Hostiensis here says it is, because in this case the payment is not exacted for the absolution itself, but for the contumacy. For a pecuniary penalty can be exacted of anyone under canon law, as in *c. Collectus, de officio ordinarii*; and so the text in *c. 1, section 5, de iudiciis* can be understood when it speaks of satisfaction. He says, however, that some hold the view that the person should first be absolved and afterwards compelled to satisfy, according to the form of the statute.

### Lastly, Innocentius here poses the question:

Whether it is lawful for a person who is being vexed to give something temporal with impunity in order to redeem his vexation. He concludes that if the vexation concerns a temporal matter, it is permissible, because in that case there is no sin on the part of the giver. But if the matter turns on a spiritual thing or even on a possession, then the giver does not sin if he pays someone to cease vexing him. However, if the person does not have possession — that is, if he has been despoiled — then it is not lawful to pay money for it, because in that case he is not merely redeeming the vexation but also purchasing the possession. Note this well: although possession of itself is not something spiritual, nevertheless it is connected and joined to spiritual things, because through it one obtains the exercise of spiritual functions. On this subject more will be said in the ensuing chapter.

A simoniac is not only deposed, but must do penance for the sin committed.

A benefice acquired through simony must be resigned or ceded into the hands \[of the appropriate superior\].

A knowing simoniac in a benefice is not dispensed by the bishop.

### Monks guilty of simony and members of religious orders.

I discussed this fully in the *repetitio* on *c. Si quis contra clericum, de foro competenti*. Those who are simoniacal are transferred to stricter monasteries; others, after resignation, may be placed either in the same monasteries or in other monasteries of the same order. The first part is the consultation; the second, the response.

### Literal Case.

Certain regular canons, with their own knowledge, gained entry into monasteries and regular churches through simony. The question is what should be done with them. The pope responds that, since many express authorities are found on this point, nothing else is to be decreed except that they must entirely relinquish the place they obtained in such a manner and betake themselves to other, stricter monasteries, there to do penance. If, however, money was given without their knowledge, they are to be compelled to resign from that house and may afterwards be restored to the same place — if they can remain there without scandal — or else placed in another house of the same order.

### First point.

From the text: one who was received simoniacally — and who is himself simoniacal — is not only ejected from the place so obtained, but must do penance for the offence committed.
