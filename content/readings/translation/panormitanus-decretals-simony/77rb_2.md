## Capitulum XXVII.

### Summary

1 Witnesses brought forward to prove someone is a simoniac can be challenged solely on the ground that they are enemies or conspirators.

2 A prelate, even if he is personally good and holy, is punished not only eternally but also temporally for the excesses of subjects whom he does not punish; the same applies to a father who does not punish wicked sons.

3 In notorious cases the order of law is not required, and therefore one accusing a notorious criminal does not inscribe himself \[as accuser\], nor is a clamorous report required.

4 Whether simony requires a charitable admonition.

5 An inquisition requires not just one clamorous report but several, though this is left to the discretion of the judge.

6 Easily believing what tends to the prejudice of another is blameworthy; otherwise it is not, because it is a sign of good simplicity.

7 A judge can proceed from his office by way of inquisition even if the accuser or denouncer desists.

8 A prelate who is under suspicion and against whom an inquisition is being conducted is not removed from administration while the inquisition is pending; it is otherwise with a guardian, because in the crime of *lese-majesty* he does not have as strong a right as a prelate.

9 Dilapidation of goods, and the conjectures from which it is inferred.

10 Witnesses, however criminal, are admitted in the crime of *lese-majesty* and heresy.

11 Simony is not always equated with the crime of *lese-majesty*.

12 A notorious fact of a permanent nature requires no proof, but it is otherwise with a notorious fact of law or a transient notorious fact.

13 An extrajudicial demand is required when the debtor is bound with an added condition — unless the parties are of different jurisdictions. But if the creditor cited the defendant without a prior admonition, he shall be liable to the defendant for expenses; otherwise not.
