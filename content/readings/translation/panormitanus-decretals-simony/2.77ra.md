But the inferior members of the community are treated differently, both because they are not so readily presumed to have known, and because they do not have as much boldness in resisting. Whence, even among those who hold office by constitutional authority, I think it should be said that if it is established that they were ignorant, they ought not to be punished — yet the prelate himself is liable for whatever offence he commits. The argument is in c. j. *de off. cum palle.* c. *finit.* c. xvi. q. vi. *si epi.*

In the gloss at that place: “But can a college \[be punished\]?” Note this gloss through to the end, for reference is made to it whenever the question arises whether a college or university can be accused or punished. And a similar gloss appears at xi. q. ii. *ope*, which begins — yet this gloss concludes nothing of value.

At the word *grave*: in homicide the capital penalty threatens. Innocentius concludes that a university cannot be accused criminally, because its acts are as though those of a madman and similar persons who cannot be held responsible. For if the entire university were punished criminally, the absurd consequence would follow that innocents would be punished; and because excommunication does not fall upon a university, since it requires a true body and a baptized soul — and indeed, when some members die and others are substituted in their place, \[the university persists\]. *l. proponebat. ff. quod iure.* Whence it would be unjust that those who were incapable of deliberation should be punished. But these arguments are refuted in *rebus eccle. non alie*; see what is noted at xxiv. q. ii. *in summa.*

Joannes argues in *l. aut facta. ff. de pe.* that in many offences the innocent is punished on account of the guilty, as can be seen in the crime of *lese-majesty*, where a son is punished for his father. *l. quod. C. ad l. iu. ma.* and vi. q. j. *fascibus militibus*. Likewise in sacrilege, concerning which see *c. felicis. de penis. lib. vi.* To the same point, the text in *l. ususfructus. ff. quando ad usufruct. amit.*, where a city is sometimes subjected to the *aerarium* \[treasury\]. The Doctors note this well. Whence I conclude this matter, following Bartolus, who elegantly examines this question, and in *l. aut facta*. Two things principally had to be examined. First, whether a university or college — as a corporate head (for the terms signify the same thing) — can commit a crime. And from this you have the second doubt resolved: namely, whether it can be punished.

On the first point, it must be said that in truth a college — if a university is said to commit a crime — is not something other than the individuals who compose the college; but according to the law, the college is one thing and the individuals another, as in *c. quod manumittit.* and *c. novit. de his que fia pia.* Whence a college or university is a certain fictitious and representative person. *l. mortuo. ff. de fideiuss.* c. *pal.* c. *novit*, where it is said that the bishop and canons form one body. And certainly this body is not a real body, but a fictitious and representative one in the proper sense. Therefore a university cannot commit a crime, because it does not have a real body; rather, in the proper sense it is the individual and particular members of the university who commit crimes — though through that corporate body, by its voice, with respect to many legal effects.

Whence it must be noted that some offences consist in omission, and such an offence can indeed fall upon a university — speaking, however, according to the legal fiction. To this effect: *C. de sa. san. ec.* Thus also concerning the university *ff. or. tatio in universitate.* This accords with what is noted in *c. cum his*, twice, concerning the offence of negligence. See also what is noted in *c. de illis. S. de co. di. appo.* Other offences, however, consist in positive action, and in such offences one must consider what properly pertains to the university itself rather than to the individuals — such as enacting unjust statutes, or imposing unlawful levies or collections, and the like. For these cannot be attributed to individuals but to the university itself. Whence in such matters the university itself can commit a crime. See *aut. iuida comitas.* in *aut. cassa. C. de sa. san. ec.* and in *c. civitas*, acting as the principal, while individual members are liable as instigators, and if the act is general, see *c. non erit. j. de sen. exco.* Still other offences, however, are those that do not properly fall upon the university itself — such as fraud, homicide, and the like — and these are \[treated differently\] because they are common and arise daily.
