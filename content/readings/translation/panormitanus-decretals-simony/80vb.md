But since the priest was unwilling to do this, \[the cleric\] promised him that he would never seek any portion in that church if \[the priest\] would present him; and so by virtue of this presentation he was ordained. It was stated that this man was \[numbered among\] the clerics. ### The first rule, from the text: not only abbots but also lesser prelates, namely rectors of churches, may present others for ordination to the title of their churches.

\[Yet such a cleric\] is to be compelled \[to abstain\] only in two cases: if he is a notorious fornicator or simoniac in orders, as in the previously cited *§ verum*. But Innocentius \[held that\] a council may so decree without a judicial proceeding, at least for notorious \[offenses\] of those churches, as appears in *c. poena, de iure patronatus*. And from this he extends \[the rule in\] *c.* \[*finali*\] and \[concludes that\] the same holds true for any sin that impedes the exercise of orders — so that one cannot be ordained without a benefice of one’s own or of another, not even after penance has been completed, because the rationale is the same in these cases. And which those delicts are is noted in *Paleae, c. nisi, § de conscientia, de renunciatione*. And I examine this article more fully in *c. cum non liceat, de praescriptionibus*. For it suffices that one be ordained to the title of another’s church. From this it follows that he ought to receive sustenance from the fruits of that church, as if \[holding\] a claim.

### ¶ An archbishop, although he does not have jurisdiction over the subjects of his suffragans,

may nevertheless abolish a depraved custom. And \[the cleric\] has a benefice constituted for him from the revenues of that church. And note well that one may seek a certain portion — \[that is,\] one ordained to the title of another’s church may lawfully seek a portion there. And understand what I said: that for investiture or only for provision, nothing should be exacted from the churches; where the portions are directed to sustenance, one ought not to \[claim\] merely on account of the portion. And it seems that a division of the benefice was made; \[see\] *c. maioribus, supra de abbatibus*; but \[the beneficiary\] ought \[to do more\].

\[The text\] deals with two matters; the second is at “the depraved \[custom\].” And \[the text\] sufficiently conveys that a cleric \[is in this position\]; \[see the\] argument in *c. proposuisti, supra de temporibus ordinandorum*. Note: the subject matter is divided, for first \[the pope\] narrates the facts, and second he orders correction. And there \[is discussed the principle\] that what was done \[may have been\] lawful, etc. — \[but\] ignorance of law does not excuse from a delict; \[yet the offender is\] to be punished more mildly; \[see\] “cum vero.” The literal case: certain suffragans of the Archbishop of Canterbury were accustomed to receive a fixed sum of money.

And \[see\] *c. conquerente, de clericis non residentibus*. These men, fearing the canonical penalty and desiring correction, anticipated the time of payment, with both sides looking to their interests. Thus the ordained cleric may directly seek a portion and is bound to reside there and serve in divine worship; but if he should fail to reside, then he could be deprived of this portion or title. According to Joannes Andreae on the said *c. conquerente*, the same applies if the resources of the church were insufficient. But it could be said according to him that that chapter does not speak in the strict sense — namely, since avarice taints the grace to be conferred, the one who first receives money from the person presenting \[the candidate\] has only himself to blame \[for the consequences\]. But the text can also speak \[of another case\]: the expressed form manifests the appearance of a sale. \[This concerns\] a successor who was not at fault; and this \[is important\] for the understanding of that *c. conquerente*. And thus the pope orders the archbishop to suppress excesses of this kind and to punish his suffragans and their officials in such a way that the fault of others not be imputed to him as a penalty on account of his own negligence. In the second gloss, at the end, \[the commentator\] says here that the text notes well. At the end, he says that the archbishop ought to extirpate from his province that depraved custom according to which, for the investiture of churches, they exacted a mark of silver. Other prelates \[exacted\] a white cow or a fixed sum in lieu of a cow. \[*Joannes*\]. And the text there \[establishes\] from the principle that one sin begets another: sins lead the sinner to further sinning. Note secondly that a variation in the time of payment, or a change in name, does not excuse a contract or \[render\] its unlawful \[terms licit\] — \[one must look\] not only to the words but also to the nature of the contract.

### The second rule: a sale is more strongly established when the price precedes the delivery of the thing from which it follows;

because the delivery of a thing can pertain to many kinds of contracts, but the delivery of a price cannot. Note also that only an archbishop can punish his suffragans, on which see *c. ut litigantes, de officio ordinarii, libro sexto* and *c. sacro, de sententia excommunicationis*; and also the officials of suffragans who offend in their capacity as officials, on which see the aforesaid texts. And from the text another limitation is gathered: although in other cases one who is simoniac in orders is suspended not only from that order simoniacally received but also from others, nevertheless — and keep in mind that an archbishop can abolish the depraved custom of his entire province \[*c. ex frequentibus, de institutionibus*; see also *c. per tuas, supra eodem titulo*\] — in the case of this chapter, the person is suspended from the illicitly received order but not from others, as appears in the text. This occurs for the reason stated above. In such a case: one who ordains or presents under such a pact is punished more mildly, provided the matter concerns the entire province and the sin is notorious; \[see\] *c. Romana, § notoria, de censibus, libro sexto*. In the gloss, in the passage “qui paius,” at the end, according to Hostiensis: care should be taken about the return \[of payments\] if the price was paid within a certain period of time; \[see\] *Digest, de aedilicio edicto, l. aediles, § finali* and the following law, and the same is noted in *l. si traditio, Codex, de actione empti*. In the final gloss, note therefore from the gloss and the text that even for an investiture already performed — that is, for induction into corporeal possession — nothing may be exacted. For an archbishop can perform investiture of fact, not of right, as in *c. ad haec, de officio archidiaconi*. And thus for possession nothing should be given where the underlying matter is involved. The same portion, when the fruits are considered, is something temporal. The abbot therefore remitted this obligation of a temporal thing in order to obtain a spiritual right. And to remit in these matters is the same as to give, as in *c. veniens, clausula prima, de testibus, libro sexto*.

### ¶ Conventional simony is more detestable than mental simony.

\[The chapter of\] *Consulere* does not need a summary because it is brief, and in other words it can be summarized thus: simony \[is committed\] for a vicariate or for any other administration of spiritual or ecclesiastical things. One ordained as a subdeacon who promised to give money to his original presenter, \[on the condition that\] nothing would be sought from him, cannot minister in the order received or ascend to higher orders without a dispensation.

### 7. Those who give and those who receive are to be expelled from that church. D\[ecretum\].

The literal case. It was inquired of the lord pope what should properly be said concerning simoniacs. The above makes clear that whoever obtains any administration for money — both those giving and those receiving — are struck with simony, and they are expelled from that church to whose dishonor they wished to receive money.
