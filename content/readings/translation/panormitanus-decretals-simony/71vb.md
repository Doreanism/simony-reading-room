Statutes that make mention of “the convicted” are not satisfied by \[the case of\] those who fail to purge themselves, even though such persons are treated as if convicted.

When and what kind of interpretation statutes receive.

Whether one deposed from a benefice because he failed in his purgation of simony can, by episcopal dispensation, be admitted to that or another \[benefice\].

### In a Simoniacal Case

One who acts as solicitor in a case for a layman — which Abbot \[Panormitanus\] calls being involved in a case at the Roman curia or in an ecclesiastical forum — \[may do so\]; and through *c. Per venerabilem*, joined with the rubric, *qui filii sint legitimi*. He holds otherwise regarding a secular forum. First the pope sets out the account of the facts presented to him, and second he orders provision to be made. The second part begins at “Unde.” The literal case: in Cistercian churches of a certain diocese, several clerics are said to have gained entry through simony, and they have been noted with the mark of infamy on this account. The pope therefore commands the diocesan bishop that, if it is public and notorious that certain persons entered those churches through simony, he should remove them. As for those whose crime is not public and notorious, but who nevertheless labor under public infamy, he should impose purgation upon them with five or six compurgators of their own order; and if they fail, he should permanently deprive them of those benefices. Bernardus and Hostiensis \[note\] that a simoniac with respect to a benefice is not deposed from orders — since he is not convicted by way of accusation — but is deprived of the benefice simoniacally acquired. On this, see what I said in *c. Inquisitionis*, above, *de accusationibus*. He could also be subjected to another penalty, since \[deprivation of a benefice\] is not, strictly speaking, a penalty. In support of this, see the good basis in Antonius, *c. Cum clerici*, above, *de pactis*.

Note secondly that purgation may be imposed upon one who is defamed from the outset, without any prior inquisition into the truth of the crime having been conducted; but one who fails in it is punished as if he had been convicted through an inquisition. Against this, however, *c. Significasti*, above, *de adulteriis*, strongly objects — on which I spoke in *c. Accedens*, above, *de accusationibus*. For where no formal mode of proceeding preceded the imposition of purgation, one who fails in it should only be suspended from his benefice; but here it does not appear that no formal proceeding preceded — therefore these persons should not be deprived of their benefices if they fail in purgation. It is difficult to resolve this objection, and \[the text\] does not address it here. But one must consider one of two alternatives: either a formal inquisition aimed at deprivation of benefices preceded, and when the proofs were insufficient, purgation was imposed; or alternatively, this can be understood as something special to the crime of simony, where proofs need not be so clearly manifest. It is not fitting that favor be shown \[to simoniacs\]; rather, they should be immediately expelled from the church when certain notable signs of the crime are established against them, as in *c. Sicut* and *c. Grave*, *de praebendis*. But this can also be well \[applied\] in \[matters of\] simony, as above under the same title. And there is yet another point on which this chapter seems to deviate. Antonius \[says\] in *c. Qualiter*, under the same title, *de accusationibus*, where it was said that, given the notoriety of the crime, however the proceeding is conducted — even if by way of inquisition — deposition even from orders must follow, since the crime is grave; and *c. Cum non ab homine*, *de iudiciis*, supports this, where one apprehended in a crime is deposed. A fortiori, if the crime is notorious, deposition ought to follow. This objection too is not addressed here; but it can be said that these persons could also have been deposed from orders, as was done in *c. De hoc*, above under the same title. But here the bishop asked only about deprivation of benefices, and the pope responds accordingly.

In the final gloss, where it says “passes into a violent \[presumption\],” etc.: note this final gloss, which I customarily cite every day to explain the true reason why one who fails in purgation is punished as if convicted. For he is not punished on the basis of a merely probable presumption, since that does not suffice for condemning anyone of a crime, as in *c. Literas*, *de praesumptionibus*. But a probable presumption arising from infamy passes into a violent \[i.e., compelling\] presumption when the accused fails to purge himself; and a violent presumption suffices for condemnation, as in *c. Afferte*, above, *de praesumptionibus*, and *l. Indicia*, *C. de rei vindicatione*. This should be understood as I explain in *c. Ad*, *de probationibus*. Similarly, note the notable gloss in *c. Cum contumacia*, *de haereticis*, book 1, where a presumption arising against one suspected of heresy passes into a violent one by standing under excommunication for a year. A similar \[principle applies\] in *c. Rurfus* and *c. Quicumque*, 12 *q.* \[2\].

Gather also from this gloss that from infamy arising against someone, a probable presumption is born; \[infamy,\] although it takes the place of an accusation, creates some degree of proof — although the doctors hold the contrary in the aforesaid *c. Qualiter*. On whether and when infamy or rumor creates a definite degree of proof, see the canonists’ discussion in *c. Veniens* 1, *de testibus*, and Joannes on *l. De minore*, section “tormenta,” *ff. de quaestionibus*.

And the foregoing can be applied to a statute providing that one convicted of a certain crime shall be punished with a certain penalty: whether one who fails in purgation is to be regarded as convicted so that the statutory penalty may be imposed. It seems that the answer is yes, because one who fails in purgation is punished as the perpetrator of the offense, as here, in *c. De hoc*, above under the same title, and in *c. Accedens*, above, *de accusationibus*. Likewise, statutes receive their interpretation from the common law, as in *c. Cum dilectus*, above, *de consuetudine*; Innocentius notes this in *c. Causam*, above, *de rescriptis*, and Joannes in *l. De populi*, *ff. de institutionibus et iure*. I hold the contrary to be true, because one who fails in purgation is constructively — not truly — convicted, as the notable gloss says at the end of *c. Inter* 1, *de purgatione canonica*. But in statutes, a constructive interpretation has no place; for the words must be taken properly and strictly, not fictitiously, because legal fiction has no place in them — as in *l.* 3, section “hec autem verba,” *ff. de negotiis gestis*, and as noted in *c. Nostra* 1, *de initio* \[title\].
