### Second, gather

that a prebend can consist in temporal things of three kinds. And such a prebend can fall even to a layperson.

On this, understand the gloss in *c. cum* \[*de vestimentis*\]. And if anyone says that a prebend is called a spiritual right, this holds true for that which is annexed to a canonry and so understood. \[See the\] *Collecta, supra de praebendis*, and see what Innocentius notes in *c. maioris*. But these matters briefly.

Either one is simoniac in receiving holy orders, or simoniac in conferring holy orders, or in a benefice. Note also that canonical right is spiritual right: hence it does not fall to a layperson. And in *c. ii, de institutionibus* and what is noted there. For the sake of good \[order\], it is not permitted to promise and give temporal things, as is said in *c. Iacob, primo eodem*.

In all orders, even those lawfully received, and at the time of receiving spiritual things, a donation of some temporal thing may be made, provided it is done freely. \[This is supported by\] *c. in praesentia, de probationibus*, together with *c. ad apostolicam, primo concordantia*; and see the gloss that discusses this in *c. emendari* \[*1 quaestio 3; 1 quaestio 2; c. quam pio, 1 quaestio distinctione*\].

In the final gloss, Innocentius says that in prohibited matters, the simoniacal intention alone induces \[the stain of\] death. Those things are called prohibited which are simoniacal with respect to spiritual things and by divine law — such as selling sacred things and the like. And this is what Innocentius wished \[to say\] here and in *c. tanta, supra eodem*.

\[These opinions\] are found in *c. eos*; and see the gloss on clerics. In simoniacal matters that are prohibited, intention alone does not induce simony — as when someone renounces his benefice with the intention of obtaining some temporal thing. \[See\] *de poenis, in verbo non nonam*.

Whether one suspended from office is also suspended from benefice — \[relevant texts\] support this. See the notable gloss in *c. ex parte, de officio delegati*. Joannes Andreae \[held\] without distinction that intention alone does not induce simony except mental simony, and there he speaks in a similar \[vein\], *distinctione c. nullus; 7 c. preter*.

And I understand this to be true regarding one who committed simony \[in conferral\], but otherwise regarding one who received the order \[simoniacally\]. The difference is greater between mental simony and conventional simony. For when another commits simony while the recipient is ignorant, even then the latter is truly suspended. In conventional simony, one is bound to restore such things — whether spiritual or temporal — unlawfully received, and cannot otherwise do penance, as in *c. \[ingratitudine\], in paroechia*. But in mental simony, it can be absolved by penance alone, as is proved \[by the fact that the recipient\] lawfully received \[the order\] and is not \[deprived\] of benefices previously lawfully obtained or to be obtained; \[see\] *c. de simoniace, 3 eodem*. There Innocentius wished this. I think the same in *c. finali eodem titulo*, where the text speaks without distinction of all spiritual things. It proceeds therefore whether they are spiritual things by divine law or by constitution of the Church, since \[the text\] does not otherwise distinguish. \[See\] *distinctione finali \[on this matter\]*.

\[The question\] what happens if the person has been sleeping \[i.e., ignorant\]. That chapter, so introduced, admirably proves this opinion.

### ¶

And from another \[perspective\]: if someone corrupts a judge or a witness in a case involving spiritual things, this constitutes simony. \[The author\] concludes that it does so on account of a certain connection with spiritual things, or it will be quasi-simony; \[see the\] argument in *c. nemo* and *c. sequenti*. But if one gives money in a purely temporal case, that is not \[simony\] — the opposite \[position appears\] in the *Paleae, c. poena*, and he considers that to be true. I conclude therefore that a simoniac bishop can confer orders, even though he is suspended by law. One ought not to act against one’s erroneous conscience before first setting it aside, and one who does otherwise prepares a seat for himself in eternal fire.

### Per tuas \[X 5.3.35\].

One ought not to receive holy orders from someone whom one believes to be a simoniac. But one may minister in those \[orders\] which one previously received from him.

### The actual case.

A certain monk of the Carthusian order received the order of the subdiaconate from his diocesan \[bishop\]. Afterwards he came to understand that this bishop had frequently committed the sin of simony, on account of which the same monk was afraid to minister in the order he had received, and was also troubled about being promoted to higher orders by the same bishop. On this matter the pope, having been consulted, responded that he may securely minister in the order he received, but for the sake of conscience he should not ascend to higher orders, lest perhaps he build toward Gehenna; and because he had fallen into greater difficulty from having had a conscience that was excessively scrupulous — yet he will not escape it unless he sets aside his errors.

The first rule, from the text: the argument \[is\] that a simoniac cannot confer the exercise of orders, because he himself does not possess it, just as one who is suspended cannot offer to another \[what he does not have\]. For no one gives what he does not have \[*c. Daibertum*\]. I shall speak on this more fully below.

The second rule: where there is no sin in believing \[something to be the case\], or where one cannot inform one’s conscience and it remains in doubt, one ought not to act against conscience — even an erroneous one — unless one first sets it aside. And to this point this text is daily cited.
