And note first from the text that from the mere agreement to give something temporal for something spiritual, simony is committed once the spiritual thing has been received, even though the promised temporal thing has not yet been handed over. For here the contract preceded the entry into religious life. And second, conclude that simony is committed not only by one who gives a temporal thing to the one conferring \[the spiritual good\], but also by one who gives to the ministers or household members of the one conferring, just as we say in the case of Giezi, who was the servant of Heliseus \[2 Kings 5:20–27\] \[I q.1, *in summa*; and *c. cito turpe*\]. Third, note and observe that the pope here appears to have diminished \[the punishment\] when he says that this wrongdoing by the abbot and monks is punished \[only against\] the abbot and senior members of the convent. But understand this to mean when only they were culpable. It is otherwise if, after consultation with all, the offense was carried out \[by common counsel\]. For then all must be punished, as argued in *c. romana*, above, on collective bodies, *de sententia excommunicationis*, Book VI. Even those who were present in chapter but remained silent when they ought to have spoken against it are punished, as Sinibaldus notes singularly in *c.* I, *de his qui fiunt a maiore parte capituli*. Finally, note that one received into the monastic state through simony ought not to remain in the same monastery, nor even live in the world, but must enter another monastery. And whether he is truly a monk \[is discussed next\].

In the final gloss, beginning “it appears that he was a monk”: Note the beginning of this gloss, from which it appears that simony committed in connection with entry into the monastic state does not invalidate that state. Nor does the objection that follows in the gloss hold — namely, that here there was no agreement that the monk should keep money for himself, but rather that he should hand the money over to the monastery. And this is not contrary to the substance of the monastic state. Likewise, money was retained by him so that he might enter another monastery and not be a burden to the second monastery; but the one entering ought not to retain it for himself. And this also appears to be the view of the doctors. Certain others, however, say that this text does not prove that this person remains a monk. For at the beginning \[the text\] calls him “priest” and “monk.” But an objection arises from the end of the chapter, where it orders him to enter another monastery — because this could have been so that he might do penance there. And this consideration is raised here by Vocatus Antonius. I follow the first opinion. First, on account of the end of this chapter, where it says that he must serve in the monastic habit. Therefore it is not merely so that he may do penance. Likewise, it does not command that he become a monk, but that he serve in another monastery in the \[monastic\] habit. Therefore he remained a monk. To the same effect I adduce the text which in my judgment confirms this view in *c. qui* I, under the same title, where it is said that one so received ought to enter a monastery “lest he damnably wander in the world.” These words clearly indicate that those who so transfer remain monks. Nor does it prescribe that he do penance, but rather that he not remain idle in the world, as argued in *c.* final, *de regularibus*. Likewise, properly speaking, simony is committed not with respect to the monastic state itself, but so that one may have a place in that particular monastery. Thus entry with respect to that monastery is vitiated, but the monastic state holds. For it is a certain obligation made to God, which the wrongful act of the one receiving money does not impede. For since this person intended to obligate himself to God, the wrongful act of another does not impede this obligation. This is supported by what Johannes Andreae notes in *c.* final, *de conditionibus appositis*. Just as we see in the case of a son, that excommunication does not impede the monastic state, as in *c. cum illorum* I, *de sententia excommunicationis*. And from this you have a case in which simony vitiates the act \[with respect to the particular monastery, but not the monastic state itself\]. — Abbas.

### De simonia \[On Simony\].

A prelate who has received money by agreement for conferring holy orders commits simony. If a priest knows something as God does — that is, in the confessional — even if he knows it, \[and even if\] the pope has revealed it to him, he is not believed \[as a witness\]. By the words “you shall admonish and persuade,” the power to compel strictly is not given. A simoniac in holy orders, if he enters a religious house, is not thereby rehabilitated to exercise the order simoniacally received. Entry into religious life does not remove defects contracted from the sin of the one entering; it is otherwise for defects contracted without sin. Whether and when simony is induced by giving something before or after \[the spiritual act\], and when restitution is required. Whether religious are impeded from being promoted to sacred orders by the mere prohibition of a superior when no other cause appears. Religious are not impeded from exercising sacred orders already received by a mere prohibition when no other cause appears. A simoniac in holy orders is suspended from that order by operation of law itself.

The abbot and senior members of the convent: one who is suspended and excommunicated by law can be suspended and excommunicated again by a human judge. And those who received money by agreement for the reception of a monk are suspended from office and benefice. And money so received is to be restored.
