### As for being induced to perform funeral rites or to render other spiritual services:

a priest may then hire out his spiritual labors — not so as to receive a temporal thing directly in exchange for spiritual ones, but in compensation for the losses he sustains in making himself present and in ministering, as noted in the chapter on this point \[ne praelati vices suas\]; and the gloss \[q. ## Capitulum XXVI.

### 1. One who receives simoniacally, and one who is simoniacally received, is deposed.

### 2. One convicted through the process of inquisition of simony committed, not in orders but in benefices, is deprived of the benefice or place simoniacally acquired.

### 3. An archbishop cannot depose the subjects of his suffragan bishop unless the offenses are also notorious, or unless the archbishop also exercises the right of legate.

### 4. A monk simoniacally received is to be removed from that monastery and placed in a stricter one; but in the latter he may remain only by dispensation.

### 5. Simony is not committed in those things that are given freely, unless a prior assessment was made.

### 6. Simony is not committed if one received as a canon pays something by virtue of a statute so disposing.

### 7. Whether simony is committed by giving money to infidels so that they convert to the faith.

### 8. Whether in simony proceedings are brought against secular clerics as much as against regulars.

### 9. Whether individuals are punished for a delict committed by the entire chapter.

### 10. In criminal matters, presumed knowledge is not punished, but only actual knowledge.

### 11. Where a delict was committed, yet not capitally, only the senior members of the chapter are punished.

### 12. Whether a universitas \[corporate body\], college, or chapter can commit an offense — whether by omission, commission, command, or ratification — and whether it can be punished.

### 13. A universitas is a kind of fictitious and representative person.

### 14. A universitas cannot be excommunicated, because excommunication requires a real body and soul.

### 15. When is a universitas said to offend as a whole.

### 16. If a universitas has been punished, whether afterwards the individual members of the universitas can be punished.

Upon the one who simoniacally receives, and upon the senior members, a fitting penalty is imposed; meanwhile they are suspended from the exercise of sacred orders. The chapter is divided: in the first part the question and its rejoicing \[i.e., the preliminary exposition\]; in the second part the distinguishing response, at the word “we”; in the third part the mandate, at the word “enjoin”; in the fourth part instruction, at the words “that thing.” The literal case is as follows: the Archbishop of Canterbury, while visiting his diocese in monasteries and other religious houses, found that the plague of simony had proliferated, so that in them many had been received for a price who ought to have been received freely. But because the multitude \[of offenders\] was vast, the pope \[wished\] to relax something from severity. And those who were accused before him — after the crime has been established through judicial process — both givers and receivers are to be punished with canonical penalties. If, however, the matter has been established through inquisition alone, those who were simoniacally received in such places are to be removed from them, corrected, and sent to stricter houses to perform penance. And upon the abbots, priors, prelates of whatever rank, and their officials, he is to impose a fitting penance; until they have completed it, he is to suspend them from the exercise of sacred orders, and is to instruct his bishops to cause this form to be observed throughout their dioceses — provided that if anything is freely offered to them, it may be received without scruple.

From the text, note that one convicted through inquisition is not deposed from office, but is nevertheless removed from the place simoniacally acquired, for after performing the imposed penance he may licitly minister in his order and is transferred to another monastery or another \[house\].

Regarding suffragans: the doctors understand the case here as special to the Archbishop of Canterbury, who exercises not only metropolitan right but also legatine authority, as in the first chapter *De officio legati*.
