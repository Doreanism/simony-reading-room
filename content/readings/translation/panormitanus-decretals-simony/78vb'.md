In the preceding decretal, because the proposed abbot raised several exceptions about the witnesses brought against him on the charge of simony — and the pope admitted some exceptions and rejected others — whence it appeared that criminal witnesses ought to be admitted in the crime of simony. Thus it was decided. Because \[suitable witnesses\] cannot easily be found, since they frequently perish over the length of time, therefore he says that in this case recourse to oath will suffice.

Nevertheless \[they should provide\] prompt proof: that is to say, where the matter is ancient — as where a person has long stood in peaceful possession of a benefice — it suffices to prove the title. How this is to be understood, \[see\] *In noverimus*, etc.

### First rule, from the text.

When one proceeds against a beneficed person only for removal from administration, the proceeding is said to be civil and not criminal; nor is the person properly said to be accused. And from this, note well the singular points in *de officio ordinarii*, book 6, and *c. Contra Moze*. For the common conclusion is that where the bishop tacitly consents, he cannot afterward — even though the matter involves a crime — proceed criminally, since \[the proceeding\] tends toward vindication insofar as the crime is punished by the established penalty. But when one proceeds only for removal from a benefice, the principal aim is not to punish the offense but \[to act\] for the public good, so that the unworthy and harmful person cannot retain the title, nor does he consent tacitly or expressly — because from a notorious \[offense\] injury is done to the church.

On this point, the relative clause can be distinguished, and the opinions reconciled and supplemented: either the matter is ancient but the superior did not retain the title and did not consent tacitly or expressly.

But the matter is ancient. These concern the crime of \[simony\]. Here he touches successively upon two initial readings: that criminal witnesses are admitted as witnesses if they have been reformed. And then the word *praesertim* placed in section 5 stands for *tamen* \[nevertheless\]. And the third reading is that criminal witnesses, even those persevering in their crimes, are admitted together with other supporting evidence; then the word *praesertim* is to be expounded simply, according to the variety of readings and interpretations, and is more fully discussed at the word *taliter*, and there I shall speak on the matter. And through this the second gloss is to be explained, which is at the word *praesertim*: if it is expounded as meaning *tantum* \[only\], the exposition is improper. Nevertheless that singular reading \[holds\] insofar as it maintains that when the word *praesertim* is expounded as meaning *tantum*, it is an improper exposition; the proper exposition, therefore, is that it be taken comparatively — as Palla. wished, above, on the word *taliter*. \[The term operates\] comparatively, because it presupposes the same thing in its correlative position or in the contrary case: so the meaning is, “especially when the witnesses have been reformed of their crime” — that is to say, even when they are not reformed they are admitted, but they are admitted more readily because they are reformed. And from this he infers that since in statutes words must be taken in their proper sense, as noted in *c. In nostra* \[*de iureiurando*\], this word *praesertim* in them properly imports a comparison. And the same is to be said of the word *maxime* — that it be taken comparatively, as noted in the gloss on *c. Auditis* \[*de electione*\] and *c. Quam frequenter*, in the section *Porro*, concerning whether even infamous persons are admitted in the crime \[of simony\] when the suit is not yet joined.

On the gloss at the word *de simoniace*, at the end: he simply holds that the same rule applies in other excepted crimes. Although the doctors pass over the matter without qualification, it does not seem true in all respects here. For I concede that those who are admitted \[as witnesses\] in simony are admitted in the other excepted crimes, but not all who are barred here are barred in the other excepted crimes. The point is clear in the crime of heresy and in the crime of *laesa maiestas* \[treason\]: there, infamous and criminal persons are admitted without distinction, as above. And in judging — given equality of persons — here in the crime of simony they are not admitted without distinction; rather, the considerations set out in the text must be observed. Although these and certain others are called excepted crimes because they do not follow the rules of other crimes in all respects, nevertheless one crime is more severely punishable than another, either because of the magnitude of the crime, or because of the privilege of the prince or of the church, or for some other consideration.

On the gloss at the word *excellentiores*: note thus the gloss together with this text, that an abbot is not judged to hold an eminent dignity, but rather an abbey is counted among the lesser dignities.

And at the end of the gloss, note: because the conclusion of this gloss does not resolve the objection but rather strengthens it. For if equality is to be observed in judicial proceedings, then greater and lesser persons ought not to be punished differently. Therefore, by way of a better resolution, it can be said that equality tends toward the declaration of what is to be observed in judicial proceedings, given an equality of persons. Where there is a difference \[between the persons\], then to maintain equality would in fact be to create inequality, as argued in *c. De multa*, at the end \[*de praebendis*\]. And on this see what is noted in *c. Novit* \[*de iudiciis*\].

On the gloss at the word *vel minora*, where the objection is raised: today say rather as is held in *c.* 1 *de testibus*, book 6.
