### Abbas \[Panormitanus\].

## Capitulum XXV.

That nothing may be exacted for conducting the dead to burial.

That the parish priest must administer spiritual things to his parishioners without charge.

That mental simony is committed when a priest goes about his office with the intention of gaining something; this is remitted through penance alone, without any restitution.

That simony is not committed if something is received for funeral rites of the dead or for the nuptial blessing, provided there is a custom to this effect.

That custom is not established through acts that are merely facultative \[i.e., done freely at will\], except in favor of the Church.

That custom is established if all the members of a community were doing something and if afterwards one person, persisting in such observance, was encouraged by others to do the same, and he then followed the practice of the others — for in such a case the consent of the people wishing to introduce a custom is apparent.

That a continued payment over a period of time, related to a definite obligation, establishes a custom.

That a custom by which one is bound to give something in a lawful case is established within the space of ten years and gives an action to anyone wishing to claim something by virtue of such a custom.

Whether a priest may, in the absence of custom, demand anything for funeral rites of the dead or the like.

That a cleric holding a meager benefice must blame himself for having accepted it, and ought to seek his livelihood through some honorable industry.

That if a priest has no means of support and cannot otherwise honestly obtain a livelihood, the bishop must provide for him, or the people may be compelled to furnish him with the necessities of life; indeed, he may withhold \[spiritual services\] if offerings are not made to him.

That a priest, whether poor or not, may hire out his spiritual labors to a stranger \[i.e., to one outside his parish\].

**\[C\]um nobis** \[When it was brought before us\]. Concerning the nuptial blessings and funeral rites of the dead, nothing may be exacted. This chapter is well divided: first the question is set forth; second, the judge’s determination, at the words “to your discretion,” etc. A chaplain of Villafranca was exacting money from his parishioners for funeral rites of the dead and for the nuptial blessing, and was unjustly extorting it. The parishioners complained to the pope. Whereupon the apostolic \[authority\] by canon law commands certain persons to admonish the chaplain to desist from such presumption and to make suitable satisfaction for his excess. If, however, he refuses to obey, they are to punish him with canonical penalties.

### Note 1.

From the text: the parish priest must administer the sacraments and other spiritual things to his parishioners without charge. Secondly, gather that any priest — whether the proper pastor or another — is bound to administer spiritual things gratuitously; if he receives anything by agreement, he must restore it. Understand this of conventional simony; it is otherwise in mental simony, where no agreement intervened but only a depraved intention. In such a case, the sin is abolished by penance alone, as in the last chapter on this title, where the gloss discusses this eagerly. In the chapter *Nico* \[de clericis non residentibus, lib. VI\], the question is raised concerning a canon who attends the divine office solely with the intention of receiving his daily distributions: this too is mental simony, not conventional, and is abolished by penance alone without restitution; on this, see what is fully noted there.

As for other matters, it is lawful for a priest to seek his livelihood by his own hands or industry. Hostiensis says that an action \[i.e., a legal claim\] is available where there is such a custom. But against this the text in the chapter *Cum in ecclesiis* on this title objects, where the text specifically speaks of funeral rites of the dead and of nuptial blessings and condemns the custom. Zenzilinus briefly and succinctly says that perhaps for the spiritual services themselves to be rendered, a custom cannot be introduced requiring that something be given; it is otherwise, however, for labor.
