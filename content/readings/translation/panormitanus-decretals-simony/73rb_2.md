The cardinal did not sin on account of the palfrey received from the nobleman. The quality and quantity of the gift \[must be considered\] in the case of the recipient. In accepting gifts or presents, the contraries must be considered.

### “But blessed is he who shakes out” \[his hands from holding of bribes\]

And note from this text against those friars \[the Fraticelli\] who abstain by law. Likewise against those who doubt in excess about things — which is to be reproved in a jurist, as in *c. cum in iure perito*, *de electione*, and in *c. cum iure perito*, *de officio delegati*. \[See also\] *l.* Labeo, *ad Carbonianem*, in *l.* I, Digest, *ad municipalem*. And note that if a cardinal is in effect \[acting as\] a nuncio, as in *c. significasti*, *de electione*. And for the honor of the cardinal, a legation ought to be assigned to him.

### Third, note in particular this text,

\[which asks\] whether in a case of doubt it should be presumed that a gift was gratuitous or not. For the circumstances of quality in the person of the giver and the recipient must be considered: from a rich and wealthy person, a free donation is more readily presumed than from a poor person or one of lesser rank. Likewise the quantity of the gift must be considered: for one is not readily presumed to confer freely things of the greatest value, because no one is presumed to throw away his own \[property\], as in *c. super hoc*, *de renuntiatione*, and *l. cum venit*, of the hunting season. For a person is more readily presumed to give when another’s necessity is pressing than where no necessity exists. And on the basis of this text and the aforesaid \[authorities\], the gloss on feuds expands \[the principle\] — a gloss well worth noting — in *l. cum ad*, Digest, *si certum petatur*, on the question: if someone said to another in the vernacular, “take this money,” is a donation presumed, or a deposit, or a loan? And certainly from the preceding indications it must be determined whether it was intended as a loan, a deposit, or a donation; and in case of doubt, when those indications are absent, a loan or deposit is to be presumed, on account of the presumption that no one is presumed to throw away his own \[property\]. And from these \[considerations\] note Bartolus in the said *l.* when he says that a witness proves a loan if he simply says “I saw money counted out to him, but I do not know whether he donated it or lent it” — on which point, state the matter more fully as he notes there.
