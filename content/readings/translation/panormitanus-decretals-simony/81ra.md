This accords with what is said above under the same title, and in *c. Audivimus* below under the same title.

Second, that simony is committed not only in obtaining a spiritual benefice, but also in any administration of ecclesiastical property; and thus this is simoniacal because it is prohibited. Concerning these administrations, the matter is treated at length in *C.2 q.3 Salvator q.1*. And from this you will observe, following Johannes, that certain things are spiritual according to their essence — some of these are incorporeal, others corporeal. The first kind, namely incorporeal things, cannot be sold even in fact, because they do not fall within the scope of agreement nor can they be appraised — such as the virtues and gifts of God; concerning these, see *C.1 q.1 c. in c.1* and the Gloss there. But corporeal things cannot be sold by law, though in fact they are sold — not, however, without the taint of simony; for example, the relics of the saints, as in *c. f. q.5 de reliquiis et veneratione sanctorum*. Certain things are spiritual by reason of their cause, such as the sacraments taken both in the strict and the broad sense. For the sacraments are spiritual by reason of their cause, because through them we obtain grace; and because these are corporeal, they are susceptible of a price — though not without the taint of simony — as in *c. Modo satis*, and *c. Cum in ecclesiis* above.

### Certain things are spiritual by reason of a statute:

such as religious life, and concerning these there is further discussion in *c. Dilecti* etc., and *c. Veniens* above. Certain things are spiritual because they depend on spiritual authority, as in *c. Ad aures* above. And others are spiritual by reason of office, as in *c. Ad nostram* under the same title. In these things, no sale may intervene. And what pertains to a cleric by reason of his orders is treated there as well. Fourth, by publication — such as ringing the church bell, closing the doors of the church, and similar acts — and if someone is an administrator of ecclesiastical property, these things cannot be sold. For they have a certain dependence on spiritual things, as do prebends and benefices, as we have explained above. Certain things are annexed to spiritual goods because they are dedicated to the use of spiritual things, such as sacred vessels and sacerdotal vestments and the like.

Concerning the right of patronage, it is certain that since it is annexed to spiritual goods, it cannot be sold, as in *c. de iure patronatus*; but it passes with the temporal estate as a whole, as in *c. Ex literis* under the same title *de iure patronatus*. Nor can the cause of the right of patronage be determined in the judgment of lay persons, as in *c. Quanto de tutoribus*. And in the Gloss there, \[it is noted that\] if women have entered improperly, they should be removed; and when others of the same order have gained access to the matter, then one must consider: either a pact intervenes, and the person commits conventional simony, which is extremely detestable; or no pact intervenes but only a depraved intention, and thus it is mental simony, which is not so execrable because it is removed through penance alone without restitution, as is said in *c. Tua* under the same title, and as is held in the final chapter under the same title.

### Simony is committed if anything is received for the consecration of bishops,

or for the blessing of an abbey, or for the ordination of clerics, notwithstanding any custom — which holds true even if payment is received after the fact. The chapter *Plata* \[*Sane*\] made mention of this first.

Simony is not committed if those admitted to orders are compelled to give something for the fabric \[of the church\], provided there is a constitution regarding this and provided the order is first freely conferred.

And it is not simony if, at the time of chrism, a census otherwise owed to the bishop is given to him, provided there is a custom regarding this.

Just as it is certain that the matter is different with respect to the penalty for past acts — and thus with respect to that penalty, one is considered not to act \[simoniacally\] — for the consecration of bishops and the blessings of abbeys and the ordinations of clerics, no custom excuses. The text first narrates the excess and its excuse. Second, it rejects the excuse, prohibits the excess, and adds a penalty, at the word *tum*.

### The literal case.

In many places, exactions are practiced by many prelates for the consecrations of bishops, blessings, and the like; and it is fixed how much must be given to this person or that, or to another of this sort, defending such depravity through custom. The Council thoroughly rejects this custom — which ought rather to be called a corruption — by expressly prohibiting anyone from presuming to extort or exact anything under any pretext for such purposes; otherwise let both the one who gave and the one who received be condemned together with Simon \[Magus\] and for simony.

Note first that a bishop must be consecrated \[freely\].

And it can also be gathered from this that monks and nuns should not be entrusted \[with the handling of money\] even on the pretext of poverty; this holds true here and likewise, just as a soldier of the armed militia must attend to arms, so too a soldier of the celestial militia must attend to divine things. But if perchance something else could be said to them, because Hostiensis himself holds that it can be induced by custom that the person being ordained should give something for the fabric of the church and for divine worship, provided nevertheless that the order is freely conferred — but afterward the ordained person may be compelled to observe a praiseworthy custom, as in *c. Ad apostolicam* below and as is noted there. And Hostiensis says this well, that not only from lay persons but also from clerics something may be obtained under the pretext of custom for divine worship and the needs of the church. And see below on this in the final question, in many passages toward the end, and in *c. Jacobus* below, where I shall discuss this.

And according to the doctors, the custom is also valid that a census otherwise owed to the bishop is delivered at the time of chrism, because it is not given principally for the chrism. Note this.

### An abbess or prioress or other female prelate does not come under the designation of “nuns.”

Under the designation of “nuns,” monks are not included.
