## Capitulum XXIII.

### 1. One who was unknowingly simoniacal ought not to be deprived of his benefice, unless fraud is discovered, as below in point 4.

### 2. Simony committed by one person, while others are ignorant, vitiates the act with respect to all. The reason is perhaps as discussed below in the next chapter.

### 3. If an excommunicated person participates in the acts of a corporate body, those acts are not vitiated with respect to the others unless he was knowingly admitted.

### 5. Simony is induced by a mere promise to give something temporal for something spiritual.

### 4. Fraud and deceit are proven through conjectures.

Whether and when simony is committed on account of the giving of money to elect \[a person\], or through another, even if the person would have been elected without the money.

\[Whether simony can be resolved\] in my judgment, and the collation was illegitimate. The first fault is that the collation is simoniacal, and insofar as it is simoniacal, it must be rescinded — there is a text for this in the next chapter. In the Gloss, Joannes Andreae refers to what is noted there. And say that this Gloss does not much restrict \[the rule\], as is said here; and rightly so, since when the cause ceases, the effect is extinguished — because an appeal is revoked when the grievance is withdrawn — and a right extinguished by resignation does not revive. Therefore no right, at least no legitimate right, was acquired by the person to whom the collation was made. And then the second opinion of the other reading proceeds: that the election was not free, and perhaps they would have changed their minds, and the intention of those involved cannot be proven since they are parties to the crime. In this case all the arguments of Joannes Andreae are conclusive. Or the money was given to a third party, perhaps a relative or friend of those who were to elect; and then, if the money was given on behalf of the person who was expected to be elected, the election is vitiated even if they would otherwise have elected him, because nothing ought to be given for the purpose of obtaining a spiritual right, as noted in *c. Ad aures, section 8, eodem titulo*, and as I shall say in the next chapter. For a place in a religious order must be denied to those who seek and canvass for it, and such an ambitious person ought to be rejected \[*Causa 7, quaestio 1, In scripturis*\]. Or it was given by another person; and then the first opinion can well stand, for the intention of the electors — who would otherwise have elected him — can be proven through those same electors, since they are not implicated in the crime; and perhaps, having deliberated among themselves, they had unanimously resolved to elect him. In this case the text in the final section cited above properly applies, and all the considerations of Joannes Andreae cease to be relevant.

### Literal Case.

When a certain parish fell vacant, the chapter of that parish gave authority to elect to four persons, who elected a parish priest. Friends of the person whose election was expected promised money to the said four — or to all of them — that they would pay them; and so, without the elected person’s knowledge, he was elected while this promise was being made. The question is whether such an election, if this is proven, should be quashed. The pope responds that unless it is established that those who made such a promise did so fraudulently, to the disadvantage of the person who was to be elected as the better candidate, the election, as tainted by the presumption of simoniacal depravity, must be entirely quashed. A second question is then raised: suppose someone has been elected to the prelacy of a certain church, and his election has been quashed on account of simoniacal depravity as described.

Abbas \[Panormitanus\].

\[The decretal concerns whether\] a dispensation may be granted. It would seem not. Bernardus \[de Bottone\] says: An election is simoniacal not only when money has been promised or given to all the electors, but also when it has been given or promised to even one of them; thus the depravity of one person taints the entire act. This is remarkable. \[By contrast,\] if an excommunicated person participates in the acts of a corporate body, he does not thereby vitiate the entire act unless he was knowingly admitted, as in *c. Si, de procuratoribus* and noted in *c. illa, de electione*. And note that a mere promise to give something temporal for something spiritual induces simony — in agreement with *c. Veniens, section 8, eodem titulo* and *c., de consuetudine*.

Note a singular case in which an election made through simony is not vitiated: namely, when the simony was committed in fraud of the person who was expected to be elected. Otherwise, the fraud may become apparent through conjectures — for example, those who were opposing the election gave money so that \[their candidate\] would be elected. For fraud and deceit are proven through conjectures, as in *c. 2, de rescriptis, Liber Sextus*, and in the passage cited \[by the Gloss\].

And consider carefully: the text first says that \[the money\] does harm — for those who acted \[in giving it\] appeared to be friends, but were not. From this, gather that fraud can be proven through conjectures even against one who appeared to be a friend. In the Gloss on the final section, it says, as it were expressly, etc.: the depravity and deed of another ought not to harm \[the person elected\], since he would have been elected in any case. Similarly, we say that those who do not induce simony \[are excused\] when the person to be elected is otherwise suitable, as discussed in *c. Tua, de aetate et qualitate*. Hostiensis held this opinion here. But Calixtinus and Hostiensis hold the contrary opinion, which follows in the Gloss. Hostiensis said, on the same title: the money was given to another. The first opinion applies \[when the money was given to a third party\]; but the second opinion applies when it was given to the electors themselves or to the ordaining party.
