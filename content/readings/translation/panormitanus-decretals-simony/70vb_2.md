### Beyond the Gloss

Beyond the gloss, the question is raised: may money lawfully be given to a priest who refuses to baptize otherwise, or should the sacrament of baptism rather be forgone? On this see the good gloss in *c. baptizandis* \[*C.* 1, *q.* 5\], where the conclusion appears to be as follows. If the person to be baptized is an adult, he ought not to contribute anything but should rather forgo baptism, because since he believes and the failure to baptize is not his fault, he is regarded as having been baptized, by analogy with *c. debitum*, as noted there \[*Extra*, *de baptismo*\], and *c. final.* \[*de presbytero non baptizato*\], and what is noted there in the gloss. Alternatively, let him baptize himself, according to that gloss — though this last point would be of little avail, because there must be a distinction between the one baptizing and the one baptized, as in the aforesaid *c. debitum*. Similarly, he may have himself baptized by a layperson, because baptism conferred by anyone whatsoever is valid, as in *c.* \[1\], at the word “sacramentum” \[*Extra*, *de summa trinitate*\]. If, however, the person is an infant, then there are different opinions. Some say that nothing should be given, and that the infant should rather be left without baptism, and that you sin \[more\] by paying so that the infant may attain eternal life — *dist.* 23, *quod ait*, Zacharias and the gloss there; *dist.* 46, *sicut*. Others say that although money ought not to be given, nevertheless rather than leave the infant without baptism, money should be paid. Understand this to apply where no other person who knows how to baptize can be found. I would hold that in this case money may lawfully be given, even though it is unlawfully received, because the donor gives out of necessity — not in order to purchase a spiritual thing, but rather to remove the impediment unlawfully placed before this sacrament. Just as we say in a similar case regarding one who borrows money at usury: if he does so voluntarily, inducing another to commit usury, he sins; but it is otherwise if he does so out of necessity, as noted in *c. super eo* \[*de usuris*\], and this accords with what is noted above in the preceding chapter.

### A Second Question

A second question is raised: may money lawfully be given to a pagan so that he may have himself baptized? On this see the gloss in *c. de pio* \[*C.* 1, *q.* 5\], where the opinions are set forth. Some say that it may lawfully be given, following the example of blessed Silvester, who promised temporal goods to pagans so that they would become Christians. Others hold the contrary. I favor this opinion: that where pagans refuse to come to the Christian faith on account of poverty, money may lawfully be promised to them. For many Jews have amassed great wealth through the depravity of usury and fear coming to the faith because they would have to restore all that was wrongfully taken. In this case, I think money may lawfully be promised to them so that they may come to the faith. But if they have no intention of coming to the faith except out of greed for the money promised, then I hold the contrary, because in such cases baptism is of little benefit — *C.* 15, *q.* 1, *non enim* — for they sin by abusing a good thing, and those who give would not escape sin, by analogy with *C.* 33, *q.* 5, *ad fidem*. And I judge that blessed Silvester acted according to the first category.

### A Final Question

A final question is raised: what if money is given to a bishop so that he will not confer spiritual things simoniacally? The doctors say that there is simony on the part of the recipient, but not on the part of the giver.

### Procuration

A prelate ought not to demand procuration \[i.e., provision of hospitality and expenses\] except in cases expressly set forth in law, or cases similar to them. Procuration is owed to a bishop not only when he visits or consecrates distant places, but also nearby ones. Note the first case in which procuration is owed apart from visitation — namely, when a bishop or prelate goes to consecrate a church. And here Sinibaldus \[Innocentius IV\] and the doctors commonly hold that in no case shall procuration be furnished to a prelate for performing his office except in cases expressly set forth in law. Note this dictum as being singular, because it is cited daily. Therefore one who demands procuration must cite an express case, according to the doctors. But I would hold that in cases similar to the express ones, procuration may also be demanded, on the basis of what the gloss and doctors note in *c. sicut* \[*Extra*, *de accusationibus*\], and the text joined with the gloss, by analogy, in *l.* 1, *Codex, de condictione indebiti*, and in *c. statutum*, after the beginning \[*de rescriptis*, *Liber Sextus*\]. For not all legal situations can be resolved by the provisions written into law itself, but one must proceed from similar cases to similar, as in *l. non possunt* \[*Digest*, *de legibus*\]; *c. dilecta* \[*de confirmatione utili vel inutili*\]. And in the gloss at the end, at the words “rather for the labor,” etc.: some held this opinion, but it does not please the doctors, because it would follow that, given the proximity of the locations, this procuration would not be owed. Note well this dictum of the doctors, for from it one perceives that however close the church to be consecrated may be to the episcopal see, the consecrator may nonetheless demand procuration. This is satisfactory, both because the text speaks without distinction, and because we see the same principle in the procuration owed by reason of visitation — for it is owed even by churches that are in the bishop’s own city, as in *c. venerabili* \[*de censibus*\]. This procuration is therefore annexed to visitation and consecration. And it is not given as payment for the labor of the sacrament, because then it would be given for the sacrament itself and would constitute commerce \[*C.* 1, *q.* 3, *si quis obiecerit*\]. Rather, it is furnished as a sign of a certain superiority, and so that prelates may more readily be induced to visit and consecrate — as Innocentius notes in *c. romana* \[*de censibus*, *Liber Sextus*\]. Thus in consecration and visitation, procuration may be demanded — but not so in the case of those to be baptized.
