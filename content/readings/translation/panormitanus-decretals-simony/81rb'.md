### A constitution declaratory of ancient law that adds a penalty is said to innovate with respect to the penalty, to the extent that it is not applied retroactively.

### Ignorance of the law excuses nuns, and even monks who do not leave their cloisters.

Whether ignorance of the law excuses clerics — since they too are soldiers of the celestial militia. Necessity excuses a person from theft with respect to both penalty and fault. Whether someone who commits a sin on account of poverty is excused. In a time of necessity, all things are held in common. Simony does not impede a monk to such a degree that he could not, apart from this, have a place in that monastery. Nuns, even on the pretext of poverty, ought not to receive anything from those wishing to enter monasteries. Simony is not committed if anything is received for the consecration of bishops, or for the blessing of an abbey, or for the ordination of clerics, notwithstanding any custom — which holds true even if payment is received after the fact.

Religious persons are to be expelled from the monastery and thrust into stricter monasteries to do perpetual penance. But those received before this council who were received in such a manner are to be placed in other houses of the same order, unless their number makes this impractical; yet they must renounce their original reception and be received anew. And there are four parts: first, it provides for the future; second, for the past, at the word *de his*; third, it orders a prorogation to be made, at the word *verum*.

### It has prevailed through simony that when a man wished to receive certain women,

he did so, and whoever hereafter commits such depravity — whether the one receiving or the one received, whether she be a subject or a prelate — those received in such a manner are dealt with by the council, and it decrees that those monasteries shall publish this \[decree\] annually.

And it should be noted that the reception of nuns pertains broadly both to others among the nuns; and observe that other nuns are permitted to be ignorant of the law.

### A constitution declaratory of ancient law that adds a new penalty does not extend to past acts.

This constitution proves the point well, because before this conciliar decree this simony was committed, as in *C.1 q.3, Notandum*.

And it can also be gathered from this that monks and nuns should not be entrusted \[with the handling of money\] even on the pretext of poverty; this holds true here and likewise: just as a soldier of the armed militia must attend to arms, so too a soldier of the celestial militia must attend to divine things. But if perchance something else could be said to them — for Hostiensis himself holds that they may be sent to a nearby place of study — and the law favors and urges and commands them \[to study\], as in various texts on election and in the *Decretum*, *dist. 38 c. Ignorantia* and the many chapters of that distinction. But soldiers of the armed militia cannot idle about; rather, they must attend to the welfare of the republic. And Augustinus says there: you dare not seek to perform mortal \[sin\], as in *c. Discipulos*, *de consecratione dist. 5*, and *c.3 de furtis*, where one is excused from theft on the pretext of poverty. Johannes Andreae objects that necessity does not excuse from fault but only alleviates the penalty. And all the more so should nuns be excused, since they do not leave the cloisters of the monastery, as in *c.1 de statu regularium lib. 6*. But concerning monks this text is to be well noted; yet Hostiensis holds that this can apply to monks insofar as they leave the cloisters — and since today they go to hear law lectures, this ignorance is readily permitted to them. There could also be doubt regarding secular clerics, since soldiers too are permitted to be ignorant of the law, as in *l.1 C. de iuris et facti ignorantia*, and a cleric enjoys the privilege of a soldier, as in *l.1 ff. de rerum*. Just as a soldier of the armed militia must attend to arms, so too a soldier of the celestial militia must attend to divine things.

And \[in *c.\] Discipulos*, where the students are excused from both fault \[and penalty\]. Second, he resolves that poverty, in the case of genuine necessity itself, excuses. This solution is not universal. And an objection is raised, as in a certain *quaestio*: a person ought rather to die of hunger than to commit perjury by idolatrous oath, and similar things; and those acts done on the pretext of poverty are not to be committed. This holds true here and likewise — see the text there with the Gloss. And therefore conclude thus: acts that are evil in themselves — such as adultery, homicide, perjury, and the like — are not to be committed on the pretext of poverty; this holds true here and likewise.
