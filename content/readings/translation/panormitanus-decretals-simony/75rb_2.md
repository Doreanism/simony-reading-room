Note also that simoniacs and those simoniacally received must be placed in a stricter monastery, or one situated in a remote place. On these matters, what must be said today is as set forth in the chapter that follows on this title.

### Point: concerning one who was simoniacally received.

One who was received \[simoniacally\] but who is not himself a simoniac can be dispensed by the bishop, provided a prior resignation has been made. And note that here no distinction is drawn between the manifest and the occult case; therefore the bishop dispenses without qualification. The reason is that such persons are not simoniacs \[themselves\], but were simoniacally received. This relates to what is noted in *c. De simoniace, eodem titulo*.

### On the Gloss, at the word *omittam*.

Where it says: “in the hands of the prelates.” Say also, following Hostiensis, that if these prelates \[act\] under the *Liber Sextus*, and regarding what the Gloss adds — that if superiors refuse to accept the resignation, these persons must nevertheless cede — say more accurately as follows. And note that this extends and amplifies *c. Admonet* and *c. Quod in dubiis, de renuntiatione*, so that the cession is made in the hands of the prelate, not of anyone else. Those who are exempt do not resign except into the hands of the pope or his legate, as in *c. Cum venerabilis, de consuetudine* and *c. Si abbatem, de electione*. And regarding what the Gloss adds — that if superiors refuse to accept the resignation, these persons must nevertheless cede — one should have recourse to the superior of the superior and compel him to accept the resignation, by the argument in *c. Nullus, de iure patronatus*. And note that this extends and amplifies *c. Admonet* and *c. Quod in dubiis, de renuntiatione*, so that the cession is made in the hands of the prelate. After resignation, the person may once again be canonically admitted \[to the benefice\]; nothing from the earlier \[tainted appointment\] is retained.

\[This applies\] not only to a benefice lawfully acquired, but also to one acquired unlawfully and through simony — as is supported by *c. Nisi cum pridem, de renuntiatione*. The point, therefore, from this Gloss and its epitome, is that even in a simple benefice acquired simoniacally, the bishop does not dispense when the person acted knowingly. This relates to what I discussed in *c. De simoniace, eodem titulo*. Note this well, etc.

## Capitulum XXII.

### One who was unknowingly and simoniacally received into a benefice.

If he claims anything by virtue of that reception, he may be admitted again to the same benefice canonically, after resignation; nothing from the earlier \[tainted\] reception is retained.

A benefice that anyone holds through the commission of simony by another, without his knowledge, cannot be retained in good conscience.

### The bishop dispenses one who was unknowingly simoniacal to receive another benefice.

\[He may be dispensed\] to obtain the benefice, but not to retain another benefice that he held simoniacally. As to why dispensation is granted for simony committed unknowingly: the person who had been unknowingly assumed through simony is afterwards assumed anew, and the bishop can \[dispense\] thus. From the earlier reception he may not claim anything in that church, or, stated otherwise: one who resigns a simple benefice to which he had been assumed through simony without his knowledge may, by dispensation, be assumed anew to that same benefice in the same vacancy. Nothing, however, from the first reception will be claimed by him — all \[rights from it\] are extinguished.

### Literal Case.

The second part begins at *videmus*. A certain person, through his father’s avarice and without his own knowledge, was received as a canon of a certain church. Afterwards, upon reaching the age of discretion, he took the sign of the cross and freely resigned the prebend he had so obtained into the hands of the pope’s \[representatives among\] the brethren. The provost and brethren, taking compassion on him, elected him anew as a canon, on condition that he should claim nothing from the earlier reception in that church.

### First point from the text.

One who has obtained a benefice through another’s commission of simony, while himself remaining ignorant of it, cannot in good conscience retain it, but must resign it into the hands of the superior. In agreement with this, note secondly that a canon can resign his canonry into the hands of the chapter without the authority of a superior intervening — which Innocentius says perhaps applies in the special case of simony, or perhaps the election and confirmation belonged to the chapter. But if the election belongs to the chapter and the confirmation to \[the pope\], then the resignation must be made into the hands of both the one who confirms and those who elect, by the allegation in *titulus, quaestio 3, Statuimus*. On this subject — in whose hands the resignation of a benefice must be made — and \[the requirement\] that the consent of the electors is also needed, see the full discussion in *c. Admonet* and *c. Quod in dubiis, de renuntiatione*, and in Paulus’s commentary on *Clem. 1, eodem titulo*; I discussed this at length in the *repetitio* on *c. Si quis contra clericum, de foro competenti*. Understand that the institution and removal of canons belonged to the chapter; in that case, the resignation may validly be made into the hands of the chapter, as noted in the authorities. However, the last opinion of Innocentius holds that the resignation cannot be made into the hands of the confirmer alone, but the consent of the electors is also required, since it is a matter that concerns them, and a kind of contract is entered into between the electors and the elected, by the argument in *c. Cum inter canonicos, de electione* and *c. Nisi cum pridem, de renuntiatione*. Hence, just as in contracting the consent of the electors is required, so too in dissolving \[the relationship\], by the argument in the rule *de regulis iuris* and in *l. Nihil tam naturale, ff., eodem*. Note also that simony committed unknowingly in obtaining a benefice does not impede dispensation by the superior, even in the same vacancy, provided that a resignation has intervened — and that the benefice is a simple one, on which I spoke in *c. Non simoniace, section 8, eodem titulo*.

### On the Gloss, at the word *elegee*.

Understand the Gloss as follows: the bishop can dispense a knowing simoniac not with respect to that same benefice for whose acquisition the simony was committed, but so that the person may obtain another benefice — he can do this by dispensation, as in *quaestio 5, c. 1*.
