And concerning public \[office\]: in the gloss above, at the end, the gloss and text raise an objection and suggest that nothing should be taken by an administrator or judge or superior — indeed, even something modest, as in *l.* I, Code, *ad legem Iuliam repetundarum*, and *l.* II, Code, *de assessoribus*, and in the *Authenticum*, “ut iudices sine quoquo suffragio,” at the passage *si quis*. \[See also\] *c. exigit*, *de censibus*, Book VI — indeed, even a fourfold restitution \[is required\]: II q.1, *iubemus*, *c.* on extortion. \[Vocatus says\] the penalty is twofold according to Vocatus, so that the contrary \[authorities\] apply in the case of one who exacts. All this holds provided fraud is entirely absent — an argument drawn from this text. — Abbas.

## Capitulum XV.

### Monachus \[The Monk\].

An abbot, if he accepts money as part of an agreement for receiving someone \[into the monastery\], commits simony. Simony is committed by mere agreement alone, provided one of the parties has performed, even if the other has not yet done so. Simony is committed not only if it is done by the prelate, but also if it is given to his household members. See below, *c. poori*, at the end. Note this.

Simony does not invalidate the monastic state. Excommunication does not impede the monastic state.

And let him be transferred to another monastery. The first part sets forth the complaints of certain priests. The second appoints a judge to whom three things are mandated, beginning at “quoniam” \[since\].

The literal case. A certain priest wished to become a monk in a certain monastery. The abbot and the brothers were unwilling to receive him as a monk until he agreed to give them thirty solidi.
