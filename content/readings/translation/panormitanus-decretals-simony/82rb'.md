The literal case is as follows. Cistercian abbots have been accustomed to give \[something\] after the fact, as is the custom where men during their lifetimes did not pay the archbishop a palfrey at his installation or some equivalent, nor anything from tithes. And henceforth such a custom ought to be observed, being directed to divine worship; \[the contrary practice\] proceeded from the root of avarice, prohibiting the solemnities of installations for elected abbots of the Cistercian order, thereby adding that the archbishops and those \[abbots\] voluntarily obligated themselves to this. The very form of profession and its intentions are as follows: that if there is a custom that something should be liberally given at the time of sacred things, and such a custom has been established — “I, Brother \[N.\], abbot,” etc., as in the profession itself. And that monks and abbots, even of the Cistercian order, are subject to the archbishops of their localities; and that these \[religious\] have many privileges by reason of privilege. Note in c. cum ordinem. But concerning those referred \[to the bishop\], they are nevertheless not exempt from the jurisdiction of bishops. Those who have \[privileges\] must therefore prove the privilege if they allege exemption. For by common law all clerics and prelates of whatever dignity and status are subject to their local \[bishops\]. The bishop’s intention is established by common law over the entire diocese, to \[enforce\] the observance of the custom; yet compulsion shall occur after conferrals, as in c. cum venerabilis de relig. do. \[X 3.36.8\], and in c. j. de offic. ordin. \[X 1.31.1\], in c. cum episcopo and similar \[chapters\] under the same title, c. j., c. quasi pro. In its entirety. And second, that even an abbot, even of the Cistercian order, can take possession through installation — because the elected and confirmed \[abbot\] introduces the installed \[person\], placed in the choir, as having been deputed and obligated to that \[office\]. He says the same if at the time of conferral no exaction arose. And Hostiensis holds the same view, giving an example that is more forcefully applied in the case of an abbot’s position. Therefore, from this installation alone the installed person acquires possession of the benefice and its properties — see this treated elegantly by Innocentius in c. in litteris de restitutione spoliatorum \[X 2.13.5\]. Third, that abbots owe three things to the bishops of their localities. Ro. \[notes\] second that abbots owe bishops subjection, reverence, and obedience. Not only, therefore, are they subject to bishops, but they must also show them reverence and obey their lawful commands that are not contrary to the common rule. In consultu j. \[the text states\] “a palfrey at his installation,” etc., and this decretal supports that point. For \[the abbot\] was giving something temporal through this installation. That pope prohibited this and wished that bishops be content with the promise of obedience, the form of which is set out here. Likewise, in Anglia \[the third point\]: note there that the Gloss does not say that these Cistercian abbots ought to be installed, but it does prohibit the solemnity of installation. And regarding whether a custom concerning tithes is valid, state the matter as follows. On this point, Hostiensis criticizes Glina, who had said that even secular prelates are installed, but not religious, and Hostiensis says that these \[religious\] could be installed straightforwardly by the monks who elect them. Johannes Andreae says one must abide by the intervention of the cathedraticum \[i.e., the episcopal tax\] and the custom observed among them over time. But I think that where no custom appears requiring that installation be performed by the archdeacon — since governing pertains to him by common law, as in c. et nimis, de officio archidiaconi. And here nevertheless the solemnity is removed, but the person is not changed. The final Gloss at the end \[asks\]: whether they are bound to obey the pope against their rule — state the matter as fully noted in c. cit ad monasteria. \[On\] obeying the rule, in stat. reg. and what is noted in the rule semel loco, de reg. iur. lib. vi. \[VI 5.13\], by Johannes. And there \[the question\] was fully debated whether the pope can dispense with a religious so that he may take a wife — and see that chapter. And whether this is true without qualification.

## Capitulum XL.

### One may hold multiple canonries in different churches.

Concerning churches by special law, on which topic see the cross-references.

### One received as a canon must not be compelled to give anything, even the smallest amount, to the other canons.

Whether one received as a canon can be compelled to give something by custom — whether the reception is conditioned on it or not — regarding this custom. The third \[point\] of the custom is that a dinner may not be exacted from a canon, and in other ways likewise. One received as a canon is owed a prebend and revenues notwithstanding contrary custom.

The literal case is as follows. Jacobus, a canon, had been received as a canon — the brother of a canon in the church of Aprutium \[Teramo\]. The canons of that church refused to assign him his share of the common revenues and prebends unless they first received a dinner from him, invoking a custom whereby they receive a dinner from a newly received canon. And the pope commands a certain prior that, if this is so, notwithstanding that custom, he compel the canons to assign him his prebends just as they would to any one of them. Bar. \[notes\] therefore that one could be instituted as a canon in several churches, and this proceeds from special law. On this title, state the matter as fully noted in c. literas de non refil. vj. And \[this is\] notable if it is found below. But some said that sacred things should be freely conferred, and that afterward compulsion would follow to observe the custom. And thus this notable point would not be gathered \[from here\].

Second, note that beyond the prebend, other revenues or income are owed to the canon, and perhaps \[the text\] speaks of common proceeds that in certain churches are called the mensa grossa \[the common table\], or perhaps it speaks of daily distributions. Regarding what beyond the prebend — on both points, see what the law reads and the custom. I discussed this in the aforecited c. Nam, and state \[the matter\] as there. And not in c. cii adndi de prae et dig.
