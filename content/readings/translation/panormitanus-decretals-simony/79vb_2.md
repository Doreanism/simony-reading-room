As to the passage — for the word *Qui* \[“who”\] functions adversatively with respect to what follows — it says that even children are bound to relinquish benefices acquired through simony committed without their knowledge. As to the statement that “it is far different,” etc.: \[the simony\] should not be imputed to one who contradicts, either as guilt or as punishment. It should therefore be understood that imputation arises from the fact that \[the person\] expressed both prohibition and his own will, by which he subsequently consented as a penalty — so that the confirmation is annulled and he is bound to relinquish the benefice.

Otherwise \[one might argue that\] the word *Qui vero* \[“But he who”\] would not be adversative at all, for it is certain that one who is ignorant and does not contradict does not incur guilt along with penalty — nor even if he afterward consented by paying or repaying the promised money. There would be some doubt concerning one who is ignorant, or concerning one who says he was entirely outside the normal course \[of events\], and these cases must be resolved as doubtful cases — *l. Labeo*, *ff. ad carbonianam* \[*Digest*\]. Nor does it avail to say that this confirmation is simoniacal, because even though simony intervened on the part of the giver, lest the culpable act of that person prejudice one who expressly contradicted, such a confirmation is tolerated, for since that person was suitable, he might well have been confirmed even without money.

### Those, however, who gave the money \[are to be punished\].

And one who expressly contradicts is treated the same as one in whose fraud the money was given; but the act of that \[other\] person ought not to be undone.

And in other \[passages\] at the end, or according to Innocentius, perhaps this church. Where the remainder \[of the discussion\] bears on the passage cited here after the word *et in sacra*: because it is one thing to yield or buy a promise, and another to consent to it after the fact. As for the other point — since the matter pertains to a question of law — ratification has no place here, because the sale or purchase — that is, the payment of money — was not made in his name, but was made by those who involved themselves in sin by approving a blameworthy act. And so I understand the said chapter. And as to what Johannes Andreae says, relying on \[*Causa*\] 25: whether this person is bound on that account to relinquish the benefice or can be deprived of it — I say yes, joining the simony from the beginning with his subsequent agreement carried out afterward. This is what the text means here at the words *nisi postea consenseris* \[“unless he afterward consented”\], etc.: otherwise there would be no valid exception. Or \[to put it another way,\] let him not act under penalty of a thousand pounds, so that one who does not comply does not incur \[the penalty\].

## Capitulum XXXI.

### One commits simony if he gives his goods to a church on condition that he be received as a canon and retain those same goods as his prebend. It is otherwise if no agreement preceded.

One does not commit simony if he purchases prayers or gives temporal gifts out of mere liberality for the purpose of obtaining a benefice.

A prebend that consists in temporal goods can also belong to a layperson. It is otherwise if it is annexed to a canonry.

### Whether simony is induced by simoniacal intention alone.

Conventional simony is not remitted except through restitution of what was given and received. Mental simony, however, is abolished by penance alone.

### One commits simony if he corrupts a judge or witness in a spiritual cause.

### A cleric confers with an agreement.

And there are two parts. In the first, the consultation. In the second, the response, at \[the word\] *devotionis*, which proceeds by distinguishing two members, and submits a determination. The literal case: a certain cleric offered some of his goods to a church, requesting that the same cleric be admitted as a canon and be permitted to retain those same goods as his prebend for his lifetime. The question is whether it is lawful to accept those goods and for the same cleric to be received as a canon without the taint of simony. The pope responds: if a cleric offers his goods with a condition or agreement so that he may afterward retain them as a prebend and be admitted as a canon, such an offering or reception cannot take place without the taint of simony, since in such matters every agreement or arrangement must cease, in accordance with canonical sanctions. If, however, he freely offers \[his goods\] purely and without agreement or condition, humbly requesting that he be admitted as a canon and be permitted to retain his goods as his prebend, and the clerics give their consent purely, such a reception may well take place without simony. At the end, the pope says that we have determined this matter should be resolved thus, since one who makes such a donation with the intention of observing it — so that through the temporal goods he offers he may obtain spiritual things, and the clerics would not otherwise receive him — in cases of doubt such persons are judged culpable before God, from whom no secret is hidden.

Note, first, from the text, that earnest prayers offered for the purpose of obtaining a spiritual benefice do not constitute simony, nor does any other agreement, even an ambitious one. This holds even if a prior donation of temporal goods made freely had preceded.
