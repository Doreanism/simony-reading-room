Nuns who receive a woman as a sister ought not to demand anything from her or from her relatives. But the relatives themselves may voluntarily offer something to the monastery, or they may tell the woman wishing to enter to bring something with her for her sustenance. They must not, however, make a bargain, for that would be simony. From this it appears that one can infer that prostitutes are not admitted to testify in a simony case, because no legal texts specifically say that prostitutes are to be admitted to accuse. It does not follow, however, that they may also testify, because greater suitability is required for testifying on account of the dangers involved — although the gloss here, and Aleranus in *Consilium* 6, beginning “concerning the first doubt,” in the first volume, hold the contrary.

Those who hold reserved benefices and give the surplus revenues as an annual pension do not commit simony; but it is otherwise if they renounce by the title of the concession — they do not commit simony. One who has been instituted in a benefice and pays a fixed pension to a cardinal — which pension was imposed on the benefice — stands sufficiently \[accused\] against simoniacs as lawful; and \[the same applies\] for entry into religious life.

### Concerning Priories and Chapels

Concerning priories or chapels to be conferred by prelates for the purpose of institution; concerning the granting of burial; concerning chrism, holy oil, and the nuptial blessing or other sacraments — no contrary custom avails. It is divided \[as follows\]: for first the text sets forth a threefold prohibition stated disjunctively, calling the transgressors and those who consent simoniacs. Second, it sets forth conjunctively another threefold prohibition with no contrary custom, and gives the reason. Third, beginning “concerning burial,” the literal meaning. In this chapter it is forbidden that any money be demanded from those who enter religious life, nor shall any priory or chaplaincy be committed to anyone for a price. Whoever acts contrary to this — whether the one who gave, the one who received, or the one who consented — commits simony. Nor shall any exaction be attempted for any spiritual thing, notwithstanding any custom, because the passage of time does not diminish sin but rather increases it, as in *c. final.* and *c. idem* \[*Extra*\]. And entry into religious life pertains to the spiritual. Hence no temporal thing ought to be exacted for it, or else simony is committed, as here — on which the matter will be treated fully in *c. quam* on the same title, where these nuns are discussed.

Note that simony is equally committed whether, from the time of institution, some temporal thing is given by agreement, or whether an annual portion of the revenues is assigned; for it amounts to conferring a benefice on account of that annual revenue, which is something temporal — on which I shall touch upon something further below.

The ruling is that both the one who receives a temporal thing for a spiritual one, and the one who consents, as well as intermediaries, are all implicated in the vice of simony, as this chapter \[teaches\]. Custom does not excuse in these matters; rather, it aggravates the sin — which should be understood as meaning that the sin persisted for a long time under the pretext of custom, on which see the following chapter.

In the gloss at the end: two good theoretical and practical methods of drafting the instrument. The first practical method is that \[the instrument should state that\] not from a bargain but rather, out of pure devotion, the girl herself or her father should offer something to the monastery. The second practical method is this: if the situation arises from a deficiency of the monastery’s resources and revenues, and by way of a certain protection or intimation, one may tell the woman wishing to enter to bring something with her by which she can live. But this must not be reduced to a bargain. And so I understand Innocentius in *c. tua* on the same title, where he disagrees with this rule of the *de institutionibus*. The same \[is held\] by Joannes Andreas in *c. qui* on the same title.

In the gloss: that churches or chaplaincies ought not to be granted under an annual rent. The question arises whether religious to whom certain churches have been granted for their own use may lawfully retain an annual pension for themselves while conceding all the revenues. The doctors here conclude that they may, nor does *c. prohibemus* or *c. significavit* \[*Extra*, *de censibus*\] stand in the way, because the rule is that a census ought not to be imposed on churches, but revenues that are owed to someone may be sold or leased, as in *c. vestra* \[*de locato*\]. On this point, note what is stated in *c. de monachis liberis* in the *Clementines* \[*de iure patronatus*\]. These considerations bear on the everyday question whether cardinals may grant benefices entrusted to them under an annual pension. On this matter, conclude as follows: either they wish to lease the commenda while it remains in their possession — and they may do so, just as any benefice-holder may sell or lease the revenues of his benefice, as in the aforesaid *c. vestra* (though spiritual things ought not to be leased, on which I shall speak in *c. quam*, *c. final.*, and *Omne*, concerning prelates and their obligations) — or they wish to renounce the title of the commenda so that the benefice may pass to another, with a prior agreement to receive a fixed sum of money. In this case there is no doubt that simony is committed, and all the more so because the cardinal himself confers nothing but merely renounces the commenda and receives money by agreement — for that renunciation is simoniacal, as noted by the gloss in *c. ex parte* \[*Extra*, *de officio legati*\].
