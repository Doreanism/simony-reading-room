Where the delict is manifest, only the pope dispenses — by the argument in c. \[...\]. Where it is not manifest, the bishop dispenses. But certainly, although the doctors pass over this commonly and \[accept\] this statement, I do not think it true. Rather, I say without distinction that the bishop dispenses with such a person — first, by the text which permits dispensation without distinction; therefore the bishop will be able to dispense, as I said above. Likewise, I adduce c. *Presentium*, C.1, q.5, where nevertheless he dispenses, and the matter could not have been non-manifest from the same \[facts\], since he confessed it when this person burdened \[him\] out of hatred for another. Nor can it be called simony such that he may dispense, by the argument of the rules *Odia*, *Sine culpa*, and *Non*. And note that in this case, when the simony was committed without his knowledge, he is not suspended from all orders; rather, as Innocentius rightly says here, he is suspended only from the order simoniacally conferred. Therefore he may licitly exercise the other orders lawfully received, retain benefices, and receive new ones — because, as we have said, he is not a simoniac. But he cannot be promoted to a higher order unless he receives a dispensation for the order simoniacally received, because otherwise he would seem to have been promoted by skipping a grade \[per saltum\] — dist. 5, c. j., *de clericis per saltum promotis*, throughout. Nor does the aforesaid c. *Ex tue* present an obstacle, nor the chapter by which opinions that distinguish between manifest and occult seem to be removed — that distinction, namely, so that he may be coerced from the exercise of the order — because one whose \[sin is\] occult, having confessed and performed suitable penance, is not impeded but rather is not coerced, since the penitential form does not give precise coercion but rather \[he\] is dispensed. It is otherwise if, having been made aware, he wishes to wait \[for judgment\]. \[The text\] speaks of a simoniac with whom only the pope dispenses — this is understood \[to apply\] if the money was not given to him but to his predecessor or to another; otherwise \[the rule is\] different, as noted in the gloss on c. *Presentium*. And by the foregoing I conclude that the position of Innocentius here does not hold insofar as he says that a bishop dispenses with a simoniac in no dignity, even if money was not given for it — and the text seems to the contrary in C.1, q.5, c. f., at the end, where dispensation is prohibited for the dignity or the church \[that was\] purchased; therefore, \[it is permitted\] for another church.

## Capitulum XIX.

### One who is elected commits simony if he gives money to dissenters from his election so that they may consent.

### A priest-confessor may reveal a sin confessed to him with the consent of the penitent; otherwise not.

\[He may not hold\] the benefice, but in that case he does not dispense unless he also ministers \[in office\], as proved in C.1, q.5 and c. j. It is therefore more severe \[a consequence\] for him to hold \[a benefice\] elsewhere than to have watched and awaited the sentence. And one who has been condemned for simony by sentence — let him commit to memory, living in this world, to gain his soul \[rather than the world\].

### Note i., from the text, that a sin disclosed in penance can be revealed by the confessor to another, provided the sinner-penitent consents.

And thus this text is understood according to the doctors; for this Carolus learned of it in penance, and afterward revealed it in secret to the pope — certainly it must be understood \[that this\] was done with that person’s consent; otherwise it would not be permissible.

\[See the\] *Codex*, *de poenitentiis et remissionibus*. Second, gather from the gloss that simony is committed for obtaining the vote of someone in an election, even a vote not necessary for the validity of the election. For this election had been made by the greater part, yet simony was committed so that the minority would cease its opposition. All the more, then, would simony be committed if money were given to obtain a vote, because it pertains to something spiritual. Third, gather that a will disturbed by sin does not excuse \[the\] sin.

In the gloss, there: “And thus he had a right,” etc. Weigh the gloss carefully, because it greatly restricts the text — \[namely,\] that it should apply to opposition based on a legal right, not on mere fact. Therefore, if money is given to those opposing a simple benefice purely on a factual basis, simony is not committed according to the gloss. In support of this, \[consider\] that the one elected would seem rather to be redeeming his own vexation than purchasing something spiritual. Symon says the contrary, stating that before confirmation, money must not be given, whether the opposition is based on law or on fact — by the argument in c. *Significasti*, above, *de electione*. And this view pleases me, because for seeking a right not yet acquired it is not licit to give money, even to redeem vexation; it is otherwise for a right already acquired — in support of which, \[see\] what is noted in c. *Dilectus*, below, under the same title — for \[in the former case\] one appears to give money in order to obtain more freely that spiritual right. But when the right is already acquired and one stands upon it, then money is properly given for \[relieving\] the vexation. And note this statement of Innocentius. Abbas: \[he is\] a simoniac.
