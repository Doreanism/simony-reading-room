And in the first gloss at the word “however occult he may be,” consider this carefully, because the gloss seems to contradict itself: for here it says that a simoniac in orders, however occult he may be, is suspended; but below, at the word *si vero*, it says that if he is occult he is not suspended as regards others. And so that you may have this point more clearly, attend to two things. First, whether the simoniac is suspended. Second, whether he can be prohibited from celebrating mass. On the first point, say that either he is simoniacal in respect of orders, and however occult he may be, he is suspended both as regards himself and as regards others — because others too sin by hearing divine offices from him \[dist. 32, paragraph *Verum*\], and note what Innocentius says in *c. Tanta* on this point. And so understand this part of the gloss. But if the simony concerns a benefice: if the simony is manifest and notorious, the person is suspended both as regards himself and as regards others, by analogy with the aforecited paragraph *Verum*, and note Innocentius in the aforecited *c. Tanta* — unless the sin of simony was committed without his knowledge, as when a father or another gave money \[on his behalf\] without his knowing it \[C.1 q.5 c. *Presentifi*\]. But once he learns of it, he must relinquish the benefice. If, however, the simony is occult, then he is suspended as regards himself but not as regards others — just like any sinner: he himself sins by celebrating, but others do not sin by hearing him. And so the gloss speaks at the end, and so the doctors hold here.

On the second point, say that either it is notorious that the person is simoniacal, and then he ought to be prohibited — indeed, deposed — according to what is read and noted in *c. Inquisitionis* \[Extra, de accusationibus\]. Or else the simony is neither notorious nor is the person defamed, and then he ought not to be prohibited. Or else he is defamed, and then he ought to be prohibited, as here. Nor does the objection of the gloss stand — namely, that this person is already suspended by operation of law, \[and so\] the text does not say that he “ought to be suspended” but that he “ought to be prohibited.” For although he is suspended, he could still celebrate in fact; therefore he must be prohibited — the prohibition, as it were, relates to fact, whereas the suspension relates to law.

And as Johannes Andreae says, a person can be properly suspended \[by a judge\] even though he is already suspended \[by law\]. For just as one excommunicated by a canon can be excommunicated by a human judge — as in *c. Ita quorundam* \[Extra, de Iudaeis\], and what the gloss notes there — so too one suspended by law can also be suspended by a human judge.

## Capitulum III.

### Simony, on account of the enormity of the crime, is called heresy:

it is not, however, that it actually is heresy. See the next chapter below.

The pope can be accused of heresy, but not of simony — unless three conditions concur, on which see the cross-references.

And the present chapter says the same as the preceding one, except that the former chapter speaks of accusation, while this one speaks of the simoniac in general terms: that one \[speaks\] of \[a specific type of\] law.

And to both \[chapters\] each authority can be applied literally.

And in the gloss here: whether and how many witnesses can be compelled \[to testify\] in a criminal case.

Whether homicide is a more horrible crime than simony.

### The literal case.

The question was raised concerning the penalty to be imposed on a simoniac. And the second part \[of the text states\] that, just as the pestilence of simony by its magnitude surpasses all other diseases, so, after some final delay, once the simony has been established, \[the simoniac\] ought to be removed and expelled from the church. And note, first, from the text, that simony is here called a pestilence: for just as a pestilence infects one person and then many, so too does simony infect, because it renders the person incapable of exercising his office. And so great is the desire to obtain benefices that it infects many, just as a pestilence does.

Note, secondly, a special rule here: in a grave and most grave crime, the proceedings may go forward on the basis of signs alone — that is, on the basis of circumstantial evidence. For as a general rule, in criminal cases the proofs must be clearer than light \[*lex Sciant cuncti*, C. de probationibus\].
