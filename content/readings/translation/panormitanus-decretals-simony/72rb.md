Hostiensis says that it is the definition of mental simony, not by fiction and propriety — as was discussed above in this matter — and that one must add: with a subsequent act following, because the will alone does not make someone simoniacal unless the effect follows. Mental simony is committed when, without any preceding express agreement, it is tacitly arranged that a spiritual thing is given for a temporal one. He defines it thus, as I said above: it is the deliberate will or cupidity — with a subsequent act — of selling or buying something spiritual or annexed to a spiritual thing. Conventional simony occurs when the parties have expressly agreed between themselves that a spiritual thing is given for a temporal one. And Hostiensis defines this as: the reception or donation of spiritual things or things annexed to spiritual things, preceded by an agreement of promise, condition, stipulation, servitude, or some temporality. And I think that, comprehending all these cases, both types can be briefly defined thus: simony is the exchange of a spiritual thing, or something annexed to a spiritual thing, for something temporal. On this conventional simony, see the note in *c. final*, and the following chapter. And so Hostiensis appears to understand this text as concerning conventional simony. Others say that, broadly speaking, simony is committed here, though not strictly. Innocentius says here that these persons are punished after the manner of simoniacs, even if there is no true simony; but this was said against the end of *c. non enim*, which states that the penal provision there declares both parties to be simoniacal. For it is equivalent whether one exercises or bestows something spiritual for a temporal consideration, or commits \[i.e., omits\] the spiritual exercise for a temporality — in both cases a kind of exchange takes place. In the gloss at the end: and this last interpretation is the better one, because no reason for distinguishing between the last two cases is apparent. But on account of the similarity between the first two cases, those are treated as one; and the same must be said of the last two cases. But contrary to this, note the text insofar as it judges as simoniacal one who receives something from a public sinner — for “if thy brother sin against thee,” etc. \[Matthew 18:15\]. Therefore, know that you are so bound to denounce. This is relevant. And also where \[the sin\] is hidden; but it is otherwise if the sinner were prepared to correct himself, as there and in *c. novit*, *de iudiciis*.

A prelate alone cannot remit something through which a right has accrued to the church.

An alienation illegitimately made by a prelate can be revoked by the members of the chapter.

Missio in possessionem \[a writ putting one in possession\] for the preservation of a thing has application even among religious.

### The right of patronage

passes with the universality \[of property\] when it is sold, because it partakes of temporality. A right that is purely spiritual, however, does not pass with the universality.

### A copulative conjunction

joins the thing conjoined to it principally, not as an accessory; the word “with” \[*cum*\] operates differently.

The right of patronage cannot be sold in this form: “I sell you the estate *and* the right of patronage that I have in it.” But it can be sold in this other form: “I sell you the estate *with* the right of patronage.” Because in the first case the right of patronage comes principally, while in the second it comes as an accessory.

A sale of multiple things, of which some could not be sold, is valid with respect to those \[that could be sold\], but not with respect to all.

A church cannot be purchased or redeemed. This is the point so far as the literal text is concerned.

### First, he sets out the complaint of the monks.

Secondly, he commits the case by instructing the judge on the merits, beginning at the word *unde quia*.

The literal case: The monks of Acra had obtained a certain monastery with its appurtenances and a certain church from the monks of Cardomo under an annual rent of fifteen pounds, and they held a charter from the said monks on this account. Afterwards, however, the prior of the monks of Acra, without consulting his chapter, returned the aforesaid charter to the said monks of Cardomo, having received money for it. Wherefore these monks of Acra complained that they had been unjustly aggrieved. The pope therefore mandated certain persons to convoke the parties and settle the aforesaid cause by concord or by judgment.

He adds that, since the aforesaid church could not be purchased, so neither could it be redeemed. If indeed the monks of Cardomo, having been lawfully cited, should prove contumacious, the judges shall put the monks of Acra in possession of those things concerning which the controversy turns between them, and shall not permit them to be disturbed therefrom without due process of law. Joannes \[Andreae\].

### Note first

from the text that a prelate alone cannot invalidate what has been done when some right of the church has been acquired through it. And secondly, note that the members of the chapter can take action for the revocation of those things which the prelate has unlawfully alienated — on which see *c. si quis presbyterorum*, *de rebus ecclesiae non alienandis*, and *c. cum olim*, *de testibus*.

### Finally, note

that even among religious persons, missio in possessionem for the preservation of a thing on account of contumacy has its place; and note this as a limitation on *c. ex literis*, *de dolo et contumacia*, where, out of regard for religious life, sequestration rather than missio in possessionem is ordered. But missio can also be made, as here.

At the end, note several points from this gloss. First, that a spiritual right passes with the universality when it is sold, even though it cannot be sold by itself.
