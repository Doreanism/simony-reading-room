And several reasons may be given as to whether a custom is valid whereby laypeople should give to clerics — not clerics to clerics. And the reason is this: for a person judges \[differently regarding\] private benefit — a gift from cleric to cleric is \[for\] private benefit, whereas what is given by laypeople to clerics is public \[in nature\], l. j. ff. de civit. et iur. j. de iure publici. And one need not worry whether the church has [it] or whether it thus remains with the laypeople, in c. cum apostolica de his quae fi. a. The second reading also cites similar reasons. With what has been said by Hostiensis, the doctors commonly agree. And from the views of Hostiensis, certain conclusions may be gathered.

### First conclusion:

A custom is not valid whereby clerics, for spiritual things received or to be received, give something temporal to others.

### Second conclusion:

A custom is valid whereby laypeople give something temporal to clerics upon receiving spiritual things.

### Third conclusion:

Something temporal tending to the increase of divine worship may be sought under the pretext of custom — that is, in recompense for spiritual things conferred.

But against the foregoing, I adduce a singular text on this matter in j. q. iij. \[C.1 q.3\], where, after many points have been raised: from a cleric being instituted or already instituted in a benefice, nothing temporal should be sought in recompense for the institution — not even something to be distributed to the poor or applied to the fabric of the church — because one who takes wrongfully and dispenses well is burdened rather than helped, as \[the text\] says there. Therefore, in favor of divine worship, nothing should be exacted for spiritual things themselves. To the same effect, c. veniens, c. c. quantato, section 8, where from one who wishes to enter religious life, not even under the pretext of custom should anything be exacted, even if it is applied to the church. Nor is it lawful to give temporal things to the church so that a prebend may be made from them, which is afterward assigned to the received canon — as in the aforecited c. quantato. Hence, in this matter, one should distinguish as follows: either no statute or custom exists, and then nothing should be exacted, even something to be devoted to divine worship, as in the aforecited laws — although a burden may be imposed on each of the canons to contribute annual payments in favor of a pious cause, as in c. fi. de his \[quae\] d. a ma. par. ca. But in receiving \[a canon\], nothing else should be exacted, lest it appear to be done in recompense for spiritual things. If, however, a statute of the church exists, or if the statute imposes this burden on the prebend, state the matter as I discussed in c. ad apostolicam and in c. sua, section 8 — even with respect to clerics. And then the fact that no exaction preceded the worship \[in question\] can be a true reason for the distinction between clerics and laypeople: because laypeople, in cases of doubt — since they freely paid — are deemed to have obligated themselves under a kind of obligation that laypeople customarily make to clerics both temporally and spiritually, as in de consc. di. j. “every Christian.” But the case appears different for clerics who are received as canons, because here the presumption does not arise that the penalty should be commensurate with the offense, xxiiii. q. j. non affirmamus, in c. difinit. de his d. fi. a ma. par. c. This is \[addressed in\] c. venice j., section 8, de test. Therefore, the obligation there does not now cease, as it says here. But \[this is\] true with respect to the one primarily \[concerned\]; with respect to the church, however, the obligation endures for a time, as \[discussed\] below. Moreover, \[what is taken\] by the pope is not restored.

### One simoniacal in conferring orders is suspended ipso facto from the conferral of orders.

### One knowingly ordained through simony is dispensed only by the pope; otherwise if unknowingly.

If a cleric who has been ordained promised his ordainer that for a certain number of years he would not seek provision from him — whether such a promise, even if sworn, is valid, both with respect to the ordained person himself and with respect to the ordainer.

### One simoniacal in orders is deposed not only from the simoniacally obtained order but from all others.

The penalty should be commensurate with the offense.

### One simoniacal in obtaining an order is not dispensed except by the pope.

Only through the Apostolic See is dispensation granted to them.
