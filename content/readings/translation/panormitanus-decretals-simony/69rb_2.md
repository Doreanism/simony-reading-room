And if an agreement had been made for a fixed penalty with a formal inscriptio \[i.e., a formal accusation requiring the accuser to accept the same penalty if the charge fails\], the notary could lawfully take something for his labor. On the first point, conclude briefly, following the gloss: either the notary receives a salary from public funds — that is, from the bishop — and then this first limitation properly applies; or he does not receive a salary, and then either his office is so necessary to the ordination that unless he writes, the order cannot be said to have been properly conferred — in which case, since his office pertains to the conferral of the administration, he may exact nothing. I, however, do not see how the office of a scribe could be of such a nature that it is necessarily required for the legitimate conferral of an order, and therefore it can simply be said that, so long as he does not receive a salary from public funds, he may rightly exact payment for his labor. And according to Innocentius, this chapter intended to abolish the custom of certain places in which all officials and ministers demanded money. On the first point, conclude that for spiritual things themselves, nothing else should be conferred, not even if freely given — as is clear from this text at the end, where the person to be ordained is forbidden to give. But if something is conferred spontaneously, not principally for the spiritual things themselves but out of a certain charity, it may lawfully be received, as in the chapter *Ubi quaestiones* and the opinion of others against the text \[arguing\] that the notary may indiscriminately receive — \[yet\] crimes ought not to go unpunished. And through this you have the understanding and the rationale of this chapter; keep it in mind.

### Whether simony applies in the Roman curia.

It does. For in the Roman curia, notaries exact money for bulls; therefore, in other places too, notaries may lawfully receive payment — by analogy with *c. In causis* \[Extra, de re iudicata\]. For the practice of the curia itself ought to serve as our instruction, just as we say of the actions of Christ, in *c. Significasti* \[Extra, de electione\]. Some doctors say that in the Roman curia the notaries do not have a salary, and therefore they lawfully exact payment. Alternatively, the title *de simonia* does not apply in the Roman curia. And similarly, in the court of the prince, the *lex Iulia de ambitu* does not apply — and this is true. Hostiensis \[draws a distinction\]: in those matters that are simoniacal because prohibited \[by positive law\], it is otherwise than in matters prohibited because they are simoniacal \[by divine law\], as the gloss says in *c. Ex parte* \[Extra, de officio delegati\]. Hostiensis says that although the pope is not bound by his own constitution in those matters that are simoniacal because prohibited, he ought nevertheless, more than all others, to abstain \[from such conduct\]. A toleration \[of the practice\] has resulted in a fixed schedule of fees, \[specifying\] how much should be paid for each bull.

### Whether the pope \[can commit simony\].

I have found this question examined by the doctors. Only Zabarella here simply says that the pope does not commit simony, as if inferring this from the foregoing, since the title to a benefice is a matter of positive law and the pope may use that law and in such matters is not bound, as in *c. Proposuit* \[Extra, de concessione praebendae\]. But I have always held the opposite view, and in my repetitio on the paragraph *Quod vero* in *c. Extirpandae* \[Extra, de praebendis\], I set forth the following resolution: whenever a benefice carries with it the administration of an order or the exercise of the power of the keys, even the pope would be implicated in the vice of simony \[if he sold it\]; but it is otherwise if the benefice does not carry with it the administration of an order or the exercise of the keys — as is the case with hospitals or certain temporal administrations. I prove this, first, by reason: for although the title to a benefice is a matter of positive law, nevertheless the exercise of an order or the power of the keys is a matter of divine law. For the order existed already in the Old Testament, and to sell the exercise of an order would be to sell the order itself and the grace of God — and these things are simoniacal in themselves and therefore prohibited \[C.1 q.1 c. *Qui studet*\]. Hence one should know that when a person is ordained, he receives the powers of loosing and binding in habit but not in act \[XII q.1 c. *Ecce sufficienter*, etc.\]. This is why no one can absolve or bind the parishioner of another, however much he may be a priest. But if a benefice is conferred upon him, then he is given the actual exercise of his order — and that exercise is a matter of divine law. For ordination was \[already ordered toward\] the exercise of an order, and to sell it would be to sell the order itself and the grace of God. And these things are simoniacal in themselves. And therefore in the conferral of a bishopric there is no doubt that the pope would commit simony \[C.1 q.1\], since they find that the episcopate is an order. And since these things are burdens \[of office\], it is no excuse to say that one receives money for the temporal elements, because when one of two connected things is sold, neither is left unsold \[C.1 q.1 c. *Si quis obicerit*\]. But in the case of other benefices that do not carry with them the exercise of an order, the pope would not commit simony — because on this question, which Abbas discusses here, see all the authorities in *c. Ut altaris* \[Extra, de officio, in Sexto\]. Nevertheless he ought to abstain, for the reasons stated above. And I think that in all cases, one who sells for an agreed sum of money to the pope is not safe as regards the office \[of the benefice\], nor can he administer in the benefice without sin. And in the case where the pope would not commit simony, since there existed a prohibitive constitution, the subordinate falls under the penalty — just as, in a similar dispute, Johannes Andreae discusses the question of a bishop’s statute under which anyone who gambles with dice is excommunicated: whether one who gambles with the bishop himself incurs the excommunication.
