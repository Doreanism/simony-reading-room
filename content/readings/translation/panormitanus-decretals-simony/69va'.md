A Catholic is to be admitted to bring an accusation of simony. \[The text states that\] anyone is to be admitted without \[regard to\] sin: \[the Lord\] gave permission to accuse even to a most wicked Jew, when he said, “If I have spoken evil” \[John 18:23\]; and all the more so, no Catholic ought to be rejected — indeed, he should rather be encouraged to assert the truth in order to repel the simoniacal corruption. And the text provides an argument that a superior can be accused by an inferior, citing *c.* 15 and *c. Zachariae*.

And the rule is that a non-Catholic is not admitted to make an accusation, not even on a charge of simony. And \[this provides\] an argument that a witness who has been solicited by a party through entreaties to give testimony is not for that reason suspect.

Regarding the Jew: but Johannes Andreae \[says\] that these consequent words were not spoken to Judas but to that servant of the high priest who struck Jesus, saying, “Do you answer the high priest so?” And Christ replied, “If I have spoken evil,” etc. \[John 18:22--23\]. So too the following gloss should be corrected. Hostiensis says the text can be understood \[as referring to\] that other authority: “Which of you convicts me of sin?” \[John 8:46\], etc.

And this latter authority speaks properly of the accuser; the former of the witness. The second can be applied to the text at the word “accusing”; the first can be applied literally at the words “to assert the truth.” These authorities are straightforward on their surface, because in truth the sense of that authority is that Christ could be accused or convicted by no one.

## Capitulum II.

### A simoniac, while an accusation of simony is pending, is prohibited from the administration of divine offices.

The pope ought to observe the judicial order by his ordinary power; it is otherwise \[when he acts\] by his absolute power.

While a suit is pending, the defendant is not deprived of his property — for instance, his benefice — unless the suit concerns some grave crime, in which case the person is suspended from his property until the case is concluded.

Whether a simoniac, even if occult, is and when he is suspended — both as regards himself and when \[others who associate with him\] sin.

A person is not called a simoniac who holds a benefice for the obtaining of which another gave money without his knowledge; but when he learns of it, he is bound to relinquish it.

Whether and when a simoniac can be prohibited from celebrating.

Whether one excommunicated by law can also be excommunicated by a \[human judge\].

Whether one excommunicated by law can in addition be suspended.

\[The text discusses\] the judicial order and should be understood \[as referring\] to ordinary power, not absolute power, as in *c. Proposuit* \[Extra, de concessione praebendae\].
