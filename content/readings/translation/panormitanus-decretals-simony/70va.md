### Custom

Custom, even if base and dishonorable, excuses from temporal punishment.

## Capitulum VI.

### Whether Simony Can Be Committed in Connection with the Sacrament of Matrimony

If an ignoble husband gives something to a noble wife in compensation for her lineage, he gives lawfully, and such a gift is valid. A remunerative gift between husband and wife is valid even without awaiting the death of either spouse, because it is not a simple gift. A priest ought rather to give the said money than to leave the baptism unperformed. Zacharias: one who has the will to be baptized is regarded as having been baptized. Baptism conferred even by a layperson is valid. One who is compelled by necessity to give money for a spiritual thing does not commit simony. One who borrows money at usury out of necessity does not commit simony. Simony is not committed if someone gives or promises money to a pagan so that he may become a Christian, unless that pagan would become a Christian only on account of that money. Simony is not committed on the part of the recipient if he accepts money in order not to confer spiritual things simoniacally.

\[A corrupt\] custom can be called a law of death. It is called a “law” because it is observed by the impious as though it were a law or privilege. Therefore it must be rooted out swiftly and utterly, as \[stated in\] *dist.* \[8\]: a bad custom is called a law of death because it brings eternal death. But as regards temporal punishment, many are excused, as noted in *dist.*, *c. denique*, and in *c. cum venerabilis* \[*de successione*\]. Secondly, from this same point I gather that a continuing sin is graver and must be punished with a heavier penalty than one that is momentary, \[for custom\] binds or chains the soul temporally and invisibly, just as chains hold the body visibly. And finally, note that all spiritual things may in a broad sense be called sacraments, as is gathered from the words “or other sacraments” \[in the text\]; but properly speaking there are seven sacraments, concerning which see *c. veniens* \[*de transactionibus*\], as stated by SLE. In the gloss at the end: this rule proceeds by the civil law. But by canon law, a custom whereby something temporal is given for something spiritual is not valid. Whether and when some custom may be introduced regarding promotion, I shall discuss in *c. ad apostolicam*. At the words “nuptial blessings” and the passage concerning grace, Nicolaus on the sacrament \[of marriage\] determines the matter by saying that grace is also conferred in matrimony. And to *c. connubia*, cited in support: in the nuptial blessings the grace of the Spirit \[is present\]; but in the matrimonial act itself — namely, the carnal union — it is not fitting for the Holy Spirit to be present on account of the repugnance \[of the act\], and because the person becomes entirely carnal. Grace can, however, be present there — for instance, if one renders the conjugal debt — so that spouses may merit \[grace\], as in *C.* 32, *q.* 5, *c.* 1; *C.* 27, *q.* 6, *c. ita habitans*. Moreover, here is the argument of the gloss: since no exaction ought to be made for the nuptial blessings because they confer the grace of the Holy Spirit, and therefore the exaction of money is forbidden — this is true of those things specifically. But for the marriage itself, money may lawfully be involved. Conclude that a dowry and a gift on account of the marriage may lawfully be given, because properly speaking the money is not given for the marriage — that is, so as to obtain the marriage itself — but for the burden of marriage, as in *c. vestras* \[*Extra*, *de dote post divortium restituenda*\]. Likewise, before the marriage is actually celebrated, a gift between the betrothed parties may lawfully be made, provided it does not amount to buying the marriage itself, as in *l.* 1, *Codex, de donationibus ante nuptias*. But in the contracting of a marriage, if one party induces the other to marry, a gift or promise made by one of the spouses is not valid, because it appears to have been made for a shameful cause — namely, the extortion of sexual pleasure. So the text notably \[holds\] in *l. si ita stipulatus*, section *si tibi*, and *l. si divortii* \[*Digest*, *de verborum obligationibus*\]. Bartolus, however, holds in both passages that if the woman was noble and the man was a commoner, then something may lawfully be given so that she contracts the marriage, because the gift is considered remunerative and in compensation for her lineage, and it does not occasion lust — which is well worth noting.
