Thus, if someone pays by way of addition or unwillingly, then that payment was simoniacal — being introduced by divine law and therefore imprescriptible, as in c. fi. de consuetudine \[X 1.4.11\]. But if someone pays voluntarily, then he cannot be compelled to pay. However, if something has customarily been given not at the time of conferring sacred things but after the fact — as is the custom where men during their lifetimes did not pay the archbishop a palfrey at his installation or some equivalent, nor anything from tithes, but at death all movable goods are tithed, and also in Brittany where at death a third part of all goods is given, to be devoted to divine worship and alms for the poor — then this custom proceeded from the root of avarice, prohibiting the solemnities of installations for those who do not observe such a custom. They can be compelled, since for such a long time they have been paying to elected abbots of the Cistercian order, thereby adding that the archbishops and those \[abbots\] voluntarily obligated themselves to this. The conclusion is that Innocentius \[IV\], in a brief epitome, sets forth the form of profession and its intentions as follows: that if there is a custom that something should be liberally given at the time of sacred things, and such a custom has been established. Laypeople who wish to enact contrary statutes so that nothing is owed, or who agitate \[against these customs\], can be compelled. Tract. 4. Ro. \[notes\] first that monks and abbots, even of the Cistercian order, are subject to the archbishops of their localities; and that these \[religious\] have many privileges by reason of privilege. Note in c. cum ordinem. But if one has not had a privilege, \[the payment\] is voluntary in origin, and therefore no one ought to be compelled. Regarding the jurisdiction of bishops: those who have \[privileges\] must therefore prove the privilege if they allege \[exemption\]. Otherwise, where something has customarily been given after a free conferral, Hostiensis says, and others too, that by common law all clerics and prelates of whatever dignity and status are subject \[to their bishops\]. One who freely pays with no preceding exaction can then be compelled to observe the custom; yet the compulsion shall occur after the conferrals, as in c. cum venerabilis de relig. do. \[X 3.36.8\], and in c. j. de offic. ordin. \[X 1.31.1\], in c. cum episcopo and similar \[chapters\]. In support of this, one may cite c. ex parte de censibus \[X 3.39.6\]. And on that reasoning, Innocentius says in that \[chapter\] — showing that where \[payments\] have customarily been given afterward — it appears that the elected and confirmed \[abbot\] freely introduced the installed \[person\], placed in the choir, as having been deputed and obligated to that. He says the same if at the time of conferral no exaction arose. Hostiensis holds the same view, giving an example that is more forcefully applied in the case of an abbot’s position. Therefore, from this installation alone the installed person acquires possession of the benefice and its properties — see this treated elegantly by Innocentius in c. in litteris de restitutione spoliatorum \[X 2.13.5\]. It was customary, as it still is, that a certain amount be given at nuptial blessings; loaves of bread are customarily given; at the ordination of clerics, hand-towels are customarily given. So he expressly held that what was given at the time of conferring sacred things to the bishops of the localities — namely, subjection, reverence, and obedience. Not only, therefore, are they subject to bishops, but they must also show them reverence and obey their lawful commands that are not contrary to the common rule. In consultu j. \[the text states\] “a palfrey at his installation,” etc., and this decretal supports that point. Moreover, this custom can be so harmful to the churches themselves — although it can sometimes be profitable — for if the head of a household were to live a hundred years, this decretal \[applies\] to the title, since he was giving something temporal through this installation. That pope prohibited this and wished that bishops be content to tithe those goods once in a hundred years, and thus the church loses out. Conversely, it can happen that within three days the father, son, and grandson die in succession, and thus those goods would be tithed three times in three days. Likewise, regarding whether a custom concerning tithes is valid, state the matter as noted in c. j. de obed. lib. vj. \[VI 1.18.1\], in c. ad apostolicae, in c. a nobis, c. tua, section de venditionibus. Zenzellinus \[adds\], and distinguishes, yet I see nothing good \[in this approach\]. He will therefore say how or whether what is to be rendered by customs has some just cause — as laypeople \[may act\] straightforwardly through monks who elect. Johannes Andreae says one must abide by the intervention of the cathedraticum \[i.e., the episcopal tax\], and then \[the payment\] is to be rendered. The same applies if one need not specify the cause of a custom observed among them over time. But I think that where no custom appears requiring that installation be performed by the archdeacon — since governing pertains to him by common law, as in c. et nimis — such that sacred things, having first been freely conferred, \[should proceed\] as Hostiensis says there. If indeed \[the matter concerns\] the office of the archdeacon, here nevertheless the solemnity is removed, not something that must be rendered at the time of sacred things that tends to the benefit of the clerics themselves in performing the installation. Then compulsion may occur; otherwise, if payment is made after the fact without fraud, then \[it is permitted\].

Again, the opinion of Innocentius, for the understanding of this chapter: see what I discussed fully in c. sua, section 8. He excludes the view that this term should be understood as referring to what is customarily given freely, whether at the time of conferral or after the fact, such that it is done generously. Presupposing a custom, compulsion or any exaction at the time of sacred things \[is permissible\] — provided that the custom was introduced not from a preceding exaction but from the pious devotion of the faithful. The text of this chapter seems aptly to prove this. For at the beginning it made mention of clerics exacting \[payment\] for the sacrament, and immediately added \[the case\] of laypeople wishing to alter a praiseworthy custom. Lightly, therefore, the tail does not surpass the head, according to the note in c. maiores de baptismo \[X 3.42.3\]. And that \[this chapter\] is understood as referring to a praiseworthy custom of giving something at the time of sacred things — and certainly this text sufficiently invites \[such a reading\] regarding donations at the time of sacred things — as if, had there been no custom that they give afterward, there would have been no doubt that clerics had exacted from the beginning. Thus the pope here provided a middle course concerning accepted practice: while sacred things are freely conferred, laypeople may afterward be compelled to observe the custom. Nor does the restriction of Innocentius apply, who understands \[it as meaning\] that while a dispute is pending, one who has not observed the custom of giving some temporal thing to the canons themselves \[cannot be compelled\]. This division \[is as follows\]: for the first part sets forth the report concerning those wishing to enact contrary \[statutes\], but not that one may otherwise be compelled; the second part gives the judgment there in the section beginning “circa.” For the text there strongly opposes him where it says “and we command that pious customs be observed.” Therefore compulsion will take effect. For although elsewhere they may be compelled to resist harassment, nevertheless for this purpose the custom would necessarily be observed, as the pope \[states\] above in this chapter. I therefore conclude three things: first, that no custom is valid whereby something is conferred at the time of sacred things from the beginning. Second, a custom can be introduced whereby something is owed for the sacraments where no exaction preceded — because at the beginning it was simoniacal and vitiated — and in these two cases I understand the modes that annul the custom. Third, that against a custom whereby something \[is given\] from the time of conferral \[is to be distinguished from that given\] after sacred things, and where \[the custom\] was introduced from the sheer liberality of the people — yet in such a way that sacred things are first freely conferred. Either, then, \[the person\] wished to give freely at the time of sacred things, and \[the recipient\] may accept, or he did not wish \[to give\], and then, lest sacred things appear to be sold, they must be freely conferred, but afterward compulsion will follow to observe the custom. And this appears to be the sense of this text.

Second, note that beyond the prebend, other revenues or income are owed to the canon, and perhaps \[the text\] speaks of common proceeds that in certain churches are called the mensa grossa \[the common table\], or perhaps it speaks of daily distributions according to custom. And this appears to be the sense of this text. Moreover, if a custom can be introduced from a free donation — and from those things that are purely voluntary — note what Bartolus says in l. cum de itinere verbo ff. de usur. \[Dig. 43.19\], and what Lex. \[says\] in j. de servi. et aqua., in l. ij. C. de longi temporis. Regarding what beyond the prebend — on both points, see what the law reads and the custom. I discussed this in the aforecited c. Nam, and state \[the matter\] as there.
