### Finally, this question is raised:

whether the one who fails in his purgation can, after the deprivation that has been imposed, be dispensed by the bishop and restored to his benefice. And the conclusion is that he cannot, because while the infamy endures he could not be dispensed without scandal. *Presbyter* \[C.2, q.5\].

But I do not think this statement is true without qualification, because a fiction or violent presumption ought not to have greater force than the truth, as noted in *l. 4*, *pro socio*. But if someone had been justly and truly convicted of simony, he could in a certain case be dispensed. Therefore, all the more so when he was not truly convicted but only by legal interpretation. ### He commits simony

who for a price or for favor conceals a sinner, or who reconciles him before penance.

### Simony is committed

whenever anything spiritual is performed or omitted for a temporal consideration.

### A priest may bear witness

to the reconciliation of a penitent.

### The literal case. Alexander decrees:

Alexander \[III\] decreed that no priest should receive any emolument from anyone who sins publicly, so as to conceal that person’s sin from the bishop or his ministers; nor should he, out of regard for any kinship or familiarity, omit to make the sin known. Secondly, he says that no one should, by reason of fire \[i.e., urgent\] penance — or still less by reason of any penance — bring a penitent to reconciliation through grace or favor, and bear testimony of reconciliation on his behalf, or on account of any ill-will or hatred withhold reconciliation. For both \[concealing the sin and improperly reconciling\] are simoniacal. Bernardus \[of Parma\].

First, note from this text that simony is committed not only by bestowing something spiritual for something temporal, but also by omitting what is spiritual when one was bound to bestow it, on account of some temporal advantage. And so simony is committed whenever something spiritual is performed or omitted for a temporal consideration. Secondly, note the argument that a priest may bear witness to the reconciliation and penance of a sinner.

The same is noted in *c. testimonium*, *de testibus*. So too a doctor \[of law or theology\] may bear witness concerning the life and competence of a scholar. See Hostiensis’s discussion; and the gloss on *c. 1*, in *l. nemini*, C. *de admovenda disciplina tutorum*. And on the basis of these authorities I have always held that, in the panel of seven doctors who must approve candidates for the doctorate, those doctors who serve as presenting masters may properly be counted, because — since they have always personally taken the scholars to be examined into their care — they can bear witness to the scholar’s competence better and more abundantly than other outside doctors who, at other times, have no knowledge of the scholar’s ability and do not vouch for it.

Those who are suspect ought to be excluded, because by the very fact that they present someone as fit, they appear to wish to approve him. Finally, note that what is just and honest ought not to be exercised out of favor of kinship or affection of friendship; for what is in itself honest and just, favor and partiality render dishonest and unjust. Similarly with a judicial sentence: it may be just in its cause and in its procedure, yet unjust in its motive — as when someone condemns a subject out of hatred rather than on account of the offense. \[C.11, q.3\].

This is relevant to what I said in *c. cum essent*, above.

In the gloss at the end, note this gloss, which is useful for the interpretation of statutes, contracts, and other dispositions: namely, that a universal term should be resolved into a particular one so as to avoid absurdity. For it would be impossible \[i.e., absurd\] that a priest could receive any temporal thing whatsoever for concealing a sin; therefore “whatsoever” is to be construed as “something, from among certain things.”

In the second gloss, at the end, there is an objection from another chapter: there is much from remote cases: for it speaks of someone who receives a reward to point out a thief or a runaway slave, and he could not receive money for not pointing him out, because that would be against good morals — as in *l. 4*, *de condictione ob turpem causam*. And whether he can be compelled by the authority of a judge to make an identification, note that passage; and I will discuss this below in *c. cum furti*, *de furtis*.
