### First note:

From the text, that one who confers an order simoniacally received is suspended ipso facto from the conferral of orders, and the ordained person is suspended from that order so simoniacally received.

### Second, note:

That for one simoniacally ordained, only the pope dispenses. This should be understood \[to apply\] where the ordained person himself was simoniacal — as when he himself gave money or something temporal. It is otherwise if, without his knowledge, money was given by another, as in c. c. de simonia — note what is said there: no obligation is induced there. Observe that the Gloss raises a question that has no mention in the text — namely, whether such a promise is valid. And the conclusion is that it is valid even if no oath intervenes. But the Gloss in c. si eps. de preben. lib. vi. \[VI 3.4\] says that even after the fact, that promise or renunciation is valid — namely, that \[the ordained person\] will seek nothing from the bishop or the one who presented him. For just as a person can renounce a benefice, so too \[can he renounce\] this canonical obligation, and \[another\] may succeed in the place of the office, di. q. f. “regarding the danger.” But a renunciation made before ordination is not valid, because it is simoniacal, as \[stated\] here. I think this statement \[is correct\] even where no oath intervenes, because this pact — standing on a base of turpitude — is not valid in law. But if an oath intervenes, then I do not think the ordained person can seek anything contrary to the oath, because insofar as it concerns him, this can be observed without peril to eternal salvation, by analogy to c. debitores de iureiur. \[X 2.24.18\], where, for this reason, even a bare and simple promise holds. But the ordainer or the one who presented him cannot, by virtue of that obligation, desist from providing for the ordained person — nor may petition be \[refused\] — because insofar as it concerns him, the promise cannot be observed without peril to eternal salvation. For by this renunciation, etc. Therefore he cannot repent unless he renounces and remits what was thereby obtained, as in c. de his, section 8, \[under this title\], and in c. ionis de acct. in the beginning. And in the Gloss, in the word “presentatorem,” at the end: from this Gloss, two readings may be gathered for this text. The first is that here there was not properly simony in the strict sense; therefore suspension is imposed here only for a three-year period. For here there is no buying or selling, but rather a remission of an obligation already arisen. This reading is followed by \[various doctors\]. Here Panormitanus says that this simony was not committed in the order itself but in obtaining the order. For the ordainer wished to ordain and the ordained wished to be ordained with no price given. If one were to consider simony \[committed directly\] in the order, the ordainer would be perpetually suspended from the conferral of orders, and the ordained person would be suspended not only from the simoniacally received order but also from all other orders, j. q. j. reperiunt, section \[under this title\]. From this reading it follows that where simony is not committed directly in the context of divine worship — by analogy to c. significati de preben. and c. unico — since it is committed in obtaining the order rather than in the order itself, dispensation is not granted by anyone other than the pope, as is proved here in the text. This is contrary to what is said \[elsewhere\], and even though one may say that \[the other case\] proceeds where \[the person\] sinned in ignorance, here \[the person acts\] knowingly. For one who sins less is punished less, as in de pe. \[title on penance\].

The second reading gathers \[the following\] there. And this should be understood: when a custom exists, then if \[something is owed\] by laypeople to clerics, something else is owed. It was an addition that restricts that chapter so that it applies only when the crime is hidden; otherwise, if it were manifest, Hostiensis and the first \[commentators\] and Sinibaldus hold this view, although he later changed his position. And Archidiaconus \[says otherwise\]. But the first reading pleases me more, because in truth the ordinands did not have free ability not to ordain \[i.e., refuse ordination\] — \[the transaction\] does not lead to any turpitude — yet \[the legislator\] wished to avoid all \[evils\] that might follow from it, just as the ordained person did not intend to acquire the order \[simoniacally\] but wished to keep his ordainer harmless. And therefore these persons should not be punished like those who directly commit simony, because here the presumption does not arise that the penalty should be commensurate with the offense, xxiiii. q. j. non affirmamus, in c. difinit. de his d. fi. a ma. par. c. This is \[addressed in\] c. venice j., section 8, de test. Therefore, the obligation there does not now cease, as it says here. But \[this is\] true with respect to the one primarily \[concerned\]; with respect to the church, however, the obligation endures for a time. And those parishioners not only wished to keep \[the ordainer\] harmless but even \[wished\] that the debt of the church to them be remitted, and that presentation, etc.

An abbot is included under the designation “monks,” because by becoming an abbot one does not cease to be a monk. And this holds in favorable \[interpretations\]; otherwise in odious ones — on which see extensively below.

Under the designation “scholars,” the rector is included.

Under the designation “offices,” dignities and rectors are not included.

A dispensation, with respect to those to whom it is granted, is interpreted strictly; but with respect to the one from whom it emanates, it is interpreted broadly.

Under the designation “parish church,” in an odious matter, a collegiate parish church is not included.

One who has the power to legitimize bastards — and likewise those born of incest — can by analogy legitimize one who is both a bastard and incestuous at once.

## Capitulum XLII.

### A simoniac is not bound to restore whatever was agreed to be simoniacally acquired.

A usurer is bound to restore whatever he received beyond the principal sum.
