It must be asked whether a simoniac is suspended *ipso facto*. \[This involves the opinions of\] many \[authorities\]. But I set this forth briefly and clearly through three categories.

Either one is simoniac in receiving holy orders, or simoniac in conferring holy orders, or in a benefice.

### On the first category:

one who is simoniac in receiving an order — say that such a person is suspended not only from that order simoniacally received, but also from all other orders, even those lawfully received, and consequently he is removed from the administration of temporal goods of the benefice. The reason is this: there are indeed many orders, but the sacrament of orders is one; therefore, if one commits simony in one, one wounds the entire sacrament of orders, and so one is suspended from the exercise of all of them, and consequently from the administration of goods. This is what Innocentius wished here and in *c. tanta, supra eodem*. The text supports this in *§ verum* \[*1 quaestio 9*\]; they are found in *c. eos*; and see the gloss thereon. And \[the discussion\] in *de poenis, in verbo non nonam* treats generally whether one suspended from office is also suspended from benefice. \[The same\] support \[is found\] in *distinctione c. nullus; 7 c. preter*. And I understand this to be true regarding the one who committed simony, but otherwise regarding the one who received the order while another was committing simony in his ignorance — even then this person is truly suspended, \[but only\] from the order simoniacally received, not from others which he previously lawfully received, nor from benefices previously lawfully obtained or to be obtained; \[see\] *c. de simoniace, 3 eodem* — there Innocentius wished this. I think the same regarding one who unknowingly received an order from a suspended simoniac.

### On the second principal category:

one who is simoniac in conferring orders. On this point, opinions vary. One view held that such a person is not suspended with respect to others. And Innocentius inclines to this here, unless \[the conferral has persisted\] for three years, as in *c. poena, primo eodem*; and thus the one receiving the order is punished more severely \[than the one conferring it simoniacally\], because the recipient has a defective order, whereas the one conferring it — though he sins by conferring unworthily — \[does not have the same defect\]. But Innocentius himself inclined to the opposite in *Paleae, c. poena*, and considered that to be true. I conclude therefore that one who confers orders through simony is himself suspended *ipso facto* perpetually from all orders — \[this is established\] through the decretal together with the gloss in the *Paleae, c. si qui a simoniacis*. The text here also supports this, as do \[other authorities\], and \[also\] the good gloss. Accordingly, from such a simoniac, so long as he remains occult, we ought not to receive any sacraments except baptism and the Body of Christ in time of necessity.

### On the third principal category:

one who is simoniac in a benefice. Either he is a notorious simoniac, and he is suspended *ipso iure* — as noted in \[the discussion of the\] *Paleae, veritas*, and as Innocentius \[says\] here and in *c. tanta, supra eodem*. Or he is occult, and he is not suspended but is to be suspended \[by judicial process\]; \[see the\] argument \[in\] *1 quaestio* \[*3*\]. Innocentius says notably here that with respect to that benefice which he acquired simoniacally, he is suspended from all orders. If someone purchased an episcopate (though not the order itself), he cannot validly ordain clerics, because that collation proceeds from that purchase of the benefice, and therefore those ordained by him do not have the exercise \[of their orders\]. This is very noteworthy; I cite *1 quaestio 3, c. ea quae*. And from these \[conclusions Innocentius\] now infers \[certain matters\] for the understanding of that chapter, for the elucidation of a few parts of divine \[law\].

The gloss. For first, this chapter can be understood \[as follows\]: this monk was erring in law, because he believed that one who was secretly simoniacal in conferring a benefice was suspended. Therefore the pope said that this man was in error. This does not please Innocentius, who says that although it may not initially be a sin to misunderstand a canon of positive law, after one has been called and instructed by a superior, one gravely sins by not setting aside the error; \[see the\] argument in *distinctione nulli*. And note this dictum: what is commonly cited — that to misunderstand law or canons is not a sin — is \[qualified by the rule\] that the person who through this misunderstanding commits some delict is not excused, because ignorance does not excuse in delicts; \[see\] *de poenitentia, distinctio 2, sciendum*, and *c. per tuae, primo concordantia, secundo*.

\[The chapter\] can also be understood \[to mean\] that \[the monk\] was erring in fact, because he believed on the basis of some presumption that the bishop was simoniacal in orders. But since the rumor was not certain, he should not have abstained from receiving orders — unless he had an excessively scrupulous conscience. But on the other hand, the letter of the text implies the contrary. The solution: Innocentius said that to receive orders is a matter of free choice, but to minister in orders already received is a matter of necessity, as in *c. queris, de aetate et qualitate*. Therefore that man should have informed his conscience according to law with regard to orders already received; but in orders yet to be received, one ought not to be compelled, for the reasons stated above. Likewise, according to Hostiensis, conscience more readily errs in matters already undertaken than in matters yet to be undertaken, because many things impede what is to be done \[in the future\], as in \[various texts\] *qui clerici vel eorum* and *quemadmodum, de iureiurando*. And Innocentius also inclines to this here: the pope dispensed in orders already received, because at that time \[the monk\] was ignorant; \[see the\] argument in *c. si qui*.
