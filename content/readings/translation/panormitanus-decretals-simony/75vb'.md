Hostiensis, however, states that a person making assertions about his own act is not to be believed: it would be easy for anyone who elected \[a candidate\] for money to claim that he would have elected him anyway, and this cannot easily be proven. Indeed, perhaps he had spoken falsely or would have changed his mind if the money had not been given, as noted in *c. Tua, section, de eodem*. But observe that Joannes Andreae does not say the election was made simoniacally and money given to third parties — as if the money had been given to another while the electors \[remained uninvolved\] — for then his arguments would be conclusive. Hence, to reconcile the opinions, the distinction can be drawn as follows: either the money was given to the electors — and then the second opinion applies, namely that the election was not free, and they may have changed their minds, and the intention of those involved cannot be proven since they are parties to the crime (in this case, all the arguments of Joannes Andreae are conclusive) — or the money was given to a third party, perhaps a relative or friend of those who were to elect. In this latter case, if the money was given on behalf of the person who was expected to be elected, then the election is vitiated even if they would otherwise have elected him, because nothing ought to be given for the purpose of obtaining a spiritual right, as noted in *c. Ad aures, section 8, eodem titulo*, and as I shall discuss in the next chapter. For a place in a religious order must be denied to those who seek and canvass for it, and such an ambitious person ought to be rejected \[*Causa 7, quaestio 1, In scripturis*\]. But if the money was given by another \[without the candidate’s involvement\], then the first opinion can well stand, for the intention of the electors — who would otherwise have elected him — can be proven through those same electors, since they are not implicated in the crime; and perhaps, having deliberated among themselves, they had unanimously resolved to elect him. In this case, the text in the final section cited above properly applies, and all the considerations of Joannes Andreae cease to be relevant. ### Whether money given to one who threatens to cause trouble in fact is recoverable if he does cause trouble.

### Simony is not committed by giving money to preserve a right already obtained for the electors or ordinators or their friends; it is otherwise for obtaining a new right.

### Whether and when simony is committed in the acquisition or preservation of temporal or spiritual rights.

## Capitulum XXIV.

### \[Decretal:\] Dilectus filius, Leopardo.

Following one interpretation, adhering to the letter: if a person is molested in fact concerning his right, the money \[given to stop the molestation\] may be recovered if the promise \[of peace\] is not kept. Or, more broadly according to another interpretation \[as follows\]:

An archdeacon was harassing a provost; the provost promised to pay him a certain sum of money if he would desist from his injuries. He paid. But after receiving the money, the archdeacon did not cease his harassment over the dignities and benefices \[of the provost\]. The provost brought his complaint before the lord pope, who mandates certain persons that, if the facts are as stated, they should compel the archdeacon to restore what he received, if it is established that he acted contrary to his promise. Bernardus \[de Bottone observes\]: first, from the text, note this notable and singular case — that money can be given to a prelate without any taint of wrongdoing by one who is unjustly harassed regarding his existing right, in order to redeem the vexation of fact. From this it follows, as a special rule, that giving money to preserve a right already obtained against undue harassment does not constitute simony or moral turpitude. Thus, \[had the promise been kept,\] the giver could not have recovered the payment, because where turpitude exists on both sides, there is no ground for recovery, as in *l. 2, ff., de condictione ob turpem causam* and the rule *In pari delicto, de regulis iuris, Liber Sextus*. Hostiensis here says that for the purpose of obtaining a spiritual right, it is not lawful to give anything, as in *c. Matthaeus, section 3, eodem titulo*. But for preserving a right already obtained, it is lawful, as here — though say more accurately as I discussed there.

And observe that the Gloss of the provost follows the second opinion that I set out in *c. Ad aures, section, eodem titulo* — namely, that for absolution unjustly denied, one may lawfully give something. But on this point, say more accurately as I discussed there.

To encompass the entire subject, the following distinction should be drawn. We are dealing either with temporal matters or with spiritual ones. In the first case, vexation may lawfully be redeemed both for a right already obtained and for a right being sought; however, the person receiving the money holds it unjustly and is bound to make restitution, as in *l. 1, l. Generaliter, ff., de calumnia* and *l. 4, section Quoties, ff., de condictione ob turpem causam*. In spiritual matters, however, it is not lawful to give anything for the purpose of obtaining a right, even to remove an impediment of fact, because such a person appears ambitious in giving money to obtain something more easily — and this is properly the case addressed in *c. Matthaeus, eodem titulo*. In such a case, the vexation is not properly being redeemed; rather, money is being given to facilitate acquisition of a special right. But for a right already acquired, where the person is in possession of that right and is being molested in fact, it is lawful to give something, as is proven here — although the recipient is bound to make restitution. If, however, the person is being harassed because the bishop wishes to depose him justly, or someone justly accuses him, or \[a claimant\] asserts a right in the benefice, then it is not lawful for the ordainer or consecrator or the elected person to give anything, because in that case what is being redeemed is not a vexation of fact \[but the removal of a just claim\], as noted in *c. Nemo, eodem titulo*, and *c. Nisi, de praebendis* and *c. Cum pridem, de renuntiatione*.
