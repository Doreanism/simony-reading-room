the doctors commonly say — even if it be a Roman \[custom\] — not even for labor can anything be demanded for spiritual things or for what pertains to them, because no spiritual service proceeds without some \[labor\], and thus for both — that is, for the labor and for the spiritual things or their accompaniments — \[nothing may be exacted\]. Whence for understanding this chapter *Ad apostolicam*, from which this gloss derives the approval of custom, various interpretations are noted there, as I shall fully discuss in that place. But I have always held this opinion: that a custom can be introduced provided no exaction preceded it, so that the custom was introduced from the pure liberality and devotion of the people. For in that case it has no taint of vice nor a vicious foundation but a praiseworthy one; it is no great thing and no wonder if those who administer by office of their order should deserve temporal things from those they serve. This seems to be the meaning of that chapter *Ad apostolicam*, when it says that the laity are to be compelled to observe the praiseworthy custom toward their church, one “introduced by the pious devotion of the faithful,” etc. But where a preceding exaction gave rise to the custom, then such a custom is invalid, because it had a vicious beginning — and thus the text speaks, if rightly understood.

### But against this

the following objection is most powerfully raised against the first member of this distinction. For from acts that are merely facultative \[i.e., done freely at will\], no custom is introduced, nor from an annual payment freely made, as is read and noted in the law *De servitiis et aqua*, and by Bartolus in *Cum de in rem verso* \[ff. de usuris\], and by Cynus. Just as receiving a friend who comes from far away does not give rise to a quasi-possession — even if he has been received many times — nor does any custom result from it. And as Bartolus says in the aforementioned passage, if someone for ten years voluntarily and out of liberality brought someone a pair of capons annually, no custom is thereby introduced that would compel him to continue against his will in the future. Likewise, as Cynus says, even if someone for ten years continued to go to a particular mill or market and thus brought profit to its owner, no custom is thereby introduced obliging him to continue in the future, because these are merely facultative acts. To the point, therefore: if the people freely offered something to the church and the church received it, this does not establish a custom binding them to the same thing against their will in the future. Against this, I say this difficulty can be resolved in three ways.

### First

by understanding the case where it already sufficiently appears that the people consented and wished to introduce that praiseworthy custom, as noted above.

### Secondly

these arguments hold where the donation is pure and not related to \[a spiritual service\]. But in our case, even at the time of the sacrament or of the spiritual thing rendered, the protracted payment \[shows that\] something was being given, and the people over a long time, by so observing, wished to bind themselves to this and to constitute that voluntary obligation in recompense for spiritual things, acknowledging God in his gifts and redeeming their sin with alms \[dist. I de poenitentia, *Medicinas*\]. The case is similar to what Bartolus notes in the aforementioned passage about the countryman who for a long time paid a pair of capons under some pretended cause, because he held something belonging to that person.

### Thirdly, the objection can be resolved

in favor of the Church: just as the people over a long time, by contributing temporal things to spiritual services, appear to wish to introduce a custom for the future. This is supported by the chapter *Ex parte* \[de offertoriis, de praebendis\], the chapter *Ex parte* regarding consent, and these points should be well noted, from which a method of proving a praiseworthy custom in these spiritual matters is drawn; and I shall touch on a similar point in the chapter *Iacobus* on this title.

### As to how much

time is required to introduce this custom, I hold that a period of ten years suffices, both because this custom binds the laity and not the clergy — and among the laity custom proceeds without distinction, as commonly noted \[ff. de legibus\] — and because this custom is not contrary to law but is rather pious and favorable, as in the aforementioned chapter *Ad apostolicam*; and a period of ten years suffices, as the gloss notes in the final chapter *De consuetudine* \[lib. VI\]. I also hold that, given this praiseworthy custom, in order to enforce its observance, a priest cannot exact anything in the absence of custom unless he lacks the means of support. Here I distinguish as follows: if the parish priest has a means of livelihood, then in the absence of custom he cannot exact anything else, as here and in the chapter *Cum in ecclesiis* on this title — but he may licitly receive what is freely offered, as in the chapter *Priori* at the end, and related chapters. If, however, he lacks the necessary means of support, then if he can honestly acquire a livelihood from other sources — for instance, by writing, by teaching boys, or by exercising some similar occupation — he cannot exact anything, and he must blame himself for having accepted so meager a benefice. For even Paulus sought a livelihood with his own hands during the time of his preaching. Just as we often see that even a son, if he can honestly support himself, \[cannot demand support\], as the text states \[ff. de liberis agnoscendis\]. But if the parish priest cannot honestly earn a livelihood by other means, then either the bishop must provide for him, or the people may be compelled to furnish him with the necessities of life. In support of this, see the good gloss \[de consecratione, dist. I, c. Omnis christianus\].
