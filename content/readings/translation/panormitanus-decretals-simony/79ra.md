Hostiensis’s view is not satisfactory, because an objection lies against it: for the crime of simony is greater than other crimes, since all other offenses are reckoned as nothing in comparison with it, and the text here says as much. Therefore no graver offenses can be alleged against \[such witnesses\]. But the doctors say, commenting on this text, that even within the crime of simony itself there are degrees of greater and lesser — as when the case concerns a simoniac who could be removed without scandal and supporting evidence is present: in that situation, witnesses — excepting the prohibition that simoniacal persons, in accordance with what is established, must be admitted as witnesses even if not excommunicated, whether or not the judge wishes it, as stated in the gloss on *c. ex parte*, *de officio delegati*, as I discussed in *c. j. eodem quaestione* — how much credence is to be given them the judge shall weigh according to the quality and gravity of the offenses alleged. Less credence is afforded where the offenses alleged are graver than where they are equal or lesser, and still less, as it were. I would, however, think this true where simony exists in its \[proper\] form — as where someone believes the grace of the Holy Spirit can be purchased for a price. But I think otherwise in the case of simony as it is committed in our times, which is of positive law.

Hostiensis, you say that although many points are scattered in this matter across many chapters instructing judges through this chapter, nevertheless the position to be maintained is that this matter is discretionary, since the pope here does not speak in specific terms but has rather set out these considerations generally for the instruction of judges. He says, moreover, that the judge ought to consider whether the action proceeds by way of denunciation, and in that case all criminal witnesses are admitted — even women and those of ill repute — provided they have reformed, both for the purpose of denouncing and for testifying. To this effect, note *q. i., si peccaverit*, and *c. movit*.

Of prejudice. If, however, the action proceeds by way of inquisition and the crime is an excepted one, then criminal witnesses and women are admitted — not under the rigor of law but according to the tempering of equity — which is to be moderated at the judge’s discretion. The judge ought, moreover, to take many considerations into account. The first concerns the mode of proceeding: that they should proceed more readily, with equity weighed, by way of inquisition. The second consideration concerns the person against whom the action is brought: so that the judge admits criminal witnesses more readily against a regular than against a secular, and more readily if the regular is of an inferior grade than if he is of a more exalted dignity, and more readily if he is of ill repute and can be removed without scandal than if he is of good reputation and scandal would attend his removal. The third consideration concerns the persons of the witnesses: so that the judge admits upright witnesses more readily than criminal ones, and reformed criminals more readily than those persevering \[in crime\], \[distinguishing those motivated\] by zeal for justice from those \[motivated\] by the stimulus of hatred. The fourth consideration concerns the offenses alleged: whether they are lesser offenses, or equal, rather than graver ones.

### The fifth consideration is whether supporting evidence is present, so that witnesses may be more readily admitted.

Some \[are admitted\] when supporting evidence requires it, and \[not admitted\] when it is lacking.
