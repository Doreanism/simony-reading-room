In truth, regarding the manner of proceeding against a dilapidator, the laws speak principally of a single denunciation. On this matter it seems true in the case of evangelical denunciation, where the sole aim is penance, provided the parties to the dispute can be brought to an agreement — unless one party supplies \[the defect\] through another means. And therefore the judge in that context is both judge and party, as in *c. Cum speciali* \[*de appellationibus*\]. But indeed the writers speak in various ways on this matter elsewhere. Hence I would assign various modes of proceeding as established by law with respect to that point where the judge is not a party. One mode concerns recusal, and they choose arbitrators — \[in which case\] there is no judge. Whence, properly speaking, regarding the same matter no one can discharge a dual incompatible office, in accordance with what is held in analogous cases.

Therefore, considering the quality of the denouncer and the one denounced, and on that gloss at the word *ex parte*, say that there the pope supplied from his office, wishing to show favor, as appears there. And at the final gloss, so that you may understand the power of alienation — know that here the contrary holds true for a defective thing purchased where the defect is latent: when the condition was not fulfilled, the proceeding continues upon suspicion, as here. Furthermore, once suspicion has been proved, a twofold action lies. One is called the *actio redhibitoria* \[action for rescission\]: at the purchaser’s petition, the sale is entirely rescinded. The other is called the *actio quanti minoris* \[action for reduction\]: in this the purchaser sues to recover the entire amount he paid beyond the just price that would have applied had the defect been disclosed. Whence, etc. The allegation in the gloss \[states\] that if the buyer sues under the *quanti minoris*, the judge can nonetheless condemn under the *redhibitoria* — that is, that the sale be rescinded entirely. And this is infamy of law, on account of which the person will no longer have the exercise of his order, as I said above following Hostiensis. For infamy removes the exercise of any dignity whatsoever and takes away an honorable benefice. But note that that text speaks of the case where the judge finds the defect to be such that the person would not have been chosen at any price — and if one examines the matter carefully, the decision is not contrary to the nature of the action, since \[the defect means\] the thing is worth nothing, or at least that there is a surplus in the price. ### Extra gloss: I ask about the common practical question.

A bishop presses against a cleric, his subject holding a benefice, that he show the title of his benefice. The cleric responds by requesting that a formal complaint \[*libellus*\] be presented to him, since he is in peaceful possession of the benefice. The bishop replies that this makes him even more suspect, as though he wishes to proceed for his own advantage. The question is: what does the law hold? Hostiensis concludes

that regarding dilapidation, two inquisitions are conducted. The first is dealt with summarily: it is examined whether the person is suspect of dilapidation, and once this is established, he is suspended from administration. Then the second inquisition takes place into the truth of the matter. The first \[summary\] inquisition takes priority because there is a fear that if the person remained in administration, he would corrupt officials and obstruct the inquisition into the truth. Therefore he is suspended from administration by a special mode and a special procedure, as in *c. Venerabilis* \[*de officio delegati*\] — unless there were fear that he might not induce canons or other clerics to show their title — without any presentation of a formal complaint; nor is an appeal admitted to the contrary, just as an appeal is not admitted against a correction, in *c. Ad nostram* \[2\], *c. Reprehensibilis* \[*de appellationibus*\]. So too no appeal lies from this act, which is carried out on the occasion of correction. For if he does not have a title, he holds the benefice in sin and deceives the souls of his parishioners, as in *c. Dudum* \[el. 2, *de electione*\]. Likewise because this inquisition into title can be conducted even in general as part of the judge’s office.
