## Capitulum VII.

### The Literal Case

One convicted of simony is to be deposed. The same applies to one who is defamed and cannot purge himself. The second principal part begins at “regem.” As Joannes Andreae notably says in *c.* 2, *de praebendis*, book 6, in the *Novella*: an archbishop before his election promised an archdeacon a certain benefice and a certain cleric a priorate, so that each would consent to his election. Likewise, other princes received money to consent to the election. The archbishop asked what he should do concerning these matters. The pope’s response was that if the aforesaid archdeacon’s and cleric’s receipt of promises for that purpose is notorious, or if they have confessed it in court or been legitimately convicted, they are to be permanently deposed from the ministry of the altar. If, however, there is neither notoriety nor conviction nor confession, but they labor under public infamy, purgation is to be imposed upon them, and if they fail in it they are to be punished. The princes are to be induced \[to make restitution\], so that if they received \[money\] from the said bishop in order to lend their assent to the election, they should restore it to that church, since they cannot retain it without peril to their souls. From this, Romanus \[Cardinal\] draws from the text that on account of the vice of simony, even when committed in the election of a prelate, one ought to be deposed from office and benefice — which is to be understood as I said in *c. Acquisitionis*.

Note secondly that one who is notorious in the crime of simony, or confessed, or convicted, is equally punished.

Note thirdly that although the crime of simony is an excepted crime and many things in it are special, reputation alone does not suffice for punishment. Note that even one defamed of a grave crime may have purgation imposed upon him from the outset, and one who fails in it may be punished as the perpetrator of the offense.

Kings, princes, and other laymen who receive money in order to consent to the election or promotion to some prelacy are bound to restore that money to the church whose prelacy was at stake, even though it did not come from the goods of the church. This is because the money was given to the disgrace of that church, and therefore it must be restored to that church as compensation for the injury — which should be well noted in practice. For the confessors of princes, whether led by ignorance or seeking to please them, neglect to induce them to do penance. Many such persons exist in the world today, and one must despair of the salvation of those who die in such sin, for sin is not remitted unless what was taken is restored — as in the rule *Peccatum*, *de regulis iuris*, book 6, and in *c. Cum tu* 1, *de usuris*.

Regarding the gloss at the word “confessi” — where it says “an extrajudicial \[confession\] does not prejudice,” etc. — understand the gloss to mean that a confession made outside of court concerning a crime does not prejudice, namely not fully. It does, however, produce a valid indication sufficient for subjecting the one who confessed to torture, as noted in the gloss and the doctors on *l. Capite quinto*, *ff. de adulteriis*. And \[a confession\] that passes into the designation of a specific offense also prejudices as to penalty: for example, if someone outside of court commits simony and boasts of having committed such a crime, he is then punished for the offense of boasting, as is the basis in *c. Quod sit* 1, *de excessibus praelatorum*. Innocentius holds in *c. Per inquisitionem*, *de electione*, that an extrajudicial confession of a crime is sufficient for depriving someone of his benefices — the contrary of which he himself holds in the aforementioned *c. Quod sit*. On this matter I have spoken most fully in *c. At si clerici*, *de iudiciis*.
