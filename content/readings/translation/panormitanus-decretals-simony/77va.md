### Literal Case.

Prelates ought to correct the excesses of their subjects with all the greater diligence as it would be all the more damnable for them to leave their offences uncorrected. This is shown by the example of Helias, who, although he was personally a good man, nevertheless did not effectively correct the excesses of his sons, and inseparably received the Lord’s vengeance together with his sons — for when his sons were slain in battle, he himself fell from his chair and died with his neck broken. A prelate therefore can proceed against subjects by three modes, with certain exceptions, and all of this can be clearly seen in the text of the present chapter.

### First observation from the text.

A prelate, however good and holy in himself, can be burdened and brought low by the excesses of his subjects whom he negligently leaves uncorrected. For he destroys them by failing to correct them. 2 Corinthians \[and see also\] *c. Ephesiis.* and liii. dist. c. *it.* c. *irrefragabilis.* 8. *De offic. ordi.* And therefore the office of prelate is no light burden, because one must provide not only for oneself but also for others here and elsewhere; and on account of this, the one who stands in eminence is afflicted more by sorrows than he rejoices in honors. *dist. nervi.*

A person is punished not only eternally in the next world but also in this one, by violent and temporal death.

A person who dies \[prematurely\] does not complete his days, according to the saying of the Psalm: *the men of blood* \[Psalm 55:23\]. The same thing that is said of a prelate applies to prelates who are negligent in correcting the excesses of their sons. For this holds. xxiii. q. iv. Note these two things. See *bo. ter.* 3 Elvii. dist. in *S. necesse facit.* c. *non putes.* xxii. q. v.

### In notorious cases the order of law is not observed.

Therefore neither an inscription nor a prior admonition nor a clamorous denunciation is required. And so the text here says to pass over notorious excesses — and yet something must be said about this, as noted in *c. nostram. S. pe. in reiu.* A canonical charitable admonition must precede. And note that the ecclesiastical admonition must be charitable — that is, proceeding from charity alone, and not from a motive of hatred or for temporal gain.

In the crime of simony, this admonition is required just as in other crimes, and thus the text here seems to accord with the ruling of the gloss in *c. inquisitionis.* in *S. Veactt.* This, however, is not entirely derived from the text, because — as Joannes Andreae says here — perhaps this simony was not committed in the reception of orders or in the acquisition of an abbey.
