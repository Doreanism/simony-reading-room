## Capitulum XXXVIII.

### Simony is not committed if, after the reception of orders, something is given to the ordainer, provided there is a custom regarding this; nevertheless, no prior assessment or surety of payment should precede, but these things should be conferred freely; and whether after ordination the person can be compelled to give.

The bishop has jurisdiction over lay persons to compel them to observe praiseworthy customs of the church.

### Custom in favor of the church is established through acts that are purely voluntary, even by lay persons.

And in England the living do not pay anything by way of tithes, but at death all movable goods are tithed.

### Things to be conferred in apostolic manner: yet the ordinary compels lay persons to observe praiseworthy customs.

And first it sets out the excess of clerics. Second, it provides for both parties, at the words *et propter id*.

The literal case. Certain clerics exact and extort money for the blessings of those being married, for funeral rites of the dead, and for similar things; and unless they are satisfied, they fraudulently raise fictitious impediments. Conversely, \[certain lay persons\] strive to undermine praiseworthy customs introduced by the devotion of the faithful toward holy Church, under the pretext of piety. Accordingly, the Council prohibits these exactions from being made. It orders that pious customs be observed, decreeing that ecclesiastical sacraments be conferred freely, and that the bishop of the place, having ascertained the truth, restrain those who maliciously attempt to alter a praiseworthy custom.

And first note from the text that although, once all temporal exaction by those conferring \[the sacraments\] has been removed, nothing temporal should be rendered by way of exaction, nevertheless something may be freely and spontaneously conferred upon churches, nor are sacred things thereby said to be sold — to this effect, *c. Grati* and *c. Collectus II* under the same title. And a custom can be introduced by which lay persons confer something upon churches; on which see more fully in the cited passage of the Gloss. And Romanus \[Hostiensis\] says there, at the word *libere*, that even where a custom of giving something exists, the sacraments themselves must be conferred freely. From which it follows that no surety may be exacted for them. Note also, at the words *per episcopum loci*, that the rector of a church cannot decide anything concerning custom or compel the people to observe this custom; rather, the reasonableness of the custom must be examined and declared by the bishop, and the compulsion must be carried out by the same bishop.

And third, note a singular case in which — in the second part, the prohibition is made at the words *cum igitur*; in the third, punishment is ordered — the bishop has jurisdiction over lay persons so as to compel them to observe a praiseworthy custom introduced toward the church. And keep this text in mind, so that the bishop may claim jurisdiction not only where the reasonableness of the custom is apparent, but also in determining whether the custom itself is praiseworthy — from this text, at the words *veritate cognita* — just as a judge examines whether a matter falls within his jurisdiction, as in *c. Super res* under the same title *de referendariis*, and in *l. final. quaestio ex aliena ff. de iudiciis*. So too can the bishop examine the foundation of his own jurisdiction — namely, whether the custom is praiseworthy — for given the reasonableness of the custom, lay persons who \[violate it\] commit sacrilege or transgress against the church itself, in which case the bishop claims jurisdiction even over lay persons, as in *c. Cum sit generalis* under the same title *de foro competente*.

In the final Gloss, in this Gloss two readings are gathered. Concerning the third reading, it posits at the end that one should understand this as referring to the custom by which lay persons abuse \[their position\] in wishing to institute clerics in patronage churches, or in wishing to elect where the church is collegiate. But in truth this reading is not applicable to the text, which speaks of a custom introduced by lay persons in favor of the church; for the fact that a lay person presents or consents to an election was not introduced by the custom of lay persons, nor principally in favor of the church or of clerics, but rather in favor of the patrons themselves, and it was introduced through the tolerance and permission of the church, and as a favor so that lay persons would be induced to construct churches, as in *c. Quoniam de iure patronatus*. Thus it was rather by force and pressure; and therefore it can be gathered that the text here should be understood as referring to a custom arising from the spontaneous will of the giver. And by this \[reasoning\] is resolved the claim that lay persons wish to punish clerics before any degradation, saying that they do this in order to purge the province of all evils — for these and similar examples are not consonant with this text; on which see the final Gloss at *C.12 q.2 Frater*, concerning praiseworthy custom introduced by lay persons in favor of the church, which now certain lay persons were seeking to undermine.

And therefore he gives a second reading, which he posits at the beginning of the Gloss, \[a reading\] unique and singular, and not proved elsewhere except here — namely, that it should be understood as referring to the custom by which lay persons were accustomed freely to offer churches certain temporal things when they received the sacraments. The Gloss itself intended the same reading of the text in *c. Sua* under the same title. But Innocentius wished to restrict this reading greatly, saying that it should be understood \[only\] when lay persons strive to alter this custom by solicitation, or \[to argue\] that something ought not to be given; but it should not be understood to mean that if something was customarily conferred at the time of the conferral of a sacrament, the person can be compelled to pay it. For he adduces the argument that if a gift was customarily given through penance, no one can be compelled to pay it.
