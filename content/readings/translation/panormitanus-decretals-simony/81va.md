\[One must rather\] suffer evil than consent to evil, as in *c. Ita* and *c. Sacris*, discussed at greater length in the Gloss. But certain things are good in themselves, and even if they are evil \[under ordinary circumstances\], nevertheless in a time of necessity all things ought to be held in common, as in *dist. 37 Sicut*; and therefore one does not commit theft at such a time, because one does not destroy another’s property. But understand this to apply when the necessity is so great that the person would perish of hunger if the thing were not taken, and when he cannot be helped by others — and then necessity excuses from all fault. And thus understand *c. Discipulos*. But when there is necessity that is not so extreme, then it alleviates the penalty and the fault but does not entirely excuse; and thus understand *c. De eo*.

One is excused from the taint of simony on the pretext of poverty, because \[monasteries\] must abstain from \[simoniacal\] reception; only so many should be received as can live from the revenues of the monastery, as in *c. Unico de statu regularium lib. 6*. And you have a similar Gloss in *c. Non satis* under the same title, and Hostiensis follows this Gloss, specifically in *c. Cum in ecclesia* under the same title, for it amounts to remitting what is owed by law as sustenance, even though \[the person\] does not have food from the monastery, as in *c. Per tuas III* under the same title. Poverty does not excuse, as is the case here; but say as I have said in *c. Non satis*, that through a pact and arrangement it is not permitted, but by way of a simple contribution it can be sufficiently sustained — as the Gloss supports. This was also the view of Dominus Antonius \[de Butrio\]; yet the safer course is that this should in no way be done, by the reasoning of *c. Perniciosi de statu regularium lib. 6*.

And there I believe that the present law must be followed, etc. The Gloss holds that today regulars received through simony ought to be deposed, whether one proceeds by way of ingratitude or by way of accusation; and according to this reading, the distinction drawn in *c. Dilectus* under the same title will be abrogated. But Innocentius and Hostiensis maintain the first reading of the Gloss, so that the mode of proceeding is distinguished — which pleases me sufficiently with respect to nuns, who do not have orders, as in *c. Monachos de poenis et remissionibus*. \[In the case of monks,\] this chapter will be observed without distinction. Observe what he says — that in effect they are deposed; and this is true of a monk who is not a cleric, but in the case of a monk who is a cleric, deposition operates more powerfully than simple suspension, at least with respect to dispensation, given what is noted in *c. Tuae de clericis non residentibus*. And in the Gloss, or according to Hostiensis, they are to be thrust into a prison cell within the walls of the monastery, and there, leading a harsher life — consider the Gloss with the text carefully — lest they wander about damnably in the world. And there it is elegantly proved that simony does not impede the monastic state itself, even though it impedes \[the simoniac’s\] place in that particular monastery; for if, with simony impeding, they were not \[truly\] nuns, they could return to the secular world without damnation — \[but since simony does not impede the monastic state, they remain nuns and cannot\], and this is maintained.

### Those simoniacally received through extortions: the exacted amount must be restored twofold.

If, however, a person is received \[in this way\], these \[amounts\] are not to be discharged in the forum of conscience.

### Simony is committed not only in purely spiritual matters but also in things annexed to spirituality.

And there are three principal parts. In the first, the facts are narrated. The literal case: certain former rectors of churches subject those churches to interdict and do not permit anyone to be instituted in them until money is paid. Likewise, when a soldier or a cleric enters a religious house, or when someone chooses burial among religious, they feign \[obstacles\] until they receive some gift. This came to the attention of the pope, who responds that — since the Apostle commands us to abstain not only from evil but from every appearance of evil — such exactions are to be deemed harsh, and whoever commits them shall restore double what was exacted, to be faithfully converted to the benefit of the places from which they were paid. Note that nothing should be exacted for the institution of a rector or for the consent given at such institution. Second, note:

The case concerns the institution of a rector; if money is exacted for institution \[it is simoniacal\]. And note further a case in which what is simoniacally exacted must be restored twofold — and this is stated in the text under the same title, where nevertheless what is illicitly exacted is to be restored; nor in any other law under this title is provision made concerning such doubling. It can be said that the former rule applies when someone acts simoniacally by common agreement of the giver and the receiver, whereas this third case concerns what is exacted by a superior, as it were, on account of the difficulties by which they sought to extract this money. And note this well, as in *c. Procurator de censibus*, and in the chapter on exactions under the same title *lib. 6*. And as to whether, in the forum of penalty, those exactors are bound to restore double what was so exacted, I believe not, because in the forum of conscience it is not necessary to pay what is imposed by way of penalty.

In the Gloss there, \[the phrase\] “because we redeem his own,” etc. — note how badly the Gloss speaks, because it takes “institution” here to mean the free collation of a benefice or the confirmation of an election — that is, institution by law. And if this undoubtedly constitutes simony, then in this case \[the superior\] wrongfully denies institution to others, to whom it ought not \[to have been denied\]; rather, the matter should have been referred to the higher superior. And this is the apt text in *c. Sicut* under the same title; *c. Nullius de iure patronatus* also supports this. Nor is such a person obliged to buy off the vexation inflicted upon him; indeed, he should rather have recourse anew, for this case concerns a spiritual right being given, not a right already acquired being defended — which is what he says.
