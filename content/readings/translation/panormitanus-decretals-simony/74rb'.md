## Capitulum XVIII.

One who gives or receives money for the exercise of an ecclesiastical ministry also commits simony. Simony is not committed by the fact that something is given spontaneously.

### Nostram.

### Note i., from the text:

one general rule — that for the exercise of an ecclesiastical ministry, money must not be given or exacted. And therefore \[the text\] says “for the ministry” and “for the mystery.” For there is a difference between *ministerium* and *misterium*: *ministerium* properly means an office, from the act of serving; *misterium* is the same as a sacrament, which has a hidden and secret power — whence a sacrament is said to be, as it were, a sign of a sacred thing \[C.1, q.1, *Multi*\]. For money must not be exacted for exercising an ecclesiastical office. Nor does it follow \[from this alone\] whether simony is committed when something temporal is exacted for blessing a chalice or vestments. On this, see Hostiensis, in the *Summa*, under the same title, the word *sed nunquid simonia*. Some say it is not simony, because they find no specific prohibition. Nor is it better \[resolved\] by this text, where it says generally “for exercising an ecclesiastical office,” etc. — for there an ecclesiastical office is involved and it is something spiritual. Regarding vicars — not only is simony committed in the institution of a benefice, but also \[when money is given\] so that someone may serve as a vicar in a church. He says this should be examined — see Hostiensis with the gloss in c. *Licet*, below, *de vicariis*. Whether, moreover, a priest commits simony by hiring out his spiritual services at some church, I shall discuss in c. *Quoniam*, below, *ne prelatis*, etc.

### Third, note there, “for chrism”:

that \[money\] must not be exacted indiscriminately for chrism. And since the text speaks generally, the prohibition applies — whether it is conferred as a sacrament, or whether it is supplied so that an inferior church may use it, or in any manner whatsoever.

### Lastly, note there:

“exact” — that in spiritual matters, exaction is nevertheless prohibited. If therefore something is offered spontaneously, this is not prohibited, according to Hostiensis. Understand \[this rule\] accordingly.

### §3. One who was simoniacally ordained but who did not become a simoniac rightly seeks

a dispensation. And before dispensation, he may not rightly be promoted to that \[office\].

One who was knowingly ordained simoniacally is suspended from all orders; it is otherwise if the simony was committed without his knowledge. A bishop cannot dispense except in cases expressly provided by law. With one simoniacally ordained who is not \[himself\] a simoniac, a dispensation can be granted. And \[the chapter\] is not divided. The literal case: certain persons were ordained simoniacally. It was asked what should be done regarding them. The pope responds that he cannot give a definitive answer on this unless we first know how they were ordained, because according to a certain species of simony, some — although simoniacally ordained — \[are not culpable\], as when they were ordained in ignorance.

### Note, first, from the text, that certain persons

are simoniacally ordained yet are not simoniacs — as when, without their knowledge, money was given for their ordination, perhaps by a friend. Others, however, are simoniacs and simoniacally ordained — as when they themselves gave money to be ordained, or it was given with their consent, tacitly or expressly. Still others are simoniacs but not simoniacally ordained — as when the simony was committed not in the ordination itself, but because they themselves gave money so that others might be ordained. \[Those who are not simoniacs\] may remain in their orders \[by dispensation\], since \[the text\] does not specify through whom \[dispensation is granted\], from which it is inferred that a bishop can dispense — in conjunction with the theory of Innocentius in c. *Dilectus*, *de tempore ordinationis*. On this point \[see there\].

### In gloss i., at the end. Concerning this

matter — who can dispense with one guilty of simony — the doctors offer some discussion here, and it is more fully noted in the gloss on the penultimate chapter, *de electione*, and in c. *Post translationem*, *de renuntiatione*, and there Johannes Calderinus, collecting the opinions of others, framed a distinction which Doctor Antonius reported here, adding nothing. But in truth, in many respects it requires, in my judgment, reformation. Distinguish, therefore, that either one obtains an order or a benefice through simony. In the first case, either the simony was committed with his knowledge and consent, tacitly or expressly, or with him personally giving the money — and then only the pope dispenses, nor does even the pope customarily do so. And such a simoniac is suspended not only from the order simoniacally received but also from all orders, as in dist. 22, §. *Verum*, \[and\] C.1, q.1, *Reperitur*. Or the simony was committed without his knowledge and was not subsequently ratified: then Laurentius and Hostiensis here — whom Johannes Calderinus follows in the passage cited above — distinguish.
