\[There follows a discussion of\] excommunication, on which see the full treatment in the extravagant of Bonifacius on this point, in *c. Cum*, at the beginning. And today many fall under this excommunication.

\[Regarding whether service is\] spiritual, and whether simony is thereby committed: although a collation may follow, nevertheless it is otherwise if \[the cleric\] had not been assigned to that service, or if the service were not of an honorable kind. Antonius, for the sake of harmony, says that in general those receiving service either have regard to a specific benefice or tend toward an unspecified benefice — and the gloss of Joannes proceeds accordingly. But I do not see a good reason for the distinction between a specific and an unspecified benefice. Nor do I think that in any case it is licit to serve principally \[for a benefice\], where the principal motive leads to conferral in exchange for temporal service received: because what is in itself owed to human affairs belongs to grace, not to strict judgment — as in *c. Memor* 1, *eodem* \[title\]. Such a hope \[of obtaining a benefice through service\] — the gloss so held in *c. Quid proderit*, dist. 72, and Innocentius in *Venerabili*, *de electione*, at the end, at the word “fiducia.” And I understand the gloss in that \[chapter\] *Et sunt nonnulli*, 1 *q.* 1, according to this gloss and that *c. Ad proderit*. And this is relevant to the question that Hostiensis raises here: if someone assigns a cleric to spiritual service, promising him a fixed annual salary until a suitable benefice is provided for him — whether this is licit — and he concludes that it is. Provided that \[the cleric\] has not specifically bound himself by pact to perform temporal service, he does not commit simony. \[Nor does he commit simony\] if he later remits the salary when a benefice is conferred, because the salary was conditional, and once the condition is fulfilled the obligation expires, as in *Institutes, de verborum obligationibus*, section *Die*. But it would be otherwise if the promise had been unconditional and perpetual: for then he would indeed commit simony if \[he exchanged\] a temporal thing for obtaining a spiritual one — by analogy with *c. Veniens*, above, *de testibus*. I would consider the first statement of Hostiensis to hold true only where the prelate is not principally moved to confer \[a benefice\] on account of temporal or spiritual service received, but because the person merits it, and the service \[merely\] provided the final occasion — by analogy with *c. Grave*, *de praebendis*. For I observe that it is not licit to receive a promise of a benefice to be conferred in exchange for a vote given in an election, as in *c. De hoc*, above; and likewise it does not seem licit to receive a promise of a benefice to be conferred even in exchange for spiritual services. But it is otherwise, as I said, if this were merely the impulsive cause. And from these \[considerations\] the first \[question\] is resolved. Concerning the second, one can gather from the gloss whether it is licit for a cleric to serve a prelate for the purpose of obtaining a benefice, and whether the prelate may in such a case lawfully confer \[it\].

### A Notorious Simoniac

A notorious simoniac, or one defamed of simony who has not purged himself, is deposed.

A simoniac with respect to a benefice is not deposed from orders, but is deprived of the benefice.

One defamed of the crime of simony, if he has not purged himself, is deposed — even though no formal mode of proceeding has been initiated against him, as is likewise the case with other crimes.

Simony is punished on the basis of presumption alone, when certain notable signs are present.

One defamed of any crime, if he is not purged of it, is punished as if convicted.

A probable presumption arising from infamy passes into a violent \[i.e., compelling\] presumption when the accused fails to purge himself.

A presumption is not sufficient for condemning anyone unless it is violent \[i.e., compelling\].

Infamy, although it takes the place of an accusation, does create some degree of presumption.
