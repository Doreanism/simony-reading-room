\[Whether one can be promoted\] to the rectorship of a secular church: concerning the episcopate there is no doubt, as in the Clementine, *de electione*. But whether one can be promoted to an inferior secular church, see with the gloss in Baldus, Clementine *de electione* — I treat this fully in *c.*, and on the question of fear, *de statu*. And Romanus \[holds\] that confirmation of one elected as abbot pertains to the bishop of the place to which the monastery is subject. Likewise by analogy, with the gloss, *q. 28, c. 1*, and the good gloss treating generally concerning the confirmation of elects in *c. per eum*, *de electione*, *Liber Sextus*.

\[The bishop stands\] in place of a son, while the abbot \[stands\] in place of a father.

### On the distinction: with truth considered, there is the greatest difference between an express prohibition and silence.

And the final notable point: that those who commit simony, even in favor of others, are to be deposed. This is supported \[by the text\]. And in other \[passages\] at the end, or according to Innocentius, perhaps this metropolitan church was patron of the monastery. Consequently, during a vacancy, they have the power of custody, as in *c. vos*, *de officio ordinario*. Thus \[the metropolitan\] could give this elect the power to administer. But today this opinion of Innocentius does not hold, on account of *c. avaritiae*, *de electione*, *Liber Sextus*, whereby an elect who has not been confirmed may not administer under any pretext offered, otherwise he incurs the penalty \[stated\] there. Therefore the opinion of the gloss holds. And as to why the chapter can confirm an elect during a vacancy but cannot confer benefices pertaining to the bishop, state as noted in *c. cum olim*, cited in the gloss, both there and in the gloss on the same chapter at *sub interminatione* at the end. Note the gloss that this comes up daily in practice among those regulars who command simply that something be done under pain of excommunication — for it is doubtful whether \[such a command\] constitutes an excommunication; rather, it is a kind of admonition concerning excommunication \[to be imposed\] if the one commanded is not obeyed — and this accords with what is noted in the title *de communibus vel episcopi*, almost throughout.

And this bears on the question of when a judge commands someone to do something and immediately imposes a penalty when \[the command\] has been \[disobeyed\] — that is, a threat of a penalty to be inflicted — see what is noted in the *Speculum*, *de exsecutione sententiae*. And you have many similar glosses in *c. ij.*, *de matrimonio contracto contra interdictum ecclesiae*, and in *c. salonitanae*, *distinctio xiii*, and in *c. unico*, *de sagittariis*, and there more fully. I shall discuss, following Johannes Andreae, whether in some case \[such a command\] is to be treated as an excommunication.

On this question — whether one who is silent is to be taken as consenting — state as noted in the rule *qui tacet*, *de regulis iuris*, *Liber Sextus*. And I discussed this more fully in *c. nonne*, *de praesumptionibus*. But note that this chapter does not speak of one who is present and silent, but of one who is absent and ignorant. It is far different to expressly contradict and to be ignorant, because one who is ignorant does not, in truth, contradict. In the gloss on the word *imputari* at the end, note that although the gloss draws a distinction for the case of this chapter between \[the situation of\] a confirmed elect, it does not openly reveal whether the confirmation was invalid by reason of simony. And Innocentius speaks here in general terms, according to the gloss.

But Johannes and Hostiensis hold that although an election so made is not valid, the confirmation is nonetheless valid. Hostiensis, however, holds the opposite, saying that such a confirmation is quasi-purchased, and is understood \[to be valid\] only insofar as it does not invalidate the election previously made of that person.

And the opinion of Hostiensis accords, according to Johannes Andreae, with the principle that one seeking confirmation is dealing with a right yet to be acquired, where he appears to have acquired a right through simony committed by another. But in favor of Hostiensis’s opinion — he who seems to draw distinctions between one who is ignorant and one who contradicts, holding that the act of contradiction has a certain effect — Johannes Andreae himself distinguishes thus: he gives Hostiensis’s opinion that the confirmation does not hold; but in the case of one who expressly contradicts, he said it cannot be denied that such a confirmation is simoniacal.

Furthermore, certain others, restricting this matter still more, say that \[the confirmation is tainted\] even as if money were given in fraud of the very person who was to be elected or confirmed, and so is simoniacal. Understand this text, which ought to be interpreted as meaning that \[the simony\] is not to be imputed to him as a penalty or with respect to the possibility of dispensation; but certainly the election is not satisfactory.
