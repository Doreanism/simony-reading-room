And secondly, note from the gloss that a copulative conjunction causes the conjoined thing to come principally, not as an accessory; and if it joins a thing that cannot come principally, then it cannot — but as an accessory it can — so it impedes the transaction. For the conjunction “and” \[*et*\] joins principally what cannot come principally; and note this concerning the copulative conjunction in the rule, *de regulis iuris*.

And I apply this gloss to the following question. It is certain that the right of patronage passes with the universality of a secular estate, as in the authorities cited in the gloss. But suppose a sale has been made in this form: “I sell you such-and-such an estate *and* the right of patronage that I have in it.” Does the sale hold with respect to the right of patronage? I believe it does not, as is plainly gathered from this gloss: because, by virtue of the fact that it is joined by the copulative “and” \[*et*\], it comes and is treated as sold principally, just like the estate — which it cannot be, as in *c. 1*, *de iure patronatus*. And on this basis I think the statement of Joannes Andreae in *c. 1*, *de iure patronatus*, in the Liber Sextus, should be qualified: it applies when the word used denotes a kind of accession and not when the copulative conjunction “and” \[*et*\] is used. Finally, gather from this gloss that, where several things are sold principally and one of them cannot be sold, the sale is not rescinded with respect to all the things, but only with respect to the thing prohibited from being sold — by the argument in *l. 1*, *de evictionibus*; see also *l. dominus*, *de contrahenda emptione*, and the rule *utile* and the note of Joannes thereon, *de regulis iuris*, in the Liber Sextus. But the gloss at the end raises an objection against the text: given that the church could not be granted under an annual rent, as in *c. non satis* and the following chapter, then since this was established by the confession of those monks themselves, the pope ought to have imposed silence on them, as he did in *c. cum essent*, in the same title.

By way of solution, the doctor says here that the church was not sold, but rather its fruits were sold or leased; and whenever one “leases the church,” this is to be understood as meaning “the fruits of the church.” It is understood that the church was granted to these religious while retaining the rent — in a manner similar to *c. constituto*, *de religiosis domibus*. Whether and in what circumstances a church may be so granted, discuss as noted there. And in the final gloss, at the end, note from the gloss together with the text that a delegated judge can order missio in possessionem for the preservation of a thing up to the definitive sentence. And this seems to me correct, having regard to the text, concerning which Innocentius here says that by these words \[in the rescript\] they were appointed conservators, and that just as conservators can defend, so too they can put someone in possession. It follows from this statement of Innocentius, which has been said, \[that\] that from the revenues of a certain dignity or church a fixed annual pension may be established for a spiritual benefice. And on this, the text is useful and notable when a letter is written to one who is not a judge by these words.

## Capitulum XII.

### Money ought not to be exacted from churches for chrism, even if there is a custom to that effect.

First, the reprehensible practice is narrated; secondly, this is prohibited, beginning at the word *quia vero*; thirdly, the penalty for those committing this offense.

### The literal case.

Certain bishops extorted money from the clergy for chrism; and some call these payments “cathedraticum” \[the cathedral tax\]; others call them “the Easter offering”; others call them “the customary payment.” But because this is simoniacal, the pope commands that on no pretext of custom or prelacy should they exact the aforesaid charges. For if they do, they may not without cause fear peril to their order and dignity. Bernardus and Liniensis, and the text, which prohibits not only the exaction of money for chrism insofar as it is used — that is, for the act of anointing someone with chrism — but also for the liquid of chrism itself. For here money was not exacted from the one being anointed or confirmed, but from the lesser churches, perhaps baptismal churches; and in *c.* 1, *placuit*. Chrism is therefore something spiritual, not only because it is a sacrament, but also insofar as it is a liquid \[consecrated substance\]. Therefore neither from the confirmed person nor from the churches to which it confers something of temporality should anything be exacted for it. Secondly, note that a change or alteration of name does not excuse what by its nature is evil — for names are but appellative labels, which are immutable (*l. si quis in fundi vocabulo*, ff. \[*Digest*\], *de legatis* 1). Only proper names may be changed at will, unless in fraud. Lynanus. C. *de mutatione nominis*. Note: for spiritual things therefore no temporal payment should be exacted, whatever name is given to such a charge.
