He voluntarily confessed having received the order in ignorance, and thus a benign interpretation should be made, so that he is treated as canonically ordained and as one renouncing \[the office\]. Because, therefore, that chapter has no bearing on the matter at hand — and by the rule \[*Regulae iuris*\], where dispensation is permitted both for monks and for canons regular when simony intervened without their knowledge — this \[applies\] as to the first principal member. But as to the second member, concerning a benefice obtained through simony, distinguish: either it is a benefice with cure of souls, or a dignity (which is treated as equivalent in this context, by the argument of c. *De multa*, *de prebendis*, and in c. *Cum in illis*, under the same title, book 6), or it is a simple benefice. In the first case, Johannes Calderinus says in the passage cited above that whether he acted knowingly or in ignorance, if he knowingly and voluntarily renounces the benefice, he can receive a dispensation for another church; but he cannot be dispensed so as to hold the one where the simony was committed, except to hold something there in minor orders, and to have a benefice in a church \[so\] purchased. He can have another \[benefice\] by dispensation and with the exercise of all orders; but the one where simony was committed he cannot have, although he may hold a simple benefice there and with the exercise of minor orders only. But Johannes Calderinus does not specifically address the case of one who does not renounce but awaits a condemnatory sentence. And in that case I think the bishop in no way dispenses, so that he might have a benefice elsewhere — nor \[in\] that same church. In support of this I adduce the text in c. *Nobis*, at the end, under the same title, where it is said that such a person cannot obtain any dispensation. Likewise, the text of c. j. is relevant, where dispensation is permitted for such a person if he knowingly relinquished the benefice — that is, voluntarily renounced — but otherwise not. And here there is a twofold reason: first, because one deals more leniently with one who confesses than with one who is convicted \[l. 2, *Nolo*\]. Likewise, because he is now rendered infamous by law — \[C.5, q. \[...\], c. *Sane*, q.2, c.\] — and a bishop does not dispense with an infamous person except in cases expressly provided by law, as noted in c. *Cum te*, *de re iudicata*. In this case, c. *Ex diligenti* under the same title can be very well reconciled, in the penultimate gloss. Likewise, a bishop regularly dispenses even in lesser and less serious matters, as in c. *At si clerici*, at the end, *de iudiciis*. But the crime of simony is graver, as in c. *Sicut* under the same title, and in gloss i. thereon; therefore he does not dispense, since \[the power to do so\] is not found expressly stated.

If, however, he was ignorant — then either \[money\] was given by his rivals, and it does not harm him, as is proved in the first response under the same title, and c. *Sicut*, because they appear to have acted fraudulently. Or \[it was given\] by friends: then if he voluntarily renounces, a dispensation can be granted to him out of great mercy — the same according to Hostiensis and others. If he awaits a sentence, \[he is dispensed\] out of the same utility or necessity of the church. This distinction simply does not satisfy me. For first, it seems contrary to the penultimate chapter, *de electione*, especially in the passage \[that begins\] *ft.*, c., in the word *pais*, for the word *vero* comes adversatively to the preceding text, and dispensation is permitted when one voluntarily renounces; therefore the contrary \[holds\] where the benefice is not simple — that is, where he does not voluntarily renounce. And for this, the text in the aforesaid c. f., at the end, q.5, where dispensation is not permitted by the bishop for one to hold the dignity or church where simony was committed. Does the bishop \[dispense\] except in cases expressly provided by law? — the case is expressed nowhere — therefore I would distinguish as follows: where a benefice with cure of souls or a dignity has been acquired simoniacally by a cleric in ignorance, under no circumstances is dispensation granted unless the benefice is vacant, so that he may hold such a benefice with cure of souls or dignity; but for another that becomes vacant, yes. He may, however, hold other benefices even \[in the church where simony was committed\].
