### Verbal formulations are not regarded

unless the substance is thereby changed.

### In assessing simony, great attention must be paid

to whether the person in question has been accustomed to commit simony, since in the case of those who habitually commit it, even a small gift is presumed to be simoniacal.

### Alms should be given

by the giver in a manner no less fitting for the recipient than for the amount. Indeed, it is a greater sin to give a pauper lavish foods.

### On the gloss, at the word “discretim,” at the end:

Can a priest who exercises poor judgment impose a penance less than is due, or otherwise exercise his office improperly, and yet the penitent be safe if he submits to the commission of simony? Or thus: In determining whether gifts induce simony, the following must be considered: the person of the giver, the person of the recipient, and the quality and quantity of the gift.

### Infamy.

There \[the text says\] “they are infamous.” Note this gloss: that simoniacs are infamous. Of the nation. \[Read\] otherwise, or better: an ordination or consecration is not deemed simoniacal even if something was given to the one conferring ordination, provided it was not by agreement. The text is divided as follows: In the first part \[the pope\] commends the purity of the questioner’s conscience and reproves scrupulous curiosity. In the second part, beginning at “in hoc itaque” \[in this matter, therefore\], he narrates the facts and resolves them by drawing distinctions. The solution proceeds in three stages: the second begins at “cum accipientis” \[since the one receiving\]; the third at “si ergo” \[if, therefore\]. In the third principal part, beginning at “quod autem” \[but as to the fact that\], he responds to the authority from which the doubt arose.

### The literal case.

A certain archbishop of Strigonium \[Esztergom\], having been confirmed, had his pallium sent to him by the pope through a certain cardinal traveling by sea. The brother of the elected archbishop went out to meet the cardinal, and since the cardinal had few horses, he offered him a palfrey — without the knowledge of the bishop-elect — who subsequently received the pallium from that same cardinal. Afterwards the archbishop, learning of this, doubted whether it should be imputed to him in any way, and consulted the pope on the matter. The pope responds that in these matters one ought not to doubt beyond what is necessary, since human laws say that some people scorn to render even those things that are necessary, and others, of a thin religiosity, doubt where there is no cause for doubt. And therefore, because his brother sent a single horse to the cardinal without his knowledge, there is no reason whatsoever to entertain doubt, since the cardinal had few horses with him when traveling by sea.

Likewise, because it is the regular rule in other public crimes that a person becomes infamous only after a judicial sentence has been rendered \[Digest, *de his qui notantur*; in the rule *infamibus*, *de regulis iuris*, Book VI\]. Note this well. And yet through a condemnatory sentence a simoniac could also incur infamy of fact, which is easily imposed on the basis of public \[notoriety\]. I understand this text in the sense that the pope says that this man should not be stigmatized, “lest the ways of those who bark,” etc. — which words clearly indicate that they speak of infamy of fact.

And in the said gloss here he adds the following: since no dispensation is granted to a simoniac for any ecclesiastical benefice except by the pope. Hostiensis says that even through the bishop a dispensation can be granted for a simple benefice. \[Citing\] *Per. elec.* Dominus Antonius says that that chapter does not speak of a simoniac. Or one might say that the simony was committed without his knowledge, and so he was not a simoniac — even though the election or collation was simoniacal. And through this, Dominus Antonius appears tacitly to hold in favor of \[the one involved in\] the simony. And the same appears from the text in *c. non solum*, *de regulis iuris*, Book VI. \[See also\] *c.* on the Psalm, “they trembled where there was no fear” \[Psalm 13:5/52:6\]. And whether one ought to act against a scrupulous conscience before laying it aside, note in *c. per tuas*, *el.* II: that lawful oaths are as if \[releasing\] from sins. And purity of conscience ought not to be bereft of the judgment of reason; and one who acts from a thin, scrupulous conscience is not to be praised, but rather reproved. He ought therefore to form his conscience on the basis of the judgment of reason. To the same effect see the text in *c. non solum*, *de regulis iuris*, Book VI. And so the bishop can also dispense for a simple benefice. And certainly neither the opinion of Hostiensis nor that of Dominus Antonius holds, because a bishop can dispense with a simoniac regarding a benefice — not only for a simple benefice, but even for one with cure of souls, as is proved \[at I q.2, c. penultimate\]. \[The chapter\] *de electione* speaks of a dispensation to be made for that very benefice for which the simony was committed: for holding that same benefice, a dispensation is not so easily granted as it would be for holding a different one, lest it appear that the benefice was obtained through the previous vice — just as we say regarding the illegitimate son of a priest, who is sent to the church of his father to deliver the pallium to the archbishop: he cannot hold it by episcopal dispensation, lest a certain succession be noted in the church of God; but he can perfectly well hold another benefice even without dispensation. And on the question of whether and in what manner a dispensation may be granted to a simoniac regarding holy orders or a benefice, state the matter as noted by Palaeottus after the gloss in *c. post translationem*, *de renuntiatione*. And you may ask: how can anyone inferior to the pope dispense with a simoniac, since the simoniac has become infamous by law, as stated above, and a bishop cannot restore one’s reputation, as noted in *c. cum tu*, XII q.? Directly, he cannot restore an infamous person; but by dispensing in cases committed to him by law, the infamy is removed as though by a certain consequence. And for this I adduce the similar reasoning of Bonagratia in the said *l.* I, §. *de qua.*, Digest, *de portuum*. This is proved in *c. at si clerici*, §. *si hoc iudicio*. Understand it in the sense established there by Abbas. \[Cited by\] Vocatus. Keep this in mind.

### Whether gifts induce simony:

one must consider the person of the giver and the recipient, and the quality and quantity of the gift.

### An ordination or consecration is not deemed simoniacal

if something was given to the one conferring ordination, provided it was not given by agreement.

### Conscience ought to be consonant with the judgment of reason,

and one who acts from a thin, scrupulous conscience is not to be praised, but rather censured.

### Whether a donation is presumed from the words “take this money”:

It is proved if a witness who saw it says “I saw money counted out to him,” even if he does not say it was by way of a loan.
