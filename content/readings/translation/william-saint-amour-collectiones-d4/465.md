The temple is the soul of the believer; adorn it, clothe it, offer it gifts, receive Christ into it. What use is it that walls should gleam with jewels, while Christ in the poor is in peril of hunger? Likewise Hieronymus, *Ad Demetriadem*: Let others build churches, clothe walls with slabs of marble, haul in massive columns and gild their capitals \[though these feel nothing of the costly ornament\], set off the doors with ivory and silver, and distinguish their gilded altars with gems; I do not blame them, I do not refuse it; let each abound in his own opinion. It is better to do this than to brood over hoarded wealth. But yours is another calling: to clothe Christ in the poor, to visit Him in the sick, to feed Him in the hungry, to receive Him in those who lack shelter, and above all in the household of the faith.

Men of this sort, desirous of the ornament and beauty of their basilicas (in which men stand in admiration), and taking their delight therein, and supposing — or pretending to suppose — that this is religion and that it pleases God and the Saints (to whom the basilicas belong), and extorting the money for these works by various solicitations from usurers and plunderers (even from those who have made no restitution), appear to be like the idolaters who suppose that the ornament and beauty of their temples and idols is religion, and that these things please their gods. Concerning whom it is read in a certain marginal gloss on Wisdom 13: The more ornate and beautiful the images are, the more majesty they are thought to possess; religion, then, is whatever human cupidity marvels at. And a little earlier there is read, in another gloss on them: They delight in gold and gems, whose luster dazzles the eyes; without these they do not think there is any religion. And below: They consecrate to their gods spoils and plunder — gods who are very feeble. For why are they thought heavenly, if they desire earthly things? Or blessed, if they are in need? Or incorrupt, if they take delight in those things in which human cupidity is condemned?

If the objection be raised concerning the sumptuous work of the Lord’s Temple and its costly ornament under the Old Testament, as is read in 1 Kings 6 and 7, Hieronymus answers in this manner: Let no rich man in Judea cast up to me the Temple, the Table, the Lamps, the Censers, the Dishes, the Cups, the little Mortars, and the other things fashioned of gold; for these were then approved by the Lord, when the priests were immolating victims, and the blood of beasts was the remission of sins — although all these things went before as a figure, and were written for our sake, upon whom the ends of the ages have come. But now, since the poor Christ has consecrated the poverty of His house, let us bear the cross, and reckon riches as mire. 14 q. 2, C. *Gloria Episcopi*.
