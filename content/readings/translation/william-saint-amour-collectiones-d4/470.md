These superstitions they devise and perform in pretense, so that even in this way they may make prey of congregations not their own. Hence on that passage of Matthew 23, “Woe to you, Scribes and Pharisees, hypocrites, who devour widows’ houses,” etc., the Gloss says: “You who by your superstition seek nothing else than to make prey of the common people.” Now these men most of all do the aforesaid things to wealthy widows, who, since they are not subject to husbands but are deprived of a husband’s counsel, are easily deceived; and therefore they more readily bestow their goods on such seducers. Hence on the aforesaid word Chrysostomus says: “Widows are easily deceived, not having a husband as counselor; and they easily hand over their goods to such men, since they are subject to no one’s authority.” Likewise, in the same place, Chrysostomus: “With long prayers, as with great nets, you fish for the resources of widows.”

But perhaps the aforesaid Pseudo-apostles will say that they cannot be said to carry off the widows’ goods, since none of them charges them with violence or accuses them.

To this Augustinus sufficiently replies in *De verbo Domini*, in the sermon *De Militantibus*, saying thus: “Nor can we say, ‘No one charges us as invaders with violence, no one accuses us’ — as though flatteries did not sometimes greedily extort a greater booty than torments do; nor does it matter in God’s sight whether someone seizes another’s goods by force or by circumvention, provided that in some way he holds what belongs to another.”

Likewise, they cajolingly carry off the goods of Christ’s faithful, who under the pretext of suffrage for souls promise to Christ’s faithful, for a fixed sum of money, the anniversaries of their own Masses (which they sanctify above the Masses of others — that is, boast to be holier). By this they can in a special way be said to entangle themselves in secular affairs, contrary to the Apostle’s teaching in 2 Timothy 2, who says: “No one serving God as a soldier entangles himself in secular affairs.” For as the Gloss there says: “A secular affair is specifically so called if someone, as though making a market, seeks carnal things from spiritual.” Such promisers of Masses and prayers in exchange for the alms of others to be obtained may deservedly be called Merchants, or Traffickers in Prayers.
