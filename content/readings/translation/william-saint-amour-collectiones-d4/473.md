## Sign XLV

The forty-fifth sign can be that true apostles are content with the food and drink offered to them, and do not seek more sumptuous fare, according to that passage in Luke 10: “Eating and drinking such things as are with them.” Gloss: “He grants them their sustenance from preaching, yet in such a way that they be content with the food and drink offered.” Again, on that passage in Luke 12, “Do not seek what you shall eat,” etc., the Gloss says: “He does not say, DO NOT SEEK, OR BE ANXIOUS ABOUT FOOD, but more expressly says, WHAT YOU SHALL EAT OR WHAT YOU SHALL DRINK; by which those seem to be rebuked who, spurning common fare, seek more sumptuous nourishment for themselves.” Again, Hieronymus, Ad Nepotianum: “Let your meal be plain and eaten in the evening; and count vegetables, pulse, and sometimes a few small fish as the highest delicacies. He who desires Christ and feeds on that Bread does not greatly inquire from how costly foods he makes dung.” But false preachers who are hypocrites (especially those having the appearance of religion, like the Pharisees) pursue gluttony as the reward of their religion, as the Gloss says on that passage in Matthew 23: “They love the first couches at suppers.” Whence that saying of Jude in his Canonical Epistle may be applied to them: “These are spots in their feasts, feasting together without fear, feeding themselves.”

If therefore any preachers (concerning whose canonical calling or mission it is not established) are as a matter of course not content with the food and drink offered, but rather become vexed, or indignant, or in some way offended, and murmur if large fish, and the finest wines, and other more sumptuous foods are not served to them, they do not appear to be true apostles, but false ones. And of such men that saying of Psalm 58 may seem applicable to the letter: “They shall be scattered abroad to eat; and shall murmur if they be not filled.”

## Sign XLVI

The forty-sixth sign can be that true apostles do not run about from house to house seeking lodgings where they may be better provided for, but rather where they can conduct themselves more honorably; and they accept hospitality only from those whom they believe are not burdened by it, but rather consider it a favor shown to themselves if they are received by them — unless perhaps they are compelled to the contrary by the greatest necessity.
