therefore, if some Preacher should say that he desires to beg, this does not seem to be true humility but false, nor does he seem to be a true Preacher, but a Pretender and a Feigner. For many Pretenders say that poverty is sweet to them, or that it pleases them for God’s sake, who nevertheless have the fire of avarice or cupidity in their heart; concerning whom what Ecclesiasticus says in chapter 40 can be understood literally: “In the mouth of the Shameless, poverty shall be sweetened, and in his Belly”—that is, in his heart—”a fire shall burn,” namely, of cupidity.

Now it must be noted that those Preachers who ask from their Hearers, or cause to be asked, hoping that on account of their preaching they will more easily obtain from them what they ask, may rightly fear that they appear to do this from an occasion of avarice, contrary to what the Apostle says in 1 Thessalonians 2: “For neither have we at any time been in an occasion of avarice.” Gloss: “I do not say IN AVARICE, but I have done nothing and said nothing in which there could be any occasion of avarice.” Likewise, they may rightly fear that through this they may be said to sell the grace of preaching, and thus fall into the vice of Simony; just as Gehazi, the servant of Elisha, because he asked for garments from Naaman the Syrian, hoping that he would obtain them the more easily because his Lord (namely Elisha) had cured him of leprosy, as is read in 2 Kings 5 \[Vulg. 4 Reg. 5\], is said to have sold to that same Naaman the grace of his healing, and thereby to have fallen into the vice of Simony, as Gregorius Nazianzenus says at 1 q. 1 c. *Qui studet*, even though he asked for those garments against Elisha’s will, as appeared from what followed. Therefore, much more, if Elisha himself had asked in the same way and with the same intention, or had caused asking to be done, without doubt he would have incurred the vice of Simony.

If therefore any Preachers (concerning whose canonical calling or mission it is not established) customarily receive from their Hearers such gifts as have been described above, they appear to be in an occasion of avarice; they also appear to preach for the sake of gain, not for the sake of the salvation of souls. And if they customarily ask for such things, or cause them to be asked, whether in their preaching, or before preaching, or after it, hoping that on account of their preaching they will more easily obtain them, they appear to commit Simony; for to receive is, whenever one receives, whether after or before, as is read at 1 q. 1 c. *Eos*. And therefore they do not appear to be true Apostles, but rather may be held suspect, lest they be Pseudo \[apostles\].
