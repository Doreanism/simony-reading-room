## Sign XLI

The forty-first Sign can be this: that true Preachers, although they have the power by the Lord’s ordinance of living from the Gospel, as was shown above in dist. 1, §. *Item ut ostendit Augustinus*, and §. *Sed contra hoc forte*, nevertheless do not always demand those provisions owed to them, but rather work with their hands to support themselves, as was shown concerning Paul above in dist. 1, §. *Item forsan*, at the end, and §. *Hoc autem*, and §. *Sed adhuc*; and as was also shown concerning the other Apostles above in dist. 2, §. *Non solus autem*. Moreover, even when things are offered with entreaty, they do not always accept them, as is clear in the case of Paul, to whom, when the Macedonians wished to give their goods not only as their own but even as common, for his use and that of the other Apostles and other poor, even beyond the resources of their means, the Apostle Paul did not consent to receive them. Hence in 2 Corinthians 8 the same Apostle says: *I bear them witness, that according to their power, and beyond their power, they were willing, with much entreaty beseeching us for the grace and communication of the ministry that is done toward the saints.* The Gloss: *They were willing, with no one compelling them, to give even beyond the resources of their possessions, in such a way that they would afterwards be in need; and they entreated me, though I, fearing, did not consent to them, that they might be permitted to give their goods, not as their own, but as common, from which they might minister to others.* And if at times from necessity the true Apostles do receive the provisions owed to them, nevertheless they do not demand them unseasonably, nor do they receive them indifferently and indiscreetly from just anyone, lest they appear to preach the Gospel for the sake of their own maintenance, and so abuse that power. Hence on that passage in 1 Corinthians 9, *That I abuse not my power in the Gospel*, the Gloss says: *Which I would certainly do, if by demanding unseasonably, or by receiving indiscreetly, I should show the Gospel to be burdensome to believers.* For from unseasonable exaction, or from indiscreet reception of the provisions that are owed from the Gospel, scandal can easily arise; which in this matter ought to be more strictly avoided, as was shown above in dist. 1, §. *Verumtamen diuersimode*.
