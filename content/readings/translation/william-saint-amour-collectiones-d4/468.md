Whence on that verse of Luke 20, *Who devour the houses of widows, etc.*, the Gloss says: *Who, that they may seem more religious, pray at great length, so that from any who are infirm or troubled in conscience over their sins they may, as it were as Patrons, receive money and praise, whose prayer is turned into sin; for they can avail neither themselves nor others unto salvation; nay rather, for those very prayers, since by them they deceive others, they shall be the more grievously damned; for it is not the receiving hand that commends prayer to God, but the hand that gives.* Whence on Mark 12, on the aforesaid phrase, *Who devour the houses of widows,* the Gloss says: *As though they were to be Patrons at the coming Judgment, they do not hesitate to take money from the infirm and from those troubled in conscience over their sins; since it is the hand stretched out to give, not drawn back to receive, that commends prayer to God; to whom this word applies:* LET HIS PRAYER BE TURNED INTO SIN.

The aforesaid men also smoothly take away the goods of others by promising eternal salvation to wealthy invalids if they make their wills according to their counsel; in which wills they contrive to have themselves, or some of their own, appointed as Executors or as advisors to the Executors—as if, forsooth, they will dispense another’s money better and more faithfully, who, having (as they claim) left all things for Christ’s sake, are believed to have dispensed their own money in the best way. Against these Hieronymus speaks in his letter *To Nepotian*: *Take care that you do not, like some once faithful and famous Steward of your kinsmen, accept another’s money for distribution. Have the simplicity of the dove, that you may never contrive deceits.* And lest the testators, while yet living, should at anyone’s suggestion alter the wills made on the counsel of the aforesaid, or revoke or diminish the immoderate legacies bequeathed to them, they are ever at hand beside the sick until death, like vultures circling and watching for corpses; of whom Seneca says in Book 4 of *On Duties*: *I call him ungrateful who sits beside a sick man because a will is to be made—*
