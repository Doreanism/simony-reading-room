## CAP. XXXVIII.

The same.

“To consult —” (And further on:) “Whosoever shall desire to obtain the office of vicedominus or any other administration of ecclesiastical goods through payment of money, both those who buy and those who sell are struck down together with Simon Magus and are justly excluded from that church in whose dishonor they willed to give and receive money.”

## CAP. XXXIX.

The same, in a general council.

As we have learned for certain, in very many places and from very many persons — like those who sold doves in the temple — base and wicked exactions and extortions are made for the consecrations of bishops, the blessings of abbots, and the ordination of clerics; and it has been assessed how much is to be paid by this person or that, by one or another; and, to heap up a greater damnation, certain persons strive to defend this greed and wickedness by appeal to a custom long observed. Wishing therefore to abolish so great an abuse, we utterly condemn this custom, which is more properly to be called a corruption, firmly decreeing that for these things, whether being conferred or already conferred, no one shall presume under any pretext to exact or extort anything; otherwise, both he who has received and he who has given such a wholly condemned price shall be condemned together with Gehazi and Simon.

## CAP. XL.

The same, in the same.

Since the taint of simony has so infected most nuns that they scarcely receive any into their sisterhood without payment, wishing to conceal this vice under the pretext of poverty: lest this occur hereafter, we absolutely forbid it, decreeing that whoever henceforth shall have committed such wickedness — both those who receive and she who is received, whether she be a subject or a superior — shall be expelled from her monastery without hope of restoration, to be thrust into a place of stricter rule to perform perpetual penance. Concerning those who were received in this manner before this synodal decree, we have provided as follows: removed from the monasteries they wrongfully entered, they are to be placed in other houses of the same order. But if by chance, on account of their very great number, they cannot conveniently be placed elsewhere, lest they wander damnably in the world, they shall be received anew by dispensation in those same monasteries, their former places changed and inferior ones assigned. We decree that this is to be observed likewise with regard to monks and other religious. But lest they be able to excuse themselves through simplicity or ignorance, we command that the diocesan bishops cause this to be published each year throughout their dioceses.
