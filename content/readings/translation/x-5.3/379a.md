## CAP. XXII.

### One ordained simoniacally, who is not himself a simoniac, may receive a dispensation.

Lucius III.

Concerning those ordained through simony, we cannot give you a definitive answer in response to your inquiry without fuller knowledge of how they were ordained, since some persons — though ordained simoniacally in a certain species of simony, as when this occurs without their knowledge and against their will — may nonetheless remain in their orders, because they are not themselves simoniacs.

## CAP. XXIII.

### If money is given, with the knowledge and consent of the elect, to one who opposes the election, so that he withdraws his opposition, and the elect is afterward confirmed, he is a simoniac and is bound to renounce the benefice.

Idem.

Cardinal Matthaeus disclosed to us by private communication that, when you had been elected to the governance of your church by the will of the greater part of the brethren, you were unable to enjoy due peace on account of a few who opposed you; whereupon a certain friend of yours, with your knowledge and consent — troubled though it was — paid a sum of money to the one who appeared to be the ringleader of the discord, and so the opposition you had been enduring fell quiet. Now you are uncertain whether you are fully free from the taint of simony so long as you remain in your administration. Since you sought the counsel of the Apostolic See through the said cardinal as to what you should do, we answer that you would do yourself great good if you promptly and willingly relinquish the said administration, keeping in mind that word of the Gospel: *It profits a man nothing if he gains the whole world but suffers the loss of his own soul.*

## CAP. XXIV.

### No price may be demanded for absolving an excommunicate.

Idem.

Know that it has come to our ears that when C. de Senevilla had been bound by the bond of excommunication on account of a debt he owed, he gave satisfaction to his creditors, but, when those who had excommunicated him demanded ten pounds for his absolution, he declined to obtain the benefit of absolution. Since it is unworthy and contrary to ecclesiastical reason that the benefit of absolution should be purchased, we charge your discretion by apostolic writ that, if the aforesaid C. has paid the debt for which he had been cited by sentence \[Latin *nominatus*, named or cited in the sentence of excommunication\], you cause him to be absolved by apostolic authority, freely and notwithstanding any appeal, should those who excommunicated him fail to do so when required.

## CAP. XXV.

### Monks received through simony, if they are simoniacs, are transferred to stricter monasteries; otherwise, after renunciation, they may be placed in the same or in other monasteries of the same order.

Clemens III.

You have wished to consult us concerning regular canons or monks who obtained entry through simony with their own knowledge and connivance. Since many express authorities are found on this point, we answer no differently than what has been decreed: that they shall entirely quit the place they obtained by such means, and betake themselves to hermitages or to other monasteries
