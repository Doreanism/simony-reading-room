## CAP. XLI.

The same, in the same.

We have heard that certain bishops, upon the death of rectors of churches, place those churches under interdict and refuse to permit others to be instituted in them until a fixed sum of money has been paid to themselves. Moreover, when a soldier or a cleric enters a religious house or chooses burial among the religious — even if he has left nothing to the religious house — they introduce difficulties and malicious obstructions until some gift comes into their hands. Since, therefore, according to the Apostle, one must abstain not only from evil but from every appearance of evil, we wholly forbid exactions of this kind. And if anyone shall have been a transgressor, he shall restore double what was exacted, to be faithfully converted to the benefit of those places at whose expense it was paid.

## CAP. XLII.

The same, in the same.

It has repeatedly come to our apostolic hearing that certain clerics exact and extort money for the funeral rites of the dead, for the blessings of those who marry, and for similar offices; and if their greed has not been satisfied, they fraudulently interpose fictitious impediments. On the other hand, certain laymen, out of the leaven of heretical wickedness, strive to undermine under the pretext of canonical piety a laudable custom introduced into holy church by the devout practice of the faithful. Therefore, we forbid wicked exactions in these matters and command that pious customs be observed, decreeing that ecclesiastical sacraments be freely conferred; but let those who maliciously strive to alter a laudable custom be restrained by the bishop of the place once the truth is known.

## CAP. XLIII.

### Honorius III to all Abbots and Brothers of the Cistercian Order.

Lest the church of God, which Jesus Christ washed in his own blood, be defiled by the stain of filthiness or marred by the wrinkle of duplicity, the sordidness of greed — root of all evils — and the deformity of venality, like a wrinkle contracted under the pretext of custom, must be entirely purged from that same church, so that the dispensation of the mysteries of God and those mysteries themselves, freely received, all the wickedness of Gehazi being utterly abolished, may be conferred by gratuitous charity; how detestable and how dangerous it is to buy or sell these things can be plainly reckoned from the fact that the Lord cast out of the temple those selling and buying sheep and oxen, and overturned the seats of those selling and buying doves. In the casting out of the sellers and buyers, the detestation of the crime defiling both parties is expressed; and in the overturning of the seats it is shown to what punishment those are subject who preside over others while selling the gifts of God and ecclesiastical sacraments. Lest this crime, which makes equal those whom it defiles — prelates and subjects alike — be able to infect them in any way, we by apostolic authority most strictly forbid that any archbishop or bishop, archdeacon or their official, presume to exact or extort from anyone elected to an abbacy — for the blessing, installation, or receiving of profession from them — under the pretext of any custom whatsoever, a palfrey or anything else, by whatever name it may be called. We also forbid you under this same pretext to furnish what is so exacted, since one must abstain not only from evil but from every appearance of evil.
