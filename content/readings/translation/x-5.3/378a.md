Hence the Psalmist says: “They trembled with fear where there was no fear.” In the matter you raise, then: when our beloved [son] P., cardinal priest of the title of Saint Lawrence in Damaso — at that time deacon of Saint Eustace and legate of the apostolic see — came to those regions for your election and to confer the pallium upon you, and when our beloved son Albanus, your brother, knowing the cardinal’s need, sent him one horse without your knowledge whatsoever, since the cardinal, having come by sea, had brought no mounts or very few with him — in this matter, you have no cause for fear at all. For in the giving and receiving of gifts, three things must be weighed above all: the quality of the persons giving and receiving, the value of the gift, and the time of the giving. As to the quality of persons — from whom and to whom — we should carefully consider whether the gift was made from a poor man to a rich, or conversely, or from one wealthy person to another. As to the value of the gift and the time of giving, we should examine more closely whether what was given was of great or small worth, and whether it was offered in a moment of pressing necessity or at some other time. When these matters are examined with diligence, we find nothing in the case you have put to us that deserves reproach, as will become more plainly evident to your solicitude in what follows.

If we rightly weigh the character and standing of the said cardinal and your brother, it was no great thing for that same brother to send the cardinal one horse — a gift that so eminent and so generous a man might well have bestowed even on a jongleur who had not asked for it. And if we consider the necessity of the moment, it is clear that this was done for no other purpose than to assist the cardinal in the situation just described. Therefore, no scruple of doubt ought ever to arise in your conscience on this matter, since the thing given was of modest value in relation to the persons of giver and receiver, and neither of them had any intention that could or should cause you the least injury — even if the horse was sent and given under the press of necessity before the examination of the election and the reception of the pallium, and done without your will or knowledge whatsoever; nor is the thing of such great price as should much move the recipient’s mind or lead him from honorable purpose to something else.

As for the Scripture text, “Blessed is he who shakes his hands free of all bribes,” this was said of those gifts that are wont to attract or corrupt the mind of the recipient. For even if the person of the one elected were to offer his ordainer or consecrator an electuary, medicines, fine wine, or similar things of small value — things that ought not to incline or move the recipient’s will — the Roman Church has not been accustomed to interpret the receiver as sinning in such matters, nor the giver either, [unless these things took place by agreement, etc.]

## CAP. XIX.

An abbot and senior members of a community who have received money by agreement for the admission of a monk are suspended from office and benefice; the money so received is restored; and the monk thus admitted is transferred to another monastery.

The Same.
