And since simony appeared to have been proved in many ways [against the same abbot] through the monks who had sworn to bear witness to the truth, he raised many exceptions against the witnesses — namely conspiracies and mortal enmities, theft and adultery — in order to remove them from testifying. We, however, lest the purity of innocence be confounded and succumb, or the wickedness of simony escape unpunished, admitted for proof only those exceptions by which, if perhaps proved, it would be established that the witnesses had proceeded not from [the] zeal for justice but from a seedbed of malice, such as conspiracies and mortal enmities. The remaining exceptions [lodged,] such as theft and adultery, we determined to reject on account of the enormity of the heresy of simony, in comparison with which other crimes are reckoned as almost nothing, since even if proved they would weaken the credibility of the witnesses in some degree, they would not wholly destroy it when other corroborating supports were present — especially since the witnesses had [already] been reformed from their crime. Witnesses of such a kind against such a person we therefore determined to admit in such a manner: not according to the strict rule of law, but according to the moderation of equity, since the proceeding was not criminal, so as to depose him from his order, but civil, so as to remove him from his administration as unworthy and harmful. And indeed such prelates can be removed from their administrations for lesser causes, especially by the Supreme Pontiff, who has power not only to judge but also to dispose, as the approved custom of certain religious communities requires. Wherefore the aforesaid abbot, not awaiting sentence, withdrew — as one guilty in his own conscience.

## CAP. XXXIII.

No fault is imputed to an elect who has been confirmed, if money was given for his confirmation against his prohibition.

Idem. To the Abbot of Gembloux.

As you have informed us through your letters: having been reared from a tender age in the church of Gembloux and made a monk there, in mature years you were at length promoted to the governance of the church of Florennes; but, after the abbot of Gembloux had subsequently been removed from the scene, that very community which had previously held you as a son called you, without your knowledge and in your absence, as father and shepherd through canonical election. And because the cathedral church of Liège was without a pastor, after you had remained in the church of Gembloux for some days, you set out to the church of Cologne, which is your metropolitan see, and under its authority administered the church of Gembloux in the meantime. Moreover, after a bishop had been installed in the church of Liège, he, when requested by you, refused to confirm the election made of you, pleading nothing other than that you had been translated from a lesser place to a greater. But when you learned that this was being done to extort money from you, you forbade under threat of anathema that any money be offered on account of the matter. Yet certain of the brethren, acting without your knowledge and against the sentence of excommunication you had issued, promised money and also paid it, as was subsequently reported to you; and so, invited by the bishop, you freely received institution from him, as you had sought. Your prudence has therefore led you to consult us on these matters: whether, on account of a promise made without your knowledge and expressly prohibited, as aforesaid, a stain of sin is incurred by you; or whether, regarding the promise now known to you, you together with the brethren who made it ought to do penance, since you had been unwilling to adhere to simoniacal wickedness and were prepared to bear labor for the Lord’s flock; or whether, even though your conscience does not accuse you, you ought to abstain from the governance you have assumed, if we so decree.
