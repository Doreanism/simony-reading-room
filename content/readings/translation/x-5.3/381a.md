And because simony appeared to have been proved against the same abbot by witnesses in many ways, he raised many exceptions against the witnesses, over which there was extensive argument on both sides: some asserting that in the crime of simony, just as [also] in the crime of lese-majesty, all persons indiscriminately — the infamous as well as the criminal — are to be admitted not only to accuse but also to testify, since the accusation of simony proceeds after the manner of a public crime and lese-majesty, with many laws and canons cited in support; others responding to the contrary that, although these two crimes are regarded as nearly equivalent in respect of accusation, they differ in many respects, since different penalties attach to each, and a distinction must be drawn between the persons of accusers and of witnesses, since crimes are proved not through accusers but through witnesses, with no fewer reasons and arguments adduced on this side. Lest either the purity of innocence be confounded and succumb, or the wickedness of simony escape unpunished, we weighed the equities and determined neither to admit all the exceptions lodged against the witnesses nor to reject them all, but admitted for proof only those lodged exceptions which, if perhaps proved, would seem to proceed not from zeal for justice but from a seedbed of malice — namely, conspiracies and mortal enmities — while the remaining objections lodged, such as theft and adultery, we determined to reject on account of the enormity of the heresy of simony, in comparison with which all crimes are reckoned as almost nothing, since even if they would somewhat weaken the credibility of the witnesses, they would not wholly destroy it, especially when other corroborating supports happen to be present. [Given at the Lateran, 4 Nones of December, 1199.]

## CAP. XXXII.

This chapter is a gloss on the preceding chapter. For it tends toward the clarification of that chapter — whether and when criminal witnesses are admitted in the crime of simony — and in effect says the same as the preceding.

Idem.

You have informed us through your letters that you are greatly in doubt about a certain decretal letter which you assert we issued concerning witnesses to be admitted against simoniacal wickedness. Know therefore without hesitation that we never issued that letter which begins “Quamvis ad abolendam,” but acknowledge that we issued another, which begins “Licet Heli.” For the understanding of that letter, we believe a distinction must be drawn: whether the one against whom action is brought for simoniacal wickedness is merely denounced, or is accused criminally; and whether the proceeding follows the strict rule of law or the moderation of equity. Likewise, whether he is a regular who has already renounced the world, or a secular who still lives in the world; and whether he is of inferior rank or of more elevated dignity. Moreover, whether he was previously of clear reputation and good fame, or was gravely defamed and highly suspect; and whether he can easily be punished or cannot be condemned without scandal. Furthermore, whether the witnesses are of good character or are criminal, and whether they have already been reformed from their crime or still persist in it. Again, whether the crimes [of the witnesses] are the same as or lesser, or equal to or greater [than the crime charged]; and whether the witnesses are believed to have testified from zeal for justice or from a seedbed of malice. Finally, whether, to expose the simoniacal wickedness, the statements of the witnesses alone are adduced, or other corroborating supports are also present. All these distinctions must be made for the understanding of that letter, as a diligent investigator can discern from the letter’s own sequence of argument.
