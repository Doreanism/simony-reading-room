## CAP. XXXI.

### An abbot may raise the exception, against witnesses who have deposed against him in a simoniacal matter in an inquisition proceeding, that they are enemies and conspirators; but he may not raise exceptions of other crimes against them.

Idem. To the Prior of Saint Victor and to Masters I. of Bologna and L. of Modena, Canons.

Although the high priest Eli was in himself a good man, yet because he did not effectively rebuke the excesses of his sons, he received the stroke of divine punishment upon himself equally as upon them — for while his sons were slain in battle, he himself fell backward from his seat and died of a broken neck. A prelate ought therefore all the more diligently to bestir himself to correct the excesses of those subject to him, the more damnable it would be to leave their offenses uncorrected. Against subjects, to say nothing of notorious excesses, although proceedings can be taken in three ways — namely by accusation, by denunciation, and by inquisition — yet so that due care may be exercised in all matters: just as a legitimate accusation must be preceded by a formal indictment \[inscriptio\], so a denunciation must be preceded by charitable correction, and an inquisition must be preceded by widespread public outcry. “I will go down,” said the Lord, “and see whether they have acted in full accordance with the outcry that has reached me” \[Genesis 18:21\]. For the outcry reaches the prelate when, through public report or frequent complaint, the excesses of his subjects are brought to his attention; and then he ought to go down and see — that is, to send and inquire — whether truth accompanies the outcry that has come to him. For according to the canonical sanctions, if anything comes to the ears of a prelate concerning any cleric that might justifiably offend him, he ought not to believe it easily, nor should the matter, while still uninvestigated, inflame him to punishment; rather, the truth must be carefully sought out before the elders of the church, so that, if the nature of the matter demands it, canonical judgment may strike the fault of the offender — not as though he were at once accuser and judge, but as though, with rumor or a clamorous denunciation serving as the bearer, he is discharging the duty of his office; with this moderation always observed, that the form of the sentence corresponds to the form of the judicial proceeding. When, therefore, things had been frequently reported to us concerning the abbot of Pomposa that differed far too greatly from regular uprightness, and monks had come into our presence, certain of them laid against him before us charges of simony, perjury, dissipation, and incompetence. When the same abbot raised the exception against them that the fraternal correction required by the evangelical rule had not preceded this denunciation, and they persistently asserted that they had indeed employed such prior correction beforehand — although in support of this two monks’ oaths had been produced — yet because they had not yet ceased to dispute the matter: we, as we have said, moved by the repeated outcries, chose by virtue of our office to inquire into the foregoing charges, binding all the monks without exception — those who had come both for him and against the abbot — by oath to set forth in full the truth they knew concerning the charges laid.
