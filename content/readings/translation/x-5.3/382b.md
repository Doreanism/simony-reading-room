## CAP. XXXVI.

Nothing ought to be exacted for the chrism or for the investiture of churches, nor does custom or a change of name excuse it.

### The Same to the Archbishop of Canterbury.

Our sins demanding it, simoniacal corruption has so depraved the hearts of certain persons — to the ruin of canonical sanction and, in some manner, to the mockery of divine judgment — that, lusting after base gain and saying in their hearts “there is no God” \[Ps. 14:1\], they cloak simony under an honorable name, as though by changing the name the guilt and penalty are transferred. Yet God, as the Apostle says, is not mocked \[Gal. 6:7\]; nor will such followers of Simon \[Magus\] escape eternal punishment in the future, even if they perhaps evade temporal punishment in the present, since neither the respectability of the name will cover the malice of the crime, nor will a word be able to abolish the guilt. It has come to our attention that, whereas certain of your suffragans were formerly accustomed to receive, contrary to canonical sanctions, a fixed sum of money for the chrism, they now — not fearing canonical punishment and wishing to evade your correction — anticipate the time of payment, receiving in mid-Lent what they were accustomed to receive after Easter; and, in order to disguise their reason for receiving it, they vary the name of the coins, calling those which they formerly named “chrism-pennies” and then “Easter-pennies” by the name of “mid-Lent custom.” Some, however, have varied neither the time of payment nor the name of what is paid in any way, imitating the old custom in all respects. Now, since one who first receives the price before conferring the precious thing expresses more explicitly the appearance of a sale than one who has deferred and continued to defer the time of receiving the payment by dissembling — though both practices are dangerous in such matters, and if grace is to be conferred as grace, lest one deserve to lose both the thing and the name of grace — we command your fraternity by apostolic letter and strictly charge you that, relying on our authority and your own, you so correct the aforesaid transgressions, restraining your suffragans and their officials by canonical admonition followed by ecclesiastical censure, with the obstacle of appeal removed, from such unlawful exaction, that no complaint about this matter may hereafter be brought to us, and that the fault of others may not be imputed to you as punishment on account of your negligence. Supported by the same authority, you should also endeavor to abolish that corrupt custom in your province by which archdeacons demand a mark of silver, and lesser deans a white cow, or a fixed sum of money, for the investiture of churches. [Given at the Lateran, 26th of June, 1199.]

## CAP. XXXVII.

One ordained a subdeacon who previously promised his presenter that he would seek nothing from him cannot minister in the order received or ascend to higher orders without a dispensation, even if he did not believe he had sinned.

### The Same.

You have informed us through your letters that, when D., the bearer of these presents, wished to be ordained a subdeacon and did not have a definite church title, he entreated a certain priest to present him to the title of his church. When that priest refused to do so, D. firmly promised him that if he would present him he would never seek any portion in his church; and so he was ordained upon his presentation, nor, as he asserts, did he understand that he had done anything unlawful in this.
