## CAP. XXIX.

### Nothing is to be exacted for the blessings of those being married or the funerals of the dead.

Innocentius III. To the Abbot of Lieu-Dieu and the Prior of Silla.

The beloved sons, the parishioners of Villafranca, have sent us their complaint that Damasius, their chaplain, unlawfully demands and extorts money from them for the funerals of the dead and for the blessings of those being married; and that if his cupidity should happen not to be satisfied, he fraudulently raises fictitious impediments against them so that the bodies of the dead cannot be buried or the blessing of those being married celebrated. Since exactions of this kind conflict with the ordinances of the sacred canons, we charge your discretion by apostolic writ that you admonish the said chaplain more earnestly and effectively to desist from such presumption and likewise to make fitting satisfaction for his excess, inflicting upon him, if he refuses to obey your admonitions, the canonical penalty without appeal. [Given etc., 2 Kalends of June, 1198.]

## CAP. XXX.

### If it is established by accusation that a monk was received simoniacally, both the one received and the one who received him are deposed. But if it is established by inquisition, the one received is sent to a stricter monastery; upon the one who received him, however, and upon the superiors, a fitting penalty is imposed, and in the meantime they are suspended from the exercise of sacred orders.

Idem. To the Archbishop of Canterbury.

The beloved son Master A., your envoy, set forth on your behalf that, when you were visiting the diocese of Canterbury according to the custom of your predecessors — in order to correct what needs correcting and to establish what you should determine in accordance with God’s will — in the monasteries, in houses of regular canons, and in religious establishments you found simoniacal wickedness proliferating, so that many had been received there for payment who ought rather to have been received without charge, and indeed invited to the observance of religious life. You therefore wonder whether, since the number involved is found to be great, severity ought in any measure to be relaxed, or whether you ought to exercise upon such persons the rigor of canonical discipline. To your inquiry, then, we respond as follows: that if a canonical accusation has been formally brought before you against those who shall have been tainted with this blemish, then after the crime has been established by judicial process, you are to execute the punishment of canonical severity upon both the givers and the receivers. But if the matter has come to your knowledge by inquisition alone, those who have been received in such places through simoniacal wickedness are to be removed from them and sent to stricter monasteries to do penance.
