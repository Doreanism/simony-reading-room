## CAP. XX.

If a candidate for ordination gave something to his presenter by agreement, it is simony; he is not, however, deposed if the matter is hidden.

The Same.

From the letter of your brotherhood and from the confession of the priest who bears this letter, we have learned that, when his archdeacon refused to present him for the order of priesthood on any other terms, he paid that archdeacon six shillings and was thereafter presented by him and received the order. On the question you put to us for our counsel, our response is as follows: if the matter is not public but secret, you should confer with the same priest privately, urging and inducing him — yet without any compulsion — to embrace a religious order and to abstain in perpetuity from the office he acquired so illicitly. If, however, you cannot succeed in inducing him to enter a religious order, endeavor to assign him some ecclesiastical benefice sufficient for his maintenance, yet on the condition that he must abstain from the priesthood in perpetuity. Otherwise it would not be safe, since his transgression is secret, to coerce him against his will from that office.

Nevertheless, do not fail to impose upon him a secret penance, as may seem fit to you.

## CAP. XXI.

This canon is brief and cannot be summarized more briefly.

The Same.

Let it be known that there has come to our hearing† — if it is true — a matter that greatly astonishes us and justly moves us against you: that in the churches subject to your jurisdiction you allow vicars to minister on no account unless money is extorted from them, and you presume to burden the incumbents of churches, as though they were serfs and tributaries, with undue exactions year after year, extorting from them money and procuration under various names — sometimes calling it the aid of the bishop, at other times the customs of the bishop and of yourselves. And if they happen to refuse to pay these amounts, you suspend the said incumbents and vicars from office and forbid divine services to be celebrated in their churches; and among other abuses you presume to extort from them at your pleasure the chrism money, to the peril of your souls, describing this exaction — in order that you may appear to levy it more freely — sometimes as the episcopal custom, sometimes as the synodal custom, sometimes as the paschal dues. (And below:) Since it is unbecoming for you to treat clerics so basely and dishonorably, we command your discretion by apostolic letter that you by no means exact money from them for the exercise of ecclesiastical ministry, for the assignment of vicars, or for the chrism; and that you presume not henceforth to burden them with undue exactions or procurations, nor to subject their persons or their churches to interdict without manifest and reasonable cause; otherwise, if a repeated complaint on this matter should reach us, we will punish you severely.
