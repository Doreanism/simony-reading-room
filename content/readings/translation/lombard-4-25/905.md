For Innocentius says that “the clerics of the Arians are not to be received with the dignity of the priesthood or any ministry; to them alone is baptism held valid, which is received in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit.” He also says that they “cannot give the Holy Spirit, whom they have lost”; and that “those ordained by heretics have their head wounded; and he who has lost his honor cannot confer honor, nor has the recipient received anything, since there was nothing in the giver that he could receive.” He further holds that “those coming from heretics are to be granted only lay communion by the imposition of hands, and that none of them is to be appointed to the honor of the clerical state, however minor.”

Gregorius also says that the consecration of the Arians is sacrilegious, when communion is received from their hand.

Cyprianus likewise says that all things whatsoever heretics do are carnal, empty, and sacrilegious, and that their “altars are false and unlawful, their priesthoods and sacrifices sacrilegious” — men who, “after the manner of apes, which, though they be men, are not men, and imitate human form, claim for themselves the appearance of the Catholic Church and its authority, while they themselves are not within the Church”; and though they are sacrilegious, they exercise a priesthood and set up an altar, although no oblation can be sacrificed there, nor is the Holy Spirit present, nor does the Lord profit anyone through the prayers and petitions of one who has himself violated God.

Hieronymus also affirms that “all things offered by heretics are defiled in the sight of the Lord; for though they may appear holy by their outward form, yet all things that have been touched by one who is polluted are themselves polluted.”
