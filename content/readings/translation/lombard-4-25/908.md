Some resolve these difficulties in the following way. They say that heretics who have received priestly or episcopal anointing and then departed from the Church retain the right to confer baptism, but do not have the capacity to confer holy orders or to consecrate the body of the Lord after they have been cut off and condemned by the Church — just as a bishop who has been degraded does not have the power to bestow holy orders, yet has not lost the ability to baptize.

What Augustinus says, they understand as referring to heretics who have been divided from the truth of the faith and the unity of doctrine not by sentence of the Church but by the depravity of their own opinion. Such persons, even though they are what they are, nevertheless retain the right to consecrate and to ordain. And those who are ordained by them before their explicit excommunication — even if those ordained openly departed with them and were condemned by sentence of the Church — need not be re-ordained upon their return. And wherever one reads that those ordained by heretics may minister with their orders preserved, or need not be re-ordained, these authorities, they say, are to be understood of persons in this category. For after heretics have been cut off and condemned by the judgment of the Church, they assert that the right to ordain and to consecrate has been taken from them, as from those who have been degraded or excommunicated.

Others, however, say that sacraments celebrated by heretics and excommunicates according to the form of the Church are true and valid, because those who depart from the Church have not lost the right to give and to consecrate; and those thus ordained by heretics need not be re-ordained when they return. But what heretics do otherwise than the Church does is false and empty; and those who appear to be ordained by such persons receive not a gift but a wound. It is according to this distinction that the doctors speak variously on these matters.

Some, however, say that the same sacraments that Catholics celebrate can be celebrated by heretics who have been cut off, provided those heretics observe the form of the Church; and that what is celebrated by them is true and valid in itself, but false and empty for those who handle them wickedly and for those who receive them wickedly — void and false, that is, because they do not bestow what they promise and are believed to confer; to be condemned as well, because they serve as judgment upon those who give or receive them illicitly; and called polluted, not in themselves, but on account of the unworthy
