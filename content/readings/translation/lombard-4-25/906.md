The same \[Hieronymus\]: “God abhors the sacrifices of heretics and casts them away from himself; and as often as they gather in his name, he detests their stench and stops his nostrils.”

The same: “They offer sacrilegious bread,” etc.

Leo attests that “outside the Church, priesthoods are not valid, nor are sacrifices true.”

The same says further: “Through the most savage madness, in the Alexandrian see the light of all the Sacraments has been extinguished: the offering of sacrifice has been cut off, the consecration of chrism has ceased, and all the mysteries have withdrawn themselves from the parricidal hands of the impious.”

From these and other testimonies it seems to be established that the ecclesiastical Sacraments — especially those of the body and blood \[of Christ\], of ordination, and of confirmation — cannot be administered by heretics.

On the other side, others seem to hold that sacred orders, like baptism, can be conferred even by heretics who have been cut off; and that those who return from heretics, having been ordained and baptized there, need not be ordained again, just as they need not be baptized again.

Hence Augustinus: “Concerning the view some hold about heretics who have been condemned — that ‘one who departs from the Church does not lose the baptism he received, but does lose the right to confer it that he received’ — this is shown in many ways to be an empty claim. First, because no reason is given why one who cannot lose that baptism should be able to lose the right to confer it. For both are Sacraments, and both are given to a person through a certain consecration: the one when he is baptized, the other when he is ordained. Therefore it is not permitted in the Catholic Church to repeat either of them. For if at any time those coming from that party, even leaders among them, were received for the sake of peace, with the error of schism corrected, and if it seemed needful that they should exercise the same offices they had held, they were not ordained again; but just as baptism remained in them, so ordination remained whole,
