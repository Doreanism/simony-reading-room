Solution: In giving, attend to the person of the petitioner — how much he needs, what he asks for, where, why, and from whom. Follow your conscience in this way; and note that you are not bound to give to this one rather than that one if you are bound to give to another, nor does the contrary hold — and this argument is not valid: “this man is dying of hunger and you are not helping him when you could, and so you are killing him.” For it should rather be said: “you are bound to help this man” — which is false, whenever the discerning Lord follows \[another path\].

And this is false: “that one is bound to help this particular other man \[when pointed out\].” If he passes by both of them. I say that because he passes by both without helping either, the death of neither of them can be imputed to him, for he has passed them both by. And the death of this one is imputed to you \[only\] if you were bound to help him lest he die, and were also bound to do so to some degree. But not before their deaths. We know you are in a military manner bound to do this, and you are bound to feed this one, and to share with him, and you are bound to feed both of them. If you cannot, do what you can in this case, and you are bound only to the possible. Solution: This argument is not valid — “the death of this one is imputed to you, and you were bound to help him lest he die” — because you were not bound to this except under such a condition, namely that you not be providing for another elsewhere, or if another were to provide for him, since in providing for him you release the other from that obligation. Consider the analogy of a legate sent by the lord Pope in this form: “I command you to go to this region or to that one.” Suppose he goes to neither. He is at fault for not going on this legation, and he was bound to go on it — but it does not follow \[that he was bound to go on both\]. The first is indeed true, because since he went to neither, he is at fault for failing both legations. This case is like that of a man who, with many others, sets fire to a city, and this particular man paid nothing at the city gates in the quarter, yet he is held liable in full — it follows. For he is not held liable on condition that the others also pay.
