And this conditional vow: “I vow that if you give me a hundred gold coins, I will go to Rome.” Consider \[the case\]: if it is my intention to go under the condition that you give me a hundred, I will go if the condition is fulfilled; if, with the condition fulfilled, I am released \[from other obligations\], then by the same reasoning I am also released from the vow if the condition is fulfilled. There are those who say that a conditional vow is never piously made unless the condition is fulfilled in exactly the form in which it was proposed. Consider \[the counterargument\]: if you gave ninety-nine gold coins rather than a hundred, he is still bound by the vow. This is most easily proved in the case of the man who vowed concerning his son gone on pilgrimage: if the father’s prayer is answered he is bound by the vow, and if it is not answered, he is still bound — because whether what he asked was granted or not, whether by direct gift or by some indirect means, he is held to it. And we grant this. A distinction must be drawn as follows: a conditional vow made toward God is made in God, and such a vow must always be fulfilled. But a vow that is made, as it were, both toward God and toward man — such as the one just mentioned about the person vowing to go to Rome if he is given a hundred — is a kind of contract.

Furthermore, and on the contrary: once that condition is dissolved, what was thus noted or agreed or contracted is dissolved. But the case is different for a vow made toward God, as we have said. Furthermore, if someone vows thus:

## iii.

“I vow to become a white monk, if I am to be among monks” — from this vow he is bound either to something specific or to nothing. If to something, what? Only to this: that he enter this or that monastery. For he is bound to enter either this one or that one, and \[the vow\] refers to that one or this one. If from this vow he is bound to nothing at all, then it does not bind him to anything and is not a vow. Either he vows something conditionally and is bound to it, or he vows something. Resolution: the \[papal\] curia judges differently: one who vows prudently is immediately bound from the making of the vow. Therefore, you who vowed last are always a transgressor of your vow, until you enter this or that cloister.
