We say that one who vows in this way is bound to something, yet is bound to nothing in particular, but is bound to this or that — that is, to entering one or the other. Furthermore, the aforementioned person who makes the vow \[in a confused or uncertain ablative form\] is likewise bound to neither one. Furthermore, consider that two persons vow thus: “I vow to build a church in that territory unless you build one there” — and the other says the same. Of these two, which one is bound to something by such a vow? If so, to what? To nothing except to building a church there. And how would the transgressor of this vow sin mortally if neither of them was thus obligated, and neither sins if he does not build a church there? Resolution: such a conditional vow binds both under the condition to build a church there — not absolutely, but conditionally. Therefore, each ought to inquire diligently whether the other intends to build a church there; and if not, he himself must build it; otherwise he sins. Furthermore, it often happens with companions that one vows thus:

## iiii.

“I vow to enter a cloister if you enter with me” — and suppose that both vow thus in the same way, and the consequence follows: nothing. Both of these persons are bound to something from this — to what? Only to this: that they enter the cloister, which each is bound to enter. Consider \[the objection\]: If neither were to enter sincerely, the condition of neither vow has been fulfilled as a good act, and neither is bound to it; add simply that

he deserves \[the outcome\]. Resolution: the following distinction must be drawn: one who vows prudently in zeal for God and in holy charity for his companions should enter the cloister — but one who vows this led by fear or by love of his companions rather than by love of God and of neighbor, if his heart were always intent on \[the cloister\], is bound by the vow to enter; but if he vowed out of fear for love of his companions, he would follow \[them\] only if his companion entered.
