The Lord gives all that is needed, laughing at the unworthy ministers of the church who in place of tribute \[merely ask to\] give \[what is due\], so that it may be returned according to \[the teaching of\] Audegilda. “Surely the merciful one ought to have shown favor?” By these and similar arguments, certain ones cannot in any way obtain that which has been established as pure in the law of the Lord. We agree with and grant \[the conclusion\] that all tithes of all things are to be paid, as the authorities and arguments declare. But they object: “What of the poor wretch who is so destitute of means that what he acquires is of no worth or vigor, and must he pay tithes on his daily earnings? If he earns

only an obol a day — if he must offer a part to God, not even a tenth of the year’s estimated price is owed to God.” Far more strongly and rightly are those who have resources from incomes bound to the payment of tithes, because \[even\] the Levites paid a tithe of tithes to the high priest. In the New Testament, and since the precept concerning tithes is moral, the same holds in the New Testament. Solution: we say that it is one thing if \[he labors\] from his own work and what is properly his, and another for a cleric who cannot \[stand as\] a possessor \[of goods\] in the proper sense — being, in accordance with the Apostle’s saying, “the stewardship entrusted to us,” and according to Hieronymus who says, xii, q. i, last chapter: “Whatever clerics have belongs to the poor.” Therefore all their goods ought to be held in common. Hence a cleric may receive from ecclesiastical goods, as steward and servant, what suffices, and give to his household a fitting sustenance in food and drink.
