For the Lord, attending to the stiff-necked Jews who were previously prone to idolatry, forbade swearing by heaven, earth, or any creature, lest they render to a creature the worship \[latria\] owed to God the Creator alone. Furthermore, he \[saw that some were\] too weak to believe on a simple word, and therefore, as a remedy for human frailty, the Lord commanded that in cases of necessity an oath should be sworn. Thus when he says “do not swear” it is to be understood as: without a reasonable cause. And when he says “do not swear by heaven, nor by earth, nor by any creature,” it is to be understood as: do not attribute too much to any creature. When he says “let your word be yes, yes, no, no; what is more than this is from evil,” this is to be understood as referring to those who do not believe a simple word — so weak is that person that he would by no means believe it without the aid of an oath. And therefore he says that whatever is more \[than a simple word\] — that is, what proceeds from the one who refuses to believe a simple word — that is the weakness that requires an oath, which grows from it. Next follows the question of the form of the oath, which is various. In the Old Testament this form was customary: “I swear by the Lord, if I do this — and may my soul die if I do not do this.” In the Roman Church there is another form, \[with\] relics and in judgment, and they swear by God, by Petrus, by Paulus, and other saints: “So help me God, and these holy bones, or these holy relics.”

says: “If there is no doubt, it is a lesser evil to swear truly by false gods than to swear falsely by the true God.” Hence the one who holds the greater position \[argues\] that perjury is more penal.
