Likewise a knight who is detaining a tithe can have force imposed upon him, and by his removal through the bishop’s authority this is argued from that chapter xv, q. i. Now whatever a monastery has corrected in that tithe which is the parochial property of this church — like the confiscation of another’s property in the manner of a lord who has stolen it — this is argued from that chapter xiii, q. v, \[c.\] “Penal,” and he has committed theft. Solution: It is established that all tithes pertain and have by right been in the bishop’s hand. Because the bishop withholds authority, he who is not possessor of the tithe — since the tithe of this parochial church belongs to \[the bishop\] apart from him — if \[a monastery\] commits theft, it does so against its own property in the Lord’s sight.

Likewise here is a shrewd point, this knight \[having\] also retained tithes: some peasant seizes in advance the modii that are to be tithed, which remains by reason of the agreements. What if a knight or someone else takes the price of the peasant’s grain in advance in place of the tithe even on the peasant’s land, so that what remains the peasant should tithe. And the church cannot defend its claim on account of this, and the space should not be lost to its ownership; unless the tithed \[share\] is lost, it must demand those \[tithes\] from the peasant, even though \[the knight\] takes the tithe first — since a knight cannot in any other way impose the tithe upon himself. For it is \[unjust\] that the church alone should bear the loss of the plunder. And the loss must be shared by the peasant who is to tithe; of the residue the church should \[receive\] the tithe. Solution: It makes a difference whether the knight has seized the tithe’s entire actual share, or whether after the whole has been divided, and the tithe separated from the grain, the knight has seized the tithe’s portion. In the first case the peasant does not owe tithes on the residue. In the second he does. Likewise, let us consider this particular occasion of the feast of \[Christmas\] — the first of those \[feasts\] — in which one of the \[heirs\] chose only the tithes and others chose those kinds of goods which the layman freely possesses.
