Hence it follows: know that this person, now having been ordained a priest, confers upon himself by that ordination the power of binding and loosing. For this reason it is called fitness or liability — which pertains to one who has been made fit or liable, that is, a debtor, by the reception of an order, for the purpose of binding and loosing. For if someone who is perfectly sound and yet less fit for such an office is ordained as a priest, he receives the order and receives the keys, and holds the authority or fitness of binding and loosing, even if he is altogether less suited for it. Furthermore, one who is slow \[tardosus\] or cloistered nevertheless holds the authority of binding and loosing — which, however, is false \[in effect\], because he has bound wings, according to the Gospel passage cited above. And given that when someone ordained receives keys and, by shedding his error, would find a better key within himself and would thereby recover knowledge — this goes against every \[observable\] life \[of such persons\] — which is false.

## Solution

We say similarly regarding the foolish: that something is held one way in the intellect, another way as a habit, and another way in act. A father has a young son who previously could not, and does not yet have the natural virile power, but one day acquires the habit. After the ninth day he immediately has the virile capacity. So too with the fool or the cloistered man ordained as a priest: from the reception of order he acquires the key in form \[in habitu\], and in performing the act \[of binding or loosing\] he holds the authority of binding and loosing, so long as the fool discerns or the cloistered man has license from the diocesan bishop or the pope altogether. He holds the key in habit, because it properly belongs to him for binding and loosing. But when he binds or looses someone, then he will hold it in act or in use.
