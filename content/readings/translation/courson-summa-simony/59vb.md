For the Lord has nourished many unto life, and therefore this \[preaching\] is by no means to be omitted. But a layman objects: “We see in practice that fields, cheese, and butter are \[sometimes\] devoured when all these things have been minutely tithed.” We respond that all fruits which renew themselves each year are to be tithed, and whatever of annual produce renews itself is to be tithed: whey, old produce, cheese, and butter,

and herrings — for the Lord gives these anew each year, and therefore we must require our portions of them annually.

But the question arises: from all persons, of whatever condition or status, are tithes to be received? The answer is that they are not, for from a robber or a usurer

\[tithes\] are not to be sought. Solomon says: offerings from the wicked are abominations to the Lord — above all \[abominations\] in receiving \[them\], for the law of the poor of the Lord \[requires\] that one bring \[something\] of little \[value\] rather than bring goods upon the altar of the Lord — and every usurer or robber lacks \[this disposition\]; therefore a tithe offered from plunder or from usury is not to be accepted. But the common opinion concerning Alexander’s \[decretals\] on tithes says that they are to be given out of \[all revenues\]. \[In the chapter\] “De archi episcopi,” \[Alexander\] commands that tithes be received, and accordingly whatever the church may suffer \[from their rejection\].

Moreover, \[it follows from\] Solomon’s statement about usury and the seed which they sow in a field that such \[seed\] is \[itself tainted by\] usury. How also even a corrupted root can bear such fruits. With such fruits — since it is said in the Gospel “a bad tree cannot bear good fruit” — likewise we say that tithes are of two kinds: some personal, others predial. Personal \[tithes\] are given from manual labor, from one’s own craft, commerce, and bodily work; such \[tithes\] come from robbers.
