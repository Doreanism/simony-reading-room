\[One who is\] confined in a cloister or who lies in a bed of contemplation ought greatly to fear such a call. Hence the blessed Bernardus was never willing to consent to such calls, even though he was repeatedly summoned — not even through the Clairvaux \[community\] and the most devout \[illegible\] of that \[tradition\] \[illegible\] — who, when he was called to \[a\] see \[illegible\] spread himself on the floor of his cell at the feet of his abbot, that he might not permit him to leave, citing that verse from the Canticles: “I have washed my feet; how shall I defile them?” and “I have put on my tunic; how shall I put it on again?” And that chapter: “He who sent his hand to me — my belly trembled at his touch.” For we often see it happen that an elect dissipates all the ecclesiastical goods, both spiritual and temporal; and indeed when a certain person was unanimously elected to the see of Milan, something like this was said about him: “The devil \[approved\] of this unanimous election of his, who dissipated all those goods of this church.” And in three \[respects\]. Solution. If one called to a prelacy senses a clear revocation by divine inspiration or some other certain sign, he ought not to accept it in a case of doubt. But if nothing moves him except the convergence of signs calling him, yet a rule can be obtained from man as well, and I would not counsel him to acquiesce in that call unless he was endowed \[illegible\] with knowledge, eloquence, and every kind of fortitude, and believed himself capable of being of primary service to the Church, in accordance with that \[text\]: “He who desires the episcopate desires a good work.” If he is moved by this for his own sake. Again, the wife of a usurer or a robber sees that she can \[secretly\] restore to \[those from whom\] her husband took many things acquired illicitly, without her husband’s knowledge; and therefore, though her husband protests, she asks whether she ought to do this without his knowledge. She knows that he is obliged to make such restitution, and she ought by every means to labor so that her husband is freed from the things by which he stands bound before God; and if she frees him in this way, she does well.
