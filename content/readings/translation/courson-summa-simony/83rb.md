Now it seems that when such a dispensation is made today with such a person or with anyone else who is able to go, the question is whether such a dispensation holds — for one who is oppressed by this world and afflicts and mortifies his flesh and its concupiscence more intensely does not carry out the commutation in the way that common justice always demands, which requires the rendering of revenues. If these are not rendered, the vow promised to God remains, and since the Lord demands even the last farthing, the vow will still be required. Moreover, on the slightest occasion the Lord Pope commutes the vows of pilgrimages of the poor and others who have vowed to go on pilgrimage. Let the question be asked whether these are fully absolved. It seems not — because rendering the emission of vows is a matter of precept and of name. \[The objection\] that only the Pope can dispense means that one who vowed is still bound. Furthermore as in \[the case of\]

## vii.

a person who has power over his own body, and a woman likewise: he cannot withdraw his body from his wife on account of a pilgrimage. If he goes with her consent of spirit, and she can revoke the vow of spirit lest she fall in her territory, then the Church will excommunicate him if he does not go — for the penalty is imminent and the penalty leads to a fall. And if he does give her up, she will fall into adultery. Furthermore, even supposing that the Lord Pope dispenses in full knowledge of the cause in all such cases — does his dispensation hold? It does not, because dispensation is not admitted in these matters when sufficient cause is known, and here no reasonable cause is present, and therefore no dispensation applies here.
