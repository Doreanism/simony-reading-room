But when he is ordained as a priest over her protest, whether he will or no, he receives the character of orders, because orders and baptism are in no way conferred \[conditionally\], and are conferred even upon those who are unwilling. In reply to the next point we say that the right is clear: whatever may be done in such cases in fact, persons of this kind are \[not\] capable of contracting marriage in law from the moment they have once made a solemn vow; and although it may be advantageous to kingdoms for them to contract, evil is not to be done so that good may come of it. Regarding a husband and wife who together establish a remarkable hospice or a leper-house in order to serve there perpetually and observe continence — we say that they ought not to contract or have carnal intercourse; rather, as the Alexandrine decretal says, they ought to dwell in separate residences.

## vii. It happened in fact that a certain scholar, placing himself in the hands of an abbot of a particular order and completing two years \[of probation\], made solemn profession as a monk of that order.

He vowed this solemnly; afterward, when two years had passed, he returned and placed himself under the order of the Cistercians \[*sanctionis*\], which enjoins continence. The question is whether he is a transgressor of his vow, and whether he is now bound to transfer from his \[current\] order to fulfill the vow. It seems that this can be shown to be the case in several ways. First thus: since he had not yet entered the cloister and been received, it is established that he is still bound by the first vow to enter the cloister and be professed; and whatever he was bound to do had he then entered the cloister and been received — because that order cannot be assumed by him — if his vow bound him to something better, he would then have been bound to that same thing.
