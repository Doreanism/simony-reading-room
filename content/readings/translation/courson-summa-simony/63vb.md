## On Plunder

On plunder. One must examine what plunder is, how many kinds of plunderers and plundering there are, what penalty is to be inflicted on which plunderers, and what must be said about the everyday plunderers and plunderings in which infinite complexities are entangled. Plunder is the violent usurpation or deprivation of another’s goods — whether property, possessions, or dignity — whether held or merely seen. There are many kinds of plundering. Some plundering involves movable things, others immovable; some involves what is fully possessed, others what is merely anticipated. Some occur through violence, others through wicked custom, others through fraud, others through suggestion, others through flattery, others through denunciation. Of these, some are committed by rulers, others by \[supposedly\] good men. Regarding the ancient plunderings that appear violent — we have already treated the likes of usury, simony, and fraudulent withholding in passing. Now one must speak of other kinds: of that which is done through violence in captivity or against prelates; of that which is done through threats, the customs of kingdoms, the license of soldiers, and the corruption of tolls; of that which is done through the suggestions of courtiers who suggest petitions and requests to kings and prelates which go beyond what is right; and of that which is done by flatterers, detractors, and those who give their assistance. Concerning all of these, the apostolic and evangelical rule holds and must be maintained: that those who exceed \[bounds\] in such plunderings must truly and worthily do penance and restore everything, down to the last farthing, at least in simple measure — since the Lord greatly commended Zacchaeus for restoring fourfold.

The Apostle moreover says: “Not only those who do these things, but those who consent to them being done are worthy of death,” and since \[the one who has done wrong\] neither abandons the fault nor restores what was taken, Augustinus says: “He does not do penance, but only feigns it.” First, one must speak of the first kind of plunderers. \[illegible\]
