If they \[the prelates\] say to them \[the knights\]: “We follow our lord the king in his war, which he wages to defend his realm and his Church, and in which he must preserve all fealty” — that amounts to saying: “Do not obey your lords in the war in which you are bound to obey.” And if they say this and detract from the king, they thereby derogate his right of royalty, in which \[the barons\] owe him fealty — unless the excommunication of the pastor unjustly excommunicates all who openly diminish the royal authority that they hold. If the prelates owe everything pertaining to the royal prerogative to the king by virtue of the original establishment of kingship, they will be either pure and faithful \[servants\] of the king, or disobedient to the lord Pope. If they are disobedient to the lord Pope, by that very disobedience they were already excommunicated, for he commanded them to obey under pain of excommunication. But since they are excommunicated, they cannot excommunicate others, and they cannot pronounce a sentence of excommunication on their subjects, since they are themselves disobedient. Solution. Either the war between the kings is unjust: if they \[the prelates\] are certain that it is unjust, then neither through themselves nor through their subjects ought they to provide assistance in that injustice. If the war was just, then they cannot counsel their liege-men or the barons of the realm to refrain from going with their lord and following his standard; they are bound to follow it, especially by the profession of kings and of the Church; and the lord Pope can \[say\] to the contrary — \[even if\] he himself speaks from the apostolic \[throne\]: “Obey your lords.” And that this distinction is more properly to be held without examination than the tradition of human law, which says that it does not pertain to subjects to examine whether a war is just or unjust, we prove from the authority of divine law, which carries more weight than human law.
