### \[XIV\]

Because they acted out of fear, so too did Abraham say to his servants when going to sacrifice his son: “Wait here until the boy and I return.” Abraham was uncertain about this as something not yet confirmed; just as a witness ought not to testify about uncertain matters, nor should anyone assert uncertain things; Abraham should not have asserted that he would return with the boy since he did not know God’s will. Likewise the canonical teaching of James holds that in statements about the future, the qualification “God willing” must be understood or added, and Abraham ought to have understood or added it. Paul likewise, when he said he would return to the Spanish with the words “I will come to you,” was obliged to recall and add or understand that canonical qualification — and either he understood it or he added it. The objection is: if these and similar cases hold. Augustinus, expounding the apostle’s statement, says he spoke falsely — that is, uttered a falsehood. But the Lord said “I will not go up to the feast,” yet even after saying this he went up. The resolution: Regarding Abraham, he received from God the assurance that he would be recompensed in his offspring — and he did not believe that God willed to destroy his son by immolation, but rather would either grant something else \[in place of the sacrifice\] or not let the boy be killed. Furthermore, if he was uncertain about the assertion, then — indeed, from \[his reception of the promise\] — he considered himself assured of returning with his son, and was indeed certain. And so we maintain that the aforesaid apostolic qualification is always to be understood in statements about the future. Paul and Christ understood that qualification; Christ was neither false nor deceptive — he neither spoke falsely nor deceived nor told a falsehood.
