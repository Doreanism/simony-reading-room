And that officer, knowing that he would understand that intent to be futile, neither accomplishes nor confirms what they swear, knowing it to be false; and since he has thus perceived it, he sins mortally by deceiving through it. Solution. For the understanding of the foregoing: it is well, as Augustinus says, that a lie is called the falsification of speech. For example: when someone speaks contrary to what his conscience dictates should be said — whether that thing is true or false — he simply lies and deceives, in that he relies on it; and to such a lie the aforementioned definition applies, because it is not contained solely in a lie made for the sake of benevolence or the benefit of someone beyond all exception. Likewise, a lie is said when someone sometimes says what he speaks against what he intends in his mind, that is, against what is true, and says the contrary. And in this usage, the more familiar and primary sense, it is the same to lie as to say a falsehood. And in this one equivocation it is taken to mean: to speak contrary to what an upright conscience dictates should be said, and to say a falsehood. Whoever lies first in order to deceive, sins mortally — that is, not everyone who sins in this way sins mortally. On the contrary, if one says a falsehood for \[the reason of\] a \[specific\] place, and neither to harm others nor to deceive the matter, one sins only venially. For a falsehood is nothing other than an imitation of truth. Concerning one at whose house the man being sought for execution is staying — whether he is perfect or imperfect — I say that he ought to follow his conscience, provided it is discerning.
