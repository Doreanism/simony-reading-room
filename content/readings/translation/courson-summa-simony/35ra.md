### Solution

I say there is none \[to be found\]. You should already know the least; you do not know the least. For whatever this man has \[comes\] entirely from God, and the least of God’s \[gifts\] in men is the very least. Whatever is in this \[man\] at the very least comes entirely from the malice of man — this is what every man incurs. Therefore the Lord places all the returning sins according to their gravity, and is for this reason more prompt to punish than to reward, because his rewarding is a work of supererogation: for unless God were to crown in us his own gifts beyond what we ask, he would \[still owe\] what he redeemed us for — as the certain wisdom of all those things teaches us, of which we say that, when they \[merits\] return, that return is the occasion of all the other culpabilities that return in this man, and from that act he becomes guilty of all, so that by reason of it one may note how it makes him their occasional cause.

Now, much could be observed about a certain canonical contradiction regarding the return of sins, because Pope Gelasius says, in C. xxiii, q. iiii: all things remitted by clemency itself for the sake of amendment; and another authority in the Sentences, art.: one who is a stranger to God remains vacant — \[regarding\] remission in the \[sense of\] return. But the comment on confessions, distinction where Augustinus says: when someone sins after baptism he becomes guilty not only of his first \[sins\] but of all those committed previously \[and now\] returning.
