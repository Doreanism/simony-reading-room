Since excommunication applies universally to all who withhold tithes, that peasant is excommunicated, because he paid his tithes in full to the knight’s authority. And because he withheld them on his own authority, those on the other side anticipated the causes \[of dispute\] and thus must endure possessing those tithes. \[It was\] somewhat lightly done for the peasant, year by year, to yield the tithes to the knight — which they were bound to yield — without coercion and by the arrangement of the Church, lest something worse occur. The sound counsel is that beyond those \[tithes given\] into God’s field, if anyone makes \[a claim\] upon him, that is nothing to him. Let the peasant do penance for having been excommunicated, and by the arrangement of the Church let the portions of tithes be restored to that church, or let it be arranged that through the hand of an upright priest or another religious person an equivalent restitution be made. For as the knight does not know the person of the one who caused this harm, the matter stands as with any other thief — if someone has stolen and another has seized your property.
