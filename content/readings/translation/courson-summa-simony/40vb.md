Further, the question is asked whether silence deserves \[censure\] — whether keeping one’s eye not on temporal matters obliges \[one to avoid\] simony — and this seems demonstrable. In a matter of fact: it happened that a certain nobleman, a dean of some church, had two young nephews and kept silence for ten years, not resisting at all while \[a bishop\] squandered all the goods of his church. Though he had full power to intervene, he wished in this way to obtain the bishop’s favor and thereby to procure two prebends for his nephews. He kept himself mute — he who ought to dare to bark — his tongue cleaved to his own throat when he should have stood firm and placed himself as a wall for the house of the Lord. For one who does not resist an error thereby approves it; one who could speak yet is silent gives his assent. If, therefore, the nephews obtained those prebends through his silence — without which they would not have obtained them — then we say that such a person incurs simony from that most wicked silence and intention. And not only the nephews but he himself is also bound to resignation; and the one who knowingly kept silent is bound \[to restore\] all past \[fruits\] in which the church was harmed through him — him whom he was able to resist and did not resist. A heart always stirring and in all things demanding the penalty of tonsure for the offenses committed daily.

### It also happens among spiritual things that a spiritual thing is exchanged for a spiritual thing —

for example, a church for a prebend or vice versa, or one for two, or a tithe for a prebend and this \[prebend\] for a tithe. When someone exchanges a prebend for another on account of the body \[of the benefice\] — because one was richer or because the fruits of the prebend were poorer — it would have been immediately accommodated to the one giving it \[only\] if he had done it not for the utility of the church but for his own \[advantage\].
