Furthermore, it often happens that someone vows to enter a particular cloister, and in the meantime he learns that through the wickedness of its inhabitants all its temporal and spiritual goods have been squandered, and the cloister has been turned into a stable. The question is whether he must enter that cloister according to his vow, or whether he may flee to another — even if he made no vow regarding it. What if he finds no other honorable one? And what if, even after being admitted and clothed, he cannot endure the effeminate cloister-dwellers intent on their innumerable abuses? What if they do violence to him when he does not consent? As is known to happen often. And what if something repugnant to his use is placed before him, something that, once acquired, would not please him? Will he thereby become a fugitive monk? Or will he remain there as if in a den of thieves and a stable of abuses? Resolution: All \[vows made\] about the future are to be understood with a proviso. Therefore, when someone vows to enter this particular cloister, a proper implicit condition is to be understood — indeed, a prudent, implicit qualification.

### Such a condition \[being\]: “provided the cloister remains what a cloister ought to be.”

Regarding one who has already entered and finds everything within unclean and flagrant, we say that he ought rather to leave for a solitary place, as we have said, or to a school of penance, than to remain a partner in their deeds.

### i. There follows the question of the vows of those in orders and of religious, which are attached to orders and professions.

It is established that according to the canons, a cleric in holy orders is bound to observe continence. If the question is raised whence this obligation of continence derives — whether from holy orders — consider: it is possible to find a holy order without a vow and continence, or \[to have it\] in the ancient and first law of the Church without anything that is of the substance of the order requiring continence.
