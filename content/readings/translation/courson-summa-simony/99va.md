Just as his conscience dictates that he should speak and not lie — if this wise man at whose house \[a fugitive\] is sought speaks something contrary to his conscience when he says to the officer seeking the man, “He is not here,” \[he acts\] with \[good\] conscience, because both his conscience and his discernment urge him \[to do so\]. And one who speaks thus does not knowingly rely on fiction. He is perfect — with regard to the officer — and says a falsehood, but speaks without harm and without going against his conscience. Against which: what was stated above by Augustinus, \[namely\] that it is lawful for the perfect, even if one admits that he is being sought. But if someone is not willing to accede to the custom of men, he says nothing. Behold, Augustinus says here that it is useless for the perfect in such a case to remain silent, contrary to what his conscience dictates — because by keeping silent one gives occasion to the one seeking \[the fugitive\] to have him killed, and his own unjust destruction and immunity become the cause of the death of another who could have been saved by a brief word from him; they ought to follow confession, because whatever acts contrary to conscience builds toward Gehenna. Likewise, suppose this perfect man is at leisure with you in such a place seeking the truth or the \[advice of a\] physician. Some most wicked man who seeks him comes, set on having him swear what he is going to do, as a penitent, and the oath was made anew. Now if this monk were to descend to the depths of hell, would he not be counseled \[to swear it\] and would say a falsehood — and if he sins, not venially, because by word or deed he might have saved a soul above him if he could? In brief, \[Augustinus\] says it is better to keep silent and to say plausibly to the officer: “Is that wicked man from here?” I reply by mitigating: it is necessary that \[the wise man\] either deny the contrary, or, if saying that would cause him to be pressed upon and killed, or another to be killed, or the officer to wrongly deny \[justice\] and say a falsehood, because by hell \[the officer\] threatened merit there — and by the denial he merits \[punishment\] in such a case, or otherwise. Suppose this were what the mind of the one acting from charity said, and what reason urged — that he should save his neighbor.
