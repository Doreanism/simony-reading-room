“but if this tithe of mine brings to us no benefit until \[it enters\] my barns, I will pour out for you a blessing.” The one word “pour out” denotes the generosity and abundance of the outpouring. Likewise Hieronymus says in the same \[work\], at the same c., q. i, \[c.\] “De,” commenting on Malachi in the same passage: that if the tithe is duly paid, even \[those who pay\] only the fruit will obtain bodily \[benefits\] as well. “Fear not — the Lord first asks for honor. For our God, who is worthy of the same, has deigned to receive the tithe. Accept the tithe from us. Be unwilling \[to risk\] future peril. But if you give it too late, first give gently a reminder, and it is far better to give at all than not to give. From commerce, from a trade, pay tithes.” Up to this point what these words of Hieronymus convey is that tithes are to be given of all things. The same point is made by the authority of Ambrosius in a Lenten sermon, c. q. v, \[c.\] “Whoever”: what he recognized in you as faithful has given his tithes on his income to the Lord — the Lord has treated him with favor; and if one who is truly faithful gives, God does not diminish it; let no one offer less of grain, or wine, or fruits of trees, or livestock, or \[the produce\] of a beast of burden, or of commerce, or of his hunting. Likewise in Leviticus: “All \[things\] were forbidden from your crops if

### On the tithe of all goods

\[something\] is possible from the field” — if all those things are present, even so. If someone is obstinate and murmuring, unwilling to pay tithes, we ordain that the parish incumbent admonish him once and again about the Lord’s commandment; and if he does not amend,

let him be excommunicated until satisfaction and amendment are made. And after that, \[it is declared\] that tithes are to be paid of all things and in all \[matters\]. That chapter on \[the example of\] Lent. Likewise holy Alexander in \[his letter\] establishing \[the matter\], \[c.\] “Licet.” He commands in his practical \[letter\] under penalty of excommunication that clergy compel their parishioners to pay — of the revenues of mills, fishponds, and income-bearing vineyards — to the churches to which they are owed, with all opposition ceasing, in full integrity. Likewise he commands in the following chapter: “Numos.”
