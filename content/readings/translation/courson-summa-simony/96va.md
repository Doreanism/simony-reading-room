The question here is whether such coerced oaths are binding. If they are unlawful, it has been shown by the foregoing chapters that they do not bind. If those unlawful \[deeds\] were performed and such assassins were enemies of the Church and worthy of death — and the law says “you shall not suffer evildoers to live” — then you ought to labor that they be sentenced and pursued by the sword of vengeance, knowing them to be incorrigible. If, however, the coerced oaths concern lawful matters, then the one who was coerced and does not fulfill them sins; as it says at dist. xxii, q. v, chapter “si quis coactus,” that anyone \[who swore\] through fear of bodily harm or out of any other cause or necessity, and who loved his body more than his soul, shall do a very lengthy penance. The question is raised whether it is lawful for one to fulfill what he has sworn under coercion, since the law says that what is done out of fear is not ratified. Moreover, consent alone makes a marriage, and a coerced oath to contract marriage does not make a marriage without mutual consent. And if someone swore under coercion that he would \[contract marriage\] with someone, or that he would enter a monastery, or take up the cross — once it is confirmed, such an oath ought not rashly to be fulfilled. If any persons swear lawful things before the Church out of compulsion and not of their own free will, even afterward such coerced persons may be compelled to fulfill their oaths. And thus certain authorities say that coerced oaths are binding. But we believe that an oath does bind the one who did not fulfill what he swore under coercion, seeing that he did not wish to fulfill them at all; yet he sinned in this manner, because he said one thing while meaning another in his mind, and he fell into the sin of lying, because he acted against his intention.
