Hence the total gain should accrue to the church — and from it one might purchase enough outside revenues to endow many prebends which bishops may confer freely. Further, in Genesis we read that Abraham had bought the place for the burial of his wife in the double cave where the fathers were buried. Hence whoever commits simony does so most wretchedly, because if today someone were to buy a place of a temple, altar, or \[other\] consecrated \[site\], he would incur simony. That Abraham bore with this and sinned therein Hieronymus shows, because Hieronymus says that Ephron sinned in that sale and therefore his name was changed: he who had previously been called Ephron was afterward called Ephran, as a sign of the disgrace of that dishonor. But against this: Abraham bought that broad and wooded place in the valley of Mamre because the burial ground was at its heart, as a matter of humility — just as if someone were to buy a field in which a thousand \[people\] have been buried. Since there is no right of patronage in such a field — because the right of patronage passes with the field, as with humility — simony would by the same reasoning be committed, yet not by Abraham. If indeed those who sell and buy such a thing have regard for the right of patronage, for which reason they sell it at a considerably higher price, then selling and buying a spiritual annex is not \[in itself necessarily\] simony. Solution. Without distinction we say that Abraham sinned in the purchase — namely in buying that field at so great a price — even though that place was designated or dedicated for burial; for in the surrounding region they reckoned this burial as pertaining to the entire field, which he demonstrated to belong wholly to the burial, and that may be permitted as \[an act of\] importance. But to sell or buy places dedicated to the Lord or similarly consecrated — whether abbeys or \[other\] spiritual places — is \[another matter\]. Nor does Hieronymus say that Ephron sinned because he incurred simony, but because, having first freely offered it as a gift, he was afterward struck by avarice and greed when money was presented — and so was said to have sinned, and his name was changed.
