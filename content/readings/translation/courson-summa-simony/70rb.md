If such a person is in need in such circumstances, and the Lord’s command so demands, I ought in that case to prefer him, in accordance with: “Do not despise your own flesh” \[Isaiah 58:7\], and also “Honor your father and your mother” \[Exodus 20:12\], and “Bone of my bones and flesh of my flesh” \[Genesis 2:23\]. For “no one hates his own flesh.” There is also a case in which, rather than the kinsman himself, some scandal is to be avoided above all else. If I were to confer a benefice on a kinsman while bypassing someone equally worthy, then a bishop would be conferring ecclesiastical benefices and preferments \[corruptly\], for he would be giving ecclesiastical dignities as alien property, subjecting them to worldly claims of this kind, whereas he ought to prefer the more useful candidate over the less useful. As the first causa holds \[Causa prima, capitulum “Milissimi”\]: if minor alms are to be given indiscriminately to all. As is shown in Genesis regarding Lot and Abraham, who were found worthy to receive angels — who received all, as dist. lxi, chapter “Quod elemosinam,” holds. Furthermore: suppose someone has abundance and could give something for the benefit of this poor man, and another who could but does not. You ought to give. Proof: by natural law all things ought to be in common, and in time of necessity they are to be shared. Also, all things belong to the just. And since this needy person is just, all those things belong to him. Also, when we give to the poor, we do not give them what is ours, but restore to them what is theirs. Also, it is iniquity to say: “this is mine and this is yours.” If this needy person is in extreme need, these things that the other is holding back are rightfully his, and they ought to be restored to him even by force. Also, if he is dying of hunger and finds bread that he cannot obtain from any other source and in such extremity, then whoever takes it or urges him to take it — if you counsel him: “take it” — is this a mortal sin? And since it offends the first \[commandment\], it is mortal. It is better to die of hunger than to commit idolatry or to scandalize a brother in this way, because it is better that a millstone be hung around his neck
