A poor man who asks \[for prayer\] is to be admitted in preference to a wealthy man who is less devout, even though the latter offers much. Thus now \[consider\] the contrary: if it can be said thus to the “sardonic” \[i.e., mocking\] one — he gives this so that one may pray for him, just as you \[give\] to a poor man so that he may pray first. For it is said in the words of Nebuchadnezzar: “Redeem your sins by almsdeeds.” Could it not likewise be said: “Redeem your sins by prayers”? This argument \[is made\] from what we read in the book of Numbers: those who offered to Moses say: “We have offered bracelets, armlets, rings, and earrings, so that God might be entreated for them.” By the same reasoning it may be licit to give a priest such a sum so that he may pray or celebrate so many masses first. Solution. In all cases, consideration must be given to which cause predominates: because if devotion is truly present, all are pure; if there is sin, all are false. There follows \[the question of\] the coins that nourish the temple, which the lords of that temple have established, so that they expose for sale vigils, matins, all the hours, and masses in almost every church this side of the Alps. For it seems one can demonstrate that coins of this kind make the church a harlot, and as it were a brothel, a house of prostitution. For on the Tuesday before the Sabbath, the \[clerk\] Maran, alone, calls out to Christ \[i.e., announces the Mass\], urging people to hasten to church. But when on the Monday before the Sabbath the coin sounds its own signal, then everyone flocks together — no longer out of love of God and salvation, but now for the coin existing overnight, and out of greed they come to those hours and masses. And they merit nothing, and rather demerit, since that work is dead, without devotion, infected with the leprosy of \[mere\] fear.
