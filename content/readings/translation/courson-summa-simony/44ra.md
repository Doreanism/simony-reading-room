### Another question is subjoined: Whether the bishop

is bound to examine \[the candidate\] before consecration — which it appears he is. For the Apostle says to Timothy: “Lay hands hastily on no man, but prove \[him\].” The gloss says that the examination ought to be more careful, the more the \[ordained\] person’s acts are capable of destroying the Church. Hence, since the examination ought to be great for all who are inferior to the priesthood, it is evidently most required first of all in the case of a bishop, so that before he is consecrated it must be established what his life and learning are. I believe \[the letter\] to Timothy does not say otherwise. Moreover this same \[principle\] first and also to the same: “Commend these things to faithful men who are capable.” The gloss says: joined \[i.e., combined\] eloquence and learning. Given, moreover, that \[a bishop\] is to be examined, by the same reasoning so is a dean, an archdeacon, and the others in succession. But what if \[a candidate is offered\] who is so manifest \[in virtue\] that without examination he is installed and examined \[at once\]? Is such a person to be heard only by the bishop, or ought such \[unworthy candidates\] to be rejected by the college of others? Solution. It seems to us that since lesser persons are to be examined before they are admitted to administration, far more so must greater ones be \[examined\].
