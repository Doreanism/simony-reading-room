Thus a voluntary vow immediately passes into necessity. Hence it is necessary for the person who has vowed to render it — that is, by necessity one is bound to render the substance of the vow — because otherwise one sins mortally. And nothing can impede the obligation to render it; nor can a subsequently contracted marriage prevent one from being bound to the same. And even in the embrace of a spouse seeking the \[conjugal\] debt, one is still bound to fulfill the prior vow. And in this it is held in common from the same vow. Against this: the law of marriage requires that the \[conjugal\] debt be rendered within the marriage, as the jurists say, and one is bound to render the debt when asked. This is proved because — as the canon says — the church can compel the rendering of the debt in law, because the church judges only from what is seen and heard. And since this is the same marriage, it can compel each spouse to render the debt — because the church does not judge concerning the hidden \[obligations of the\] vow. If perhaps someone says that the bond of the vow of continence binds her after a contracted marriage, always before carnal \[consummation\], and if it may be dissolved through carnal union — against this: carnal union is of the substance of marriage, as is held at cause xxvii, q. ii, cap. “cumq.,” and the following chapters, where it says that consent alone makes marriage — and \[only\] on account of carnal union, which is of the substance of marriage, does the bond of marriage overcome the bond of the vow. But if someone says: the vow by itself, if solemnity does not dissolve the marriage — the argument \[runs\]: every solemnity is also a matter of church constitution; and if the church can establish nothing that dissolves the bond of marriage, then the solemnity annexed to a vow will not dissolve the marriage. Likewise, the solemnity is not \[intrinsic to\] the vow nor does it accrue to it, because it is present and absent without the corruption of the subject. If it is absent, they say it is not dissolved; and if it is present, it dissolves the bond of marriage, which is a matter of natural law.
