Solution: We say that if you know you will be leaving people dying of hunger to perish while others stand idly by, you are bound to pour yourself out entirely for them before they die, and to expose yourself — provided your \[resources\] are sufficient — rather than leave them in such extremity. But if you have nothing beyond what will suffice for yourself, you should rather provide for yourself than for others. Just as in a shipwreck, if you are holding a plank and another wants to seize it, you ought sooner to push the others under than to give up your plank to your own death. Since you ought to be dearer to yourself than to another.

## xiii.

Concerning the distribution of either corporeal or spiritual sustenance: Gregorius in the Pastoral \[Rule\] assigns eight distinctions, saying that someone sins mortally who gives to one to whom it ought not to be given, or who gives to one to whom it should be given in a way that is \[nevertheless\] wrong, or who gives where it ought not to be given, or does not give where it ought to be given, or gives less than should be given, or gives for the wrong reason, or gives in the wrong manner. What shall we do, since almost no one now knows how to distinguish among these differences? Let us examine the reason beforehand, prior to \[ruling on\] whether in the giving of spiritual and corporeal sustenance, it is a mortal or venial sin. Nevertheless, if someone unworthy of spiritual \[goods\] is given ecclesiastical benefices, or if an obscene cause is found, it is clear that those \[giving them\] sin mortally. Moreover, if the rules permit one to distribute to anyone without distinction, the full weight of mortal sin is incurred — because what is received freely must be given freely. To another \[the rule\] is: grace is not to be held back. As Iohannes says: one who receives unworthily plunders what he receives. If those who do not need it receive it, Augustinus would say \[it is determined\] by the quality of the gift, by what it is — whether it is lawful for some persons to hold several churches or prebends.
