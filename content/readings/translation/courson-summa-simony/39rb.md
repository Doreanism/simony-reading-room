\[Continuing:\] the one believed dead returned and requested his cell back. It is clear that the monk who came in his place is indeed a monk, because he privately made his profession to the abbot for that office. By the same reasoning, a canon who has been admitted by the chapter and installed in choir is a canon of that church. By the same reasoning, one who has been admitted to a bishopric in place of someone believed dead is likewise to be ordained as bishop if he is consecrated — to be sure, for that place. The question is also raised: if the one believed dead were to reappear, would the \[newly consecrated bishop\] retain his position as bishop, or should it be taken from the one who arrived later and returned to the one who reappeared — given that it had been arranged that the former had already become \[bishop\] through that process, making the other unworthy of the bishopric? It is established that the one who rightly received the bishopric cannot, for the same reason, be made unworthy; nor \[can\] one sub-instituted to a canonicate in the place of someone believed dead \[be removed\]. Further, the question is whether canons are bound to compel \[the simoniac\], to excommunicate him, or to keep him among themselves. It seems so, because a simoniacal canon stands outside the law with respect to the fruits \[and rights\] of the others, and the canons are bound to assist \[him\] fully with the fruits, lest scandal arise among them. Solution. Let us say in accordance with the canons that the arriving monk is to be counted a monk and the arriving canon similarly a canon. We admit such a person as a canon, but as a canon of no prebend. Similarly, a bishop consecrated in the case described above is a bishop, but of no bishopric. The canons ought to present the case to the bishop, and he \[in turn\] should confer a prebend upon \[the newly arrived canon\] if he is worthy. If the matter is resolved in the Carthusian order, the prior is bound to assign a cell to the thirteen monks; for there is no difference between the religious cleric and the religious monk.
