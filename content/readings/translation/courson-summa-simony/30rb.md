But suppose that \[illegible\] exceeds the measure or omits something of the contingent things — for what is done without intent does not fall under the canon. Solution. For this it is to be noted that, in order for something to fall under the canon, three things are required together: namely, force \[molentia\], an intent to harm \[intentio malignandi\] — which some call dolus — and \[the act itself\]. If you ask in what force consists, we say it is found in pressing, in striking, in wounding, or in marking with any instrument, whether hand, foot, or any other means. Similarly, things equivalent to force are considered to include directing urine or any other \[illegible\] matter at the face of a priest before witnesses in contempt. Similarly, force may be diminished in certain lesser acts: if you sprinkle water on a cleric, or wet his ears or garments, or speak contemptuous words to him, or seize him by the hair, or corrupt another person, and he \[the cleric\] declares this, he falls under the canon. Indeed, if with intent to harm \[malignando\] one is present while someone strikes or beats \[a cleric\], one falls under the canon. A master who beats a pupil, or an abbot a monk, even if he exceeds the measure in correction, does not fall under the canon, as it is said. \[illegible\]
