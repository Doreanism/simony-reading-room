## On usury

Take the pure case in which someone, wishing by the church’s authority to redeem a tithe from lay hands, enters into an agreement in this form: “I agree: I give such a sum of money for ten or more years, at which point I am to receive it back in full, and in the meantime I shall receive from that tithe eight or ten modii whatever may accrue, whether a gain follows or not.” It seems that such an agreement holds, because if it holds for the entire tithe it would certainly hold, and it holds even more strongly for a part. For if the entire tithe yields one hundred modii, one can receive those \[hundred\] modii beyond the principal sum by the bishop’s concession; by far stronger reasoning one can lawfully receive ten modii. Solution: The form of the aforesaid agreement contains usury. For it is not lawful in such a contract to specify at the end and as the reason for receiving \[the income\] in whatever case may arise — because if the general agreement were to go on for years and it happened that nothing were to come from the tithe, he would nonetheless be obliged to pay out of his own grain the stated sum in addition to the principal.
