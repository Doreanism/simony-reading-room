\[Continuing on the matter of\] blood-relations: concerning goods \[received\] from kinship, I say without distinction that those who receive a share of goods are bound to restitution — namely those who received something in the name of a poor person. For no dispenser of the goods of the poor can lawfully give anything to those who are not poor. Or \[the same applies to\] all those whose deserving nephews \[receive goods unlawfully\], and anyone else who received such things for private reasons is bound to restitution toward the next of kin, and they defraud the poor by appropriating goods in this way, like bad stewards. Hence it is a dangerous thing today for the simple-minded, and for the nephews of abbots, priors, or bishops, since they accepted gifts — whether in \[private\] purses, or by reason of blood, or out of affection, or worldly favor — and thereby sin mortally and are bound to condign restitution, both to those \[from whom they received\] and to others. The same must be said of concubines who received everything from abbots, bishops, canons, and others who have nothing except from the patrimony of the crucified — they are bound to restore whatever they received through such quasi-servile devotion from those who were permitted to give. But \[we ask\]: what shall we say about those religious who have fathered children by concubines, that they cannot place such children in a monastery by the concession of the abbot and the lay-brothers?

Nor may they give anything either to them or to the poor from separate goods, such as aromatics, spices, remedies, and electuaries which religious sell, and pounds sold as incentives to luxury — we say they sin mortally, and buyers are bound to restitution. And those who buy such things for bodily healing \[must be distinguished\]: but where they buy cloves or a very beneficial electuary for bodily care and as an incentive to luxury, I say all are bound to restitution toward the poor. And those little dogs of theirs which today monasteries habitually keep through the bishop — those who squeeze their own nuns are bound to restore \[what they have taken\]. We have absolved the bishops of those little dogs, \[arguing\] that \[they are kept\] for this reason alone: \[illegible\] for some prelates.
