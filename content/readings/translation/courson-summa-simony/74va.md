### On Prescription

Within this same part, on the exceptions relating to patronage rights, we think it best to treat prescription first. We shall accordingly speak of prescription: what it is, what are its impediments, what is its effect, and what interrupts it. Prescription is used broadly to mean reception \[of possession\], as in dist. xvi, q. ii.

Prescription in the strict sense, according to some, is a summary seizure of an untimely action, which interposes the plea that the action is prescribed. Prescription in another sense, according to some, acquires direct ownership \[of a thing\]. Similarly, usucaption — akin \[to prescription\] or \[illegible\] — seizes the substance of an untimely \[claim\], because unless the time of possession is continuous, there is no prescription; as in dist. xvi, q. iii. The individual \[provisions\] above may be added to immovables to distinguish them from usucaption.

Moreover, usucaption acquires ownership through the continuation of a period defined by law. Direct ownership is also acquired by physical seizure, from which direct ownership also \[flows\]. But both prescription properly so called and usucaption never receive approval for public property nor for the ownership of things held in common; as in Digest 8, on usucaption, law 1, and dist. xi, q. ii, on revenues. Everything else that applies to prescription also applies to usucaption, except in respect of time. For according to the laws, the period of usucaption is three years. The time of prescription is reckoned differently by the laws and differently by the canons: for according to the laws there is a prescription of two months, another of three years, and yet another of three years. According to the canons there is a prescription of ten years, one of thirty, another of thirty, and another of forty years. A prescription of ten years runs between clerics and laity who are present; of twenty years, between those who are absent; for the term “present” we use the legal definition.
