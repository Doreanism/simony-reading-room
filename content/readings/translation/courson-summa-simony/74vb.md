Five things are required in prescription. First, in the case of immovables: that there be good faith \[illegible\] — that a possession in bad faith \[illegible\] — that the time \[illegible\] be present. Good faith is required; as in dist. xxxiii, q. ii, chapter “Si quis.” Just title is similar — that through which ownership is acquired, such as purchase and sale. Continuity of possession: but indeed if the time is continuous and one loses possession, then it is necessary to begin computing the time again from that point; as in dist. xvi, q. iii, since a thing \[illegible\] cannot be prescribed, as is written on usucaption. Prescription is impeded by the absence of three of its required conditions, and especially by \[a deficiency in\] the thing itself or the person: as when the thing is of a kind that cannot be prescribed — either on account of a privilege of its nature, as with a free man, or on account of sacred things, nor even religious \[property\]. Likewise, something held clandestinely cannot be prescribed; nor can a possession that is held as an ecclesiastical benefice, or a tithe held by a layman. A similar defect on the part of the person against whom prescription is brought impedes prescription — as when the matter concerns a public treasury, against whom prescription does not run by nature, \[illegible\] or a lunatic.
