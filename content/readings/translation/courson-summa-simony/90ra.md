In such a case, if the prelate ought to release a man already so notorious, indeed so strong may the presumption against him be that he should not release him, yet he cannot condemn him either; rather, he must shut him away between two walls on bread and water for as long as it takes until someone comes forward to accuse him, or until he confesses and repents, or until he undergoes an adequate purgation that will satisfy the people and the Church. And if the ordeal by fire or water were proposed, the official must refuse it, nor would any other remedy be found, for one thereby tempts God. Hence Pope Alexander is reported to have replied \[illegible\] what should be done in such cases: “Follow the custom of the land.” We gather this from the decretal *Ad abolendam*. ## There follows the most difficult question, concerning perplexity

Here and in other places perplexity presses acutely on those who labor in the field of penance, and some cases arise about perplexity of one kind or another — greater or lesser. That such perplexity frequently arises, Gregorius shows in the *Moralia*, book 18, in certain chapters, saying toward the end: one who fears both greater and lesser sins and is constrained by them, the lesser are always entangled, so that whoever is hemmed in on all sides takes flight; and for the sake of brevity we may introduce into this discussion a perplexity of this kind: the heretics we have refuted say that they can be so perplexed that they cannot be saved, and yet they boast of the perfection of their sect; and afterwards they eat meat or do something contrary to it; and at the point of death they cannot be consoled by their companions nor take any brief counsel in all things; they also say that they cannot be saved, as if by authority \[illegible\]
