As is provided in Cause 12, question 1, “Duo sunt genera;” and how things ought to be common to clergy is shown in the same question, the chapter “Dilectissimis,” and the chapter “Tua sanitas,” and the chapter “Secus.” Likewise the authority says: “None of them is permitted to convert to their own use or to hold, from subject peoples or from other places, movable or immovable goods.” As is provided in the same chapter “Nullus.” And this is proved more efficaciously through what Hieronymus says on that Gospel text: “Go to the sea, you will immediately find a coin; take it and pay the tribute for me and for you.” There Hieronymus says: “The Lord counted it a sin to convert any of the things that the poor put in his purse to his own use; and therefore the Lord created a coin in the mouth of the fish, giving us an example.” Likewise on that Gospel text: “The soldiers rushed to guard the tomb.” The Gloss says that those who go under the judgment of the Jews and the Pharisees are like those who shed the blood of Christ — that is, those who spend the goods of Christ on other uses than on the uses of the Church.

Likewise indeed that clergy ought to have private property, as is said in that chapter “Ad easdem causas episcopis de rebus provisis vel acquisitis heredibus suis de:” “Whatever \[comes\] from the provision of their church — whether from fields, from produce, or from offerings — all this they adjudge to be reserved to the right of the church.” And this same \[conclusion\] is proved by very many chapters that follow in the same question and cause, and the chief chapters in the canons of the same. To resolve these opposing \[views\], many are accustomed to distinguish and \[say\] that clergy are established in the Church either in such a way that they renounce all things, or in such a way that it is manifest they do not renounce them. The former kind is spoken of by those chapters and authorities that deny that clergy have private property. The latter kind is spoken of by other chapters; and by this nothing is resolved, because still the question remains
