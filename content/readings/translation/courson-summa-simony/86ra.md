## iv. Alexander says in the chapter *Sane*: if a married man and his wife have agreed to his ordination, the wife’s consent must first be obtained; and he adds: by the authority of the Apostle it is prohibited that married men be ordained as bishops or primates, or that a wife who has first professed continence receive the veil imposed upon her.

But what if the wife was an old woman at the time, and only becomes an old woman afterward? She may remain in the world without the veil, unless the habit of holy orders or religious profession is assumed. Solution: what he says here is a special case applying to him; in another case of old age, old age may excuse a wife who remains in the world when her husband enters religious life. ## v. Objection.

If a married man, over his wife’s protest, enters a cloister and assumes the habit, he is bound in law to render the debt to his wife; and whether monastic profession or the vow of profession is binding, since the wife protests — consider whether the husband, remaining unmoved, proceeds to holy orders and is consecrated a priest in fact — whether an order thus conferred is valid, whether it imprints the character of orders, since continence is an inseparable property of orders; and whether a husband can take a vow of continence over his wife’s protest; and whether one so ordained becomes a priest in this way; and whether one so promoted to the monastic state over her protest is thereby a monk.

## vi. In fact and by apostolic dispensation, noble professed monks or nuns were at times permitted to contract marriage for lack of heirs in kingdoms, lest the commonwealth and the Church perish.

If it is established that such persons, having maintained continence after their solemn vow, were in a valid marriage — and if they are in a state of salvation — it seems the Church received them, since it permitted them to contract marriage even after a solemn vow. Solution: In reply to the first point we say that in all those sacraments which God instituted, such as baptism, or in those he granted authority to confer, such as the conferral of holy orders, a character is imprinted. Hence when orders are conferred on someone, a character is imprinted on him.
