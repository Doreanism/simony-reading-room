The question is raised whether this \[agent\] was accommodated by the cleric.

The cleric is bound to restore whatever he acquired through usury in this fashion — which seems \[correct\], because one who provides an occasion for harm is considered to have caused the harm. Moreover, a poor person would have done this, and the cleric had provided material that this poor woman would never have had \[on her own\]; and in that sum of money itself — contracted by the cleric, through which she received pledges from those to whom she loaned so great a sum of money, to the revocation of the cleric’s \[share\] — the usury nonetheless grew among those from whom she received pledges. The cleric was the original source of such usury, and since sin is not forgiven unless what was taken is restored, he is bound to restitution. He proves this otherwise by the following analogy: suppose someone saw an incendiary fire burning in some place and, immediately moved by penitence, he recalled that person and stopped her after the fire had already begun to consume the city — he must restore \[things\] to their former state because he placed such material \[for the fire\]. Again, suppose you, O prince, committed an act \[of command\] along with your \[troops\], having in your firm control three \[divisions\] and \[illegible\] someone at the limit. And if those three owed something in fee, and you immediately wished to revoke the order which you had issued in fury, withdraw so that you might no more harm that year’s \[crops\] of the lords \[illegible\] by their nature, and you wished to revoke that order — would you not be judged guilty? Thus also the cleric will be guilty for the foregoing, because of the usury that grew \[from his action\].
