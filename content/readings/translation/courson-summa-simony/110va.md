### XXII.

Item: no one is less bound to continence, for he who has always lived under the obligation of continence — in this respect a subdeacon can also \[be bound\] if he has committed himself to maintaining it. But what if a subdeacon has \[not maintained\] continence? Does not the Apostle’s \[teaching\] to Timothy permit \[a man\] to have his wife in the ordinary way? Item, let the episcopate there teach that having fewer wives is permitted, and similarly deacons may have their wives; this was conceded as permitted by the Lord in Leviticus, just as \[it was permitted\] to Aaron and his brothers. And not with a repudiated woman, nor with prostitutes. If we are joined to those named above who are still \[bound by this rule\], who is prompt in it and will apply it in the church — if he still observes all of this, as many assert today. Item: also if a cleric who is an acolyte has a public \[marriage\] contract of the present sort, and is thereafter ordained before \[consummation\] into the diaconate or subdiaconate or episcopate, what should be done \[in such complex cases\]? Does his wife precede him, or does ambitious promotion \[take effect\]? It is established that in both cases — after which, to lie together jointly, and to exercise the office in holy orders, sharing life in common — it seems that the \[marital debt\] ought not to be rendered to the wife. For the smoke \[of the argument holds\] that of the three prior to carnal union, if he \[renders\] and the husband is promoted to the grade of supreme pontiff, whether \[the debt\] ought to be rendered to them or not, and that he has known his wife, and afterward, even if a supreme pontiff marries, and if the wife seeks him — any contract from the cloister that would compel him to return to his wife, since this is above him \[in dignity\]. We resolve this thus: that in the intervening period, the apostolic statutes in the practice of the church and the time of \[Gregory\] — if they are \[in force\] for the subdiaconate as an order — then it cannot be lawful to contract \[marriage\] today either as a subdeacon or acolyte, if he cannot maintain continence because of the annexed obligation of continence. And indeed this was established first by a general council: however one has received the order of diaconate and received the burden of his household, individually let no one \[proceed otherwise\].
