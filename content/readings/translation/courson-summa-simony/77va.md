And if after repeated \[warnings\] he is not willing to hear the correction — if he defects to the side \[of wrongdoing\] because those who flatter him are favorable to him, or out of fear, or on account of some temporal advantage they hope to receive from him — the priest may ask what he should do about his own subject. He is left in confusion: when he sees that the man is a pernicious member who scandalizes his entire parish, if \[that member\] is permitted to remain in the parish in this way, the whole \[parish\] may perish from the corruption.

But if the priest excommunicates him on his own authority, \[the offender\] will appeal to the hearing of the bishop himself, who has sided with the worse party; and then, in contempt of the bishop’s mandate, he will associate with those who similarly scorn the excommunicated man and will depart from his place; and thus, by the bishop’s side \[being\] the party of those more readily to be excommunicated, the entire remaining flock is scattered. Moreover, the priest will have no recourse to the metropolitan, the cardinal, or the supreme pontiff, because those authorities would more readily believe the bishop than an impoverished priest. How is one to heal \[the situation\] in this difficulty?

### Solution.

When the parish priest sees that his bishop does not support the shepherds but rather disciplines them \[unjustly\], he should keep the matter concealed, and proceeding to remedy it he should correct the bishop according to the evangelical order: first in secret, second before witnesses, and finally he must bring the matter to the hearing of his superior — that is, the metropolitan, the legate, or the supreme pontiff — so that the bishop may there be confronted by the presiding authorities concerning the handling of the \[matter\]; and thus he \[the bishop\] shall be held as a pagan and a publican, refusing to hear the admonitions of the subjects delivered by the parish priests; and in the end \[the priest\] must carry out his office according to the institutions of the canons as the law prescribes — as many do, because what is to be done under such iron laws at this time is laid down in the Gospel and is to be held indiscriminately. For it is not asked here what should be done \[in general\], but what the parish priest shall do; and all his superiors to whom he must have recourse will be found to be mute dogs.
