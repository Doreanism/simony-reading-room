Hence the apostle: “He who is the brightness of his glory and the figure of his substance” \[Hebrews 1:3\]. And by reason of accommodation it is said that Christ is a lamb, a victim, a turtledove, a dove — not because any mention is made of these for their own sake, but because what belongs to Christ is accommodated to them for some fitting reason. In this signification Jacob said: “I am Esau your firstborn son” — that is, I am he who owes what Esau owed, namely the right of the firstborn and the other obligations of primogeniture. The distinction runs as follows. But if the statement “I am Esau” is false, it is false in its plain sense; but fulfilled under the stated meaning, since it was spoken to him in this way — it would not have been said unless he were Esau. It must be said, rather, that it is another sense, because he said he was the one who owed the birthright; and just as Jacob had purchased the firstborn rights from the firstborn, so he sought and the debt to be conferred by his father, asking a blessing from him. So too in virtues: when they come to the sons of Abraham according to the flesh, those sons are called first and yet are last, and those last are first — for the life of Esau shut out the nations, and behold they are now the sons of Abraham by flesh and by the initiation of religion. Hence in Matthew: “The Lord is able from these stones to raise up children of Abraham” \[Matthew 3:9\]. And on this basis we resolve the question about Jacob: by that locution he said he did not speak from heart and heart \[i.e., with duplicity\], as the Spirit proceeds from the Spirit and \[the Father\]. And what he said by that locution “I am Esau” he could lawfully confirm by oath. He could lawfully swear that he was the one who owed the firstborn rights, and this he did when he spoke \[thus\] in spirit through that locution. As for what follows: we shall say that Isaac first had the intention of blessing Jacob, since he had been \[so\] inspired and moved beforehand, without his children knowing it. Afterwards, when he perceived that the Lord willed the blessing to be conferred upon Jacob, \[he confirmed it\].
