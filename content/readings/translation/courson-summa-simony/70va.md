My counsel is that you take nothing by force or power, whether by seizure or theft, for otherwise you incur temporal liability; nor ought you to scandalize anyone in order to feed yourself or another. What then \[shall be done\]? I say that you ought to commit yourself entirely to God, who will preserve what is yours, and trust in nothing else. Hence Augustinus: \[illegible\] a cruel God \[illegible\] withdraws, and \[illegible\] deprives — nor ought you to seize anything on behalf of yourself or another, according to that rule: “Shall evil things be done that good may come?” \[Romans 3:8\]. Likewise: a lord ordered his servant to give \[something\] to the poor in common, and the servant kept it for himself. I ask whether he thereby diminishes the lordship of his master in any way, since through his action the goods were not distributed. I say that insofar as the minimum is concerned — so that he does not procure for himself a quicker release from evils — no. Likewise: why did Daniel tell Nebuchadnezzar to redeem his sins with alms \[Daniel 4:24\], even in the state of mortal sin? Since all works done in the state of mortal sin are dead.

On this there is a twofold opinion. The great theologians say that works of this kind done in mortal sin are of value with respect to the abundance of temporal goods, to the habit disposed toward grace, and to rendering one more receptive to receiving grace, and to a more tolerable punishment in hell — which we will disprove. Cato said this. If they are of value to the end that, when in place of good deeds men commit evil, they become less evil, and become accustomed to good works, and are thereby disposed toward grace — what shall I do when I see a destitute woman, or two dying of hunger, and I can help neither? I hold \[one ought\] to divide \[what one has\] between them equally, and to one who is unequal \[in need\].
