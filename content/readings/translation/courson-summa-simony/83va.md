Resolution: It seems to us that it must be said without distinction that there is no dispensation concerning a vow, nor commutation of a vow, unless sufficient cause is known — either concerning the incapacity of the person who cannot fulfill the vow, or concerning the benefit to the Church which receives greater utility from the commutation of the vow — while also taking care that no useless example or scandal arises from the commutation. Hence we believe that such a person is led astray from his vow who, without any deliberation of cause, undertook and made it. For once the vow of the will has been emitted, it immediately becomes necessary — and it is indispensable unless some cause or necessary utility arises within it. Although some great grammarian publicly preached that women could revoke their husbands who had taken the cross, and thus generated great scandal. This must be heard — rather, the following distinction must be drawn. When a person takes the cross, he either vows to serve perpetually under the banner of the cross in defense of that land, or he vows to go once to the war in the Holy Land, given the gift of the Lord Pope and the gifts of the Church. In the first case, the wife’s consent is required, and she can object — just as she could if her husband vowed himself perpetually to a religious order. In the second case, he ought not to remain — for just as he could move himself to visit Saint Denis over the wife’s objection, and by the same reasoning to Saint Thomas, and by the same reasoning to Saint James, so similarly to the Holy Land, which is the last \[of such pilgrimages\].
