The resolution: The father’s words are interpreted as indicating that he did not wish him to be Esau in that sense, but to be the one to whom the birthright was owed; for in the stipulation and the laying-on of hands it was borne by Esau as \[Isaac\] declared. Furthermore, Isaac did not know with whom he was speaking or what he was confirming with the blessing. Against this: he knew he was giving this particular blessing, and was giving the blessing as it were to a particular person, and he knew he was giving the blessing to Jacob, and he knew he was giving the blessing. Moreover, he knew he was giving the blessing to this person. That is to say, he rightly names the name \[in question\] and gives the blessing. The resolution: In this and similar cases we shall say that a pronominal demonstration establishes somewhat more \[certainty\] than a pronominal demonstration combined with an appended name, where only one person is to be understood; and still more through the name itself, which expresses a quality that is a collection of characteristics found in no one else among those present. But the argument from a simple pronominal demonstration — or even combined with a \[proper\] name’s demonstration — to the proper name, or from knowledge or doubt \[of that proper name\], is false. It is false to say: “this is he,” namely “this is Jacob,” when this or \[another\] name or even the name of a man may lead us to understand who or what kind he is. If we understand a proper name, we know who the man is as a distinct person; the same judgment we form concerning Mary, who thought he was the gardener and thus indicated him in that manner. And of him we say the same as we would say of a certain certifiable pronominal error in the opposite case — that the matter stands contrary to what one would expect. Hence this form of argument has no valid inference in itself or from what is joined with it, and to make a thing out of it the other thing must be joined to it. For there should be added: and it \[must be\] so combined with that — namely, that Isaac did not know he was speaking with him, and that he was speaking with Jacob.
