\[They\] ought to pay nothing in freedom, because we have established that all freedom from payment \[must be honored\], so that no lord shall impose any exaction upon others. Hence Ambrosius says in Cause 12, question 1, the chapter: “If he desires tribute, if he desires fields, the emperor has the power to claim them from the fief \[*feo*\] — let it not be given. I, Ambrosius, as he demonstrates in the land and shows his peace, as fully \[as possible\], and likewise \[Ambrosius\] renders \[the same\].” And the final question, the chapter “Contemning,” where he says: “If he compelled me to give something of mine, or demanded it together, or gold however much, or silver — that would be a gift freely offered from my own goods. But nothing could or ought to be taken or handed over from the Temple; nor shall I accept it in custody.” The solution: In all these matters we believe a distinction must be drawn as follows. Either the Church was endowed as by the registration of Constantine, or subsequently new towns, villages, and fields were granted with royal honors. In the first case, the Church owes nothing to be spent in wars, except against pagans and heretics. In the second case, for rendering land from the tenant, \[illegible\] I distinguish that what is owed to Caesar, let it be paid. Properly speaking, nothing else.

Likewise it is asked whether a prince has the right to confiscate the goods of churches when a cathedral see is vacant, and whether donations of prebends made by them are binding. On this point it seems permissible to argue from that chapter “the Lord,” and that chapter “In synod,” with their concordances. Likewise from \[the fact\] that it is shown Hadrian and Pope Leo gave to Charlemagne the power of electing pontiffs and of conferring \[ecclesiastical\] dignities, and that Pope Leo likewise granted this to the orders of the German kingdom. But against this: in the \[subsequent\] tradition it would bring to later generations what was granted only by reason \[of particular circumstances\], as is argued from \[the decretal\] “Cum adamis,” and that \[illegible\], and that chapter of the canons, and that \[illegible\], “If the later \[grants\]…”
