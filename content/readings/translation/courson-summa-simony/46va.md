Likewise, if you accept gifts against an innocent person who is placed by them into litigation with an advocate, and thus sins mortally as it is said in the psalm: “Because you devour the poor in secret, often oppressing the poor man for a bribe.” And therefore he treats the poor man as though he were the guilty party. Again, the Lord in the gospel: “I was a stranger and you took me in, sick and in prison and you came to me.” Again, the Lord commands: “Depart, you accursed, into eternal fire.” But every poor man who is disinherited, stripped, a stranger, in prison, and too heavily burdened in his bed knows this — if you are willing, by doing so you could devour his coin; but you could also spend it to restore him to his lodging, to free him, to heal him, and to relieve his sickness; it would be to serve God rather than money, but instead you come under that condemnation: “Depart, you accursed, into eternal fire.” Rightly so, because he sells what is most dutiful and most just — the fear \[of God\] itself — brings dishonor upon it, and sins mortally. Far more gravely does he sin who puts up for sale that most precious of his members, his tongue — he sins mortally. But the common false opinion circulates, or that \[misreading\] of Augustinus. It is not lawful \[to do\] what he is said to say is lawful: namely, that an advocate may sell his patronage, or if so for a consultant \[his advice\]; that a judge may lawfully sell a judgment; or that a witness should rightly sell his testimony — the same \[refutation\] holds in all these cases. But indeed, “the worker is worthy of his hire”; let him live from his labor and reward, because his lawful services are entirely \[his\] and \[he may claim them\] from the poor man, when he has no other means of living. Since the Lord says in the gospel: “Carry neither gold nor silver,” etc. — that is, nothing beyond food, namely evangelical sustenance from those under his care — by the same reasoning an advocate may seek sustenance for his advocacy from his clients.
