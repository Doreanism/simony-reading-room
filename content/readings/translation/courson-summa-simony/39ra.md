Consider this case: a simoniac assigns someone a prebend. Solution. The case is twofold. If a simoniac known as such is placed under interdict by the head of the Roman church, a collation to a prebend or to another benefice within his gift is clearly void. If the Roman church has been deceived in the matter and believes him to be free of simony, one who accepts a prebend from him does so worthily and lawfully and is not bound to resignation. The case is entirely similar with a robber and a usurer: there is now a case in which neither the robber nor the usurer is bound to be regarded as such, since they refrain out of fear of punishment.

But a simoniac’s having given prebends corrupts neither this nor that character, nor anything similar. Now, since the simoniac is as a husband in the church as a canon, the question is how far such a person is to be counted a canon, since he confers nothing. Item: freely.

Concerning a matter of fact: it happened that a canon of a major church was believed to be dead, and immediately from that point his prebend was exchanged for another and someone else substituted \[in his place\], in error concerning the \[original\] possessor. The question is whether the one once instituted is to be canonically received, having already been admitted by the chapter and installed in choir. But it seems not, because those who invest him use this form of words: “I give the prebend belonging to that person.” But this form of words gives nothing at all, since one cannot give a prebend while its possessor holds it and is unwilling to leave; and since no prebend is committed to him, he is \[only nominally\] a canon. But against this: in the Carthusian house there can be no more than thirteen monks.
