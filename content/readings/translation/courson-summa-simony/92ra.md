Again, concerning the penalty of one who is accused of homicide or of another crime that he himself committed, and who has duly confessed and is sufficiently contrite, \[the question is\] whether the priest ought to advise him to submit to that accursed ordeal or should forbid him from submitting to it. For if the priest says “submit to the ordeal,” on account of this he is obliged to swear that he did not kill the person of whom he is accused, and thus through the priest’s counsel he will incur perjury. If on the other hand the priest says “do not submit to the ordeal,” the king’s servants will immediately arrest him for refusing to submit to the ordeal and will compel him through torments to confess to the crime. For the answer to the first question return to that decretal *Ad abolendam*, and to the reply of Pope Alexander, reported in a new \[decretal\]: when asked what should be done about one who is to be purged through such an ordeal, he is reported to have answered: “Follow the custom of your land.” Again, in the third case it happened that the lord Pope had strictly enjoined a certain legate sent to the kingdom of the Franks to excommunicate all the knights and barons following the king’s standard in a war between kings, and had commanded all the bishops of the realm under pain of excommunication, after due warning, to excommunicate all who refused — and to impose other penalties on their lesser subjects — except for a newly arisen cause that was sufficient to delay that sentence, unknown to the lord Pope, who, had he known it, would by no means have issued that mandate. The prelates and bishops appear perplexed here, for either they execute the lord Pope’s mandate or they do not. If they do not execute it, they are charged with disobedience, and by law the Pope himself can suspend them from every office and benefice as disobedient.
