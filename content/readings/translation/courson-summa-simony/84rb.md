### ii.

Now: the vow annexed to the order requires this. Consider the objection: suppose this person intended to receive orders without expressly making any vow. He is nonetheless bound to be continent after receiving orders; if not from a vow annexed to the order, then he is bound to continence by vow. And every vow is either mental or vocal; and whether he is bound to continence by a mental or vocal vow, this would be conceded. One constituted in \[holy\] orders is bound by the vow annexed to that religious state to observe what was mentally conceived when he intended \[to enter\]. But if one who receives orders did not intend this, he is bound to continence not by any vow he has made, but rather by something of the substance of the order, or by something inseparably co-annexed to the order — that which has been attached to that order by inspiration at a general council, which decreed that orders ought not to be conferred upon anyone without the accompanying obligation of continence. It may be doubted whether an order can be conferred, with such continence, over everyone’s objection — for it would be unjust to confer matrimony, or penance, or the Eucharist, or another sacrament. If someone says it is unjust to confer baptism or unction or confirmation — the objection arises: that person \[who is baptized against his will\] was not unjustly baptized; indeed, such an objector would be contradicting the scriptures, and thus the Church would, against his will, baptize him as the Church \[requires\]. Consider: the Church would communicate with such a person; and outside the Church, some say that when someone is unjustly baptized, he is not baptized until he later consents.
