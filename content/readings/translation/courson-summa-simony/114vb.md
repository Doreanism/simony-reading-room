### XXXII.

There follows \[the treatment\] of infamy. Hence we must consider: what is infamy, what are the modes by which infamy is contracted, and whether someone may be condemned on infamy alone in the absence of a formal accusation. And first let it be known what infamy is. Let us first set forth what reputation \[*fama*\] is. Reputation is the established standing of one’s honor, or the appearance and opinion of reason in honorable conduct. Infamy is twofold: one is infamy by law \[*infamia iuris*\], the other infamy by fact \[*infamia facti*\]. Legal infamy arises either through an infamous sentence, or previously through legal confession or condemnation, whereby someone is condemned by sentence — either by an action or by a criminal charge that causes infamy.

### III.

Likewise, from the nature of the penalty \[illegible\] infamy is contracted. For just as a soldier loses the insignia of his rank and fellow-service, or when any cleric is degraded, through a secret crime or through secret \[means\] no one \[formally\] incurs infamy — but if someone suffered effeminate acts upon his body, or hired out his services \[as a gladiator\], or fought with beasts in the arena, or did such things as are openly apparent to all and make him infamous. If infamy attaches to one who, from regard for the wickedness of another, omits something he himself did not commit, he becomes infamous through malicious accusers or slanderers. As is read concerning blessed \[illegible\]: for it is established that he knew of a certain stain upon himself, because \[certain persons\] were accustomed to visit him frequently. And thus the whole question must be put to each witness, or to whoever was the first \[to speak\] in due time. Infamy is the deprivation of good name; or thus: infamy is the brief \[mark\] itself \[upon a person\], infected with the charge of the same dishonor.
