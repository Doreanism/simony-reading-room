### VIII.

The seventh \[form of accusation proceeds\] in this manner: “I, the plaintiff, allege that a certain person committed such a crime, in such a place, at such a time.” And the accusation written in this way must be delivered into the hand of the judge. And if the plaintiff happens to be deficient in proof, he must undergo a worthy penalty as determined by the judge, and the canon holds that the judge must accept the \[measure of\] penalty for the deficient \[plaintiff\] — that is, he must sustain the same penalty that was intended to be imposed \[on the defendant\], if the charge brought against him were proved. And this is expressly stated at C. \[2\] q. 3, \[in a certain\] canon. Thus, the nominal \[form of\] accusation distinguishes there whether someone institutes a \[formal\] accusation in the proper mode or by bare voice \[merely\] alleging a crime. In the first case, if the proof falls short — or if \[the accusation\] goes to absolution and falls short through withdrawal — \[the accuser\] is punished for calumny. In the latter case, if he falls short in his endeavor, he bears all the penalty that the law imposes for injury. As the law says there, in the last clause where secular law is invoked on this matter, and by the heated spirit \[of the jurists\] against the vice of calumny.

### IX.

And on this account no penalty is imposed here with full force. There follows the question as to who are to be admitted to \[bring\] an action or to testify. In the Gospel it is read that those present are to cast the stone of accusation \[John 8:7\] against \[the infamy\], because they frequently know themselves \[to be\] both guilty and full of \[their own\] \[illegible\] — all from the least to the greatest.
