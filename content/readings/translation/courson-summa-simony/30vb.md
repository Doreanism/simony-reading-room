But Alexander says, regarding a master who beats a cleric on account of an error — that even if without intent to harm \[sine animo malignandi\] he exceeds himself so far in beating the cleric that he even \[illegible\] — does not such a one fall under the canon? If it appears that the cure makes him into a homicide, in the chapter of \[illegible\] — that this is so in the case of two who are practicing archery without knowledge, and we should deal with them in mercy and not in anger — otherwise not; ask whether such a one falls under the canon. We must be careful not to judge easily, since these are matters of Polish justice \[de Polonia iustitia\]. We generally wish to refer such matters to the pontiff — such a one falls under the canon, unless perchance he wishes to mitigate the continuum; then he does not fall. Wherefore the sound counsel is that whenever anyone doubts whether he has fallen under the canon, he should consult the supreme pontiff if he can, because in omitting there is danger, in acting none. Regarding a cleric who chastises himself, we say that he does not fall under the canon, though he may sin mortally in another respect, and observe the regular form: dist. LV, *Siquis amedienti* \[c.\]. If a physician applies all possible diligence to healing the sick, as is set out in the same chapter *Siquis amedienti*. Likewise, a question is raised: if a madman were to strike a cleric, would he be liable under the canon of that \[decree\], given that it is established that through his own fault he had deserved to fall into madness, as we impute all things that tend toward guilt to one who has even been silent \[about them\]? We give a brief solution: we say that such a person, although he is guilty as to the fault, does not from anger fall under the canon; for we ought to be more inclined toward absolution.
