\[The response was:\] “It does not belong to us to define the mode of punishment for such persons, for you have your own codes concerning the various penalties that emperors succeeding one another have decreed against those who hold the faith in contempt — just as we have our own codes, namely the Old and New Testaments. But in that which is permitted to us as clerics, we may show mercy. Yet this resolution still moves and troubles me. For when we say the prince has a code of law in that \[book\], it is as if we were saying: ‘have mercy on such persons,’ since that is the only thing that can be read in his code on such an article.” To describe things by name and so soften simple souls. What if I saw right before me another man — an incendiary, a destroyer of churches, a ravisher of virgins, a sower of every heresy? Would I say to him: “Embrace no hope of absolution,” while the prince holds such a man arrested or confessed by law, and asks me what to do with such a plague? Then in the end I shall counsel him, without further admonition, that he should rid the world of such a pest. I know that I would admonish him to act in admonition — and if I am slow about this, I am already in the wrong. I am already obliged to urge him to this good work. Whether for any reason this falls under our name — if someone argues that a distinction must be drawn: many are the works that belong to laymen alone, and others that belong to clerics alone; and a layman ought not to usurp the works of clerics, such as to celebrate Mass, or to bind and loose; nor should clerics insert themselves into the works that belong to laymen alone. Granted there are grounds for asking the prince to spare us the inflicting of death or mutilation of limbs — this response does not yet fully resolve our difficulty for us, because apart from the iniquity of inflicting the death penalty for this crime by its office. By this reasoning the Master and Modicus Passus and many others argued that when a wrongdoer worthy of death is captured and the prince asks the cleric what penalty he ought to inflict, the cleric may lawfully answer that such a wrongdoer deserves death for such a crime — and this he may say from love of justice so that a better and lesser \[sentence may be pronounced upon\] him
