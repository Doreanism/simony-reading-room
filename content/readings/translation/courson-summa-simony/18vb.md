The same person makes an offering at another church and another third of his punishment is remitted; then at a third church again a third, and he is fully absolved from this venial punishment through these oblations. Perhaps someone will say that so great a part of punishment is not remitted. But on the contrary: a part is remitted to another person in such-and-such a way; therefore \[a proportionately smaller part is remitted\], even a thousandth or a hundredth. And suppose a person approaches a thousand or a hundred priests, and the penalty is divided into a thousand or a hundred parts — so he is fully absolved. But against this: either God punishes or man punishes; but neither God nor man \[now\] punishes this person for his offenses. How then is he absolved, given that his wrong remains unpunished? Likewise, this person is absolved through general remissions because he makes an offering; the poor man makes no offering and therefore nothing is remitted to him. And from this it follows that the condition of the rich man is better than that of the poor. But since Augustinus speaks about that psalm “In his establishing” \[illegible\], he says: “Whoever comes forward with equal charity — Zacchaeus, the woman in the Gospel, the poor man — but not all in the same way: one gives cold water, and another who has nothing \[offers only his will\] — \[Augustinus\] declares that none of these merits more than another.” And further, one who makes even the smallest offering merits as much as one who gives the greatest, because as \[Augustinus\] himself says: where God is content with the will, not the means. We say that the aforesaid absolutions have effect through the intercessions of the Church both for the diminution of penal punishment, for the obtaining of grace, and for the deletion of venial sins. For so great is the grace both in the intercessions and in the devotion of the Church that through them something of penal punishment is remitted, and through them first grace is infused — for although no one can merit first grace for himself, another can merit it for another. Likewise, venial sins are gathered up through daily prayers and the sprinkling of holy water and through the reception of the Eucharist. That the prayers of the faithful accomplish these things is clear from innumerable examples: through the prayers of Daniel the people of Israel were released from cap\[tivity\].
