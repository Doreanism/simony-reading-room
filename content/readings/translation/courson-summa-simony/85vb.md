And since he is bound by no vow, he is bound to render the debt when asked. And therefore a lawful capacity for contracting marriage still remains in him, so that when required he must render the debt, even when it is demanded. So too he ought not to have contracted \[again\], because he was bound to observe continence, as the one who was his \[religious superior\] was bound to follow \[him\]. Hence he sins, and because he is not granted \[forgiveness\] for this, he must do penance. But suppose he has not yet contracted \[again\] with the second woman, and the first wife has now died: is he bound to fulfill what he vowed? It seems that he could have vowed that monastic profession, which was within his power; and so he is absolved from it, and he is still bound to return to that monastery. Resolution: if in law that monastic profession is binding, but he is released from the marriage bond, he is bound to return to that monastery; yet by reason of that vow he is bound to observe continence and never to seek the debt, just as we said he is bound to render it if he did in fact contract. Furthermore, since a husband and wife are one flesh, as the Apostle says, one should not remain in the world while the other takes flight to religious life — unless the wife was old and barren, in which case she might possibly remain in the world while he enters religious life and pledges chastity, as Alexander says under that same title, in the chapter “Committem sat.” Furthermore, as Alexander says in the same place, in the chapter “Coniugatus”: no married person is to be promoted to holy orders once the wife has promised continence, so that henceforth, regarding carnal union of spouses, it becomes spiritual — as is argued at xxx d. “Serm-”
