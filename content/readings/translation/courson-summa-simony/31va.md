## On conditional excommunication

Something must be said on conditional excommunication, regarding which curious persons have woven sophistical little questions. Against it they object thus: if one baptizes under a condition, then the baptism holds conditionally — so if one says “I baptize you if you are not already baptized,” \[it holds\]. Likewise, if a vow or oath is taken under a condition, it holds: once the condition is met, the vow or oath stands. By the same reasoning, if one excommunicates with a condition attached, once the condition is met the excommunication holds. Suppose a bishop excommunicates an archdeacon in this form: “I excommunicate you unless you do this within fifteen days” — and afterward within a year; and that archdeacon is elected metropolitan, or the archdeacon does that thing within the year. If this comes to pass, the condition is met, and therefore the said excommunication holds. But against this: that man is a metropolitan and thus cannot be excommunicated by the bishop, since a greater \[authority\] excommunicates a lesser. Solution. Similarly with oaths and vows that have been promised concerning things pertaining to their time, the implied condition of good sense should be understood to be pending throughout — as is understood in the canonical letter; for today this is to be understood as implied in all excommunications pertaining to the future. If you ask what that condition is, we say together with the Cantor \[of Paris\] that it is that condition which a good, prudent, and discreet person, when the time has elapsed, would recognize as having been implied.
