In the meantime, let us consider whether these general absolutions or remissions that are granted by the lord pope and bishops have any force. That they can have force may be shown by four arguments: because the lord pope has fullness of power here, while others are called to share in the responsibility \[in partem sollicitudinis\]. And if this is so, and if he has full power here to bind and loose, just as Peter did, then the keys have been conferred upon him and upon all his successors, where it is written: “Whatsoever thou shalt bind” — and so on. Likewise, in the Old Law, Aaron held the general office of casting out all lepers and of keeping them there: if the leprosy was plainly manifest, he cast the person out; if it was not so plainly apparent, he shut him up for seven days, to determine whether or not the case was truly leprosy. Thus he was himself able in some cases to grant a dispensation for a period of time, and in others to expel immediately. So the lord pope succeeds to this fullness of power and can similarly both expel some by binding them and dispense to others by loosing them. Likewise, regarding that Gospel passage in which four men let down the paralytic \[through the roof\], the Lord said: “Be of good courage, son; thy sins are forgiven thee.” There Augustinus says: “See how much one’s own faith avails, though another’s also avails but not in the same way.” For there the man recovered through another’s faith not grace but bodily healing; and so he was fully absolved from guilt through others. Far more, then, through the lord pope and through the intercessions of the universal Church, is a person absolved from penal punishment \[a pena pene\]. Likewise, Augustinus \[says\]: “The intercessions of the Church profit a person so that either remission may be complete or damnation may be more tolerable.” If these intercessions are performed by authority of the lord pope through the universal Church, then through them either damnation will be more tolerable or there will be complete remission of punishment.
