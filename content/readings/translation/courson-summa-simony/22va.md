Hence when the Lord had called Peter the Apostle along with the others, and had immediately established his primacy, and had known him just as he knew the others — and when Peter himself denied him three times and the Lord restored him, saying “Feed, feed, feed” — and when they had departed from him, and he established \[the Church\] and was then increased in grace, in the manner in which \[the Lord\] breathed upon him and \[gave\] the Holy Spirit, and when \[they were bidden to\] be clothed with power from on high, namely on the day of Pentecost — that authority speaks \[there\] of a condition, of which it now says “I will give you the keys.” For when he had placed the error upon him \[i.e., allowed the fall\], \[Peter would return\] — supplement: so easily — and rightly so; and because God is faithful so as not to give \[temptation\] along with the trial pure \[and simple\], and \[would not allow\] him to fall if he were to punish \[him\]. And it should be noted that because the aforesaid authority or fitness of binding and loosing is wont more often to conflict against the knowledge of discernment between leprosy and leprosy, therefore such knowledge and the key are both called by either name \[i.e., “key”s\]. And we must say that there is one key — the aforesaid authority or fitness conferred upon someone — because it makes \[persons\] fit for binding and loosing. Yet \[Scripture\] speaks in the plural in the Gospel, “I will give you the keys,” etc., and the plurality refers not to a certain key but to the diversity of its uses. Similarly, those who speak of “keys” \[in the plural\] are like one lampstand in the tabernacle by the Lord’s command; and yet the Apostle speaks in the plural in the letter to the Hebrews: “The first tabernacle in which there were lampstands,” etc. He says “lampstands” in the plural because of the divided \[arms\], that is, the divided branches designated in the arms of the lampstand.

## What follows: on the diverse uses of the keys

Now if the uses are multiplied: one is in absolving, one in binding, and a third is joined to these two, namely in discerning between leprosy and leprosy, between the clean and the unclean. And this binding or absolution concerns guilt — from which God alone absolves — and \[the other\] is twofold: the penalty that is a punishment, the penalty of excommunication.
