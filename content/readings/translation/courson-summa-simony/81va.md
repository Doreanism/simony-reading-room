\[ural\]. The third solution, which we have stated, is that the opposing \[texts\] speak of distinct cases, about the dissolving of continence — not \[of marriage\]. For where one solemnizes a vow by the reception of orders or of the habit, a subsequent marriage dissolves that vow, as Jerome says in that chapter “Vovebas”: “If you marry.” And the opposing chapters there speak of a private vow. Hence Theodorus says: “If one who has a vow of virginity joins himself to a wife, and afterward sends the wife away” — and this is to be understood according to this solution where the marriage had been pure and consummated by carnal union. And then if it is said to you that you must do penance for the breaking of the vow for \[such-and-such\] years. As proof of the solution: when someone vows simply to continence, he binds himself to this — that it will not be in his power to seek \[the conjugal debt\]. And if afterward he is in fact with some woman, she can seek \[the debt\], and then he is bound to render it — unless before the rendering he withdraws to the religious life and solemnizes the vow there. For if he solemnizes the vow there, he is bound to render it afterward, as is held in that decretal “ex publico.” For when he vows simply, he does not bind himself to that vow to the degree that he neither renders nor seeks \[the debt\] in any contingent case whatsoever — because in all vows or oaths concerning the future, a \[tacit\] condition is to be understood as implicit: “as long as \[circumstances allow\].” The reception of the habit or orders excepts such a condition — and this is not shown in the instruments of proof in the case of religious profession. Moreover, such a solemnity, whether of the habit or of orders, proceeds from natural law, because even before the church renewed it, it existed among the Nazirites and others before the \[Mosaic\] law, who in a similar manner bound themselves to God.
