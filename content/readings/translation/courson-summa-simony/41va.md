For the fruits of a prebend can perfectly well be sold up to a certain assessed value of the prebend, in accordance with the Paschalis decree: whoever does not have proper standing — because the portions of portioners \[are akin to\] tithes; likewise a priory, like the fruits of a prebend, can be sold and is not inseparably bound to the prebend or to the priory; \[the fruits\] can remain with someone so that he may draw income from them for the poor. Note that the lord pope, and even a bishop with the lord pope’s indulgence — the full cause being known \[to them\] and no infamy being involved — could confer the entire portion to another, introducing worthy persons and dismissing those who scandalize the church.

### What follows concerns pensioners and vicars and citizens when they incur simony.

Suppose someone accepts a church or priory under a pension, intending principally toward temporal gain — simony likewise. So too for a vicar substituted in another’s place and intending principally \[toward gain\] — simony. Further, concerning a citizen who buys from an abbot a prebend in bread and wine, not attending to his own ambition in any respect: it is no wonder \[that he commits\] sheer usury or simony, since in these transactions a spiritual thing — or what is annexed to a spiritual thing — is bought. Solution. In all these cases the response is to be found in counsel. If pensioners or one introduced profess that persons \[of the church\] are their principal end — then, when the utility \[of the church\] is recognized and the absence of the principal person is established — this will be simony. But if \[temporal gain is\] the frequent secondary end and the \[church’s good is the\] principal end, they \[still\] incur simony. Concerning the layman who buys a daily food-prebend: we say that he sins if he intends to buy only the daily fruits under a twofold uncertain case in which it is not certain that he will harvest \[a return\] for himself — his capital is placed at risk — but he exposes himself to the vicissitudes of fortune like a merchant; then he engages in commerce, but not usury.
