In the same place, Alexander determines what is to be said regarding secular boys not yet of adult age, regarding notaries, regarding the wearing of long hair \[coma nutrienda\], regarding those caught in defilement \[in stupro\], and regarding those who lay more violent hands on a cleric — saying that if secular boys below the age of puberty strike each other they are themselves to be sent to the apostolic see, since that too excuses them; but if adults strike each other out of hatred or envy they are to be sent to the apostolic see, and if perchance the one further \[illegible\] has struck, these two are to be feared. But if a notary of a prince or an official of a private person strikes a cleric in the exercise of office, he can be absolved by the bishop, unless he has gravely wounded him. But if he has gravely harmed him in a crowd, he falls under the canon. Likewise, one who unintentionally and trying to defend himself gravely injures him does not fall under the canon, since common laws permit the repelling of force by common right. Likewise, one who finds a heretic in defilement with his wife, or in his own wife or sister or daughter, and strikes him, is not immune from the sentence; but if — not knowing that person to be a cleric because he wears long hair — he strikes him for some cause, he falls under the canon; but he can be absolved by the bishop or priest.
