\[Subsequent grants\] derogate from prior ones — later \[princes\] are not bound by the authority of those \[earlier\] ones, and princes today cannot defer \[to them by\] confiscating the goods of churches and granting vacant prebends when the see is vacant. According to that saying of Gregorius: “He who abuses the power granted to him deserves to lose the privilege,” as stated in Cause 11, question 3, the chapter “Privilege” \[illegible\]. \[We conclude therefore\] that princes today who confiscate the goods of the Church when the see is vacant are bound to restore everything seized in the interim, and those who grant prebends are bound \[to answer\] before \[the court\] \[illegible\]. But if they have no such privilege and \[nonetheless seize\] prebends and all episcopal goods, they are bound to preserve them for the future bishop, as stated in the decretal “de officio et potestate iudicis ordinarii,” the chapter “Cum nos plerumque.” \[illegible\] The cause that counts from the year of Pope Leo for conferring such things on Charlemagne and Otto has ceased. When the cause ceases, the effect ceases, as \[stated in\] the first part \[of the Decretum\].

### On almsgiving.

Likewise it is asked whether a prince or prelate is bound to compel his subjects to give alms in a time of dearth. To this it seems it can be shown that they are bound \[to love\] their neighbor as themselves, and in such a critical moment they are bound to render assistance. Much almsgiving, both bodily and spiritual, is a provision of food. Hence Ambrosius: \[illegible\] chapter, “Pascere,” “\[Feed\] the one dying of hunger \[illegible\] of the poor.” This is argued through the chapters of Cause 11, question “Aurum gula,” in many of which it says that golden and silver vessels are to be sold for the sustenance of the poor. \[illegible\] truly what glory it takes away \[from\] the ornaments of the Temple \[illegible\] to give to the poor \[illegible\]. From these and their concordances \[illegible\] it is established that the prelate who \[is set\] over the flock entrusted to him is indeed bound to compel subjects, through the miraculous \[example of\] almsgiving, to generosity. For the prelate knows that the goods of canons are the goods of the poor, and he sees them as greedy stewards unwilling to give anything to the poor even in so great a crisis, and he should wield the sword of correction \[illegible\] to compel \[them\].
