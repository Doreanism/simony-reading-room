### Again, a certain hermit

Again, a certain hermit, frequently called upon to give counsel to the souls of usurers and of others in their final agony, asks whether he should leave his cell or cave at the summons of such people, or whether he should advise them to come to him. It seems certain that he ought to go \[to them\], and this on the following grounds: the greatest part of souls that he \[tends\] — it is not a great thing to feed a soul that is about to die quickly, but the greatest thing is to feed souls for eternity with him \[God\]. Again, Origenes \[says\]: no sacrifice is more pleasing to God than zeal for souls. Moreover, if your brother were dying of hunger and you could help him, you would be obligated to do so; otherwise you would be a murderer, for the authority says: we are held guilty of murder if we fail to give temporal assistance \[to one in need\]. How much more, then, on account of the salvation of souls ought you to devote yourself and all you can to your neighbor. Moreover, those usurers who wish that hermit to remain there, to abandon their usury at the admonition of the priests who claim precedence over him, saying that if they \[the usurers\] heard the hermit they would believe him \[as one who has their true interest at heart\] — and so great is the hope of confession for those people on account of the hermit’s presence, that he should remain; for one reads the same of Hilarion the hermit, to whom a certain matron came, whose daughter was ill, and begged him to come and pray for her daughter. He at first fled from her.
