Item: inquire whether a dispensation can be made in some case allowing the receipt of interest or usury, which seems to be supported by that canon in C. 22 q. 3, where Ambrosius says, “Take interest from him if you desire to harm your enemy.” But against this: the commandment of the law is firmly grounded in natural law. Theft is the alienation of another’s property against his will, and this is from natural law and from \[divine\] provision, and no dispensation can change it in any case. We concede and say that the aforesaid canon does not \[authorize usury\] — for usury is taken there under the name of exaction, which may be exercised by right of war when war is just against enemies. Item: neither the Roman church nor the pope nor a general synod can alter the statutes of the apostles, because those were established by those who established natural law. The statutes of the supreme pontiffs can be altered by dispensation in a case as we said above. If we read in the Apostolic Canons that a subdeacon is received in holy orders, from the time of Gregorius the subdeacon has been held to belong to the sacred order.
