Likewise, the second distinction divides thus: one vow is private, another solemn. Private is that which is made without any solemnity, without the authority of the church. Such a vow prevents \[a future\] marriage from being contracted, if it was made before a consummated \[marriage\]. A solemn vow is one made with added solemnity in the face of the church — for instance, when someone receives an order or solemnizes the rite of a vow of continence, by the reception of that order or habit. Such a vow both prevents a marriage from being contracted and dissolves one already contracted, as is said in that chapter “Agathosa” at cause xxxvii, q. ii. Also, the third distinction is made thus: one vow is public concerning the present, another public concerning the future. The vow of the present is when someone says “I vow continence”; the public vow of the future is when someone says “I vow to observe continence after two or three years.” Also, the fourth distinction is made thus: one vow is simple and absolute, another conditional and determinate. Simple is that which is emitted simply — when someone vows a pilgrimage, or religious profession, or some such thing without any addition. A conditional vow is one promised simply with a condition thus: “I vow such a pilgrimage if God grants me health.” Let us now examine in order the points of uncertainty concerning these species of vows, and first on the vow of necessity. The Cantor of Paris \[Petrus Cantor\] plainly distinguished this kind of vow, saying: one vow is of first necessity, another of second, another of third. The vow of first necessity is to renounce the devil and all his pomps, and to hold to the things of the Christian religion, under whatever form — to decline from evil and do good, and \[to observe\] the precepts of the Decalogue and the Gospel, and all those things without which there is no salvation.
