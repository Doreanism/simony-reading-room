We say, therefore, that if he dispenses without will or necessity he sins mortally — which he would be departing from — even if he confirms a marriage within the seventh degree, whatever his intention in doing so. That marriage holds, and that which is delegated by the Lord he cannot do otherwise, because he can frequently sin under the law in this matter. If people who were to be joined in marriage were beyond the seventh degree, the pope cannot dissolve that marriage through dispensation, nor can even an angel from heaven, because marriage belongs to natural law, and God alone, who is the natural law regarding marriage, can relax it. As he did with Noah, who granted to him the taking of sisters, and by inspiration it is read that he relaxed this: the dispensation of Jacob to take many wives, and also to many others such as David and Solomon — but these are not to be followed as precedents, because they were matters of particular inspiration. No man who has assumed authority over such a matter under the Lord can supplement anything through dispensation, and it is said that positive law supersedes another positive law known by dispensation, but the Lord’s law \[natural law\] never supersedes positive law, nor positive \[natural\]. Moreover, a solemn vow, or the exercise of necessity, or a solemn vow once made, never admits dispensation, by the will of him who intends the natural \[law\]. A contingent outcome admits dispensation, because its generation concerning such penances as are arbitrary ones, when reasonably known, may properly be commuted or relaxed. If someone has vowed a pilgrimage and if virtue has animated such a vow, it admits dispensation in due time. We concede that a solemn vow, or a solemn vow once confirmed, does not admit dispensation — as in the case of a monk or one ordained into the clergy — nor can the pope make it so that he is not a monk or a cleric, nor that he is not bound to the exercise of his order or clerical status, which is not possible.
