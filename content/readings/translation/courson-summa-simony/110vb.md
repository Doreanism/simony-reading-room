Hence the pope cannot dispense privately with a subdeacon so as to relieve him of such an order — just as he cannot dispense with a monk so that he does \[the things that pertain to a monk\] — even when a general council through some hope of its own holds that the laws of both might admit of novelty. We say that if, before carnal union, one becomes a subdeacon or deacon or supreme pontiff — even if \[the parties\] were also in named public contracts of the present sort — then he does not render the marital debt. After carnal union, if one is promoted to those orders, then by right he must render the marital debt, as even the canons \[provide\]. And similarly the supreme pontiff must render it, and the general council of cardinals and the \[legal\] position should compel him — or else the law asks whether the subdiaconate existed before the order \[was established\] before the time of \[Gregory\]. We resolve this better by others as well: that if it was an order from the time of the apostles, and if there was no exception before the time of Gregorius — just as in his time the law \[was fully in force\] and \[has held\] from the time of the apostles — then neither the Greeks nor the Latins are permitted to maintain continence in the subdiaconate and above. In this there was a difference: the Greeks could and can be promoted to holy orders without the yoke of continence, since they can \[lawfully\] have a just marriage throughout \[their lives\]. The Latins can be promoted to holy orders without the yoke of continence if they take the lesser \[orders\], as the canons speak of children in the distinctions.

### XXIII.

There follows the treatise on canons concerning servants and the unfree. Concerning those irregular, we state five matters of rigorous \[law\]: unless those to be promoted \[are free from impediment\], and how all by dispensation can be promoted. Apostates are those who, after assuming the clerical state, passed over to lay status, setting aside the tonsure which in the primitive church was first a common mark of \[clergy\], then afterwards a mark of penitence, and \[then\] the adopted mark of perfection in imitation of Christ — who \[bore a crown\] at Calvary and in Calvary, and is crowned in spirit with the crown. Hence, those who have rejected this sign are not to be received into its privilege, for the church does not defend those whom sin \[has separated from it\].
