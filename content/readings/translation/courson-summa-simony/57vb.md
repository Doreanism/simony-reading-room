\[In a\] similar \[case\]: if I give a loan to my \[neighbor\] lest he become a thief, and I invest the loan in land, but they say it was consecrated for this purpose — similarly, if a loan were given lest you suffer robbery at the hands of robbers, this incurs usury. Again, the question arises whether it is lawful to compel someone to take a loan by pledging strict pledges, or by being driven to usurers who receive nothing good from usury except the precise money they are owed — which seems \[correct\], because such usurers are not the lords of what they possess and cannot bring it into the lordship of anyone; and it may be lawful to receive pledges from such persons in a loan. What if a father in need tries to find others to give him a loan or to redeem his revenues under pledge until his return? A similar objection arises regarding all others who receive loans from usurers or robbers for recovering what was their own — and therefore if it is possible to bring it into some \[lawful\] possession. We say therefore that to pledge one’s money or rather one’s revenues here is lawful, even though such plunder places one in the company of usurers at court.

With these matters disposed of, it is clear that whoever gives as a loan any commodity — whether grain, goods, or anything else in a better season — attempts usury through expectation of time and falls into it. Suppose someone gives cheap grain that is damaged or spoiled, donating it to another so as to receive back in harvest time grain that is far better — this person is held to be ensnared in usury; and a cleric in the \[external\] forum should be barred not only from contracts of this kind but from all trading, as Hieronymus rightly says: “commerce is more shameful for a cleric than usury is for a layman.” But if a usurer has received something above the principal from someone, that person from whom he received it must seek recovery with full effect under ecclesiastical discipline, since he cannot communicate with one who has received from him — this must be noted; and the usurer who announces that he has paid usury to him should restore it, hence the decretal says: if he has already made restitution, the matter of usury may be recovered at its root.
