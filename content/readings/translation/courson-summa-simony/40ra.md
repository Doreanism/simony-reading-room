## Further, concerning the right of patronage, we distinguish as follows: in the right of patronage there are two elements — one that is lay, which is hereditary, and another that is spiritual, which is ecclesiastical.

The lay element can be sold, even together with \[the property\], with due humility, in respect of the place — since the patron of a place has the power to choose a cleric and to present him to the bishop for the purpose of obtaining the benefice established in that place. The ecclesiastical element, however — such as investitures, by which satisfaction is made to the bishop regarding that benefice, since he confers it along with \[responsibility for\] the cure of souls — can in no way be sold; but what pertains to the layman insofar as it concerns administration can be sold. We believe, however, that the right of patronage which a layman can sell or buy is itself a spiritual annex. Hence one who sells or buys a village with regard to the right of patronage thereby sells or buys it at a higher price, as we shall explain more fully below.

Further, these two things are annexed to one another: selling prebends and giving prebends freely. Whoever sells the one must not leave the other unsold — but if prebends can be sold, then someone \[may also\] buy them. Consider: a certain \[bishop\] is held in the highest esteem by the bishop’s circle and is therefore compelled by necessity to sell all the prebends; then the canons of his church give him a large sum of money so that he should no longer sell the prebends nor press the clergy into servitude. The question is whether it is more lawful to give money as a payment before the fact than for the other party to sell them to the wicked — by a lease arrangement — so that the robbers will not steal or plunder; by the same reasoning \[it would seem\] the simoniac may sell prebends. This compensation benefits the utility of the church. This in fact occurred in the church of Lyon, in which there was a certain canon who was a disturber of the whole church and wished to resign his prebend into the bishop’s hands for a certain sum of money. But there was need to free his church from that most wicked adversary.
