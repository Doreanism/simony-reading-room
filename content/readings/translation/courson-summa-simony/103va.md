Likewise there was a simulation by which Jacob simulated being Esau, placing the dressed skins on his neck and hands, as Genesis records. The best exposition of this with reference to the fathers is set out there. And there was deception, and therefore sin. Against this: error is a bad thing, and Isaac’s error went against his father \[i.e., against what Isaac himself stood for\], and it was bad that Isaac erred in this way and sinned. The resolution: No one says another deceived him in the trial by fire of a turning wheel. Hence, since Jacob would not have placed the skins \[on himself\] to deceive Isaac — for if he had received him \[directly\] he would have known the truth, since he would not have considered that \[Jacob\] was bringing it to him — indeed, \[Isaac\] himself already approved of it when he said “let him be blessed and blessed shall he be.” And so it follows: we say that simulation is of several kinds. One kind is by signification, as the simulation by which Jacob simulated being Esau, which became a significant sign of the firstborn through the Son — who was first to be preferred over Esau in the Jewish people, according to the apostle. Simulation of concealment \[is that\] which is read in Tobias, by which the angel Raphael simulated being the son of \[illegible\]. Simulation of teaching, by which \[the risen Christ\] simulated going no farther, penetrating even to the hearts of the disciples who at first believed him to be a pilgrim; and afterwards their eyes were opened at the breaking of bread. Now the error that was in Isaac was a bad thing by reason of impotence — it was bad, that is, a thing called bad from the weakness that was his, because the dimness of age had settled on him and he did not recognize \[Jacob\]. But afterwards the Lord gave him new eyes by which he recognized that hand of his and Jacob’s, and that the birthright was owed \[to Jacob\]. Likewise, when Isaac said to Jacob “you are my son Esau,” there was something \[that Jacob\] by this did not fully and plainly \[assert about himself as\] Jacob; and when that nominal locution expressed what was exceedingly ambiguous, it contained things of opposing character — namely the thing predicated could be that of Esau, and if those were the quarters of prudence or caution in the traditional sense of signification
