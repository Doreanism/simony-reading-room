## xxii. i. There follows the question of the conditional vow, about which many doubts are usually raised.

Consider a case in which someone makes a conditional vow in this form: “I vow that, if God shall grant me entry into this cloister, I will enter it.” This person thus vows to enter the cloister under this condition — if God grants it to him — and the question is whether he is bound to fulfill that vow even when the condition has not been fulfilled. This parallels what happens in contracts and manumissions proposed under a condition. It is proved to the contrary that whether the condition is fulfilled or not, he is still bound to fulfill what he vowed. For the Apostle says: “We do not know how to pray as we ought.” When this person prays that God grant him this, he does not know whether God will grant it in the way he intends — for perhaps God does not grant what he asks, and if he grants something else, he may be conferring something more beneficial; and if he places him in that position, he deals more generously with him than if he had given him exactly what he requested. And all the more is he then bound to fulfillment of the vow — that is, when God gives him what he asked — and he is bound to it both when he gives what was asked and even when he does not.
