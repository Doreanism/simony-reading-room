## XX.

\[The text\] establishes duplicity. It should also be noted that there are several cases in which an oath is quasi-administered for two distinct purposes. The first is for preserving peace. The second is for confirming a treaty. The third is for confirming the truth of a claim. The fourth is for making the innocence of a person manifest, especially when witnesses are lacking. These purposes are found at C. 21 q. 1, c. “Omne quod.” But beyond these there are several more species of oath, such as the oath against calumny, of security, of impunity, against disobedience, and against infidelity. But it is known that without an oath no one can swear in two ways: either with intent, or the person swearing cannot be absolved from his unworthy dignity — he is bound to obey in these matters, even if never in unlawful ones.

Indeed, by virtue of his dignity or by an oath sworn to an archdeacon or bishop at the start of fidelity, while the bishop performs and expresses the exhibition of fidelity — although Pope Gelasius appears to assert the contrary at C. 25 q. 6, c. “Almus,” where he says that the Roman pontiff deposed the king of the Franks because he was unjustly in the kingdom, established another Catholic as patriarch in his place, and absolved all the Franks from the oath of fidelity. By these canons the lord pope did this because \[the king\] was in many ways \[deficient\] in the kingdom. The lord pope can do this regarding any prince or Pilate who \[holds power unjustly\], and he can even cut him down like a barren fig tree and absolve all subjects from the oath of fidelity. This pertains to the person and dignity of the oath and the form of the sacrament, as set forth in the epistle at C. 22 q. last, c. “Reforma fidelitatis,” where it says that whoever swears fidelity to his lord must always keep these things in mind: his security, safety, honor, utility, possibility, and ease — and that he should understand nothing in the oath that is contrary to the security or honor of his lord.
