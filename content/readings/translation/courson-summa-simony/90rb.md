Perplexity of law is said to occur when authorities or canons seem on the surface to contradict one another and cannot be reconciled by any rule of law; but in that sense no one can ever be in genuine perplexity, for no one can be in a state in which the law does not come to his aid and he cannot obtain the grace needed to dispel all perplexity. But perplexity of fact is numerous in its forms, as Gregorius shows in the aforesaid chapter, saying: \[illegible\] perplexities there are. A man assailed by an exposed vice has built such a resolve in his soul that, will he or not, he commits acts of sin; and for him the counsel of Gregorius applies — namely, that he should choose the lesser evil and avoid the greater. This perplexity is well known, and it appears in the situation of those who treat the property of others as their own, who serve the state more than Christ, and in the condition of mercenaries and merchants. Consider one who enters a cloister and finds therein excessive abuses, plunder, and the extortion of exactors; perplexity confronts him, for if he remains he will, will he or not, become a partner through his association with those who live there, partaking of their plunder and running with thieves; yet if he departs he will immediately be excommunicated by the abbot as a fugitive and shunned by all the monks.
