and \[yet some\] are not to be repelled — as when one is the killer of his wife. Pope Calixtus condemns \[the contrary\]: accusers or suspicious witnesses may not be received; neither members of the household nor those proceeding from the home or by blood of the accuser may testify against them \[the accused\], since the affection of familiarity and proximity and kinship is accustomed to impede the truth — and natural love, and the love of fear, and avarice frequently \[cloud\] human \[illegible\] senses and \[illegible\] opinions, \[illegible\] thinking many things, and those who expect money as a reward. I concede that such persons — if they were to be admitted — would thus \[be\] as witnesses. Item, the argument in favor is from that psalm of the Psalmist: Peter says, “Since you \[illegible\] recite my miseries,” and \[illegible\] was the argument from that canon of the Apostle, “who preaches but \[illegible\].” Since voices \[otherwise\] are sent only for \[mercenary purposes\], we resolve from the rule: those who are \[illegible\] infamous or brought to testimony — nor such as \[the law\] condemns — in all things it is established concerning all persons in common, except in all cases of simony, and of heresy, and of lèse-majesté — and in these \[the law\] denies to his own \[parties\] or to none the license of accusing. As is contained at C. 3 q. 5, approximately throughout. But if blood-relations were in a remote degree of consanguinity, or such persons — whether of your household or not — who were \[relatives\] of all parties and are unwilling to testify — against this argument: these must be admitted. And it judges that they are to be admitted up to a fixed end. As the argument from C. 3 q. 6, c. “Leges” \[shows\]: religious persons can \[testify\] and neither through blood nor \[otherwise\] — anything that pertains to testimony on behalf of you or your blood-relative, even so is not to be repelled. Item, it was cautiously established that if it is a public cause, there the cause is always pleaded.
