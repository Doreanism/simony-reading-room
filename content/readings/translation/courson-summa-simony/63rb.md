\[the community’s\] common animals pastured by shepherds are said to be subject to tithes on their animals and on all that is renewed from the \[parish\] by right, since predial tithes — not personal ones — are to be demanded from those in that parish, as we preach. Moreover, tithes in the hands of laymen are not to be redeemed, as it is evident \[from the canon that\] where it is established that tithes which the people refuse to pay should not be redeemed from them for any payment. As we read in *C.* 13 *q.*, ch. *Decimas*; *C.* 3 *q.* 3, ch. *Questum*. But we resolve that conciliar decree as follows: it is to be prohibited that any such redemption take place; rather the Church ought to defend laymen by law lest they redeem many \[tithes\] — but the one who would redeem tithes ought more properly to be forbidden from receiving anything by force or by deceit. Pressing further: any person is bound to pay first-fruits just as tithes. And \[we say\] that this is not to be required of everyone in the Church by strict command rather than by testimony. First-fruits are the first portion of the crop owed to God, which consists, as Hieronymus says, in a fiftieth or sixtieth part: namely, that one who gave a fiftieth would give a greater \[portion\], and similarly one who gave a sixtieth would give a smaller first-fruit; one who gave a median proportion gave a middling amount according to Hieronymus. Now first-fruits were generally to be given just as tithes were given. For they were a precept of the Old Testament just as tithes were. And similarly they were prescribed in the canons, as in *C.* 15 *q.* 1, *Revertamini*; ch. *Doctus*, and moreover the commandment concerning first-fruits is moral and immovable. Since no constitution has been issued in council \[to the contrary\], they ought to be paid just as tithes. But objection: why then pay first-fruits?
