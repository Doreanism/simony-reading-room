I reply that they ought not to depart from their undertaking to stand by the arbiters’ decision \[even\] against the will \[of the other party\], and if they thought the sentence was thus void, it would be voided. As for what the arbiters say — “We say this for the sake of good peace” — this venom concealed in the dregs has deceived many simple souls. But if someone litigates there and awaits the most just calculation according to the allegations and the \[case as\] adjudicated, and the dignity or prebend is awarded according to juridical examination — weighing the cause in the balance — provided that no money, kinship, promise, or some good inducement was brought forward to attract the mind of the arbiter, \[the ruling stands\]. Likewise, regarding the rescript by which, proceeding first through admonitions, second through citations, third through excommunications, someone is directed to some ecclesiastical benefice: we say that if these rescripts are obtained without payment, the \[claim\] has no force \[unless\] the lord pope committed it \[to judges\] — or unless \[the person\] is such that by his own authority he can weigh the merit of the needy church and remove \[the unworthy\] rather than let \[matters\] fester without being questioned. If it be objected that the lord pope has the fullness of power and can in all things confer ecclesiastical benefices as he wills, we reply that he does not have license to abuse \[his power\], nor does he have license to deviate in anything contrary to the New or the Old Testament. Nor can he remove from the law that canon which prohibits him from conferring a benefice except upon a vacancy — for this canon has its origin from that passage of the \[Old\] Testament where also the Lord appointed Eleazar to the Jewish priestly offices after the death of his father. As for those who at the mandate of prelates or bishops run and re-run to Rome indiscriminately, transacting illicit business: we say that those who thus extort prebends through such undue services — partly due, partly undue — incur simony, and are the cursed animals in the Law that neither chew the cud nor have a cloven hoof, for they do not divide \[good from evil\] through discernment.
