If, then, for fraternal hatred all previously remitted sins return — by the same reasoning, by the same reasoning all previously remitted equivalents return; and so for any other \[sin\] they return.

Likewise, another opinion is refuted thus: it judges that for equal sins equal \[sins return\]; from greater, lesser \[sins return\]. It is established: two simple fornications equal one completed adultery. If for one adultery committed in this manner another adultery would return — one that had previously been committed — then by the same reasoning for those two fornications which are equivalent: from lesser sins greater return. Another opinion is taken up: that in the ingrate the sin returns. It is indeed established in the ingrate that those things which returned in place of all sins \[are there\]. It is established that either the punishment or the stain of sin against the ingrate is such a thing as to deserve eternal punishment for anything whatsoever — and a mortal sin. I ask how this \[idea\] modifies sin, since every sin, properly speaking, is a word, deed, or desire contrary to the law of God. Therefore the same thing is contained in the ingrate as sin — either a word, deed, or desire contrary to the law of God. Likewise, it is proved that every sin is in the ingrate: because in the ingrate the maximum evil fully returns. The lesser ingratitude is not to return good for good, what you bestow on us — and in this we incur the greatest ingratitude.

Likewise, to render back to God less than good for good — that is, by ceasing from the good works which we ought to do — we incur a lesser ingratitude toward every sin or offense, namely the ingratitude we commit by omission toward every mortal sin in which we die — which we grant: as by an extension of vocabulary, every sin is pride. Where Master Stephanus Belvacentis and others say that sins return — that a certain proneness to sinning returns — which he showed thus: when someone fights for a long time against lust or another virtue, and turns back and is confirmed in adultery, he becomes more prompt in sinning; hence such a proneness is acquired \[illegible\]
