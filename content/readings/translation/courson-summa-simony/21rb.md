He ought not to commute one punishment into another; and he ought not to impose upon anyone restitution \[commuted\] into the pledge of a Jerusalem crusade, nor is it permitted to leave any mark of leprosy, about which the Lord says in Leviticus: “The garment and everything in which leprosy is found shall defile the priest and shall be burned in flames.” Likewise, princes and soldiers who take the cross choose greater or lesser priests to redeem \[themselves from\] tithes, tallages, and exactions, and \[to secure release from\] their protesting wives. This proves that the Church has no authority here to act \[on their behalf\], since it cannot dispense against the Old Testament or the New. For it says in Leviticus: “If they have offered a ram by error, let the one who has caused the harm make restitution and add a fifth part, placing it on the altar and giving it to the priest, who shall pray for him.” And further: “Let complete restitution be made for all the frauds that anyone has wished to commit against his fellow.” And likewise in the Gospel, Zacchaeus says: “If I have defrauded anyone, I restore fourfold.” Let us say, then, that no relaxation or license or pledge is of any worth unless they first make restitution for their spoliation to the extent they are able; and then they have something \[to offer\] by way of meeting their counter-obligations — not through arms but through devout supplications. But if they have anything that they can bring with them, let them offer it to those they have despoiled; and then, if without any violence or fear those \[persons\] are unwilling to accept anything, they \[the crusaders\] may receive \[absolution\]; otherwise not. Regarding the protesting wife, we say that she can retain her husband against his wish to depart from her; but if, for the sake of his soul’s progress — namely, for two or three years’ \[absence\] and with the intention of returning — he declares that he will return within that time, his wife ought to give him permission, especially in view of the general \[consent of the\] Church, just as she would give him permission to go to Saint James \[of Compostela\] or to Saint Giles. It should also be known that, because the merit of the martyrs redeems everything, and because one who takes the cross is, as it were, a martyr — since he has left all things for Christ and exposes himself to martyrdom — a special indulgence should therefore be granted to him for the col\[lection of tithes\].
