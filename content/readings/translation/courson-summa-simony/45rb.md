Likewise, the seal \[bearing\] the image of a bishop is not imputed to the bishop personally — \[illegible\] — and to sell it is like selling him himself and his \[authority and\] form. Likewise, if selling this is simoniacal, then a notary commits simony when he sells his notarial note \[charter\]. Hence Gregorius says in his Register that a notary ought to sell his pen \[i.e., the instrument of his labor, not his spiritual service\]. But if anyone freely gives something after the notary’s work — that is, small gifts — well and good, so that he receives \[the payment\] the same way and \[performs\] the business. On that account, receive from someone who gives you wax, parchment, or ink, since anyone may sell these; he receives \[payment\] for his labor at a just price, as any other copyist or scribe. As for Gregorius saying that a notary ought to sell his pen — this is said because such notaries are accustomed to impose heavy exactions for such things. Similarly, let us speak of all the offices and appendages that are annexed to the major offices — such as deacons and subdeacons who minister at the altar, who carry the paten, the chalice, and the incense thurible — and who can also sell these offices by that \[rule\]: every such one \[illegible\], for that canon applies in all those things that are immediately annexed to spiritual \[offices\]. Already we could sell the fruits of a prebend or of tithes, which are \[now thus\] annexed; and since we could sell those things, \[we can sell\] that. But by no means does the pope allow this where two things are so annexed that both sides are spiritual. These are: a prebend or an episcopate or an archdeaconate and the duty to be performed that pertains to such a prebend — for instance, to one prebend belongs the duty of singing in choir or ministering at the altar or reading the Gospel and the like.
