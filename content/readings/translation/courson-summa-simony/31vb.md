Likewise, suppose the bishop excommunicates the archdeacon thus: “I excommunicate you if that archdeacon excommunicates his subject.” Likewise, suppose that archdeacon departs and excommunicates his subject: it follows that that archdeacon is excommunicated. He is so much excommunicated that, as the canon says, he who is excommunicated is \[thereby\] excommunicated. But this condition having been met, the archdeacon \[is about\] to excommunicate; and the bishop’s excommunication holds, and the bishop excommunicates the archdeacon, and the archdeacon is excommunicated, and \[the effect\] is not communicated, and the stated matter is corrected. Solution. Granted that the said excommunication holds, it must be said that action and passion are not always simultaneous. Sources begot Plato: for Plato is generated by the source and with the vein inhering in the \[source\]; yet in the meantime it is the recipient who receives. For the bishop excommunicates the archdeacon conditionally; nevertheless the archdeacon is excommunicated, but the time after this will be when he is excommunicated, and now at last he is excommunicated. Likewise, suppose it is said to the \[bishop\] of Pisa: “I excommunicate you if the \[bishop\] of Chartres excommunicates.” Similarly it is said to the \[bishop\] of Chartres: “I excommunicate you if the \[bishop\] of Pisa does not excommunicate.” Between them: is he excommunicated by the one or by neither of the two? If by the one and not by the other — what is the reason? For the same \[formula\] is stated to each. And what if both excommunicate? If neither excommunicates, the \[bishop\] of Chartres has not excommunicated — for the condition \[for Pisa’s excommunication\] is: “if the \[bishop\] of Chartres excommunicates,” and the \[bishop\] of Pisa excommunicates. But if \[Pisa\] excommunicates, and yet neither has excommunicated under this condition — “if no one excommunicates” — but such a condition does not obtain, then neither is excommunicated. Solution.
