### On the species of the jocose lie

What follows concerns that species of the jocose lie which without reason \[illegible\] to deceive through play, concerning which Augustinus says that if it is done with great fault — like something from which no fault at all arises from joking — but from the contrary, that is, from an idle word. Every playful word is idle that serves no benefit to the speaker or hearer. Likewise, lest one sin \[illegible\] and \[illegible\] that what is false does not proceed from a pure intent \[illegible\], which tends not toward another’s deception but rather toward recreation — one who says a falsehood. I ask also whether one who idly and jokingly says a falsehood without intent to deceive sins more than one who idly and jokingly says a jest without intent to deceive. Likewise: someone idly and jokingly speaks \[illegible\] or something of the sort, not wishing to lead anyone into error — and someone who hears him believes he is speaking in jest, and looks elsewhere \[illegible\] to his own detriment. If this happens, he has deceived jokingly and in a manner liable to \[cause\] joking; and such a one sins more than if he had idly said a jest or \[spoken\] otherwise idly. We grant this; yet he sins only venially, not intending to deceive anyone; indeed, he even does a greater injury in a more important sense; and because a falsehood in its own kind is such that it more readily draws toward malice than jest, therefore one who says a falsehood idly and jokingly sins more than one who says a jest idly.
