and lawfully you ate the food sacrificed to idols, and lawfully you reclaimed \[your goods\] from the one under judgment, and lawfully you accepted support in your preaching; and you should act as if you have no other way \[*unde*\] by which to make yourself expedient for this. And if this is lawful, you ought thus to be a free volunteer when Gregorius says to you, a contrite one: “The unmerciful man — nay, the cruel one — is one for whom whatever is lawful is also pleasing.” Against the first case, the objection is made as follows: it is the custom in many churches that clerics and monks eat meat on Saturday; you are considered learned and of great authority and do not eat with them. The whole city is scandalized at seeing you eat meat on that day in the assembly of clerics and monks, since laypeople by no means eat meat on that day; if you eat meat on that day with the others, there will be scandal from you — because you are of such reputation among them that by your eating the scandal will greatly increase; if you do not eat, you offend the abbot or bishop and the whole assembly of clerics and monks who call you a Cathar or a bigot. What will you do in this situation? I have seen \[illegible\] monks, like infernal pigs, on Saturday devouring meat to the point of nausea, to the scandal of all humanity, and on Friday \[consuming\] fatty broth and liquid meats — namely, bacon, up to the snare \[of damnation\] — and the great dish likewise on Saturday; and since no meat could be found for sale in the market, they even built a new butcher’s stall of \[their own\] and devoured the choicest meats to the ridicule of all the clergy; and the Apostle \[himself\] often saw that this suited those very prelates who are \[supposedly\] senators, or the plunderers, or the simoniacs, who offer their bastard sons their prebends in the Church as their gifts — their salmon, their water-wolves \[pike\], \[illegible\] — which they set before those reclining \[at table\] like \[illegible\] in immense quantities; and there remain from those things which are almost as food sacrificed to idols that you eat — because your simoniacal patron, who is like Dagon —
