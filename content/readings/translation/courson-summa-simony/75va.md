And it is clear that it would be to proceed further and more steeply to treat here the matters that pertain to the general cure of bishops or priests. And what shall we say of the ancient princes who installed bishops and conferred prebends, and of those who, when sees are vacant, confiscate the goods of churches and wrongfully seize the goods of the poor, which should be reserved for the future bishop? To this it seems to us that the answer must be given that neither a prince nor any layman ought to confer any ecclesiastical benefice or prelacy on another. And the sole right of the patron is this: that the patron, by the concession of the Gospel and by divine grace, should present a worthy person to the chapter by \[authority of\] the lord pope. For the chapter or the metropolitan \[then\] installs him as bishop. For presentation is not the same as donation. It is analogous to matrimonial consent or the assignment of a donation. Regarding princes who confiscate the goods of vacant sees, we consider them in the same \[category\] as those who sell those churches together with the right of patronage: of neither group do we find any who leave \[anything\] behind.

It is further to be noted that the right of patronage may be simple or composite. From this it follows that \[a share of it\] does not belong to any of those who claim it \[individually\] — that it belongs to none of them — which can be proved as follows. Suppose that three \[co-patrons\] possess that prebend whose patronage is to be exercised, and \[suppose\] it must be held by a fourth and that they ought to present a cleric to the bishop: \[the question arises\] whether all the co-patrons must do so or not; if not, whether one or two suffice. There is no greater reason for one to do so rather than another, nor more reason for two than for one — the conclusion follows that all must act together as one and present \[the candidate\] of necessity. Likewise the situation arises in which no one \[individually\] can do what must be done, as when there is an unjust contest, and yet \[the right belongs to\] several — but to none of them individually — just as in \[the analogy of\] carrying a stone: where someone carries a stone in common with others, the carrying belongs to several together and yet to none of them individually on account of the shared partnership.
