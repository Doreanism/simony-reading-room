And suppose the one who has given money to the bishop afterward freely confers one prebend on a worthy and learned person: if by chance the prebend was obtained as a result of the money — insofar as, had he first given money to the bishop and the bishop had pretended otherwise, \[the recipient\] would have obtained it after the money was withdrawn — then the money served as the principal cause of that collation, especially since others who had previously paid money would not have \[otherwise\] freely conferred that prebend on anyone. And so he commits simony. Solution: as we say — or as the teaching goes — those who then condemn the stain of simony by their efforts \[should urge\] the five simoniacs to freely confer the prebends they customarily sell. \[This\] cannot come to pass where the plain case of money given to the bishop is the \[sole\] cause, since the prior love of \[money\] would compel him to sell the prebends. And if he were in a greater \[moral\] peril, simony would remain in this case as well; for whatever is done in such a promulgation or grant of money is done for God himself and for the common benefit of the church — as the principal \[concern\] of the prelate in question — and then he confers the prebend.

Concerning the \[distinction\] by degree: it is twofold — in this way he confers a prebend from such a bishop in the best removal \[of the abuser\] from the money. For if this option designates a remote and secondary cause, without which the \[offer\] would nonetheless proceed, yet is not the principal cause — then \[the assessment of simony\] does not hold.
