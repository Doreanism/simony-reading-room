\[Fixed\] revenues: according to that Gospel text, “nothing beyond what was first enjoined in truth.” But what shall we say about what habitually occurs today whenever a prince wages war and demands unjust levies \[*firmae*\] as subsidies from all who are under abbots and bishops — and if they refuse, he seizes them by force and inflicts greater damages on those who deny him — and so the Church is tallaged for the use of the royal war against her own \[dependants\], which is plunder, as I shall call this exaction, and they cannot absolve themselves in this way, except at first through a mixture \[of means\]. And yet they contend that they are defending Church goods by every means. And they are bound by ecclesiastical censure to restrain princes from seizing the goods of churches, which belong to the poor. Furthermore, the prelates themselves, though they fraternize with those who seize anything, must then make restitution in peace to the Church. We grant this without distinction, saying that all these things arise from the full agreement of the community. For the prelate in whose territory the minister opposes his power to the harm of goods must stand against this, lest any subsidies for wars be furnished from the Church; and just as branches mutually burden the churches, the prelate can then say: “Neither my iniquity nor my sin.” As for what \[actually\] occurs: it is asked whether in any case \[a prince\] may exercise tallage upon his subjects — the answer is no, because every tallage is a species of plunder, and in general a mixture of plunder, as stated above.

### On the form of tallages

Collection in the Lateran Council. “They heard themselves \[striving\] in that \[ruling\] on truth. The Apostle: ‘Let tribute be rendered to whom tribute is due.’” However, \[the council says that those who are\] necessary and manifest \[causes for war\] should give moderately and charitably, as a help in obtaining \[what is needed\]. From this passage we argue on the basis of price that those who were bound to pay tallages should be distinguished as follows. Some tallages were those of custom or law by which \[subjects\] were bound. Other tallages were simulated benevolences, which they call liberalities; prelates exercise such tallages upon factors, and lords upon churches, and abbots upon many \[dependants\].
