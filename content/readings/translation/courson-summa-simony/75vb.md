Among the more frequent \[duties\] in the first place: what pertains to the correction of a brother — and that every person is bound to correct a brother — is established by multiple proofs. First, that evangelical precept: “You shall love your neighbor as yourself.” And the gloss says: “as yourself” — that is, in the same way that if you yourself were in error you would want a companion to instruct you, so you must take up that same care and instruct \[him\]. Likewise, this is supported by what is said in Deuteronomy: “you shall not pass by your brother’s ass lying under its burden, but shall help to lift it up.” He is your brother, and to relieve \[him\] is in some sense \[your duty\]. Likewise every person is bound to the multiplication of the talent. Just as Gregorius says: understand by the word “talent” every good thing bestowed; and when you have the talent of teaching and correction, to hold it back — impeding it through self-interest, withdrawing \[counsel\] from someone in error — you will incur that curse: “Wicked servant, you did not seek to return that talent to the table.” Or \[the curse\] concerning declared error. Likewise \[the argument from\] diminution and condemnation says: “Surely you did not give them food to eat,” and so forth. condemned by Christ to eternal fire — how much more \[must we care for\] the soul than the body, and how much greater is the poverty of the soul than that of the body, and how much more grave the infirmity of the soul than of the body. And if you have one loaf and give half of it to a brother in \[illegible\] infirmity — \[for his\] health and hospitality — and you allow that same \[soul\] to die, you incur the same condemnation with which the Lord charges that other. Likewise Augustinus says: not wishing to feed the great \[flock\] and leaving them in disorder — it is the greatest \[sin\] to neglect the feeding of souls, since the bishop cares nothing for them.
