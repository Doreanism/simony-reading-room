The substitute ought to be as good as he whose place he takes; and just as a good school-master is bound to provide what is necessary, so the substitute in his place must serve without those exactions — serving in place of him, that is, so as to seek no \[fees\] from his pupils, either by himself or through another, through such or other exactions. Further, it is asked whether such a person sins when he sells the governance of schools, while he is himself bound to govern them, or imputes expenses and rudiments to an uncertain \[party\]. Also, what of one who enters \[the arrangement\] on the understanding that he sells them in multiple ways, or declares himself less than sufficient and holds that the governance is either spiritual or annexed to a spiritual office? Solution: Let us say that school-masters who have been assigned revenues on the condition that they teach and summon others to teach — or else relinquish the revenues — may lawfully be excused if there is a just and reasonable cause: for instance, if they have become infirm, or if they have been sent to the curia or elsewhere for a greater affair of the church, or if they are insufficient \[for the task\] — in which case they must either relinquish the office entirely, or with the permission of the bishop or chapter be absent for a time, and place in their stead a sufficient person, keeping him free of expenses and not making collections upon those whom they are gravely bound \[to teach freely\]. But if they were to sell the governance of the schools, they commit simony. But if instead, cleverly, they receive an extortioner in their place, they commit extortions and are bound to make restitution of everything that their substitute exacts at their command. Let us consider whether the mastership of schools carries with it something that obliges him to assign to his cantor \[portions\] for reading, or whether he may hire out his services. We also note whether he ought to take over the governance in a better manner. It is also asked whether stipendiary priests may hire out their services. It is established that from such an arrangement they contract neither filth nor use \[of it\].
