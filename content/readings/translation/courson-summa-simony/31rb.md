Likewise, just as in secular matters it is commonly said that a horse may be possessed by two owners — or rather by neither — so in certain cases within a given time it happens that a single parish is held by two priests, or \[one holds\] this cure in fee \[in feudum\]. We have similarly heard of the case in the English church of the parson and vicar who govern it jointly, as the principal holders, forming ties of this cure of souls together for the same \[church\]. It happens that the vicar excommunicates someone over the parson’s objection. This sentence either holds or does not. If it holds, then it is ratified by his right; \[illegible\] we would wish the parson to hold it in the same way — and by the same reasoning, if the parson excommunicates it holds, because neither is above the other. But what if, as it happens, the parson holds that the vicar can do nothing either in binding or in loosing, that nothing has been delegated to the vicar or that he has absolved, since that which the vicar has he has from the bishop, not from the parson, since such an office can be conferred upon the vicar without the parson’s authority?

On this point we say \[illegible\] that no parish cure ought to be given to two priests; but \[illegible\] the principal \[holder\] is given the cure, or the subordinate \[filial\] has something, he has it from someone as from a superior — just as the bishop is superior to the archdeacon, and what \[illegible\] the archdeacon’s \[power\] to take — or else the entire cure and the authority of binding and loosing under that parson is conferred upon the vicar, and then so. But that two priests cannot hold one parochial \[church\] jointly in full \[in solidum\] we hold established in the decrees, since thus the church would become a sort of brothel \[sororium\]. But if someone among the said parishioners holds \[property\] under two lords, he can be bound and absolved by different priests, as a parishioner of different priests.
