### xxxiii.

Concerning this, the pain of extreme indignation presses upon us — that these men live a life defiled by abuse, and are promoted through association with the same \[vice\] — and the more perversely they snatch at honors, the more severely they will be punished with extreme condemnation. Someone raises a question: You are a canon in some church, and if you knew that such a person was to be promoted but you nonetheless refused to consent to his promotion — if you withhold consent, you are guilty rather than a corrector. If you remain silent, he will be promoted to the subversion of the whole Church. The solution: I shall first show \[the case of\] those who purchase \[offices by simony\] and who can most effectively obstruct \[his promotion\], before exposing him rather than promoting him.

### Whether it is said that clergy may have private property, and whether a cleric \[may have\] one \[thing\].

### \[xxxii.\]

Now first it is \[asked\] whether \[a cleric\] can make a will, and if he gives the goods of the Church to others than the poor, whether he is to be considered a plunderer. That a cleric has nothing of his own can be shown from the authority of Hieronymus, who says: “If a cleric has anything of his own, his portion is not the Lord.” But if he possessed gold, silver, estates, or varied household goods — with those portions the Lord disdains to be his portion — and this is signified by the name of clerk, derived from *cleros*, which means “lot,” as is provided
