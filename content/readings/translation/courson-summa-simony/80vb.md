that a solemn vow does not bind more before God than a simple one, and that the solemnities tend to render the effect of the vow if it is fulfilled. For insofar as an appended solemnity binds more — as the canon says — than a simple \[vow\] or even a simple assertion, in the same degree the solemn vow binds more than the simple one. We grant this other \[point\], saying that the solemnities in a vow bind the person who vows more to the carrying out of the vow, because they intensify the effect of the vow and its nature as regards a greater intention; and they make the transgressor of the vow more guilty. And particular solemnities are perpetually committed in vows — namely, the reception of orders, which imprints a character; and a character, once it has been conferred upon someone, cannot be taken away from him \[even if\] he carries out \[the functions\] of that order. And in the first instance \[at tonsure\] he loses the general \[standing\] of a layman, because he becomes subject to the jurisdiction of an ecclesiastical judge. And because an order once conferred cannot be restricted for a \[monetary\] price, and continence is annexed to it by the institution of the church — it is clear that the reception of orders renders the effect of the vow definitive. And we say the same of religious profession, to which continence is likewise annexed; and by the reception of the habit this vow of continence is further confirmed and rendered definitive. For since \[the bearer of\] two characters has no room for any dispensation, it is evident that those who have once been tonsured as cleric or as monk, if they subsequently return to the lay state and change their \[tonsured\] hair, are to be excommunicated — as the canon says at cause xx, q. iii, cap. “cos qui semel.” There are also other solemnities in vows that do admit of dispensation for a known cause — as in the vow of pilgrimage, and the taking of the cross, and in vows made in the face of the church before the altar, or in the hands of a priest or abbot. Such vows bind more than private ones — which is proved by inference: because the transgression of such vows offends God and the church more than the transgression of a private vow. And the solemn vow binds more than the private one — which we grant.
