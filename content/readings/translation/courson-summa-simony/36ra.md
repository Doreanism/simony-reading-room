But \[there is\] the coin of Caesar, with which I demonstrate: “Whose is it?” The answer: “Caesar’s.” “Render therefore to Caesar the things that are Caesar’s” — and so for this \[reason\] these things. Unless the prince designates armed requests, the first of the prince’s designates armed requests; he affixes his seal, or his advantage, and this coin likewise makes simony. The third coin is that of the common people or of a powerful man of repute, by which some benefit is conferred simoniacally. The fourth coin is that of obscenity — that \[which is\] infected, corrupted, and befouled — \[given\] in exchange for any impure price to confer a spiritual thing: simony. This is the coin of flattery, of accumulated flatteries, of the depravity of persons, of shameful acts, of temples and of panderers. The fifth coin is that of greed, which pertains broadly to every temporal gain.

Toward this the prelate directs his eye in conferring \[benefices\] on friends, for a benefit is given either for counted money or for other \[goods\] presented — whether for the church’s \[future\] conferral of \[suffrages\] like masses and offices, or for a loan to be provided, or for any other temporal profit. For here indeed the money-changers set out their coins and their currency in the cloister, in the schools, in the marketplace, in the choir, in the court, already in the bull and in every place of lordship. And certain ones in the schools of the fine arts, at least, or led astray by deceptions, draw to themselves these money-changers within \[the Church\], and in whatever service of whomever — whether due or undue — \[they seek\] spiritual benefits. Regarding this it is asked whether all such persons incur the stain of simony.
