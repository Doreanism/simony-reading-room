Furthermore, some people collect all their money into one great purse of body and soul. A certain prelate puts a prebend up for sale. If you give your purse as the price, you commit simony — all the more so if you have put both your body and your soul \[into it\], which we concede falls into vileness. Likewise, there is a question about doorkeepers \[custodians\] whether one who takes them in under this pact — \[namely, that\] others are to be admitted \[on the same terms\] — incurs the taint of simony. Suppose the Tarcians \[a group\] institute their custodians to observe the customary \[dues\] of the priests, so that all of this kind of thing is taken from them — namely, that no one is admitted without this sum of money. If you therefore make your preparations with less money, you will receive for lesser oblations and obsequies and even for nothing those things that are spiritual, and you commit simony with \[the exchange being\] too much \[illegible\]. For John says that he is the door, and “whosoever climbeth up by another way, the same is a thief and a robber.” The same question applies to the masters of Paris who admit people to the fraternity of the hospital of Saint Thomas under this sum of money. Solution. We say, as has been said above regarding those who contribute money from their purse to a monastery: just as there is manifest simony in that case if the pact precedes the payment, so it is here — if the pact precedes the money. But such fraternities may be admitted lawfully if \[candidates\] enter principally for God and by a free act of admission; for it is lawful for anyone to contribute from his own goods as much as he wishes and as devotion requires. Or a brother of the devotional \[fraternity\] who is thus admitted may sell one seal \[i.e., a token of membership\]. Likewise, regarding sellers of the seals of prelates and princes — that these commit simony \[is argued\] by the decree of Pope Pelagius: if anyone sells one of these things without which the remaining \[thing\] cannot be had separately, he \[thereby effectively\] sells neither
