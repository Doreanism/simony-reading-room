### Solution

It is to be said that this is generally true in this way: that an exception is granted to every defendant — because it is not lawful for the excommunicated \[to proceed as plaintiff\] unless we see it in all excepted \[cases\]. But where in the Decretum it is said — as Hugutio says — that the excommunicated can oppose or propose an exception, this means: oppose a \[plea\] as a defendant so as to avoid proceeding on a claim, not propose an exception which would hurt him as a party acting or opposing another as plaintiff. Likewise the question is raised: whether an excommunicated person can be sued by another, and it seems that his condition is much preferable to that of the absolved, since the absolved is held to \[respond\]. Likewise the Apostle says: a servant either stands or falls \[before\] his lord, and an excommunicated servant is protected on behalf of his lord if he absolves him, or falls if he accuses him. Just as a lord can sue his own excommunicated servant: to this let us say that he can sue him, which is apparent from the fact that one can give him silver, because anyone can also \[deal with\] an excommunicated person for the sake of correction in cases where trust fails.
