By the first coin: \[one who\] offers \[illegible\] favor commits simony on the part of the giver or on the part of the receiver. By the second: one who commits simony by service, on the part of both the giver and receiver, if it is unjustly conferred or unjustly administered — as in the case of flattery. Likewise injustice. Likewise advocacy of slander. Likewise monetary profit or the exaction of the poor. Likewise complaint. Likewise defamation or calumny. Likewise any other illicit thing done for the favor of the prelate. For the last coin, simony is committed on the part of the giver who has the fold of very great greed in the eye of the mind. On the part of the receiver, similarly with this corrupt intention — whether on account of faith undeservedly \[placed\], or from this party or from that party — there simony is present. To the cleric or chaplain who serves the prelate, we say that if they serve in those things they owe by right and with many obligations and full pledge \[illegible\] — and \[the prelate\] confers \[the benefit\] and a prebend on him — there is no simony, because the service that is entirely owed does not render a contract of buying and selling. But if they serve indiscriminately for illicit or alien \[purposes\] and are thereby drawn into \[commitments\] to obtain in the future the favor of others — and if at length they are aided in obtaining a prebend — it is clear that they incur the simoniacal stain through solicitation and undue service. And for a fuller understanding of this:

It should be noted that there is a twofold pact: one that is always prior in purpose, by which he who enriches \[another says\]: “I will serve you” — with this lying compact, whoever says “I will love you and you will provide for me first from your brotherhood in such a matter” commits simony with this pact. The other pact is one that is done either prior in purpose or \[in this way\] and likewise commits simony. A cleric may, however, say to a prelate: “I will serve you in all things as such and as you” — \[serving as\] a coadjutor summoned to share in the burden while the principal holds the fullness of power; he himself serves others, but \[he claims\] a benefice of secondary right \[that is\] defined.
