Solution. To the first point we say that a spiritual thing may be converted into a spiritual harm, as that canon *extra de examinatione, intelleximus* argues. Moreover, \[the text\] says that each person ought to remain in the calling in which he was called and not look back, but rather stand bound fast to virtue. Hence one who falls from the dignity of an episcopate or archiepiscopate should be noted \[as obliged\] to carry out the good he formerly possessed, and even more.

To the second point we say that there is a difference between buying and ransoming. Tithes cannot be sold or alienated, nor can a prebend; but they can be ransomed. For whatever reason, since they are already in the possession of the Church or of religious almshouses — to which rightful possession belongs — a bishop may laudably ransom from the hand of a layman what is his own — receiving \[this right back\] from the prelate or from the one under whom the layman holds the right of patronage. Regarding the transfer of persons to prebends, we say that one may assist another in a \[diseased\] condition by buying out that right of patronage. For it belongs to his office not to receive an unworthy person; yet it can be said that that lay right of presenting someone may be lawfully sold by the original holder to the one to whom the remaining right — namely, the right of investiture — belongs. This is done for the common utility and necessity of the Church, though he is accustomed to sell what he sells at the end \[of proceedings\] with humility.

On the other matter, we say that it is a future duty of a bishop — or lawfully of priests — to raise money to impede the promotion or the removal of plunderers.
