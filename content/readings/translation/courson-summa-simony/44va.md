Similarly, even after my investiture, \[there is a lawful oath\] for me to swear such things as where it says: “He shall never dare to obtain any security from ecclesiastical persons without prior \[consent\], so that whatever is directed may remain freely and openly \[in force\].” But care must be taken that in all such things one appends this due qualification: “preserving all the good customs of the church — except those that are contrary to God, or to the harm of the church, or to the injury of hope.” But if the oath is exacted in this form — “Swear that you will give satisfaction to whoever \[asks\], or that I will give a prebend to another” — we say that he who receives this prebend, if he places it first as the principal \[object\], \[directing his\] fruits to the prebend and only secondarily to God, and says in the aforesaid \[formula\] that there is \[thus\] simony — if he places God as the principal cause \[of his acceptance\], though secondarily keeping an eye on the fruits \[of the prebend\], there is no simony. Likewise, consider someone who travels to Rome either to ransom his right, or to litigate with an adversary, or to plead before the lord pope, or to obtain a rescript from certain judges directing that he receive some prebend. In these cases: either it is established that he has a right to a prebend or to some other benefice, and then he petitions regarding this. Many who have been drawn into the case cannot simply buy their right outright — they may only ransom it — for this is lawful for any poor person when his complaint reaches the highest \[authority\]. But if he litigates there, and the matter is genuinely doubtful in fact, and he begins \[with\] something uncertain under any \[arrangement of\] giving, retaining, or promising — in those cases \[simony\] has no place in such matters. For those things \[involved in\] going there \[to Rome\] may lawfully be given, retained, or promised.
