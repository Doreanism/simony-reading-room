## x. A further, more precise treatment of that difference between a private and a solemn vow: for in asserting what is solemn, nearly all hold that a private vow prevents a marriage from being contracted, but does not dissolve one already contracted.

They hold that the solemn vow both prevents a marriage from being contracted and dissolves one already contracted, as Iohannes asserts at dist. xxvii. Suppose now that someone has vowed private continence in words of the present tense, and then seeks the conjugal debt. Here the question is whether he is obliged by the law of marriage to render the debt, or to preserve the continence he had previously vowed. By this question other consequent questions are understood. There are here two bonds — the vow and the bond of marriage. The question is which of the two is stronger. It seems that the first — namely, the bond of the vow — is by far the stronger; because that bond binds her to God, and the bond of marriage to a spouse. For we have more faithful enemies against the fear of marriage, and we ought to obey God more than men. And the bond of the vow binds more strongly than that of marriage. This is proved in another way by inference: the bond of the vow dissolves the bond of marriage in a certain case — namely, where one of the spouses, before carnal consummation, withdraws to the pursuit of a better life, over the objection of the other; and within the space of two months there remains

the opportunity for the other to contract with someone else — as Alexander shows in the title on the conversion of the married, in that decretal “et publico.” Moreover, this is proved to the contrary by the constitution of the canons, through the universal sources that are of greater authority in establishing this — namely, through the authorities of the Old and New \[Testaments\]. For Solomon says in Ecclesiastes: “If you have made a vow, delay not to render it, for it displeases God, and a foolish promise is \[to be avoided\]. It is better not to vow than after the vow’s promises not to render.” He says clearly that whoever makes a vow to God must needs fulfill it — and if she has privately vowed virginity, she is obligated to render it. Likewise, it is written in the Gospel and in the law concerning a woman: “You shall render to the Lord your oaths.” By the same precept you are bound to render your vows to the Lord, as is said in the Psalm: “I will render and \[fulfill\] my vows which my lips dis\[tinguished\]”
