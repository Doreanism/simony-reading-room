And Augustinus, on that passage in Matthew — “If he has sinned,” etc. — says: this applies to the lesser correcting the greater, the greater correcting the lesser, and equals correcting equals; and thus it pertains to all. ## He defines the watchman \[of the flock\]: Herod ordered the head of John to be carried in on a dish

\[illegible\] The gloss \[says\] that this \[act\] of Herod is to be witnessed against by all, since they \[all\] were rebuking Herod. Therefore almost all are guilty in this matter — as Augustinus testifies: all the lesser \[clergy\] are guilty if they have withdrawn into monasteries, since they were drawing back from \[confronting\] the obstinacy of the \[worldly\] pillars. For just as the lesser are bound to correct the greater, this is also argued from what is said in dist. xxiv, q. iii, “Platon,” concerning those who were princes \[illegible\]. Likewise \[illegible\] dist. \[illegible\], q. vii, “Quapropter”; dist. \[illegible\], q. i, \[illegible\]. That fraternal correction pertains to all has thus been established by a continuous \[chain of argument\]. But there is something special belonging to prelates — namely, to bring \[the matter\] to a greater authority when someone alone is caught \[in wrongdoing\]: the argument “if he has sinned” establishes this, because it first treats of all generally when it says, before that chapter, something about these little ones who are \[seeking\] me, and represents \[the matter\] by way of the individual case: “If he has sinned.” And then it appends something special concerning prelates in the words: “Whatever you shall bind on earth” — and from this we understand that this pertains to Peter and his successors. Likewise: \[Miriam\] was accursed because she \[illegible\] shame, and \[was struck with\] leprosy because she rebuked her brother unto death; the same leprosy and curse must be driven from whoever reproaches his prelate or his superior; and \[correction must proceed by\] the method of correction in secret first, then correction before witnesses, and finally the matter is to be brought before the Church. Likewise indirectly: if that precept pertains generally to all, then all monks and hermits would be bound to go out to rebuke the excesses of men; \[illegible\] they sin mortally. \[illegible\] since one is in mortal sin from such an omission when all are mute dogs in reproving.
