Alexander says that if in his letters mention is made of the \[prior\] letters of commission, they are valid; and the difference \[between\] letters of commission is useless \[when comparing\] less favorable to more favorable ones: the position on the letters of commission of one of lesser standing was \[overruled by\] \[those\] of greater authority preceding it. ### Solution.

The answer is also \[established\] in privileges: a special mandate derogates from a general one, whether or not mention of that \[general\] \[grant\] is made, as the constitution of Alexander states — that is, a special \[mandate\] never derogates from a \[later\] special \[one\]. Or whether to revoke the special one. Likewise, what a certain decretal states is argued: *mullovere lesas* \[i.e., *nolite laedere*\]. If however someone says that if one party had more favorable letters and the adversary afterwards obtained letters making mention of the prior ones — the one who had obtained the more favorable letters, then he who obtained the letters, whether by fraud or by negligence, conveyed \[his rights\] into the \[later letters of\] commission — the more favorable \[letters\] are valid even if mention of the letters of commission was not made therein; yet the more favorable \[letters\] are always valid. And when there is a question as to whether fraud or negligence can be established, since it is said that authority holds that those who allege fraud must prove fraud — and \[if\] the adversary alleges fraud — that is, the more favorable letters produced as superfluous are by law required to be proved, and on whose authority it \[is argued that\] when it is doubtful whether they are more favorable or less, \[they\] are to be restored.

### Solution.

In truth, just as the one who holds more favorable letters has \[them\] established before the \[courts\], nonetheless it is the better counsel that fraud or negligence is not to be found unless \[it is\] fully \[established\] in the record. Likewise, if someone obtained false letters, whether superfluous, or obtained through admixture of falsehood, and another has extracted more favorable ones on the same matter — as to whether none of the \[former\] letters have admixed falsehood and the others are without admixture — and if in those \[more favorable letters\] no mention is made of the \[prior\] letters of commission, the one who obtained \[letters\] falsely: the question arises whether the second \[letters\] are valid to the contrary — and the more favorable ones, as valid, \[are to be preferred\] and \[since\] the more favorable ones are legitimately obtained they are valid. Likewise, if someone has falsely obtained letters on behalf of certain \[parties\], then no \[lawful\] judge \[appointed thereby\] is legitimate. That one in those letters neither excommunicates, nor suspends, nor does other things that another judge would do — whether the excommunication imposed holds, whether it holds, and that it does. Because a sentence \[passed\] by a lawful judge.

### VI.

In truth \[it holds\], because a sentence passed by one who is not a judge does not hold. Solution: an excommunication passed by such authority does not hold. If it were \[passed\] against him \[who lacks\] such authority repeatedly.
