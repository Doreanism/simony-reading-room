What should be said about those signed with the cross \[crusaders\] and others who find themselves in some dire need, in which it is neither possible nor fitting for them to be carried, and if they must take it with them they will otherwise remain behind and be excommunicated until they make restitution? Again, what should be said about mercenary soldiers who almost without exception unlawfully acquire their pay from princes through usury or plunder, and speak of the daughters of usurers whom they marry — as it were daughters of men whose wealth was unlawfully acquired from a father? The question arises about the sons of usurers in monasteries — what of the wife, the entire household and retinue of usurers? All of these are stricken by the leprosy of plunder, because all run with fury as we have shown. The answer given is that abbots and all churches that receive offerings, rents, and corrections from such persons must surrender them and reform, unless there is some reason arising from the full legal right and the universal condition of this retinue of plunder; for just as in the case of hair or hides or merchants or bourgeois or market-stalls under the public ways, none of these are to be changed without being thoroughly purged under the penalty of law. But who will purge these things, and how? We answer: it seems to us that, just as in the medical art of healing the body, a customary remedy is applied through a gentle and prolonged course of treatment without decoction \[of remedies\], but rather through the most salutary path of purgation; so in the penal forum, the most severe purge is the Christian spirit, and what seems today most difficult to everyone is also without it insufficient.
