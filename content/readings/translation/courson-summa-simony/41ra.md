\[Continuing:\] case. The first is where the exchange is made for the utility of the church. The second is where one acts for his own advantage and thereby incurs simony. For if an exchange of spiritual for spiritual is made without any recognized \[mutual\] utility — suppose you hold a prebend in France and have the advantage of \[knowing the\] language, and you have a Flemish colleague who holds a prebend in Flanders — between you a mutual exchange can be made so that each moves to the place where he has the advantage of language. It is lawful for a bishop to make an exchange of the fruits of a church. Concerning translation from one see to another, we say that it may not be done except by the authority of the supreme pontiff, and then only if the one who is transferred intends principally the glory of God and the utility of the church. Further, it customarily happens that priories are sold in this form: “I sell you this priory for you to possess for your entire life for a hundred pounds.” In such a contract simony appears to be committed, and this can be shown by the following argument. In the church of this priory some \[of the monks\] ought to be rendered harmless — namely those former black monks \[i.e., Benedictines\] who are unwilling to communicate with their community, who oppress and scandalize the true flock, who promote those who should be removed, and who spend all the goods of the poor on gluttony and luxury. But to restore the church from its condition of private servitude to its proper freedom is a work and a ministry; and you can accomplish this by buying the priory from these unworthy custodians and placing it in the hands of worthy and freely acting \[persons\] — and for this you deserve merit. Further, Augustinus says that a limb which scandalizes the whole body should be cut off with the strongest iron. But you are here able to do this — since these persons together with the community scandalize the church along with the entire neighborhood. Therefore, if you remove such a limb when you are able, by superior authority, you do not sin. Moreover, you can redeem tithes from lay hands by grant of the bishop; but these priors are worse than laymen, for they confuse part of the people, the church, and all its \[goods\].
