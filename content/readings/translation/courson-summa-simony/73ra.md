If this is in no way lawful, it is far less lawful for an unworthy person to obtain several prebends while worthy persons — who are great pillars in the Church of God — are excluded, and those who confer several prebends or dignities simoniacally and deceitfully on lackeys, while holding worthy persons back \[from benefices\], sin mortally. Likewise, no one can serve two masters, just as no one can enter two houses, for according to the second \[argument\], salvation lies only in unity. What then: suppose someone is simultaneously the obedientiary of the bishop of Paris and the bishop of Chartres? What will he do if one commands him to do what the other forbids or has already initiated? For example, it is the custom of the church of Paris that on a certain day all the canons assemble, and no one who is physically well and present in the region may be absent in person — and the same custom holds in the church of Chartres, under penalty of excommunication and the bond of obedience. The church of Chartres commands the contrary, and if someone on the same day simultaneously receives a mandate from both — the argument goes: this man owes equal obedience to one as to the other; if so, and if he is bound to come at the summons of one person, he is also bound to come at the summons of the other. Since it is certain that he is bound to come at the summons of one of them, he is then also bound to come at the summons of the other, and he is bound to go to both churches on the same day — but this is impossible, and \[so\] he is bound to something impossible. If so, he cannot remain in such a state with a clear conscience. For no one has ever been stripped of the yoke. Likewise: suppose one church litigates against another, and one calls him to its side by the bond of obedience; if he obeys it, he is disobedient to the other. He is disobedient to neither, unless he is charged with disobedience in both cases. Likewise, no monk can be an obedientiary in different churches of different institutions.
