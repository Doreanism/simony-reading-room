\[Like\] the dead wood he overcomes — as Nebuzaradan arriving \[illegible\] — and so he, sluggish as the overflowing of surplus humors, \[lets\] things that should be noted and sought become hateful, to the detriment of the poor. Another little dog is the lawyer of the hunt, who serves God and \[illegible\] the abbot so that he himself \[illegible\] uses this as a pretext and occasion for going out of the cloister. On the ceremony and perpetual cause of why today such \[lawyers\] more than all others \[deal with\] court books \[illegible\] — such little dogs are therefore bound to restitution for everything thus acquired, because they share in the gains of thieves. Hence Solomon in Proverbs: “Whoever shares with a thief hates his own soul.” Likewise, the abbot has that woman for the work of the king’s war or \[illegible\], or by royal authority, through her \[receives\] such revenues or payments annually. It is asked whether she can redeem herself from such \[obligations\] by purchasing her freedom, and if perchance she has done this, whether the abbot or the lord who receives the price of such redemption can lawfully keep it. The solution: I would not counsel \[illegible\] the abbot to redeem herself in the aforesaid manner, because the following abbot would recall all things amid murmuring. The lord is experienced in \[such matters\] and the full heirs \[are bound\] by his seal to certify the subjects concerning the freedom so redeemed. So doubtful it is whether a knight in the guardianship of such redemption can receive anything, since he himself is \[bound\] to make such tallages; and we believe a distinction must be drawn, and we set it out as follows. Subjects in these matters are either serfs bound to service and more — such as were \[found\] in Apulia \[illegible\], like sheep and cattle — or they are census-bearing serfs, serving only under a fixed tribute, as \[is the case with\] almost \[all\] serfs in Gaul. In the first case, the lord is permitted to exercise all tallages and exactions upon serfs, because they have nothing that is not \[his\], and everything they have was the lord’s. In the second case, the lord is not permitted to impose tallages or exactions beyond the fixed tribute.
