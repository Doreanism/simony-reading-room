But ask whether a merchant is bound to restitution when he sells something unjustly without knowing it, because there is nothing that was his own, if indeed the body is that of the despoiled; and since the despoiled lose what was theirs, the goods of those \[despoiled\] always remain theirs in substance, whether they have passed to a usurer or to anyone else through any transaction, like \[property described in\] Leviticus \[25\]: if God gave \[it\] through grain or through a horse or through some thing as a census of those \[goods\] on his feast days, you may be held \[accountable\] from your own goods — how much more from goods that have been plundered? But if \[the original owners\] are found, give the goods back to them. If however they cannot be found, give them to the nearest poor. Hence townspeople who hire out their lodgings to such people are bound to return everything they received from them; but those who plunder \[others\] must make restitution, or give the proceeds to the nearest poor, and this must be said everywhere of all robbers and usurers wherever one calls upon them and such people. If it be asked whether a simple peasant who has come from some place might be excused by his simplicity from making restitution of what he received from such a person in the common \[transaction\], I believe we say that a simple person may also be excused from correction in this \[matter\] on account of some \[deficiency\] and prior lack of instruction. Of the aforesaid question: ask in what manner all the aforesaid ought to conduct themselves in their respective states so that we may consider them saved — that they should sell nothing except justly, by the authority of Solomon in Proverbs \[20:10\]: “A double weight and a double measure, both are abominable to God.” And they say in Leviticus \[19:35–36\]: “A just scale and a just measure, a just ephah and a just seah, an equal measure.” Therefore if anyone deceives his neighbor by selling his goods beyond the proper price.
