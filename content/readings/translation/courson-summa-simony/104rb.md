Fifth, whether in any particular article \[concerning\] capital offenders the cleric may hand them over to secular judges under the pretext of the matter for punishment by the secular court. That it is indeed lawful for an ecclesiastic to exercise capital judgment may be shown by authorities — named ones as well as those from the sacred canons — for Hieronymus says in Exodus, chapter 22: “You shall not allow sorcerers to live” \[Exodus 22:18\]; and if you are a prelate in the church, let the sword be among the guilty; if any murderer or anyone else worthy of death is discovered in your community, expose him at the least to public courts by name. “He that loves not his son” \[Proverbs 13:24\]. And the Lord says to Moses in Numbers, chapter 31: “Know that by God’s right you are more than the Midianites”; and Moses, in obedience, gathered thousands of armed men and destroyed them and killed Balaam himself \[Numbers 31:7–8\]. Likewise in Exodus, when he came down from the mountain: “Let every one who has committed \[this sin\] pass through the camp” — and they went from gate to gate merciless in their service \[Exodus 32:27\]. Likewise in the book of Kings, Samuel killed the fat king Agag over the altar with a sword \[1 Samuel 15:33\]. And Phinehas is read of in Numbers — “and the Lord was appeased by Phinehas” who was an ecclesiastic, son of the priest Eleazar \[Numbers 25:11\]. Similarly in the book of Kings it is read that Elijah killed five hundred priests of Baal upon a rock \[1 Kings 18:40\]. And Peter, inflamed by holy zeal in the Acts of the Apostles, struck dead Ananias and Sapphira \[Acts 5:1–11\].
