### On Oaths and Their Penalties

After these matters we must treat oaths and their penalties, the knowledge of which is most necessary for a priest in the penitential forum. We must consider what an oath is, and what things are required for an oath to be rightly sworn; whether it is lawful to swear in licit causes; what solemnities make an oath more binding; and how the difficulties that arise from oaths in various cases are to be resolved. From all of this it will be evident what is to be penally punished and in what cases an oath is to be avoided with greater care, since the same discipline applies. An oath is the confirmation of something by invocation of a sacred reality, or by something consecrated to a sacred reality, or by the solemnity of a sacred reality. There are three things required for an oath to be legitimately sworn, which are called the three companions of an oath, as Hieronymus attests in his commentary on Jeremiah, cited at dist. xxii, q. iii: “It must be noted that an oath is to be sworn with these companions: justice, judgment, and truth. For if these are lacking, it is by no means an oath but perjury.” If I swear that I will lead someone \[to the altar\] tomorrow and she in the meantime dies, the oath was sound. If I swear I will never eat meat or fish, and afterward I eat meat or fish on account of illness, the oath was made with discretion. And so an oath \[requires\] discretion. But if I swear \[something\] with just cause to someone in need, it is a just oath. If someone swears completely with discretion, he swears justly; for if he swears without \[proper\] address he swears without discretion. And whoever swears justly swears truly.
