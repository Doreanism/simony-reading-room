Furthermore, it seems that if scandal were a mortal sin, by equal reasoning every sin would be one — for instance, one who publicly keeps a concubine commits scandal, and this very act, namely fornication, is a scandal; yet no one can find something that is not a scandal. For if one says that the scandal in him and the fornication in him are different things, and that in fornicating he commits scandal, he commits two sins — namely, that of fornication and that of scandal — by the same reasoning, whoever commits any sin and thereby offends others commits two sins; which is false. Or else one must say that his fornication, being a crime, is itself scandal; and by the same reasoning every other sin by which he offends others is scandal, and there is no other mortal sin which cannot come about without scandal.

### Solution

The word “scandal” is used equivocally: it sometimes means mortal sin itself, that is, the crime itself — the deed or some act that is a crime by which one scandalizes one’s neighbor; and sometimes it does not mean a venial sin but rather a certain arousal or provocation to sin on the occasion of someone’s word or deed; and sometimes “to scandalize” means a sin or an act — the act of a man causing scandal, just as the word *diabolus* \[devil\] is used emphatically for the person himself. In this last sense the Lord said to Peter: “You are a scandal to me.” Whether every mortal sin can be scandal: it is accidental to an actual sin to be a scandal, because if it is committed before many and by its occasion they are brought to consent to sin, it is a scandal; but if it is done in secret with no other person knowing, then it will not be a scandal.
