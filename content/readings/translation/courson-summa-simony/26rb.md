Therefore by any mortal sin one is placed outside the catholic Church and is bound in the heresy of the malignant. Therefore mortal sin binds one in common and separates one from the unity of the Church. And since we are all equally outside the Church just as he is — from the moment that mortal sin causes one to leave the Church — when excommunication supervenes he becomes commonly outside it, and nothing new is done to him by excommunication. Solution: mortal sin chiefly offends God in the first instance, and already the first \[offense\] is amplified by multiplying \[guilt\] two by two and three \[fold\] before God, because the new guilt of punishment is one thing with respect to the Church, namely satisfaction; with respect to God \[it is\] a double bond, one of satisfaction. For in this way one is held to satisfy the Church by giving alms at the judgment of the priest, nor is one held to remit the grudge toward God first; and he \[the wrongdoer\] is considered to be obligated even to give outward signs of rancor’s removal and to exchange the kiss of peace. The Father’s disposition by reason: when \[the reconciliation\] is perfect, it is sufficient to remit the grudge in one’s heart. “Offer your gift at the altar, and if your brother has something against you, leave your gift there and go and be reconciled to your son.” And while mortal sin binds someone, as we have said, with one chain before God, excommunication binds with another chain, because the binding of sin: excommunication shuts the sinner out from entering the Church and from participation in the sacraments, so that excommunication distances one farther from the Church than \[mere\] sin without excommunication.
