## On lying

What was said about the oath must now be said about lying. Therefore one must consider what a lie is, what is remarkable about it, what its kinds are, and the questions that commonly arise from the various complexities of lying in many cases. Augustinus describes lying thus: a lie is a false vocal utterance made with the intention of deceiving. If, on the contrary, a false vocal utterance provokes no false meaning, or if a false utterance makes manifest no false proposition, then there is no sin of lying. Note this well. This term “false vocal utterance” is taken in two senses: either it refers to a false meaning conveyed by a word, or it refers to a pact of false signification with intent to deceive. In the first sense, no false vocal utterance is a sin. In the second, it is a sin, because an action signifying a falsehood with intent to deceive is a sin; and this is the sense in which it is taken in the first definition. Now it is true that going against one’s mind \[contra mentem ire\] is something else; and this expression “going against one’s mind” is taken in two ways: sometimes it means going against one’s intent, that is, against what one thinks; sometimes it means going against what a discerning mind would judge should be said. Going against one’s mind in the first sense is not always a mortal sin; going against it in the second sense is always a mortal sin, as will be shown below. Ambrosius, however, takes “going against one’s mind” broadly, namely to say or do what is contrary to the mind; and according to him, whoever sins against his mind goes astray. Every sin, then, in this second sense, is a lie. Hence he says: if it is only in counterfeit women that lying is deception.
