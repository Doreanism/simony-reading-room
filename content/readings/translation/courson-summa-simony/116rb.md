### Solution.

That he is absolutely \[to be condemned\] because no one \[is so thoroughly protected\] — by the same reasoning, without delay of accusation he is not to be acquitted; but against this, one who has not been accused has been acquitted by the Lord and by Paul at Corinth, and \[matters\] remaining without accusation. We proceed: the soul of the notoriously \[guilty\], as we preach — do not \[deny\] that the one who labors under public infamy, as was fully shown above, \[is to be condemned\], concerning absolution.

### De absolutione supra.

The entry into accusation \[illegible\] is fitting whether manifold: public, private. By public law for some notable success or for a distinguished day — once it had been established at the emperors’ \[courts\] that the names of accusers would be enrolled in the annual accusation registers, and the people would routinely set down the unexpected memorials — \[the records\] are accustomed to place names of accusers, and thus they are absolved from \[their liability in\] accusation except for the excepted accusations. For the first \[kind of\] absolution is customarily granted fully by special grace through perpetual prior \[grant\]. By law, either through the \[specific act of\] absolution from the accusatory process, or by the one \[bringing the charge\] wholly desisting from the accusation, or by his being in a position to pursue the accusation yet unable to do so. Concerning the difference between minor and serious offenses: minor ones can easily be recognized and punished; serious ones require a full inquiry. And note that in accusations, proofs are required through witnesses — first, full proofs by reason, all that which creates full credence in a doubtful matter. Presumption creates semi-full credence; and simple presumption is either rash — and that is rejected, as in q.i., first chapter — or probable — and that is admitted, as in q.iii., chapter *potest* — or violent, and that is admitted as a proof, as the chapter *significati pram* on the cohabitation of clerics provides. Now, presumptions and the circumstances surrounding \[a case\] — whatever they may be, such as place, time, and other attendant details — establish a presumption and in truth often point to a common cause. xxv., which chapter *ordinatum*. The means of proof are accordingly: proof by authentic document, by reputation, by human presumption, by denunciation, by witnesses, by \[illegible\] present.
