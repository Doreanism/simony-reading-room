And when we say “we owe what we swear as a religious obligation,” \[the distinction\] distinguishes what is properly the genus of right, and whereas “oath” signifies a sworn act, “religion” \[here\] signifies the solemn religious act surrounding the oath; the greater the solemnity added, the more the religious weight of the oath increases, and the more ponderous \[the obligation\].

### An oath is unlawful for various causes.

\[It is unlawful\] on account of the one who swears, on account of the thing sworn, on account of an evil and perverse cause, and on account of an ecclesiastical rule. On account of what he has already done, when someone swears something that commits a new sin or injures a brother or offends good morals — if, for example, he swears to do it again, it should be cause for wonder, as can be proven from dist. xxii, q. iv, chapter “leve prorum” and the following chapters, and also the chapter “in utrumque”: he promised faithfulness again, breaking the oath. \[It is unlawful\] on account of an evil and perverse cause, \[for instance,\] if someone first intended to spread corruption and then swore he would be reconciled with someone. \[It is unlawful\] on account of an ecclesiastical rule, if you have received the sacraments or taken monastic vows, and then swore you would marry someone — since no one constituted in holy orders or in religious vows can contract \[marriage\]. As Beda says in a homily cited at dist. xxii, q. iv, chapter “si aliquid”: “If it happens that someone swore something that, if observed, tends toward a worse outcome, it should by healthful counsel be changed, and it is better to persevere \[in virtue\] than, having sworn, to persist in error.” Thus David swore to kill Nabal \[illegible\], but by the intercession of Abigail he returned his sword to its scabbard.
