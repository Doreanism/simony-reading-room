And since this redounds to the common utility, we say of one who acts through \[the revelation of\] another’s confession as a heretic to be promoted: he can and ought to act \[to impede the promotion\] both by revealing \[that confession\] and by \[other means of\] promotion \[prevention\], because as Augustinus says, he both can and ought to do so. On the last point we say that by reason of the \[accumulated\] errors and the multiplying crowd, if we consider more carefully, the churches owe their portions to their bishops. Yet we find in the life and \[rule\] of Anastasius that religious houses were so remote from cities and so desolate of council and counsel that bishops in times of sloth were able to enter them frequently and there sow the word of God and otherwise discharge their office.

Regarding their offices — though they seek to exceed their bounds — let glorious authorities be set against the questioner; and this wonderful \[teaching\] holds: no \[plunderer\] shall treat the worthy, who are compared to trustworthy men, as though their reward were \[of no account\], for “he who serves the altar shall live by the altar.” There \[such a one\] may hold a staff of suspicion, and one sustained by the Gospel \[may say\] that where he has sown spiritual things he may reap them. But if he has sown sins and scandals there and devoured the goods of the poor, he is bound to make restitution for all of it even before a moneylender \[must\].

Likewise, just as the right of patronage annexed to a spiritual office cannot be sold, and the right \[derived\] from \[that\] right — if selling a right appears to be something that diminishes the royal administration, if it restricts governance within the kingdom, if it \[pertains to\] the power of punishment — by the same reasoning, neither can \[what is derived from it\] be transferred.

### \[Reply\]

Reply: those things that are part of the body of the kingdom may be sold — castles and counties. If, however, the consecration that is joined by the bishop to a \[lay\] power is sold, just as one may buy the granting of salutation and incorporation, so one may neither sell nor give a sacrament; thus neither the bishop’s blessing nor absolution can be sold.
