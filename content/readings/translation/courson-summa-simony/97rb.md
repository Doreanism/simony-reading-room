These are the words of Hieronymus. With these accord those words from Exodus: “You shall not take the name of your God in vain”; “You shall not swear for nothing in the name of your God” — or in another reading: “You shall not take the name,” etc. From the aforesaid, affirming by praise: do not associate the name of God with falsehood. Thus also: \[the question is\] whether vain and idle oaths made in common speech are committed without mortal sin, since they were contrary to that prohibition “You shall not take the name of your God in vain, you shall not swear for nothing.” If you say that habituation or common usage excuses such oaths — against this: others sin \[by them\]; and usage or frequency increases sin, as in drunkenness and lust and other sins — by the same reasoning it aggravates sin. For indeed, what arises from change \[of habit\] or custom greatly sins before God. Solution. But we say that such oaths in a \[specific\] case — namely when arising from the occasion of scandal, or on account of the swearer in the \[preceding\] examples where other aggravating circumstances are present — are mortal. But if they are made through mere inadvertence or a sudden impulse of the mind in the common flow of speech, they are not mortal. And because it is harder to restrain the tongue than any other member, as James attests — “For he who does not offend in tongue, this one is perfect” — let us judge the habit of swearing as we judge the habit of drunkenness or lust. Hence, say: “Set a guard, O Lord, over my mouth and my lips.” Also according to the canon.
