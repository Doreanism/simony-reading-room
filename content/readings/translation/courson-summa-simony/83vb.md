## ix. the same judgment must be applied to vows of continence and vows of abstinence.

It is established that the Church dispenses from vows of abstinence once sufficient cause is known. By the same reasoning, it does the same for vows of continence — as when it happened that on a kingdom’s falling to an heir, the Pope promised to extract the heir from the cloister so that he might contract marriage, lest the kingdom perish for want of an heir. And thus if someone has made a simple vow of continence, and in the imminent need of another a sole heir in the kingdom \[is required\], what will the Church say to him? Will it grant him the freedom to contract marriage? If so, to contract is a mortal sin for him — and evil must never be done that good may come of it — and the Church, if it grants him permission to contract against his vow, commits evil. And yet if a marriage has in fact been contracted and confirmed, the continent \[person\] who contracts after a simple vow sins mortally. Hence if the Church gives him counsel to contract, the marriage nonetheless holds once contracted, as in that decretal “Veniens,” under the title

“cum clericis.” Whether those unwilling to contract can avoid it — if it is a solemn vow, then the vow is indispensable, as argued at xxviii d. “Vouemab.,” at xxvii ca. q. i “De indulgentis,” and in the chapter on the blessing of nuptials at the beginning. And whether the vow be simple or solemn, the Church ought not to dispense in that case, for she would sin in either instance — and we concede this. The case is otherwise with vows of abstinence, which can be redeemed for many intervening necessary causes. For example: a person prone to headaches would suffer from weakness of the brain if he fasted as he vowed. In that case, redemption of the vow is necessarily required.
