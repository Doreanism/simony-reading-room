And the Apostle \[writes\] to the Philippians: “He who labors, eats,” and he adds, “Let each man eat his own bread.” The Psalmist says, “you shall eat the fruit of the labor of your hands,” and elsewhere, “I have labored in my joy,” and from there he condemns the idle, saying \[Ps 72:5\]: “They are not in the toil of men, and they shall not be scourged with men.” What then shall we say about those who do no work yet devour everything? Woe to the lawyer who wrings ten pounds or more from some poor man through delays — which we call “inducements” — obtained unjustly from someone who owes nothing. Likewise physicians rapaciously seize the bread of medicine. Likewise theologians rapaciously seize temporal stipends as their livelihood for preaching and for lecturing on sacred scripture, and so they contract the taint of simony through such labor. Similarly there is a question concerning masters who teach in Arabic or in other disciplines, and who devour \[the resources\] of princes and contemplatives; and concerning those who attach themselves to the sons of usurers, or of archdeacons and deans of a childlike sort, whom they nourish in luxury and who ought not to be counted among their proper students.

### Good Works

To make all of this clear, one must note that there are kinds of works, kinds of men, and kinds of eyes. There are works of nature, works of grace, and works of sin. Works of nature stand under God, as the works of those who in a pure time sin not \[mortally\] under God. Works of grace are those directed toward God, which are always fully meritorious. Works of sin are those directed against God in temporal matters, namely mortal sins. The first \[works\] belong to the middle eye. The second to the temporal eye. The third to no eye. Those that belong to the middle eye are those of which Augustinus said that certain things are indifferent — such as plowing and sowing — which may be done by a good person, a bad person, or an indifferent one.
