And that rule speaks generally of all who are in the novitiate — including this person. This person was in the novitiate and is free to leave if the order does not please him, and he may return to the world unanointed. If you say he must always become either a canon or enter another order, and if he cannot do that, he may return to the world — and that rule there freely allows departure, for it does not mean that he is obliged to bind himself to another order on that account simply because he was in the novitiate of those monks. But this is contrary to the original institution of that order: for it used to be the custom that clerics or prelates enriched with whatever dignities or revenues, once examined in the novitiate, if they were not pleased to remain there, would return to their revenues in the world entirely freely.

## x. Furthermore, it is decreed that a religious cleric differs from a religious monk, and that even a religious canon differs from a religious monk, and that he is bound on account of the greater religious life to press forward toward the monastic habit.

This we grant where no prior vow exists; but where a vow exists, as in the case of the scholars mentioned above, it must be said that he who presented himself in the hands of the \[*albis*\] and was to be professed through a solemn vow was to receive another vow according to what he understood the vow to be when he took it, according to what he understood at the time of undertaking it — just as is the case with an oath, which is always to be interpreted according to the understanding of the one who receives it.
