Likewise, the same passage says that if someone holds two dignities or churches, he shall retain one and relinquish the other. And behold, both in the new and in the old law it remains the case that no one should hold several churches or prebends, and that law — even though renewed — has instructed \[the Church\] on this notorious point, and it is still to be observed, even though common usage has not kept to it, because all contrary customs are to be abolished. Likewise, in the primitive Church, owing to the rarity of religious persons and of those who had converted to the faith, and owing to that rarity of worthy persons, a plurality of prebends was made where there was an extreme dearth of worthy persons, and so at that time when one city had one hundred sixty prebends or more, and there were only one hundred or five worthy persons, it was then of necessity the case that several prebends were entrusted to worthy persons for distribution to the poor, and so it was then established that one person might hold one titled \[prebend\] and another in commendation, until a worthy person could be found to whom that prebend \[held\] in commendation could be conferred. Yet it was never lawful for anyone to hold two titled \[prebends\]. But in the contrary situation that has arisen in the Church — namely, that there is poverty among the holders of prebends and a great multitude of worthy persons — when the cause ceases, the original effect ought also to cease; and when new conditions arise and the \[original\] vigor is restored, and when worthy persons are so many and prebends so few, several should not be conferred on one person while other worthy persons who have no benefices at all are left behind — because just as the fact that there was such a multitude of prebends and such a dearth of worthy persons made it lawful for someone to hold two titled prebends, so today, since there is such a dearth of prebends and such a multitude
