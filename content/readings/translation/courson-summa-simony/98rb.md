### XXI.

in what cases all persons generally are to be compelled to take oaths; whether oaths must be administered at all times and in every case. The canons say this is not true for all persons at any time, as stated at C. 22 q. last, c. “Parvuli” and c. “Honesti” and c. “Decretum,” where it says that children who are without reason, or who are under fourteen years of age, are not to be compelled to swear; and that from Septuagesima until the octave of Easter, and from the Advent of the Lord until the octave of Epiphany, and likewise during the three Ember seasons, and during the greater litanies, on Sundays, and on the days of the Rogations — except for agreements concerning concord and peace — no one shall presume to swear upon the holy Gospels. And \[it says\] that during the year preceding puberty, in spiritual matters, those who swear for any reason other than peace and concord and rightful intent shall be repelled as the canon prescribes; and likewise those who transact such matters on solemn days, just as C. 15 q. 3, c. “Placita” says: that secular pleas committed on those days, or on other pagan feasts, or on legitimate business days, we have decreed are by no means to take place. The canon also decreed that clerics may not presume to take an oath against any cleric, as stated in the last chapter, “Nullus.”

### XXII.

What follows concerns the oath against calumny. One must consider what the oath against calumny is, what it contains, by whom, and when it is to be administered. The oath against calumny is that by which, according to some, the soul is called to witness; but according to others, it is simply the purgation of calumny in legal proceedings. For example: if you are the plaintiff, you will swear that you have come to litigate not with intent to calumniate, but with the belief that you have a just cause.
