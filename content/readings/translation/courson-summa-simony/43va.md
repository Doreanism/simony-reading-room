The bishop may give a prebend to such a minor cleric in view of such an office — one that suits him — and may then pass over someone far worthier for whom that office does not suit. But if this church has no need of such lesser \[clerical\] ministries, and instead is in greater need of a most capable archdeacon, dean, or treasurer — then if he installs a less suitable person, he is culpable \[only\] when he could safely have had one far worthier. \[In that case\] he sins mortally. It should also be noted that, as the decretal of Alexander says: if someone is known \[to me\] and is honorable, I may confer a prebend on him in view of his distinction, passing over an unknown person who is less well known yet performs \[the duties\] better and is far more learned, though less distinguished in rank. But if two candidates are of the same condition and eligible for the same grade — and one of them is a kinsman — if you trust your kinsman you do not sin, provided he is worthy, so long as necessity does not require that you fear the scandal of many, \[who might say\] that you give the Church’s goods out of regard for kinship. Likewise, Petrus asks: what should those do who, after their election, come to recognize that they are unworthy and insufficient — especially in the situation where they plainly see many worthy candidates who would administer better than they themselves? He clearly shows that they are bound to resign.
