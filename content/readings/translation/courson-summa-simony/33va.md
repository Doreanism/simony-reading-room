Avarice and prodigality — if therefore contraries are in the same \[subject\]. Solution. They return thus in act or in crime. Such is the guilt of one who has committed sins on a journey. Therefore: one who commits adultery and homicide, for which he has done sufficient penance, and then commits simple fornication: hence thus — when he fornicates he shows contempt by fornicating; if then both adultery and homicide return, for each of which arises from contempt — he who shows contempt by committing adultery, by scorning homicide — it arises from the guilt of adultery and of homicide. If \[his\] contempt departs from its place and passes away together with its actions in the mode of contempt now committed. Likewise, whence it follows that the contempt that here ceases in fornicating — and the fact that sins return. For opportunity aggravates sins and the return of sins. Since one circumstance aggravates the sin of fornication in another respect, and hence one sins more in fornicating than another who has never sinned — \[yet\] now fornicates with other equal circumstances. If together the two commit equal sins — suppose they sin in equal fornication — they sin equally and equally show contempt. They sin in the same \[way\] and equally show contempt. If equally they sin and are equally sinning, and just as both sin against their own evil, they are equally evil. If the common \[person\] triples his malice — namely in adultery, fornication, and homicide — the other is less evil in fornication than the other \[i.e., than the one with tripled malice\]. Likewise, whenever one makes himself guilty of three things: homicide, fornication. Likewise, another makes himself guilty of these. Likewise, it is proved that one to whom sins return is somewhat guilty and more guilty — for he is guilty of the guilt of fornication and more he will be.
