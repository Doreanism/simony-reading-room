It is and for them. Simony is named after Simon Magus, who wished to buy spiritual things. He distributes, moreover, by means of little gifts. We have already stated \[these points\] above. There is moreover a culpability in the first-born and thus he himself was a simoniac by intention alone — in the New Testament and in grace, \[simony operates\] in act and in operation. Now simony exists in three modes: by service, by tongue, and by hand, as Gregorius says concerning that passage: “Blessed is he who has freed his hands from every gift” and holds fast — i.e., to the chapters of his firm resolve. There are kinds of spiritual things with respect to which simony is committed. Some spiritual things that come from investiture by place are sold — investitures and the stain thereof — as is \[discussed\] in the question concerning the same matter in the savior. There are also other spiritual things that come from ecclesiastical offices, still further added — such as orders or ecclesiastical appointments administered: baptism, the Eucharist, matrimony, and the other sacraments. These cannot rightly be sold, nor can those others be harshly \[treated\]; yet today they are sold to the harm of one who is zealous for \[their integrity\]. There are yet other things that accord with ecclesiastical rights, such as tithes and first-fruits, which clerics sell by place and in exchange, \[as treated in\] C. xii, q. ii, and C. xxviii, q. ultima: “To no one.” There are still others in which spiritual things are administered, such as chalices, patens, and other ecclesiastical vessels. These are sold by place and by investiture, as in C. xii, q. i.

### Moreover it should be noted

As it is customarily said, \[the canon\] does not permit them to be sold. It has established this as a penalty \[reflecting\] the nature \[of the offense\]. For as is said in the law: those things that have been dedicated are sacred by their consecration, and those \[persons\] are to be assigned to public uses.

### iii.

\[One\] judges that at the end the species assigned in the name are sufficient. For there is simony committed by those who imitate Simon in their chorus or in their rule — that is, those who are struck by law or by the law’s provisions or burned by fire. Such are those who usurp armed \[powers\] against these persons and trade spiritual things with them simoniacally. This \[form of simony is\] neither by service, nor by tongue, nor by hand.
