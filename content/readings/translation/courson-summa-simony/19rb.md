He ought to admonish him to do so, and to fulfill his intention, because whatever cause is attended to will avail either for the remission of sins or for their augmentation. Furthermore, the reason why such remissions are sent is this: four causes — namely, pilgrimages and similar things — often lead men to advance in mortal sins. Therefore, by way of caution and support, such remissions are firmly established in the Church; and whoever afterwards sends them out should instruct the recipients under this form: “We absolve, attending to this place \[illegible\].” But instead it reads: “We absolve those who thus come forward, if they can make an offering devoutly, or if they cannot bring an offering, let them come forward properly ordered; we absolve them.” But God bestows \[grace\] according to the affection of heart, yet in no way defrauds the poor. As far as this is concerned, if a person attends devoutly or becomes devout, let him be aided by the curia. But against this someone objects: I say that if such a remission is granted \[illegible\], the whole chapter, the body of the Church, are interceding for the offerers if they are not in mortal sin. If this is the case, it is clear that their intercessions are invalid, because God does not hear sinners; on the contrary, their prayer becomes an impediment to them, for all their works are dead — therefore these offerings avail nothing. The same person says: even if someone were to assemble metal \[illegible\] statues receiving the breath of the unclean spirits which, standing there, emitted \[sounds\] on behalf of the offerers, the calculations and the intercessions of said Church’s members dwelling in mortal sin would be of no value. For it is said: “He who is displeasing \[to God\] is sent to intercede.” For the spirit directs attention to private devotion; and the same \[Augustinus\] says: “When his \[own sins\] weighed upon others \[illegible\].” And likewise: “He who does not burn does not set \[others\] on fire.”

## Let us resolve this

There are three things here that must be noted carefully, which remain firm; because whatever happens in the aforesaid church renders its effect invariable: namely, the unity of words, the unity of the sacraments \[and\] the office of the Church — because the whole Church, both present and future, \[intercedes\] for us \[illegible\].
