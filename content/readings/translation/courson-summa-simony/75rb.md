## Continuing on the right of patronage.

He states and explains what the right of patronage is, how it is acquired — first by possession — and what its benefit is, and what the duty of the patron is; whether it can be held by many, or \[whether\] it belongs to none of them. The right of patronage is the right of presenting or electing a cleric to a church, arising from the fact that someone has built a church, or endowed it, or nominated \[it\] for construction. This right Gratian also calls a “grace” in dist. xvi, q. v, “Sepe”; and he also calls it “auxiliary protection” in the same distinction and question, chapter “Premevas”; and it pertains to the church; as in the same distinction, question, and chapter “Demmum.” And because a right of this kind is attached to a spiritual \[matter\], it cannot be sold or pledged in part, but only in its entirety together with the whole vill or fief, and the land from which it depends; as in on acquiring ownership of things, law “quedam.”

The benefit of the right of patronage is such that if the patron is reduced to want, he ought to be supported by the church according to the capacity of that church and the dignity of the patron; dist. xvi, q. vii, “Omnimq.” \[illegible\] if there was. The duty of the patron is such that if he cannot restrain the management of the church’s goods, he must report this to a superior — namely the bishop, or archbishop, or lord pope, or king — through whom he may bring that church under control against wrongdoing; and note that this right passes to heirs just as the right of sepulcher; as in dist. xvi, q. vii, \[illegible\], chapter “ad crebellianum,” law “perm.”

It is further to be known that the patron acts on behalf of the cleric when he presents him; if the patron alone surrenders the church, the patron only presents. \[The right\] is held to be impaired. Five things are said to give \[rise to\] the right, as human law confirms. The necessity of another’s election — lest the benefice remain \[only\] in liberty. On legacies, according to the law one \[is sufficient\], and Digest on annulling legacies, law “rem legatam,” already incidentally noted. In any election, every description whatsoever of an election — oil \[illegible\], of the tabernacle, or anything sought from the derived household. Hence Dathan \[and his companions\] were swallowed up.
