\[Here the text begins and\] has a return to \[the foregoing\]. The king \[Melchisedech\] spoke rightly: “And the division of tithes of all things is there.” And if the patriarch already distributed tithes of all things to priests, you who are less than he are bound to pay tithes of all things to the priests. \[You ought not\] to usurp or seize from priests, for that would be sacrilege. From this it is clear that it is not lawful for laymen to hold tithes and claim them for themselves, and that those who conceal them are unable to offer tithes worthily. The argument is at eri, q. i, last chapter, and prima, q. \[c.\] “Pervenit,” and in the decretal the reasoning concerning tithes is seen clearly, namely: that one who commits theft under the cover of a ring commits sacrilege against the church. This kind of example proves that those who steal tithes commit sacrilege. The twin concordances that the Apostle annotates with \[their\] gates \[i.e., passages\]. Moreover, through the same pretext of the Old \[Testament\] the same \[point\] is proved, because those of the Old \[Testament\] were punished by the Lord who filched from the church what was justly owed: king Ozias was struck with leprosy; Ozias \[i.e., Nadab and Abihu\] was consumed by fire; Dathan and \[those with\] Aaron — these are not hidden — fell. But there are certain flatterers who soften the word of God, \[removing\] the strictness of the canon from dominion, \[arguing\] that laymen who can hold tithes — if the lord pope is silent about their possession of the tithes — may accept \[them\] and abuse them, and that with this mortal \[sin\] stated, this is permitted since \[the pope\] is silent. And they assert that \[those who\] sin in refusing them \[tithes\] sin \[less gravely\]. But there is nothing to their argument, for the silence of the pope’s full authority, if it can excuse any moral sin — which a sin implies — if all were simoniacs the lord pope is silent nonetheless eternally. We say absolutely that neither does one escape by presuming to the contrary through adulterating \[the law\], nor does the lord pope’s silence excuse anyone who withholds a tithe from the church unworthily — for he would sin mortally. But

what should the parish priest do when such a knight, a detainer of tithes, comes to the altar on Easter day and demands the Eucharist or communion from him?
