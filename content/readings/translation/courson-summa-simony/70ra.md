From what has been said, the solution to the third question is apparent — namely, whether the goods of the poor may be given to those who are not poor — for it is clear that if you lavish the goods of the Church, which belong to the poor, on dishonorable ends, or on entertainers, jugglers, and \[others of that sort\], you are bound to make restitution. But what if a father is in need along with a kinsman and a stranger who are equally worthy — to which will you give more? To the kinsman, according to the verse. And: “The king brought me into his wine-cellar and set charity in order over me” \[Song of Songs 2:4\]. And it says that charity, being ordered, gives first to children and household members, and afterwards to outsiders — for he who does not care for his own household unless out of vice is denied this, as the Apostle says. But against this stands Gregorius: “What nature bids one to give is one thing; what the Lord’s commandments oblige one to give out of obedience is another.” And Hieronymus says: “The sole reason for piety is no knowledge of the flesh.” And elsewhere: “Only let him be chosen as bishop who does not know \[his relatives\].” He who said to his father and mother: “I do not know you.” Wherefore the same author says: “treading underfoot father, treading underfoot mother.” And it was said to Abraham: “Go out from your land and from your kindred” \[Genesis 12:1\]. And in the Gospel: “He who loves his father, etc., more than me is not worthy of me” \[Matthew 10:37\]. And elsewhere: “Who is my father and who is my mother?” And authority says that spiritual affections are to be placed before carnal ones, and if you find some poor man more worthy than your kinsman, you ought to prefer him to your kinsman in conferring ecclesiastical goods; and if the stranger is equal and virtuous, you ought not to put him behind the kinsman, according to that principle: regarding the love due to the poor. For since both the one and the other stand in need from the same source, preference ought rather to be given to the stranger. Give first to the stranger, for flesh and blood inclines one to favor one’s own.
