Lay servants can lawfully buy and sell the fruits \[of such things\] with a bishop, and by the same reasoning they can legitimately sell lay offices, but not spiritual ones. Solution. Let us say that this goes too far. To a husband and wife — since the revenues of this prebend or deanery come under the \[husband’s\] authority — such a lay office, by which one holds an immunity for the church, can be sold; and the fruits of a single \[term\] can be sold, just as \[the fruits\] of a prebend. Concerning a matter of fact: it happens that laymen are made canons in cathedral churches — indeed, the count of Anjou is still a canon in the church of Tours, and kings and princes customarily receive canonical dignity in other churches and \[are entitled to have others\] chant the psalms in their presence. Hence these churches once enjoyed greater authority \[in secular affairs\]. From what we have seen, it seems we should say that such persons were truly canons even as laymen; but at that time nearly all were literate, and the courts of those days formerly admitted them by canon law as \[proper\] canons.

### Further, concerning the spiritual annex in this form: suppose someone is made a canon in a church; when \[the chapter\] grants \[only\] a half-prebend and confers it upon him, he afterward permits another half or an equivalent of the half of the prebend — which was denied as the first half. Has he bought a spiritual annex? Because he bought a spiritual thing, did he commit simony? But against this: he previously had the full right of the prebend, which he obtained through a collation, and afterward from the fruits and emoluments commits simony — just as one would not \[commit simony\] if he were to farm the tithe fruits. Solution. The case is twofold. One, in which someone bought a canonicate of a half-prebend and then also bought a second half-prebend from which another person was similarly made a canon: in this case the simony is manifest.
