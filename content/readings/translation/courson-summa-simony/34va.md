Charity rises again and the perfect works are rewarded so that those works are rewarded \[appropriately\] to perfect charity, so that he may be rewarded for them, and on account of this also according to the quality of charity that he now has. Just as Augustinus says: punishment not only blots out offenses but restores what was taken away. Likewise, if someone rises in as great a charity as was lost, even in the very \[state\] where he who rises \[returns\] — those works given for rewarding are rewarded according to the charity of that \[state\] in which they were done and for which they were fitting. Moreover this man \[stands\] both in this respect and in that other, namely those \[works stand\] for this: that they might be ordered toward the humility of recovering grace. But against this: those perfect works were of great merit, and all are to be rewarded with a greater prize for which they were to be rewarded. Response: for they were vessels of piety in that perfect charity, and \[the Lord is\] most merciful, who went beyond what was his custom when he was with the man whom the Lord \[received\] after the first fall, because if he dealt strictly with him, neither that man nor any other would be saved. The parallel in common life: behold, a perfect familiar of a king has done many works deserving a dukedom from the king; therefore he \[the king\] intends to give him the insignia of a dukedom on account of those works. Afterward the man stirs up sedition against the king and is then reconciled; and that man is to be rewarded according to the works of his perfect devotion. It was not so \[in the end\].
