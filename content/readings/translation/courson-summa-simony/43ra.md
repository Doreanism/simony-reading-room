Consult the serpent, because it bites; consult sensuality, because it craves illicit things; consult the Jonah-Martha \[type\] because it renders disservice. Consult what is highest — namely, the most pure \[judgment of\] a father at Rome — so that you may give \[the benefice\] to the worthier rather than to the less worthy who has been put forward. Just as in providing a tutor you would choose the worthier one for the pupil, by the same reasoning, in the election of a prelate for some church, one who will be found to be, as it were, a guardian for all and, like a tutor, is to be set over the pupil-church — which is again like the true Father who sent the Apostle Thomas, the finest craftsman, to labor for the king of India. For if in his place one had chosen a less worthy man who was merely willing, this would have redounded to the harm of the Church; by the same reasoning, when you see someone worthy but another far worthier who has been passed over for a less worthy one, whoever acts otherwise sins mortally. Likewise, the common opinion of ordinary people holds that a navigator or architect who, with equal ease and equal cost, could have two \[options\] but lets go of the better and emits \[only\] the lesser — is a foolish merchant. Similarly, all judge foolish and fraudulent the architect who, when at equal cost he could have upright, equal columns, \[instead chooses\] rotted or weak ones and sets them under the structure, whose weakness causes the whole building to collapse upon its inhabitants — and so such a man is guilefully guilty of their deaths. By the same reasoning, one who builds the spiritual edifice of the Church upon rotted, weak columns is both guileful and foolish and lax, and is guilty of all the evils that come about through their insufficiency; and therefore such a tutor ought to be removed from the pupil-church. For all of ultimate nature protests that whoever is not \[fit for\] what belongs to him and is entrusted to his hands must be wholly rejected.
