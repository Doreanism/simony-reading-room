Hence he said: “But you go and proclaim the kingdom of God.” Likewise, it is lawful in itself to accept the staff as evangelical support from subjects, in accordance with Mark, who says: “Take nothing except a wallet.” But it is not expedient, as Matthew says in the case where: “Take nothing, not even a staff” — that is, where there are dogs, pigs, or false prophets; where it is inexpedient to receive anything from subjects in the course of evangelical work. In reply to this in the first case, we say that the abbot with his community and the prelate with his clerics sin most gravely by scandalizing \[illegible\] on Saturday; therefore he must be restrained from such eating as a scandal-giver. And if you are learned and of great reputation, you ought not to consent to your prelate by eating meat at table with him on that day, lest he scandalize the people, by the example of the Apostle who says: “If my flesh scandalizes my brother, I will never eat meat.” And since you rightly know that the gifts brought before \[you\] are from a senator — because no prebend of honor or anything of that sort is to be placed \[at table\] — you ought not to eat of them at your prebend. Yet a prelate may \[accept them\], by the example of Judith and the Maccabees, who, so as not to eat pork, \[were martyred\] in a pan. And you may eat at table, setting aside the unclean things and not eating the corrupt \[foods\], in accordance with Tobias who said: “We are sons of the saints.” Likewise, in reply to the objection in the second case, we say that it is lawful for me to reclaim \[my goods\] through litigation if I am imperfect, so as to pursue my cause and acquire my right; and since injury is done to me, I am not bound on account of scandal to abandon every avenue of my justice. And the prelate ought not to involve himself in secular affairs, as the canons decree; but he may use an advocate or an agent to conduct his external business on his behalf.
