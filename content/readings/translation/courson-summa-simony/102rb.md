Likewise in the Acts of the Apostles it is read that the apostle said to Ananias, who was the chief priest: “God shall smite you, you whitewashed wall.” — “Do you revile the chief priest, Paul?” Then the apostle said that he did not know him to be the chief priest, since — as the gloss states — the apostle knew that people well. The resolution: It could be said, as above, that the apostle spoke in a contrary sense to what he knew ought to be said or done by mind and understanding, and therefore \[spoke thus\]. The Cantor \[Petrus Cantor\] already said that he spoke to this outspoken man to signify the thing under a vulgar sense, as if he had said: “I shall not recall this to mind as a chief priest.”

### \[XV\]

Then it is asked whether \[deception\] is to be assumed on the basis of the examples of certain kings, as well as of David and the patriarch Abraham, since — as Hieronymus says in dist. xxii, q. ii, c. \[citation incomplete\] — \[Jehu\] commanded all the priests of Baal to come and sacrifice to Baal, and had all their priests executed. And Jehu feigned heresy in order to escape the hands of the king’s wife. She said: “Observe yourself to be as my sister, that it may go well with me and my soul may live on account of this.” If deception was used here, then both the deceiver and Abraham are liars: Abraham wished his wife to say she was his sister, and in this he acted falsely, since she was not his sister. Furthermore, Abraham was obliged to love the soul of his wife more than her body, and he sinned in this. Augustinus resolves this in his commentary on Genesis, as found in dist. xii, q. ii. For according to the custom of Scripture, just as cousins call one another “my brother” and those of the same kindred call one another “sisters,” so \[the one\] who was the daughter of Abraham’s brother is called his sister.
