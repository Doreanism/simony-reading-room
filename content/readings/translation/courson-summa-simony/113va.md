Concerning appeals.

### XXXII.

Not only through contumacy can the judicial order be impeded, but also through confession in court: if the defendant concedes what the plaintiff intends, there is no need to proceed further with the judicial order. And also through a properly interposed appeal — for both parties mutually and \[the order is stayed\].

Hence one must understand: what an appeal is, and who can appeal, and from whom, and to whom, and how many times, and in what cause, and from what time to what time one may appeal, and concerning the time for the fulfillment of the appeal, whether it has effect, and the office of the judge from whom \[the appeal is made\], and then the office of the judge to whom \[it goes\], and how an appeal is necessary, and when it is forbidden, \[and\] whether a frivolous appeal should not be admitted.

An appeal is a provocation by way of sentence from a lesser to a greater judge. To appeal is to challenge oneself from a lesser judge to a greater. One may appeal in a civil cause.
