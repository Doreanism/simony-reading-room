Likewise, those who hold a gaming-house — and in this there is certainty — because the truth of the light of life must be proclaimed; and therefore it is better to cause scandal and die than for you to abandon the truth. If you are placed in a pulpit or in a chair and you teach the law or preach, and you know that certain legal distinctions, as the rigor of doctrine demands, will offend the teachers or directors — there it is better that scandal arise than that the unity of your teaching be abandoned. Likewise, you are appointed as a judge in a case and you scandalize many by deciding unjustly, and you abstain from that injustice — let scandal arise rather than that you forsake justice. And what the authority of the Gospel confirms most strongly: when the disciples said to him, “Do you not know that when the Pharisees heard what was spoken they were scandalized?” the Lord replied: “Let them be; they are blind guides of the blind.” As if to say: they are to be left rather to their own scandal than that the things said should be abandoned. ## v. of the genus of truth; and to those two; and the inappropriate

introduction of refutation by Camillus. First, let the things be true, because danger is thus described as the approach of death or of death itself; and if anything such as that brings death near, it there constitutes a mortal sin. This we grant. But note that one must distinguish as follows: the truth of reward — that is, as to the last hope \[*ultima spes*\] — is for those who abandon what is forbidden by precepts; the other restricts matters as to those things that are to be observed. As to the first, in whatever communities — that is, the truth of doctrine — it restricts prelates who are bound to instruct their subjects in faith and good morals. But if this term is understood broadly as encompassing any preacher or member \[of the Church\], it contains both the individual and the common dimension.
