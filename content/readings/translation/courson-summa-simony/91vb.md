She \[replied\]: “Do not flee me; remember that he who often sought the Savior is here.” Then, moved to compunction, he went with her to her daughter and healed her. On the other side, many reasons stand in opposition. For one reads of very few hermits who left \[their hermitage\]. Hence John the Baptist went to the Sadducees and Pharisees to give an answer in his person, and they came to him asking: “Are you the one who is to come, or shall we await another?” And then he submitted himself to martyrdom. And Antonius never left the desert except to expose himself to martyrdom. Moreover, that hermit has professed according to the order of hermits and vowed to live as one enclosed; and if he is an anchorite or better, he ought rather to go out — he would summon that ship of hope. Again, he fears or helps both parties: his cell can endure for some time yet, because all who come for one common cause will change the place itself, and if it endures, it does so badly and incurs partiality against the poor in common charity. What therefore is he to do? Solution. Since no one ought to be a burden on another’s charity, since the loss of one’s soul profits a man nothing if he gains the whole world but suffers the loss of his own soul, and since to act contrary to one’s vow and profession is to put one’s hand to the plow and look back, let us say with the prudent singer that he should rather remain in his cave than go out, and that he may helpfully minister to the absent from within it — through writing, or by letter, or through others who can speak on his behalf; he should labor to correct the excesses of the incorrigible; and in this we say he attains the perfection both of the contemplative life and also, in a certain sense, even of the vocation of souls, all perplexities resolved.

## Concerning the perplexity by which \[he\] is moved.

It seems that a priest is constrained from one side by the prince of the land, his bishop, and the custom of the realm, to bless hot iron and water for those ordeals by which God is tempted — which are diabolic inventions. And \[this is confirmed by\] Gregorius.
