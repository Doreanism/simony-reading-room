Likewise in the Psalm \[15:5\]: “Who has not given his money at usury.” Augustinus in the sermon on the Lord’s mount requires that someone exact no loan above the principal with any expectation of temporal return. Likewise in Leviticus \[25:35–37\]: “If your brother grows poor, receive him as a stranger and sojourner; do not take usury from him nor more than you gave; give your money to him without usury and do not exact interest on \[his\] grain.” Likewise in Exodus \[22:25\]: “You shall not lend at usury.” This is a commandment of the Decalogue. The gloss, commenting on the text, says: “It is theft to receive another’s property.” They misappropriate the name of usury and the commandment generally wherever someone receives another’s property with great profit, and usury or another species \[of it\] mortally \[harms\] if one receives another’s property as usury. Under the name \[of usury\] the New Testament likewise shows \[the same\], as in the very Gospel of Luke \[6:35\], where the Lord says: “Lend, expecting nothing in return,” and from similar arguments it is found that usury is to be permitted in no case. Hence a certain chapter \[instructs\]: “Exact usury from no one.” Nor \[does\] Ambrosius \[permit it\], but \[usury means\] to cooperate in bringing about future relaxation, because the passage from Deuteronomy \[23\] is not to be expounded as meaning “you shall not lend to your brother,” as a penalty immediately imposed in law, “but to a foreigner, if it happens that you lend to a foreigner” — the law set a good penalty above. We therefore petition God \[on behalf of\] the most mortally \[sinful\], and exempt \[them\] similarly \[as\] from the bill of divorce. For if someone gave his wife a bill of divorce, they punished him by law, for he then sinned mortally. But if he himself dismissed \[her\] by the bill of divorce, the law punished \[it\]; he sinned mortally. The canonists thus expound that authority “You shall not lend to your brother” as meaning in the Holy Land, but not so abundantly \[to all\], such that “you may lend to a foreigner” — so as to show that lending at usury to a foreigner is permitted.
