If such \[a union\] was contracted with a sinful motive of greed, then the irregularity remains and \[baptism\] removes the fault. Furthermore, there was a controversy between Augustinus and another \[authority\] concerning such an irregularity: for some maintain that if a man had a wife before baptism and, after her death, takes another after baptism, neither he himself nor any prior \[union\] stands in the way. And Augustinus says — and one concedes to say — that if a man had \[a wife\] before baptism and another after, he is not to be promoted, because he has lost the seal of the sacrament that \[such men\] once held. It must be said, from the first chapter: did not Hieronimus rightly understand — asserting so great an efficacy in baptism — that it not only removes every fault but every irregularity? Because baptism makes a man wholly new and removes every penalty and fault, such that no irregularity ought to remain in one newly regenerated.

— he is to be barred from promotion. But \[the objector\] stirs up that if one to be promoted received baptism, which blotted out every fault and penalty and nothing ought to impede promotion, then whether the end or the irregularity of death \[should prevent promotion\] through baptism \[is the question\]. And there were arguments in many forms \[illegible\] toward mercy before baptism, and those that deter \[a person from\] a single course \[of action\] through baptism leave behind, along with the irregularity, \[a reason\] not to promote. But what if, before baptism, \[he had the same\] subsequently, having renewed \[illegible\]?

For \[it is maintained\] that the lord pope regards as irregular those who, standing \[in his service\] at his command, slew many Saracens in \[deeds of\] mercy; since piety is the good of obedience and \[there is a rational basis for\] showing mercy to wrongdoers \[in reason\].

Solution: As the Apostle says to Timothy: “A bishop or a priest must be without crime.” To Timothy: that every \[such person\] must be blameless; and every mortal sin \[committed before ordination\] impedes \[his promotion\].
