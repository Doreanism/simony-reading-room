The blade is held by the handle; for just as the blade cuts the hand of the one who grasps it if he does not hold it by the handle, so too the power of binding and loosing, which is like the blade, injures the flock if knowledge of discernment between leprosy and leprosy does not preside in him \[who wields it\]. He asks \[what this power\] consists in. But he shows in the following manner that the key is neither knowledge, nor an office, nor something composed of knowledge and power.

### For if the key is knowledge

it is either knowledge of a \[particular\] place, or it is a skill acquired from the seven virtues — that which is called a fitting or suitable capacity. If it is the skill acquired from the seven \[virtues\], then whoever receives a key receives such skill — which is false. If it is a virtue, then whoever receives a key becomes better — which is plainly false. Furthermore, suppose that a naturally foolish monk is ordained as a priest. He receives the order of priesthood, and since no such order exists without keys, he receives keys; and thus he receives greater knowledge than before — which is false, since he remains in the same folly. Thus it is clear that the key is not knowledge. But that

the key does not consist in the office of binding and loosing is clear from this: a person in a cloister who has the priesthood does not have the office of binding and loosing — as is clear from that passage, “Who will give me wings like a dove, and I shall fly away.” Augustinus says of such persons that they have bound wings. Furthermore, if the key were a power composed of the knowledge of discernment between leprosy and leprosy and the power of binding and loosing — then if a layman were to ordain someone as a priest, he \[the ordained\] would not have such knowledge, and would not have something assembled from such knowledge and power, and thus would not have a key.
