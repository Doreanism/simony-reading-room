## Which He Proves Otherwise

This customary rite of buying and selling is wholly directed toward the excommunicated, and they do not engage in the Catholic commerce of selling and buying \[with the faithful\]. All the more strongly \[one may argue that\] the act of communication with nuns in every respect is to be absolutely forbidden \[for the excommunicated\], and the sacrament of matrimony is not to be granted, because to associate by \[joint\] demonstration means that a Catholic wife who was an associate would be \[joining herself\] to an excommunicated person, by open display, as in “Causa \[illegible\], question IV, \[the chapter beginning\] quam multos viros.” Therefore, if some Catholic in good faith consents to this excommunicated woman, and she herself proves nothing \[against the marriage\], Augustinus says that consent alone makes a marriage; and since she is thus united in matrimony, the marriage can be consummated by the excommunicated person’s consent; and if they have carnal relations, a true \[marriage\] will be effected, and no one can dissolve it — because indeed such persons are not legitimately disposed to contract, as is expressly \[stated\] in Leviticus, nor by Gregory’s ruling where the example establishes \[prohibition\] below the seventh degree of consanguinity or affinity. But it seems that the marriage should stand, whether Catholic or excommunicated. Solution: there are many things that impede the contracting of marriage, which dissolve the \[contract once\] contracted by simple use. Let us say something similar about excommunication: that it impedes the contracting of marriage, as shown in the case from Ambrosius that a Catholic \[marrying\] a heretic — unless they are otherwise illegitimately disposed — does constitute a true marriage.
