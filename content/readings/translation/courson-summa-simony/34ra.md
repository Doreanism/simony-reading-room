These are expressions \[in grammar\], used as often as names indicate a quality or a culpability. They are to be lamented — why? Hence these two sin equally, and these persons are equally sinful; likewise they commit sins of equal gravity. These matters are indeed lamentable, these persons are equal and similar, being made equally evil and rightly punished as such. Yet one is far worse than the other, and all comparison occurs according to a figure of speech, and this argument holds: “these sin equally and are equally evil.” Likewise, “these sin equally and are equally wrathful.” To that objection we say that evil wickedness consists of all culpabilities. Therefore one mass \[of culpability\] comes from all individual masses, and one judgment from all judgments. Just as that one man will be shown \[guilty\] by all his own masses, and that other man will be shown \[guilty\] by the sum of his own knowledge collected from all, so we say that this man is guilty \[and\] evil when his culpability, gathered from all his acts, is shown; and this is true — or else grant that some man is more culpable or less culpable by reason of that man’s culpabilities. Likewise that some man is culpable and more culpable. Likewise that some man is good and more good than another. Likewise some man is good and more good.

Also by way of definition to the same point: the same comparison, the same principle, namely that all the good works a person did in a state of perfect charity return and rise up all together to the summit of perfect charity. The same reasoning holds regarding the unjust man: all relapsing sins return to that one punishment for which they were first deserved. Moreover it is clear that when someone relapses all \[sins\] return; I ask whether any evil man could be \[so\] named except through the impenitence that he once had as a habit. For if he committed infinite evils, all of them return when he dies impenitent. Likewise, whatever good man dies departs in the state in which he was who never had it as a habit.
