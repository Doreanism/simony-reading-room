Also, all authority of binding or absolution resides with God, because he himself actually binds or looses; the priest binds or looses who shows \[the person\] to be bound or loosed; and since the priest does nothing in binding or loosing except to show the binding or loosing that \[has already occurred\] in the heavens, after this \[the Gospel\] says: “Whatever he shall have bound on earth shall be bound in heaven.” For first the binding takes place in the heavens and afterward on earth. Likewise the authority says: “That is ratified in heaven which rests upon the judgment of Peter.” Suppose further that a prelate passes a sentence that is neither just in spirit — for he excommunicates someone out of anger — nor in order — for he has omitted the order of law — nor for a rational cause. This binding is done by the judgment of Peter or his vicar, and the authority says that a sentence of a pastor, whether just or unjust, holds on earth; and the same holds in heaven.

## But this binding is a mortal sin

and what is a mortal sin is approved by God and the heavenly court. But against this, God himself prohibits it and does not approve it. But some supply \[a qualification\] in the said authority thus: “Whatever he shall have bound on earth” — supply: *justly*. Others supply: *if not erring in the key*, “bound,” etc. Those who say: if the sentence was just on earth, it holds in heaven — \[those take it\] with a conditional *if*. But since that canon, which determines that a pastor’s sentence holds whether justly or unjustly imposed, is not \[affirmed\] without presumption — therefore, what does that canon do? The heavenly court approves it, and the wondrous binding is approved by God.

## Solution

We attend to a threefold judgment here. The first is God’s, the second is Peter’s in the Church Militant, the third is again in the heavenly court. The first precedes the other two in dignity and effect, because it \[God’s judgment\] closes by binding and no one opens it, opens by loosing and no one closes it.
