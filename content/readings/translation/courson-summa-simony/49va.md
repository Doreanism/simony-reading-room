For the prince is plainly bound to assist in preserving the right of the church wherever he has imposed such ecclesiastical sentence. In the same way we speak of crossbow-makers. The poor make crossbows to attack pagans and \[defend\] the church against \[tyranny\] — and thus against pagans or also for defending a kingdom they can be tolerated. There is also something to be said about those who sell cloth or any wares crafted through contentious sophistication from the goods of others, in deception of outsiders, and about hucksters and other craftsmen who devote their labor to things that provoke men to luxury and pride — as for example showing goods that are made to incite lasciviousness, \[styling\] hair with curling irons, and instruments \[of adornment\] of which they are masters. In all of these it is established that, since two swords are necessary in the church — according to that \[saying\]: “Behold, here are two swords,” and the Lord answered: “It is enough” — the prelate and the prince, if the ports \[doors\] are incorrigible, ought, by mutually assisting one another, each to strike \[these persons\] with his own sword, entirely cutting off and expelling the recalcitrant. We say that a prince setting out for the parts of Jerusalem, or one who is defending these parts \[at home\] of the kingdom against enemies, can accept all the movable goods of poor plunderers — for the benefit of those who have been despoiled — by concession, and this for the purpose of \[mutual\] defense until the kingdom is defended or until he returns from pilgrimage, or however long before he makes satisfaction from his own resources; and from your pure will you may receive them for the pilgrimage, just as they ought to do this, making that restitution once \[they return\].
