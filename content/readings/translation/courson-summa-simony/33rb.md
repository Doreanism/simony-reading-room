I believe the Lord imputes to him all that he brought back to punishment, unless he has made satisfaction for that last offense by which he inflicted the final injury. Once he has made full satisfaction for that \[offense\], the punishments of the others are remitted, those which had sufficiently borne upon him before — like that which we read above: “behold, I received from a certain prince many castles,” and so forth. But as to the objection concerning \[those\] deserving punishment for the debt in the same folio: but to the same objection: suppose that person has done the highest penance \[supremis penitia\] for all his sins, and \[another person\] has performed only simple fornication and has never repented, and is punished in hell for all the sins returning \[upon him\]. It is asked: what did those highest penances avail him, since the full punishment in kind \[in specie\] for those \[pills\] is now \[again\] due — a punishment already \[once\] rendered? The practical answer: because the works of seven years of penance are good, and therefore they are pleasing \[to God\]; and no good deed goes unrewarded before God, and those good deeds must somehow be rewarded. In what way are they rewarded? Solution. Before God there is a twofold reward: eternal and temporal. God always rewards the worthily penitent, indeed beyond their merits. It is fitting for God, who is thus just, to reward in temporal things those who seek \[reward\] in temporal things — like those who study in a good fashion, or who serve for gain elsewhere — since he added final prebends of this kind, rewarding unworthy plants by conferring on them such things as are fitting for the damned, like a peasant who on market day gives alms so that God will give him profit; the Lord confers temporal things for temporal \[purposes\]. So it happens in this case: for the works were inwardly perhaps evil, and the Lord rewards those \[pills\] in temporal things, and made him more capable of receiving greater grace, and sustained him and made him stronger and more resolute against falling, and therefore those works bore upon them with a multiplied reward. Likewise, sins purely return — my point being that greed and prodigality return at the same time.
