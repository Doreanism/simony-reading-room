It seems to us, as we have received it from the canons of Paris, that every person, whether greater or lesser, free or slave, is bound to correction — correcting with the ardor of merciful zeal — yet not at every moment in every circumstance and not in every place, for “there is a time to be silent and a time to speak,” as Solomon says. And David says: “In my heart I have hidden your words,” etc. For correction must not be poured out incautiously, lest the one corrected be made worse. And if any contradict the view that fraternal correction pertains to everyone, their mouths are to be stopped by that authority of John the Baptist: for \[John rebuked\] Herod, who was a foreigner and incorrigible, yet because it was \[his duty\] to speak to his face openly, saying, “It is not lawful for him to have his brother’s wife” — for the same reason it is fitting to speak openly; indeed every person is bound to reprove any \[member\] of the Church wherever he encounters him, since if he sees the other to be incorrigible, the one who is more bound to love himself than another thereby absolves himself, so as to be able to say with the Psalmist: “Neither iniquity nor my sin.” What shall we say of a certain private \[person\], whether one inclined \[to wrongdoing\] or a fool or an ignorant man — \[as to\] whether they are bound \[to correct\]?
