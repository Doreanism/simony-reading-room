But we, following the Cantor \[Petrus Cantor\], shall say that neither by law nor in practice should a cleric say or do anything that is an efficient cause of someone’s death or mutilation of limbs. He may indeed indefinitely declare what penalties are by law to be inflicted on particular wrongdoers: “by such a penalty the murderer is to be punished, by such the adulterer, by such the abuser.” And if someone proposes to the cleric the argument “if you allow all wrongdoers to live — here is this wrongdoer — and you allow him to live, since you have jurisdiction over him,” the prince dissolves the premiss or the assumption, or demands proof of it, because, in order that he may see him to be a wrongdoer beyond obvious doubt, he must confess — he should not claim not to be a wrongdoer, indeed he should say openly that he is a wrongdoer in justice. Now this stands in law as it declares. But if a wrongdoer is cited before a cleric at his court, and his crime entails a blood penalty, the cleric ought not to deal with that cause either as judge or as assessor; and if the man is confessed or convicted and brought before his tribunal, he must not condemn him to death or to any mutilation of limbs. The pope or another may flog or imprison him, as is said in dist. xii, q. v, the canons, and in the following chapter, “their punishment.” The pope, or his official or another authority, ought never specifically to condemn a heretic or wrongdoer to death or mutilation of limbs. If princes or officials are unwilling to punish anyone with death unless they first hear a letter of instruction concerning the abortion of his \[spiritual\] status, who is the prelate in their domain wielding the sword against them, and they have a wicked counselor in the five \[senses\], and they release wrongdoers in favor of the church — unless it is expressly declared to them that death or mutilation of limbs is the punishment — it seems to us \[improper\].
