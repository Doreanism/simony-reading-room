### viii. purpose of fighting enemies.

The second division follows — namely, on the solemn and the private vow. The question is: what constitutes the solemnization of a vow? And whether the solemnity itself belongs to the substance of the vow? It seems that a profession made in a \[monastic\] enclosure, or in the final enclosure \[week\] in the face of the church, or before a bishop, solemnizes the vow, as is held at cause xxvii, q. i, cap. “viduatus,” and cap. “omnes femine.” Moreover, it is established that the reception of orders and of the habit solemnizes the vow. The question then is why similarly the taking of the cross and a profession made in the hands of an abbot, or in the face of the church, or in the hands of a priest — \[though\] not from God — does not solemnize the vow. But if all these things solemnize the vow, they make one vow different from another simple one — which is proved thus: when it is a simple \[vow\], it prevents \[a future\] marriage from being contracted but admits dispensation; when it is solemn, it both prevents a marriage from being contracted and dissolves one \[already\] contracted, and admits no dispensation. And it seems that solemnities are of the substance of the vow. This is proved by the argument that every vow can be made more binding, since multiple solemnities can be found; and the more solemnities there are in a vow, the more binding it is. And some \[solemnities\] can be found multiple times \[in the same vow\]. If furthermore a vow can be made yet more binding — and anyone can bind the whole \[measure of himself\] — since no one can be more bound by the solemnity of a vow than the Lord Pope in observing continence — because he is somewhat bound as a subdeacon, more as a deacon, more as a priest, more as a bishop, more as a metropolitan, more as a primate and legate, more as the supreme pontiff — so that however many \[solemnities\] are accumulated, and no one can be more tightly bound in the solemnity of a vow than the Lord Pope.
