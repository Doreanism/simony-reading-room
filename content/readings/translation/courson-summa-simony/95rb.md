But if it happens that those who swear by false gods do so as though swearing by mere stones, attributing nothing excessive to the false gods, and what they are swearing by as a creature they refer to the Creator — in such a case Augustinus speaks when he asserts that it is a lesser evil to swear truly by false gods than to swear falsely by the true God. The question is raised whether Jacob \[sinned\] when he received the oath of Laban who swore by his idols, and whether the Jews sinned when they accepted oaths tendered by the Romans to establish terms between them, and if those Romans swore by their idols, as we read in the books of the Maccabees, \[then\] they sinned — because those who demanded oaths from them were cooperating in the \[idolatrous\] worship that was rendered to idols in the swearing. We establish, as we say elsewhere, that a faithful lender gives \[money\] to a usurer, and a faithful speculator gives to a gambler. If it be said that the \[Jews\] descended to swear oaths \[to the Romans\] upon their idols when those \[Romans\] demanded it of them — Solution: I do not believe they would have demanded from them oaths upon their idols with the intent that \[the Romans\] would thereby render them some act of worship in their customary manner, but they demanded oaths from them lawfully, just as one may lawfully request a loan from a usurer in my necessity, even under usury in a case of my necessity — if the \[usurer’s\] intention is directed toward idolatry, it is his sin; if it is my sin, it is also his.

## Next follows the question of the solemnity of oaths, and those things that increase

or diminish the religious weight of an oath, namely: the placing of relics, the bodies of saints, the Gospels, the holy cross, the tomb of the Lord, the facade of the church, and similar things. These increase the religious weight of an oath more or less according to the devotion of those swearing and according to the custom of the religious community, or of religious persons, or of various local customs.
