We concede this, saying — with the Cantor of Paris — that today larger tithes have more broadly succeeded in place of first-fruits and tithes, for the maintenance of the church’s ministers, built up even more. And since \[the firstborn\] were valued more where boys were offered, today no greater \[obligation\] is required, nor is anyone bound to payment, given that the labor would be greater than the value in calculating them. But objection: commenting on that passage of the Gospel, “Render to Caesar what is Caesar’s, and to God what is God’s,” the Gloss says: “Render first-fruits and tithes to God.” Surely, since we are expressly commanded to offer tithes and first-fruits to God in the New Testament — who then can change this precept since it is moral? Solution. There were two kinds of moral precepts. Some were precepts of moral discipline; others were precepts of moral law or nature. Precepts of moral discipline were established for the restraint of a people prone to tumult, or to fulfill \[a particular need\], or to prevent transgression; and such were the precepts which in some manner the beloved Apostle assumed \[as binding\] upon the Lord \[illegible\] so as to restrain them, because they were wandering in the matter of what was to be offered to God, lest they drift toward unlawful things. Such a moral commandment, being for discipline, held then for a time and \[then\] ceased.

But a moral precept of law or nature holds always and everywhere, as do all those things which are in the Decalogue for the formation of equity and law, and which proceed as if from natural law — such as that \[command\] not to make an image of the most high \[God\] in the manner of the most foolish. And such is the commandment concerning tithes: just as Levites who sow spiritual things among laypeople ought by natural law to reap their carnal goods in return, “for the worker is worthy of his wages,” and “those who serve the altar live from the altar.” Hence the precept concerning tithes is moral and immovable. And these are the things to be said about all the kinds of first-fruits and tithes.
