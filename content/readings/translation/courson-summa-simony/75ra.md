## On Prescription

Here \[the question is\] whether, once prescription is complete on these four points, if someone has been aware that a thing belongs to another, he remains bound to restore it. This is stated in the gloss on prescription in chapter 10, in connection with the phrase “quod deo,” where it says that if you know you possess something belonging to another, no number of years defends you. \[Reference\] to dist. xxxiii, q. ii, “Si ungo”; and moreover, if he puts aside the wrongdoing, let him restore what was taken; as in dist. xiii, q. vi, “Si res aliena.” For it is evident that \[he\] possessed \[it\] for forty years; as in extra de prescriptionibus, “de quarta.” Hence if you seek \[judgment\] by oath, by the authority of the law you seek something that no one does; as in dist. xiii, q. v, “Si spectas.” Solution.

To this we say without qualification that even if someone has prescribed goods without knowledge that they belong to another, he remains bound to restore them if he afterwards becomes aware that the thing is another’s, since if he puts aside the sin \[he must restore\] what was taken. We have answered both this chapter and \[the case of\] “quarta” and all the authoritative texts. Indeed, we say further that he who retained another’s property by himself is that much more guilty before God. And one point further still gives pause: even in the secular forum, prescription ought not to defend anyone, because it is a natural right — established before this institution — that no one should ever be able to retain another’s property; and yet neither the pope nor anyone else could act against this, as in dist. xxv, q. i, “Sicut quidam.”

Solution. To this we say that by natural right, if no one ever retained another’s property, then before this institution there was never any prescription; nor therefore is this institution contrary to natural right. For by natural right all things were held in common. Hence just as civil law established, in a restrictive manner, the scope of ownership of things, so it could establish that after a certain period one who possesses \[a thing\] becomes its owner — provided that, according to the canon, good faith had not lapsed and title \[was present\].
