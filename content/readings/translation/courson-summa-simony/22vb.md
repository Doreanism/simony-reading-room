From the first \[penal penalty\] God and the priest together absolve, and both God and the priest bind in the same way; similarly from the second, which is the penalty of excommunication — for the priest excommunicates when he separates the contumacious from the Church, and the Lord does so when he leaves them to be submerged in the ark of the flood, as \[with\] \[illegible\] and the pagan. Concerning the third \[penalty of purgatory\] we likewise say that the priest through the suffrages of the Church celebrated absolves the one entering purgatory; the \[penitent\] himself perfectly binds himself to that penalty when he disdains to complete the penal works. And the priest binds him to that penalty when, as he is dying, the priest imposes the penance that he will discharge there, and the Lord does likewise. The last penalty, that of hell, once imposed is loosed neither by God nor by man, because God and man bind the impenitent to it.

### Question: whether the priest remits sins

Whereas the authority of Augustinus says: “No one remits sins but God alone.” And Ambrosius in the book *On Paradise*: “God alone remits sins, who alone died for our sins.” Likewise Ambrosius: “The priest fulfills his office, if he exercises nothing of his own power.” But against this, Ambrosius says in the same book: “It is certain that the Church \[has both\]” — namely, binding and loosing — \[illegible\] not both there. Likewise, upon that passage “whatever you shall have bound on earth,” \[there is\] the authority: “The heavenly court approves as bound and \[approves\] what is to be loosed” — that is, what the priest binds and looses. And so the priest binds and looses, which is figured by the Levitical priest who, having recognized leprosy, casts the leper out from the camp, and readmits the cleansed one into the camp; and so the Levitical priest bound some and loosed others, and these things apply all the more to the evangelical priest. It seems one could show that the priest can remit no penalty to anyone: thus. Behold someone who yesterday had sufficient contrition, by which all his committed sins were deleted; today he comes to a priest who absolves him. Did that priest remit some sin to him, or none?
