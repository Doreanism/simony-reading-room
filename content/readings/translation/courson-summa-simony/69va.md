And so the question is whether a cleric can be a personal disbursement-holder \[*expenarius*\] by right. For if it is said that he cannot have property from the goods of his prebend, there is no distinction between him and a monk, or a cellarer of a monastery, or an abbot — just as the one who serves under an abbot cannot give anything except at the abbot’s command, so neither can the cleric give except at the bishop’s command. Likewise the Apostle in the epistle to the Corinthians: “Thus let man regard us — as ministers and stewards of the mysteries of God.” Likewise Ambrosius says on this: “\[They are\] stewards in the Lord.” Likewise the Apostle adds: “Here now it is sought among stewards that one be found faithful.” And Ambrosius says: “When someone sees that a steward comes to be found faithful, who can be trusted to dispense well?” Since therefore the stewardship of the Lord’s ecclesiastical goods is entrusted to us clergy, we have nothing of our own in them. For it is established that we are not lords, and therefore in such goods we are neither proprietors nor usufructuaries. Now, an *expenarius* is one who has use of a thing; unlike the usufructuary, whose use is not limited by a fixed definition of the field as another’s — if anything is purchased from the fruits it is his, but when the whole tenure and estate or its inheritance passes to others \[it reverts\]. But this is what happens to us, because if we purchase any estate from the goods of the Church, that estate will belong to the Church; and the same must be said of books and \[other things\] purchased from Church goods. And for this \[reason\] it seems to us that a distinction must be drawn, as above, namely concerning goods: some are patrimonial and personal, possessed by right of patrimony or acquired in respect of the person — as through one’s own property or another such source, as Paul would say. Such goods are properly the cleric’s own. Of those he can make a will, just as a layperson. But other \[goods\] are pastoral or ecclesiastical, which are conferred upon this or that person or church — and he is a steward, not a possessor.
