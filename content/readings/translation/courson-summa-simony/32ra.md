Likewise, ask what is to be said about conditions of this kind on excommunication: “if you honor your father,” “if you believe in God,” “if you appeal.” Suppose he immediately appeals — for the condition is met, and thus he is excommunicated. But against: a sentence pronounced after an appeal does not hold, nor is he excommunicated. Because suppose that he has honored his father and remained at the first point, and has not believed in God — with this contingency arising, the condition is met, and such an excommunication is pronounced: “I excommunicate you if you honor your father, if you believe in God” — which one \[meaning which is the real reason\] is not usually stated. Solution. I do not believe that with such strict determinations the form of excommunication holds, because judges commonly attach conditions of this kind. Likewise, suppose that certain delegated judges, \[whose commission was\] delegated by a \[now\] deceased \[mandator\], pronounce sentence against a contumacious person: it is asked whether such a sentence holds. \[Answer\]: it does not, because when the mandating appointer dies, his mandate also expires. Solution. According to Urbanus who came after Gregorius and was learned in law: we say that such a sentence holds if the one who was delegated had received that commission from the mandator before \[the mandator’s death\] — in those \[cases\] in which the mandate has not yet expired. And it is to be noted that if a sentence is thus pronounced against two, justly binding the one and unjustly expelling the other, it is established that the unjust one binds; but the person who pronounces it unjustly — the binding holds by the Church’s authority, because the Church does not favor what is unjust, nor does external authority dissolve it, but by its own motion, by its own malice which is therefore done by a human being before God, safe from everything before God. Hence on this reasoning, what is bound by God is thus dissolved by \[divine\] authority — so that God’s authority refers to the action; and if it refers to the deformity, it is false —
