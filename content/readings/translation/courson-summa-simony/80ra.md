### V. Or abstinence.

If one asks what the meaning of that way of speaking is — “I renounce the devil and his pomps” — and what is meant by “pomps,” here is a probable answer. For by the renunciation made of the devil we understand renunciation of the devil and all his angels and all his works. To what end then was “and all his pomps” added, since all his pomps are \[included in\] his works? Solution: by “pomposity” is understood that which, by antonomasia, reigns \[most characteristically\] from the devil, namely pride — which made an angel into the devil, just as lust makes a man into a pig. And against this he waged a special combat against pride. And the one who is made a member of Christ through baptism ought to conspire in the same combat, renouncing especially that pride which is the principal sin — namely, the first step in withdrawing from God and the last in returning to him. This is signified in the pride of all its members, and therefore it is indicated in general by the word there: “O Lord my God, if I have done this” — that is, a moral act demonstrable through a general pronoun pertaining to the individual members, in which when one wishes to show what they want to call “pomp” — that is, \[what the French call\] *robant* \[robbing / boasting\]. Similarly, appearing in the Gospel: “It says to his disciples and to Peter” — not because Peter was not included among the disciples, but to name him explicitly lest through the negation \[of having denied Christ\] he might despair.

### VI. Objection.

And if someone fornicates, he sins through transgression of that vow, because he thus consents to the devil and does not renounce all his works — and he thus sins through transgression of the vow.
