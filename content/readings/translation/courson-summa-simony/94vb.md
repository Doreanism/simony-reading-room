truth and justice in an oath. Solution. Just as one who possesses one virtue possesses all, so whoever swears with discretion swears justly and truly. Yet the three companions are not enumerated idly, for every just oath requires the justice of an oath, which consists in justice, discretion, and truth — just as the elements of good life are enumerated as justice, piety, and sobriety, so that “we may live soberly, piously, and justly in this world,” and yet whoever lives justly lives soberly and piously. That it is lawful to swear is shown by the Psalmist: “The Lord has sworn and will not repent.” Also: “He swore and established it.” \[illegible\]. Also, Joseph swore by the life of Pharaoh. Also, Jacob and Laban swore a covenant of peace. Also, the Lord swore to Abraham and our fathers concerning the promise. Hence in the Canticle of Zechariah it says: “The oath which he swore to Abraham our father.” The Apostle also in the letter to the Hebrews says: “Men swear by one greater than themselves, and an oath is confirmation to them.” And in the Apocalypse: “The angel swore by the one who lives forever and ever.” And in the Gospel: “You shall render to the Lord your oaths.” Since it is more admirable \[to say\] “render to God your vows,” the more admirable reading here is “render to God your oaths.” And so if it is more admirable to swear, this is proven by the holy canons: dist. ii, q. v, chapter “Ideo”; and chapter “mandatur”; and chapter “si legitimas.” Gregorius wished that one must swear in a doubtful case, \[and\] was able to prove a sacrament concerning an imposed purgation. Hence blessed Gregorius ordered Bishop Leo to purge himself of a new charge laid against him, having given surety over the body of the blessed Petrus, as is stated in that chapter. And this is found at dist. v, q. v: “By the testimony of the Old and New Testament and by the authority of canons, swearing is lawful — indeed, it is \[sometimes\] obligatory.”
