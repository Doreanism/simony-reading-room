## \[IV\]

“Your brother came deceitfully and took your blessing.” If no one’s fraud ought to benefit him — indeed, he was despoiled of his promise — then, since one who deceitfully gains access to orders or to \[a benefice\] is \[invalidly ordained\], Jacob sinned by claiming another’s property for himself in this way. Against this: a patristic authority on Genesis says that Jacob justly received the blessing of the firstborn, since he took it by fraud. And Jacob received what was rightly his. Likewise Isaac gave the blessing even though he did not wish the blessing to be given, and he gave it to Jacob, and wished the blessing to be given to Jacob. Against this: he did not wish to give it to Esau, and gave it only when he wished to give it, and he wished to give it to Esau. The resolution: We shall say that in the higher wisdom of heaven — in which things are signified more often by metaphor than by the primary meaning of words — it is better to look to the sense in which words were used than to the sense they \[formally\] produce. For a thing is said to be something in Sacred Scripture sometimes in the strict sense, sometimes by reason of alien signification, sometimes by reason of likeness or imitation, sometimes by reason of the purpose of assumption, sometimes by reason of the mode of accommodation. By strict sense: Christ is man. By reason of alien signification or allegory, as when it is said: “Abraham had two sons, one by a slave woman and one by a free woman” — and they were two covenants, that is, they signify.
