Therefore let them take heed: if I give a loan to an advocate so that he may plead on my behalf for ten pounds, that incurs usury. Let them take heed, therefore, if I grant another a loan or he grants it to my house — where I must then purchase another house outside — this is an even greater usury. The same applies to one who has the friendship of parents and advocates, which enables him to exchange \[services\] with me at a prince’s court to prevent the tax-collector from invading my goods or dragging me \[before him\]; for as Gregorius says, familiarity with a prince has this quality — that one is bound to multiply it lest one be unjustly troubled. But that talent given by the Lord — which the advocate sinks into his treasury — he is bound to spend it \[rightly\]; and it is a sin on the part of one who thus receives it \[as a fee\], since he owes it to me \[as a duty\]. Similarly, if he concedes a house about which he ought to do something \[in an urgent matter\], since he in no way wished to rent it out — because he profits in no other way — he acts as he ought in giving me the loan out of charity and in receiving something above the principal, \[which is\] a sin.

To the foregoing and similar cases, let us say in resolution that the complication may be present in the situations above, because whenever a loan is given, it can be given either for the purpose of exchange, or for the purpose of false charity, or for the purpose of equivalent remuneration. In the first way it is usury. In the second it is a meritorious work. In the third it is \[like a\] matrimonial \[bond\]. Again, if your servant drew a soldier of the prince to plunder your goods, and he obtained that \[the soldier\] should not come, and you give me a loan to rouse me to expend \[funds\] — you do not owe usury. But if you withdraw yourself to a man in private, you ought not to say “I paid ten pounds” whether as a gift or in any other way, and you seek absolution from him there, and now you give a loan — you do not incur usury. We say the same concerning a gift. I was troubling you, and I \[stand as your\] advocate or representative and am bound to assist you when called upon in the way of remedial care. If for this you give me a loan, out of urgency to have me do what I am already bound to do, \[this is\] not usury.
