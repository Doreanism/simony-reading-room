If your own prelate does this — keeping silent and as it were unwilling and not daring to bark \[against evil\] — if this is not to be wondered at on your part. Moreover, Hieronymus says: “It is better that a scandal arise than that truth be abandoned without justice or teaching.” Therefore it is better that you cause such a scandal than that by deferring to truth you incur excommunication. Solution: we say that a sentence of a priest laid in the aforementioned manner holds; and if you know that someone has committed theft contrary to that sentence, you are excommunicated if you associate with him. Therefore if you ought to associate with him, whether in private or in public: in private, you ought to express to him the reason why you do not associate with him, so that if \[you do so\] in public you may be a corrector of your brother rather than his betrayer. To the other point, we say that a sentence issued against fornicators — whether laid by the lord pope or by his legate — binds all who presume to act contrary to it, and thus all such persons are held to be transgressors \[of the sentence\] externally. For each person ought to be certain that they have merited such a sentence, since this becomes known through their own confession or through direct observation, because not by rumor alone nor by the offense becoming fully established. As for those who might associate with such persons: \[see\] the chapter on heretics in “Causa XI, *q. De vitiis*.” And it shows how Gregory VII, by the greatest privilege \[drawn\] from the provisions of excommunication \[illegible\], subjected to the yoke of excommunication many to whom he grants communion with an excommunicated lord — namely wives, sons and daughters, slaves and bondswomen, all bondspeople, and all who unknowingly communicated with the excommunicated; guardians; pilgrims passing through the lands of the excommunicated, to whom he gives permission to buy from them; and those who of necessity must support the excommunicated on the last occasion of human mercy.
