something that seems pertinent. Suppose a priest excommunicates in general terms anyone who has committed a certain theft, and it happens in fact that one of his parishioners knew about that theft. If by reason of that offense the lover \[i.e., one who has sympathy for the thief\] incurs the general sentence, and that sentence laid by his own pastor holds — which also appears to be a matter of custom — does it hold if someone is bound under two \[jurisdictions\], unless a delegate to two absent parties, pressing him, is exposing themselves to excommunication? Solution: we shall say, as the canon says, that by reason of the offense one may be drawn into another’s jurisdiction in the aforesaid case, if no free man subjects himself to excommunication, since in this he would be derogating from the right of his own proper pastor. For if \[the sentence\] is issued and takes effect, it would cause the loss of his dominion and authority. Moreover, Origen says on Numbers:

“A priest will purge you yourself,” and a priest can absolve another, which he shows otherwise. Suppose that a priest excommunicates in general terms thus: “Whoever has stolen this, let him be excommunicated,” and afterwards that person steals and is clearly understood to fall under the actual sentence — can that same person absolve himself? Solution: just as no one administers communion to himself in celebrating Mass, and just as no one baptizes himself, and thus neither such a person nor any other person absolves himself, yet someone is bound by a sentence issued by another. But if this position were true, someone excommunicates himself. What Origen said — “a priest will purge you yourself” — is to be understood thus, and this reflexive \[pronoun\] is resolved into a simple final sense: “a priest will purge you yourself,” that is, a priest \[will purge\] a priest like you. Addressing \[the reader\]: whoever receives the determination spoken in the Apocalypse where it says, “Hold fast what you have, lest someone take your crown” — that is, the crown that is rightly yours. Nor is a like relation between owing and being owed \[implied here\], as in the relation “a woman who loved to save herself.” Moreover, whoever.
