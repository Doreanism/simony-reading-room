This is what some princes do with bishoprics, one of which is sometimes worth twenty thousand gold coins. Therefore it is equally permissible for a king to take sixty thousand gold coins on the first day by simply retaining the revenues while holding the benefice. I reply: it is against natural and divine law to hold benefices in this way, outside the hands of those to whom they ought to be assigned, since the church is meanwhile defrauded of its head or member, and the intention of the founders is violated. If it is lawful for a bishop to hold one prebend with a dispensation from the Roman pontiff, then by the same logic he could hold two, or all of them — though the sin would be greater with each. But when with a dispensation there is a period of waiting to determine who is the suitable candidate, and there has not been sufficient deliberation on account of certain reasonable causes that may arise, there is no sin; but six months is commonly quite sufficient for this purpose. There is no good reason to hold a benefice for two or three years while collecting the revenues in the meantime. I argue secondly: it is lawful to sell the right of patronage, and yet the right of patronage is something annexed to spiritual things — therefore. The major premise is clear: if Sortes sells to Plato his lordship over the estate of Caldus, Plato acquires the right of patronage. This is evident from the decretal *Ex litteris* in the same title [*X* 3.38.19], as Alexander III wrote to the bishop of Hereford and the abbot of Ford. The argument runs: Sortes sells to Plato all the right he has in Caldus; the right of patronage is the right he has in Caldus; therefore he sells that right to Plato. I deny that it is lawful to sell the right of patronage directly — it passes with the sale of the lordship without being separately sold, unless the contrary is specified, as stated in the said chapter; and nothing may be given on account of it beyond what the lordship is worth. If the lordship is worth a thousand and no more, then no more than a thousand may be given by reason of the patronage. And I deny the major premise: he does not sell the right of presenting a suitable candidate to the prelate. If you say that Plato acquires the right of presenting a suitable candidate, and by no other title than purchase — therefore he bought the right of presentation — I reply that he acquires the right of presentation by another title, namely that of attachment or accession: just as one who buys a slave does not thereby purchase the slave’s good or evil angel, though they come with him. Yet I grant that the purchase is the sine qua non cause of the right of patronage.

## I argue thirdly:

It is lawful to buy all the fruits of a benefice — such as tithes — and yet these are annexed to something spiritual — therefore. The major premise is clear: this is common practice. Perhaps you will say: it is lawful to buy these tithes — grain, lambs, goats, and the like — and to sell them, but it is not lawful to buy the right of collecting tithes, since that pertains to an ecclesiastical person who holds the title on which the right of collection is founded.

## Against this I argue:

The right of collecting tithes is something — therefore it is either really the tithes themselves, or it is the man who collects the tithes. If the first, then I argue: Sortes the layman may lawfully buy these tithes; these tithes are the right of collecting tithes; therefore it is lawful for Sortes to buy the right of collecting tithes. And in the same way I will prove that it is lawful for another to sell them. Furthermore, a layman who receives the fruits of a church from a prelate for three years has the right of collecting tithes — for otherwise he would collect them unjustly.
