## But against that I argue:

By means of this fourfold distinction, a cover is provided for simony. One who buys a benefice can say: “I do not give a thousand crowns for the benefice as an adequate price or as an ultimate final cause, but as a subordinate end.” Furthermore, one who sells a benefice can say: “I do not receive a thousand crowns for the benefice, but I receive them in order to give to the poor — and if you give them to me, I will give you the benefice.” To the first I say: although a crafty person can hedge and equivocate before men, yet God is the searcher of hearts. I deny that he can rightly say “I give the thousand crowns,” etc., because the money is not a proper means ordered to that end — it is comparable to saying “I wish to kill this man for the glory of God.” Something can be ordered to an ultimate end, but not just anything — he cannot say it, that is, he cannot reasonably say it; for otherwise, anyone could say that the Arabian Gulf is not the Red Sea. To the second I say: no weight should be given to his words or to what he claims — the benefice must be freely given. A person interpretively sells a benefice when, in conferring it, he has no just cause for taking money. Furthermore, it is the same thing to give money to the bishop as to give it to someone to petition the bishop on one’s behalf. It is a different matter if one gives twelve coins to a doorkeeper so as to gain entry to speak with the bishop. Furthermore, even if things were as he claims, it would be a species of evil — and the Apostle teaches us to abstain from every species of evil. But you say: a bishop can take the revenues of a prebend in its first year for the fabric of the church, or for some pious purpose, or reduce the revenues for a time — therefore he can take those revenues on the very first day, directing his intention accordingly, without committing simony; therefore on the first day he can take a thousand gold coins while granting the benefice freely. I deny the inference, and also the antecedent, if this is made into a formal compact with the person receiving the benefice at the time of entry. However, when a rich prebend falls vacant, it is permissible to declare beforehand — before conferring it on anyone — “My intention is, for two or three years, to take one quarter or one third from this benefice for such a reasonable cause; the remainder will support the minister.” But it is another thing altogether to take all the revenues before they have been gathered, and in doing so one commits a species of evil, neglects one’s reputation (which must be preserved under pain of sin), gives scandal to others, and provides a cover for simony — no one will believe whatever he says. So he does not receive the revenues in a pious spirit, and God does not wish pious causes to be advanced in that way. If his intention is in truth to retain them for himself, that is worse still. And although a small gift is not considered sufficient to induce someone to act, yet before God there is no distinction between great and small — save in the gravity and smallness of the sin. But you say: a bishop can hold a benefice without conferring it for two or three years and thus collect three thousand gold coins.
