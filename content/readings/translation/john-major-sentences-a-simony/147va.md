And if such persons are simoniacs, you will condemn them all indiscriminately. I respond: the Gregorian distinction concerning the threefold gift is well known — namely, a gift from the hand, from the tongue, and from service. The same applies to gifts of blood and other illicit causes of conferral. A conferral is not gratuitous when a benefice is given to an unsuitable relative. If you say that the bishop receives nothing — certainly this is true amid a multitude of petitions. Let us pursue what we have begun: first, from the side of the petitioner; second, from the side of the one conferring. A person may lawfully petition for a suitable candidate, and at times is bound to do so, extolling him and urging that provision be made for him so that he may benefit others. A person may also modestly petition on his own behalf, as was said in the preceding distinction, whether for a cure of souls or not, provided it is certain that he is not seeking a charge beyond his capacities. This is in no way simony. But if someone directs his petitions on behalf of an unsuitable candidate, he acts simoniacally. This is clear: he wishes that something annexed to a spiritual thing be conferred not freely. Offering petitions — especially when one judges them to be efficacious — is the same as giving money. Perhaps the petitions of the one asking are worth as much to the one petitioned as a thousand florins, as is commonly said of the “armed prayers” of princes. Strictly speaking, money is more disgraceful. Let those who petition on behalf of unsuitable candidates consider, I beg, the response of blessed Bernardus to Count Theobaldus, who wished to ask him to secure a benefice for a certain young son of his. Speaking in his own manner, Bernardus said: “You know that I love you — but as much as God knows, better than you do, I do not doubt that I am also loved by you. On whose account, if I offend God, there will be no reason for you to love me, since God will no longer be the cause. Therefore, lest I offend God — and perhaps it is not expedient for you either — I would certainly offend him if I did what you ask. For I am not unaware that ecclesiastical honors and dignities ought to be given to those who both wish and are able to administer them worthily and according to God. Moreover, to acquire them for your young son by my prayers or yours — know that this is neither just for you nor safe for me.” And shortly afterward he continues: “Indeed I wish well for your son in all things, but above all I wish him God. What is this? I do not wish him to have anything against God, lest he not have God. And if another should wish otherwise, I do not wish him to have it through me, lest I too lose God.” This response was worthy of such a man. And the Apostle says: “If I were pleasing men, I would not be a servant of Christ” (Galatians 1:10). Concerning service: a person runs to the tumults of the court in various ways. In one way, so that he may relieve his poverty — if a benefice should present itself, he sees that it will be given to him sooner than to another. In another way, principally for the sake of the benefice. The first is licit, provided he is suitable — suitability is the fundamental root upon which the title to a benefice must be grounded. The second is manifestly illicit. As for the one conferring: provided he gives to a suitable servant whose prudence he has tested in service, the one who is a suitable recipient of the benefice acts well. But if he gives it because the person served him, he acts simoniacally. He ought to reward him in some other way than with a benefice.
