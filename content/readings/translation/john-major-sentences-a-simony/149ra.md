## The fourth doubt:

Whether one is bound to make restitution in the case of mental simony. The reply is that he is not, capitulo ultimo of this subject matter. But why, then, is one bound to make restitution for mental usury, as is recounted in Causa 14, quaestio 3, capitulo 1, and De usuris, Consuluit, and by blessed Augustine on Psalm 36? To this question the canonists customarily offer multiple distinctions, none of which is valid. Both simony and usury are forbidden by divine law. The argument of Johannes Andreae is invalid, that in simony there is error on the part of both parties and thus the condition of the possessor is stronger — for sometimes there is error on the part of only one party. Nor is his argument valid that in the case of a benefice the giver gives more of his own: the opposite is true. Who would place a distinction between these two contracts? Sortes gives a bishop a thousand gold pieces, saying, “Lord Bishop, I give you a thousand freely,” and the bishop confers a benefice, saying he gives it freely — though he would never have given it except for money. Plato says, “I give so that you may give” — that is, “I give a thousand so that you may give the benefice.” It is always equivalently pactional. The second party, as is evident, has no title to the benefice but is a thief and a robber, and the longer he holds it the more he sins. A thief is always in default. He does not properly resign the title he holds in the benefice, but rather the title presumed by men. If he is nevertheless of good reputation, he ought to have the intention of resigning as soon as he conveniently can, without defaming himself, when the matter is secret. Nor is the Pope’s permission required in this situation, even if the person is a bishop. This is evident from the Gloss on capitulo Nisi, De renuntiatione: as is stated there, an intruder and a simoniac have no title. This is evident from Causa 1, quaestio 1, Ordinationes. Mental simony in the present context can be committed in various ways. In one way: I would wish to give money and I offer it, but the bishop confers the benefice on me freely since I am suitable — just as when I wish to steal your horse, you give it to me as a gift. In another way: if I had money, I would wish to buy a benefice, and the other party would wish to accept it if it were offered; yet nevertheless, categorically, he gives the benefice without any payment. Furthermore, the discussion here concerns simony that is prohibited by human authority — speaking in the manner of the canonists. In such cases, penance suffices and restitution is not required. Accordingly, the same rule applies to both usury and simony with respect to restitution and non-restitution. Secondly, it is evident that someone confers a benefice through an act that is sinful, and yet the other party truly receives the benefice. The same applies to the recipient. That a simoniac is called a heretic is an extension of the term, as in Causa 1, quaestio 1, capitulo Cum liqueat. Frequently a right judgment stands in the intellect while simony resides in the affection and in the effect. And these things concerning simony — from which may He preserve us who gave to Simon Peter the care of feeding His sheep and lambs.
