Reply: I deny that he bought the right of primogeniture from him. Rather, he redeemed himself from a vexatious situation, waiting for the opportune moment. For when Esau came in from the fields utterly exhausted and nearly consumed with faintness, Jacob seized the moment to advance his own affairs. And the birthright was given — as was the divine will in the matter — which had been made known to him through his mother, who had been informed. On the matter of the case, the text of Genesis says: “And Rebecca went to consult the Lord, who answered her: ‘Two nations are in your womb, and one people shall overcome the other, and the greater shall serve the lesser.’” This, it is thought, he had received from Melchizedek and Heber, prophets of God — it does not matter whether directly from God — and this the mother signified to her son. The son, though simple, without duplicity or guile, yet prudently always waited for his moment in matters that concerned him. God can grant a right without the father’s knowledge or even against his will. If you ask how a man of such exemplary character as Jacob could have acquired the birthright for so small a price, and how a man as nobly born as Esau could have sold his birthright for so little — especially since he was entitled to a double portion of his father’s goods, as is evident from Ephraim and Manasseh, the sons of Joseph, who received a double portion and the territory of two tribes — as regards Jacob, it does not matter whether he paid much or little. Jacob had redeemed himself from a vexatious situation. Although he said to his brother “sell me your birthright,” he meant “give me the birthright, as you yourself shall judge, by way of sale” — foreseeing that Esau would fall into hunger and preparing food for him. The other, making light of the matter and thinking he would die if help were not given, said: “Behold, I am dying.” Whether Esau sinned through intemperance or through contempt of the birthright in selling it is beside the point. And in order to prevent him from retracting, Jacob rightly asked that it be confirmed by oath.

## Against this you argue:

It is not lawful for a man elected by the greater part of a chapter to give money to the minority so that they desist, even though this would be in defense of his own right. This is evident from *De electione*, chapter *Cum in cunctis*. Therefore the solution is null; the assumption is evident from *De simonia*, chapter *Matheus*. The reply is: he did not have an acquired right. It is simoniacal to give money for a right being acquired; the case is otherwise for a right already acquired and where it is established that it is fully acquired. And where one person were as suitable as Hieronymus and others were also suitable, he should not confer anything for a right being acquired. The case is otherwise with Jacob, who had a right acquired from God, who does not need the consent of Isaac or of anyone else. God alone transfers dominions, and no one can resist when he so wills — sometimes partly with the cooperation of another, as he does in the common law. So also in baptism: where a person with a disability cannot find anyone to baptize his dying child, and a wicked person refuses to baptize the infant without payment, the father may give money — not for the baptism, whatever the other may say, but to redeem the harassment that he unjustly suffers, and especially so in a sacrament that is necessary for this infant.
