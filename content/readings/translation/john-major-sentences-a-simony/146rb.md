## I argue against the conclusion:

Daniel 4 says: “Redeem your sins with alms.” And Gregory in a homily says: “The kingdom of heaven is worth as much as you have — one man acquires it for a cup of cold water, a widow for two small coins” (Luke 12). Reply: the kingdom of heaven is not bought, since God receives nothing from us and has no need of our goods; rather, we merit it through good works. The language of buying is metaphorical, not proper. I argue secondly: a priest celebrates Mass for six small coins, and an anniversary Mass is established for four or six crowns — in each case something spiritual is given for something temporal. Therefore, not every giving of temporal things for spiritual things (or vice versa) is simony, or else countless people must be condemned as simoniacs. To resolve this argument, a masterly fourfold distinction is applied to the matter. A priest who celebrates for six small coins can do so in four ways. First, as his chief and ultimate end. Second, as an adequate price, as though governed by commutative justice. Third, for six small coins as a lesser, subordinate end — one that is ordered or orderable to a further, higher end. Fourth, he wishes to celebrate for six small coins and to have the money for his sustenance. The first two intentions are simoniacal and illicit; the latter two are licit and, in one who has grace, meritorious. There is thus an amphibology here: “Sortes celebrates for six small coins” — the word “for” can signify a final circumstance, and this in two ways: either the circumstance of an ultimate end, or of a non-ultimate end; the other two senses follow from these. On this basis it is commonly and rightly said that a canon rises during the night in order to receive his distributions, and yet does so for the sake of God — and is not thereby guilty of simony. Similarly, many come to the choir office at the hour when distributions are given, who would not otherwise come, and yet they do so without the stain of simony.

## But against this:

He would not have his six small coins; nor would the canon come to the canonical hours if there were no distributions. Therefore money is the principal cause: when it is present the attendance follows, when it is absent the attendance ceases — therefore it is the principal cause. I grant the antecedent but deny the inference. When a lesser principal cause is present together with a principal cause, the effect is produced; neither of them alone is sufficient to produce the effect, and if either is removed, the effect ceases. But it does not follow that the six small coins are the principal cause. That cause is more principal which moves more greatly in terms of esteem — even if it carries less weight in formal degree — or to which the other is reduced. He wishes to have the six small coins in order to be sustained; he wishes to be sustained in order to serve God, and not the other way round — just as Socrates used to say that he wished to eat in order to live, not to live in order to eat.
