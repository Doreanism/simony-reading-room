## Seventeenthly it is clear:

A person sometimes enters upon a benefice as a simoniac through his own invincible ignorance — for instance, when his parents give something on his behalf, as is common practice. One says of such a person that he is simoniacal but was not simoniacally promoted. The term is also defined in another way: simony is the will to buy or to sell a spiritual thing or something annexed to a spiritual thing. A book and those things annexed to knowledge — that is, to the created spirit — do not render a person simoniacal. A reader in theology, law, or the arts who receives payment from students (where he has no salary for lecturing, as is the custom in Italy and in England) does not thereby sin; however, in the *Decretals* under the title *De magistris*, there is a prohibition against receiving anything for the license to teach — that is, for granting the authority to lecture — and to do so would be sinful. The chancellor of Paris, though he does receive money from bachelors, freely grants the gift of licensure; yet some of his staff receive payments from arts students for straw and minor items. There is little difference between these definitions.

## Having set this out, I state my conclusion:

Every act of simony is a grave sin. Proof: every act contrary to natural law, divine law, and human law, from which many monstrous consequences follow in the commonwealth, is a grave sin. Simony is of this character; therefore. That it is prohibited by divine law is clear from 2 Kings 5 and Acts 8. That it is contrary to natural law is clear: the simoniac sells something over which he has no ownership — according to 1 Corinthians 4: *Let a man so account of us* — something he cannot transfer to another, and for which there is no proportion between the merchandise and the price. Whence Wisdom 3 says that wisdom is more excellent than all riches, and no desirable thing can be compared to her. For this reason Petrus rebuked Simon, because he thought the gift of God could be acquired with money. *Freely you have received; freely give* (Matthew 10:8). The Gloss says: this text condemns simony and the taking of money for preaching. That it is prohibited by human law is clear from many questions of the First Cause and in the titles *De simonia* [on simony]. For this reason it is compared to heresy [C.1 q.1, *Qui studet*]. Of the daughter-vices that follow from it, Baptista [Mantuanus] says in the third book of his *Calamitates*: “Temples, priests, sacred altars, garlands, fire, incense, prayers — heaven itself is for sale, and God too.” When Antiochus sold the priesthood to Jason (2 Maccabees 4), the sacred fire that had endured for seventy years of the Babylonian captivity was extinguished under water. If the one who steals a chalice, a book, or the like from a church is a sacrilegist, how much more so is the one who seizes an entire benefice by theft over many years — or at least harbors such an intention. That he is a thief is clear from John 10[:1]: “He who does not enter the sheepfold by the door but climbs in by another way is a thief and a robber.” To enter by the door is to enter by a just title.
