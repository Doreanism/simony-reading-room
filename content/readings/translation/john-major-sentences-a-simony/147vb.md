## The seventh argument:

Saul, in 1 Samuel 9, gave money to Samuel so that he might render judgment about the donkeys. His servant said to Saul: “Behold, there is found in my hand a quarter of a shekel of silver; let us give it to the man of God, that he may show us our way.” The same is evident from 1 Kings 14, concerning the wife of Jeroboam, who brought to Ahijah the prophet ten loaves, a cake, and a jar of honey. Furthermore, the Roman Pontiff can sell a canonry, since in the Roman Curia simony has no place, just as the *Lex Iulia* on electoral corruption ceases to operate in the city of Rome [*Digest*, *ad legem Iuliam*, *lege una*]. Likewise, there is no prohibition on the sale of a canonry in either the Old or the New Testament. To the first point: we do not read that the good prophets accepted anything for the gift of prophecy. Samuel fed Saul and his servant, but we do not read that he accepted anything. Secondly, a bishop may rightly accept something given freely, without any agreement — he accepts a palfrey offered liberally. Elisha accepted nothing from Naaman the Syrian; it appears he did the same in the case of Benhadad, king of Syria. The Jews brought only small gifts to the prophets; foreign gentiles alone brought great ones. To the other point: it seems to some that the Roman Pontiff can sell a canonry, and they say that certain things are simoniacal because they are prohibited, while others are prohibited because they are simoniacal. In the first place, the supreme Pontiff can grant dispensations; the prince is released from the laws. But this distinction is empty: all simony is prohibited because it is evil and is contrary to natural and divine law. Therefore the Pontiff sins against every such precept if he commits simony, just as anyone else does — and indeed more so on account of the dignity of his person. It is, however, quite true that certain things are prohibited on account of their proximity to simony, under certain human penalties by which simony is prohibited. This is evident from *De rerum permutatione*, chapter *Quaesitum*, and the same title, *Cum olim*. In those chapters it is stated that no prior agreement concerning the exchange of benefices should be made, and that those who exchange them on their own authority lose their benefices. And yet this is not simony: I see that a benefice would be more useful to me among certain people wavering in the faith, so that I might instruct them in it and so that I might be removed from my relatives, or because the people hold me in contempt; for another, the place where I am might be more useful. For these reasons we exchange benefices. If there were no prohibition or nullification, this would not seem to be simoniacal in any way. But because under this pretext men greedy for gain can frequently accumulate the goods of the Church avariciously, the Church has prohibited it. This is not simony. Let them, I ask, produce some example of something properly simoniacal and show that the Pope can contravene it without sin.
