If he is a simoniac but was not promoted simoniacally, the dispensation is easier. If he is both a simoniac and was simoniacally promoted, the dispensation is more difficult. Although such a person acquires orders through an unlawful means, nevertheless the orders are truly acquired, as happens in many cases of this kind. Concerning the acquisition of benefices, the question is graver and more difficult for simoniacs. A simoniac who holds a benefice simoniacally is bound to relinquish it into the hands of the person to whom it falls, and to have the intention of relinquishing it as soon as he conveniently can. He is further bound to surrender all the fruits collected, minus the expenses he incurred in collecting and preserving them — and this if he was a simoniac promoted simoniacally. This is evident from *De restitutione spoliatorum*, chapter *Gravis*, and *Digest*, *Familiae herciscundae*, *Fundus*, *quaestio* 1, *responsione in fine* — that is, concerning fruits collected. In the case of a simoniac not simoniacally promoted, once he has been informed of the truth, he too is no longer a possessor in good faith. To hold a title simoniacally is the same as having no title at all: he enters as a thief and a robber. Now a thief is bound to restore what he has obtained by theft. And I believe this should be understood of the person who holds a benefice through money. If someone obtains a benefice from a collator by reason of kinship, prayers, or service, he is not bound to make restitution any more than anyone else, provided he is suitable — even though prayers, kinship, or services greatly influenced the decision. He is not bound to make restitution; otherwise no flesh would be saved. It suffices, it seems, to repent of the sin by which he procured the title, or because he unworthily received it. And thus the saying of the lords the jurists is partly true: there is no simony except pactional simony — that is, simony subject to the canonical laws requiring restitution. And I understand “pactional” to mean either formal or interpretive. An example of the formal: “I give so that you may give” — that is, “I give a thousand so that I may have this canonry or this cure.” Some simony is interpretive, differing from formal simony only in words: at the time of the conferral of a benefice, Sortes says to the collator, “I will give you a thousand pounds sterling freely; give me the benefice freely” — while nevertheless having his mind’s eye fixed on the benefice, and the collator acting in like manner. Or, as I have understood of certain persons: he says to the collator, “I will wager six hundred gold coins against an equal sum. You would not dare confer the benefice on my son, would you? Or will you not confer it?” But if you suppose someone assenting to this proposition — “I give you a thousand freely” — at the very moment of the conferral of the benefice, with the external execution of the benefice, to assent to that proposition which he utters: I say that he cannot assent to it freely. And whatever may be said, it is ridiculous to claim that such presumed simony is not punished under the penalty for formal simony. The recipient is bound to restore the money to the church that has been wronged — thus a bishop who receives such a payment must give it to his church. But if a multitude sins, the whole amount must be given to the poor. Do not investigate too scrupulously whether great prelates were made simoniacally — that is not your concern. Excuse them in all things through invincible ignorance, and God supplies the rest. Nor is it called invincible ignorance because it cannot be overcome, but because one is not bound to overcome it. It suffices for you that he receives from the multitude as your prelate. Go boldly to receive orders; obtain a dispensation for a vow, a benefice, a marriage, and in other matters when there is a reasonable cause for having one. Here a rough moral certainty suffices — namely, that it is so considered by others and the opposite is not established to you. Few are those — or fewer than you think — who acquire benefices by money. No one possessing the appearance of respectability ascends by that path. You frequently interpret your neighbor’s case in the worse sense. Even when a bishopric is conferred: the order is conferred and the character is impressed. Simony does not impede this, even though it impedes the exercise of orders. And even granting that the order were not conferred, the character is still impressed, and this suffices. In this respect it is proportionate to the sacraments as regards reception. If you speak of the case of a single Pope: Pope Nicolaus says [*Distinctio* 99, chapter *Si quis pecunia*], “Let him who is elected by money be regarded not as apostolic but as apostate.” The Gloss says this is true when he is elected by persons other than the cardinals and those lacking authority. It is evident that this Gloss is from the school of Orleans. Others have no power to elect, whether simoniacally or otherwise — and this contradicts the text. I do not admit that the lord cardinals would commit this vice. A sufficient portion for election would not be corrupted — namely, two-thirds — and perhaps all of them. And this is confirmed by the fact that so many mendicant religious and others have been elected; it is a sign that money accomplishes nothing.
