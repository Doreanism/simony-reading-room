But suppose such a man were to be sold, as Paulinus bishop of Nola sold himself to free the son of a widow. Then the argument runs: Plato buys this priest; this priest is the right of collecting tithes; therefore Plato buys the right of collecting tithes. I deny the inference. One who receives a benefice for three years collects justly and has the right of collection — but not by reason of office; a beneficed person has the right of collection by reason of office. It is otherwise with one who receives from such a person. And grant for the sake of argument the impossible case that a beneficed priest were a slave: even though it is contrary to reason, his lord would have no dominion over the goods of the church, which are not the lord’s, nor may they be alienated in that manner. If the lord’s own goods are placed at the disposal of the tithe collection, I grant that similarly Plato buys the right of collecting tithes — but he does not buy the right of collecting tithes by reason of office. “To buy” implies an act of the will and thus entails a rational designation. The complex expression “the right of collecting tithes by reason of office” either refers to the prelate — connoting that it is incumbent upon his office to collect these fruits — or to the tithes themselves — connoting that they can be collected by virtue of such an office.

## I argue fourthly:

It is lawful to purchase burial in a sacred place, and yet burial is something annexed to spiritual things — therefore. The major premise is clear: it is the common practice of parish priests. Furthermore, the patriarch Abraham bought the double cave at Hebron, the city of Arba, from Ephron son of Zohar (Genesis 23). And one cannot say that the place was not sacred, since Adam and Eve were buried in it — therefore. I reply by denying the major premise, as is clear from the chapter *Abolenda* on burials [*X* 3.28.2], and from Causa 13, Question 2, *Questa est* [*C.* 13 q. 2]. After the fact, however, it is lawful to compel the friends of the deceased to observe praiseworthy customs — but not beforehand. There must be no exaction and no compact in advance. Perhaps you will say: the custom had an evil origin and arose from the avarice of parish priests — therefore no one should be compelled to observe it; for wherever there is a bad custom or corrupt practice, anyone can be compelled to fulfil it. I answer: it is to be presumed that the custom had a good origin, since the contrary has not been established before us — it is otherwise with a corrupt practice that is established as such. But our parish priests pursue this matter more zealously than they pursue sins in their subjects. The Apostle, however, said: “I seek not yours, but you.” To the other point: there is nothing inconsistent in something being a matter of counsel for a time, and then becoming a matter of precept as time goes on in order to eradicate abuse. To the argument from the patriarch: it is said that he bought those fields in which the double cave is enclosed, just as it is lawful to buy a district or village in which there is a church. Or he asked for it as a gift — the text says “give me” — and although Ephron said he wished to give it to Abraham, he nonetheless assessed the price above the just value, and without offending him Abraham could not have obtained it. Abraham had need of Ephron’s friendship, being a stranger, and so he redeemed himself from the difficulty. In those days the custom was different, as the blessed Ambrose says.
