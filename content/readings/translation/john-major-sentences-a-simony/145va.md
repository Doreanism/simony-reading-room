Simony can be defined thus: simony is the will to retain or to give a spiritual thing, or something annexed to a spiritual thing, not freely. “Spiritual” can be taken broadly enough to encompass what is annexed to the spiritual; we need not dwell on verbal dispute. “Will” is placed as the genus: every simony is a will, or its proportional equivalent — a refusal — as when someone is unwilling to administer a spiritual thing freely when he is bound to do so and in the manner in which he is bound. I said “placed as the genus,” yet this is not the quidditative genus of simony; the predication “simony is a will” is indirect — a less connotative term is predicated of a more connotative one — and the connotation of the term “simony” expresses the thing’s nominal definition. The remaining parts function as the differentia. “To retain or to give” is stated disjunctively so as to include both those who receive money and those who wish to give it. By “spiritual” some understand a gratuitous supernatural gift of God that cannot be had from purely natural powers alone — and this accords with the reply that Petrus gave to Simon Magus: *thy money perish with thee* [Acts 8:20], adding the reason: *because thou hast thought that the gift of God may be purchased with money* — such gifts as the varieties of tongues and the gift of knowledge, of which the Apostle speaks in 1 Corinthians 12. The sacraments are spiritual in the sense that in them something spiritual is given, namely grace (unless there be an obstacle in the recipient), even though in their material constitution they are corporeal, as we explain when treating the individual sacraments. The right to receive the fruits of a benefice is spiritual — that is, it belongs to the one who bears spiritual responsibility and hierarchical authority by reason of such a benefice, such as the duty to preach and to celebrate the liturgy. Likewise prayer and blessing are spiritual, being ordered toward a spiritual effect. One must attend not only to what is spiritual by essence, but also to what the term imports: the term “sacrament” carries a spiritual connotation. That may be called spiritual which is immediately ordered toward the benefit of persons in the attaining of eternal life; civil polity, by contrast, is ordered immediately toward the tranquility of peace. The vessels of a priest and benedictions can be called spiritual. What is “annexed to a spiritual thing” includes the fruits of a benefice, the right of patronage, and the like — things attached to spiritual goods — for in these cases the accessory follows the condition of the principal. Thus it is clear, though not easily explained, what is to be called “spiritual.” I do not follow the approach of others who classify the spiritual by essence, by causality, by consequence, and by concomitance. Just as Simon Magus — from whom this plague takes its name — disguised himself enigmatically under various forms, so it is with this monster: it is no easy matter in either case to distinguish simony from what is not simony.

## From the foregoing, certain consequences follow. The first is:

There is such a thing as mental simony — indeed, every simony is mental, though it cannot be recognized except through external acts.

## Secondly it follows:

Some simony consists in an act and some in a habit. From this a third consequence follows: a person may be in simony without imputable sin, as is clear in the case of one who retains a habit after sufficient detestation of it, since the habit has not yet been entirely expelled.

## Fourthly it follows:

A contract is called simoniacal when it arises from simony and is commanded by it.
