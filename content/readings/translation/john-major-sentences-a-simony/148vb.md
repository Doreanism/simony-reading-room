Fifth, what I posit about the consent of the one confirming is merely a presumption of simony. If someone has sincerely held a single benefice and it is afterward said — though falsely — that he obtained it through simony, even if he believes this and later discovers the truth, it suffices to repent of the evil act; his title is not thereby undermined. But concerning the resignation of a simoniacally acquired benefice, there is a den of thieves: they resign with one hand so as to receive with the other. If it were asked of them whether they would resign if they had no hope of recovering the benefice, I do not know how I would answer. It would perhaps be good never again to confer these benefices on such persons. I said “perhaps,” because if there were no other hope of recovery, they would perhaps not resign but would always retain the benefice with great loss to their souls. Straightforwardly: where someone resigns a simoniacally acquired benefice into the hands of the competent authority, purely and simply, and receives it from the one who has the power to dispense and confer — provided he is suitable for performing what is incumbent upon that benefice — he accepts the benefice with a clear conscience and now holds by just title what he formerly held unjustly. If Barabbas were to steal the horse of Plato, and Plato in the meantime conferred it upon him as a free and lawful gift, Barabbas would now justly possess that horse. But the fruits expended throughout the entire period of the simony, which belonged to him no more than to any other person, must be applied to pious uses; if they are no longer extant, their equivalent must be provided. It does not suffice to say, “The Roman Pontiff remits these things to you so that there need be no further mention of them,” because this has not been committed to him by Christ except for edification — nor will he ever attempt it. You will depart under a great burden on your conscience. It is better for a man of formed conscience to resign the benefice purely and simply, and to allow the collator to give it to whom he will, so that the matter may be clear.

## The second doubt:

Against the foregoing, one who holds a benefice through gifts of the hand [munus a manu] is bound to restore the benefice into the hands of his superior. But a gift of the tongue [munus a lingua] sometimes moves a person just as much as a thousand crowns; the same applies to service and kinship. Therefore, if the one is bound to resign, so is the other. I deny the consequence: the cases are not otherwise equal. You see that one who receives a gift of the hand is bound to restore it. It is otherwise with a gift of the tongue and of kinship. For service, a servant should be paid in proportion to his labors — indeed, to give a benefice and nothing else is a strong presumption of simony. Secondly, the one case is certain and shameful, and gifts commonly have more influence: they blind the eyes of the wise. When someone is suitable and serves through kinship or prayers, the presumption is in his favor, and on account of the particular uncertainty the benefice may be retained. But when it is patently established that someone was unsuitable at the time the benefice was given to him, and he is now suitable, the wise course would be to seek it anew from the one who has the right to confer. If he does not obtain it, I do not know whether he is bound to make restitution — perhaps not. But where the matter was doubtful from the beginning and he is now suitable, he is in no way bound to make restitution. If someone confers a benefice on a child in order to collect money meanwhile contrary to reason, you see that he collects money for something annexed to a spiritual thing, and the solution is ready at hand. Nor does it suffice for the child to remit the money to him when he has grown up, even in the Church triumphant; the entire amount must be restored. You will not be pressed in the Church militant, since no one other than that person will demand from you an account of your stewardship.
