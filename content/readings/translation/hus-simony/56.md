And so you now have it: that a bishop ought not to consecrate churches, chapels, altars, or clergy for money. Therefore, a canon addresses both cases: \[Gratian, Decretum\] “We establish that just as nothing shall be taken for the consecration of altars, of churches, or for ordination to the priesthood, so also nothing shall be given by the priests who receive the oil \[that is, for consecration\] — either for balsam \[that is, for consecration\] or for lights; but let bishops purchase balsam and lights from the revenues of their churches, each in his own church.” This canon — when will it be fulfilled by those who take money for the holy oil, and by those who receive it and then sell it dearly, thereby falling into heresy?

And these sales are condemned by the Synod of Tribur and by a canon which states: “It has been said that in some places it has been customary to give money for receiving chrism or oil, and likewise for baptism, and for the reception of the Body of God. That this is simony, the holy synod has condemned and anathematized, and has decreed that henceforth nothing shall be demanded for ordination, for chrism or holy oil, for burial, or for the reception of the Body of God; but that the gifts of Christ shall be freely distributed by voluntary giving.” — There you have the decree of a great council, which has its foundation in Christ’s commandment, who says: “Freely you have received, freely give.” \[Matthew 10\] Therefore those who give these things for money have no excuse for not being
