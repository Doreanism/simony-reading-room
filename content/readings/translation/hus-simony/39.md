And from this argument, without need of further proofs, it is evident that whenever a pope decrees or commands something unworthy, and the Christian community recognizes it as such, then that community, and indeed every Christian, upon recognizing that the decree is unworthy, must regard it as unworthy and must in no way obey him therein — lest, following the blind, one fall into the pit of eternal damnation.

But perhaps a little demon will appear and say that no one should judge the pope’s actions, nor say to him, “Why do you act thus?” To this little demon one must say: be silent from your blasphemy, and do not attribute to a sinful man what belongs to God alone. God himself is the one whose actions no one can rebuke, nor judge as evil, nor say to him, “Why do you do this?” But that a person should not, in his own mind, regard openly evil acts as evil — whether they be the pope’s or any other man’s — how can the little demon rightly prevent this? For virtually all reasonable Christendom judges that Peter, Christ’s first vicar, sinned mortally when he denied his gracious Savior under oath! And we must also judge that we have all gone astray along with all the popes and are corrupt to the last — save the one who, having committed no wickedness, redeemed us straying ones from eternal death. And Saint John, the beloved of God, whose holiness the popes of today do not attain, says: “If we say that we have no sin, we deceive ourselves and the truth is not in us.”
