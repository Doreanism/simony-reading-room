For the king is established by God to place worthy men in office and to drive out the unworthy; as Saint Gregory and Saint Augustine say, and as Saint Peter and Saint Paul have been cited above as witnesses. And this the king promises at his coronation, when he speaks as follows in the name of Christ: I promise that I will command and powerfully fulfill in deed that the Church — that is, the assembly of God — and all Christian people shall at all times maintain true peace by my commandment and judgment. Behold, he says in his promise: “that all the people shall have true peace;” and since no one has true peace who does not have peace with God, and since simonist priests have no peace with God — for by simony they wage war against God — the king is therefore obliged to press the priests toward having true peace with God. For if he were to disregard the battle of the priests by which they war against God, he would already be unfaithful to the Lord God; just as a king’s servant would be guilty and unfaithful if he took no heed when priests wrongfully defamed the king and waged war against him. And furthermore, since the king rises against a bishop when the bishop wages war against the king and wrongs him, why does he not rise against bishops when they wrong God by simony or other manifest sin — knowing, O king, that you are the more bound to defend God’s honor than your own?
