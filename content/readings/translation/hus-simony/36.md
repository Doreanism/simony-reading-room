Yet someone will say: After the death of a bishop, the pope may hold the bishopric as long as he wishes and keep its revenues for himself, and the same with other benefices — why then should he not rightly take the first-fruits from the one who wishes to become a bishop or other prelate? And that he may indeed do this is argued on the grounds that, first, he is the supreme lord in the world; second, that without any agreement he gives the dignity to a worthy person, and so is no simoniac since he is only taking what is his own; third, that since the pope has so ordained, no one ought to speak against it — as his heralds, the priests, and his lawyers say.

To this there is a ready answer. When it is said that “after the death of a bishop the pope may hold the bishopric as long as he wishes and keep its revenues,” I say that the pope cannot rightly do this according to the law of God, nor ought holy Christian Church to permit him to do so; for a pope could be so avaricious that as bishops and other priests died off, he would retain all the revenues for himself and leave the people without shepherds. And so he himself would not labor, and would send out no others, except such as would seek profits for their own purses, and not the salvation of souls and the glory of the Savior Jesus Christ.
