The second way of this simony is the various ordinances he issues for temporal gain contrary to the law of God — not openly, perhaps, but in ways that can be shown to be contrary to the law of God. Is it not contrary to the divine order for him to decree that his cooks, doorkeepers, grooms, and physicians should have first claim to benefices of higher dignity in lands whose language they do not know? Or again, that no benefice may be announced \[as vacant\] until money has been deposited? And how many other such ordinances there are!

The third way of papal simony may be that he appoints bishops and parish priests in exchange for money. Evidence of this in our own time is plainly set before us: many thousands of gold pieces were paid for the archbishopric of Prague.

### \[C\]

Behold, in these three ways a pope can be a simoniac! But if any pope guards against these things and follows his Savior in holy living, then he has rightful use of all things in the world, just as the apostles had, and he should not be proud of it.
