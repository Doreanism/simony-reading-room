The devil has yet another excuse: that when people come to the pope seeking a bishopric or some other dignity, he exacts nothing, nor speaks of money, nor of how much the petitioner must give — only whatever the one seeking the dignity wishes to give; and so the pope commits no simony and incurs no guilt. To this excuse the action of Saint Peter gives the answer, who, unwilling to receive money from Simon lest he be a party to his traffic, said: “Your money perish with you, because you thought to purchase the gift of God with money!” And who could excuse Peter and the other apostles if they had accepted money from Simon?

But perhaps Lucek \[a hypothetical defender\] says: Simon said, “Take the money and give me that power”; but this \[other\] man says nothing — he gives \[money\] beforehand or afterward, once what was sought has been granted to him, silently, and so there is no commerce. To this Lucek, Hodek the baker or Huóda the market-woman would give the answer: that when a man has bread before him and someone comes, and before taking the bread or after, lays down a coin in silence, Hodek or Huóda will say that he has bought the bread. The same holds here. Therefore, simony is correctly defined as the evil will to exchange a spiritual thing for a temporal one.

But Lucek says: The pope had no intention of receiving money. Lucek! If that be so, then \[the pope\] would have said as Peter said: “Your money perish with you!” And thus no one would venture to come to the pope with money, just as no one ventured to come to Peter with money after \[the case of\] Simon. But how are we to believe this, when we carry great sums of money to Rome for the Prague archbishopric
