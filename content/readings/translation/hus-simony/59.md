But alas! they hold the title, yet — as they ought to be \[present\] among the people to whom they were made bishops — they are of no benefit there! Therefore the Czech who gave them the name “suffragan bishops” \[*planí biskupové*, literally “idle” or “empty bishops”\] perhaps did so because they profit little among the people, and especially among the people of their own bishopric. And the fourth answer: let them be asked why they had themselves ordained, knowing that they had no intention of going to that people, and also that they do not know the people’s language? And if they told the truth plainly, they would have to confess that they obtained the bishopric for worldly honor, for bodily pleasure, and for freedom. And therefore these suffragan bishops and patriarchs are often monks, as is evident — for in this way they escape the obedience of their senior brethren in their order.

### \[H\]

One might still say: If there were no such bishops, who would ordain clergy, consecrate churches, altars, and other things? I will answer: the archbishop or bishop of his own diocese; for that is what he is established for. But you say: Some do not know how, and some are not priests, and some do not wish to consecrate. Truly, in saying this you are indicating that they are not worthy of the bishopric, and that if they do not repent, they will pay dearly. And indeed a strange arrangement has come about: formerly, the higher a man stood in dignity, the more he labored in his office for the benefit of the holy Church.
