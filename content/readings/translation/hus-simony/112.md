Behold, for these reasons it is a most fearful thing to hold any right of presentation.

And know that the right of presentation is not the church, nor the altar, nor any material thing, but is a spiritual power; and having this power, the lord is able and obliged to ensure that the benefice previously given serves well to the glory of God. Therefore the right of presentation is called in Latin *patronus*, that is, father or defender; for as a faithful father he must be diligent that the church, altar, chapel, or given benefice stands well in the spiritual order.

### \[E\]

And a person becomes a patron — that is, a holder of the right of presentation — for three reasons, \[198\] namely: for the site, \[199\] for the foundation, and for the endowment: for the site, because he gave it so that the church might be built on it; for the foundation, because he founded it; and for the endowment of the benefice, so that the priest might live from it. And for these three reasons the patron or right of presentation carries three other things: honor, burden, and benefit — honor from the people and from the priest, who must hold him in respect; burden, that he must take care to give \[the benefice\] to a good person, so that good spiritual care may be maintained there, and also that he must not grow proud in this; the benefit is to be eternal reward, provided he does not destroy it.
