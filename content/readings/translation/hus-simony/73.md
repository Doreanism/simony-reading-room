And they are no less excused than bishops, because by their vow they are more separated from the world, by their rule bound more strictly to poverty, and because by their outward signs — such as the cowl, the tonsure, and other insignia — they profess poverty and the rejection of the world: therefore, in spending unworthily the estate that has been given to the poor for God’s sake, they are — as much as bishops, and even more so — struck down by simony according to the words of St. Gregory. For they have already despised by pleasure and other evil customs the rule which they vowed to fulfill in poverty, in suffering, and in separation from the world; for what they promised to God and to their patron, they do not keep, and so they are faithless both to God and to holy Church and to their patron beyond their oath. And that they spend badly, St. Bernard testifies against them frequently: that not only do they spend excessively toward the world, but that they make and adorn excessive and unnecessary things in the monastery.

And whoever does not have the books of St. Bernard, let him read in their \[own\] deeds: for, as we see, at their tables they surpass worldly lords in servants, in dishes and their preparation, in plates and goblets, in drink and spoons; and in travel by wagons and on horseback they surpass worldly lords and knights.
