Again, thus: A layman who has hewn and erected an altar has performed greater labor than the bishop who merely blessed and anointed it a little; and the layman readily received less \[payment\] for his labor — why, then, does a bishop, being a father, take more from his children? Nor is there any excuse for one who would say that the bishop’s labor is more valuable than the layman’s carpentry or masonry; for the bishop’s labor would be more precious only because it is united with a spiritual thing. And since the labor is therefore precious on account of the spiritual thing, the argument stands that it is for spiritual things that the bishop takes that money; for otherwise it would be a great shame to a bishop, being so well endowed and dignified, to work for money among peasants!

Again, thus: Since a bishop is endowed in the land so that, having means of livelihood, he may willingly fulfill all his spiritual duties, he is not entitled to take money from the people for that labor; like a workman who, having received one wage for his work, would demand that a second be given him!

Again, I would ask how dearly the labor of consecrating one altar is to be valued?
