The second manner of simony is: giving bishoprics or other dignities in exchange for service. And therefore many serve at courts; others, abandoning their parishes, serve in hopes of obtaining richer ones. The third manner is: giving dignities on account of kinship. In this many nobles are guilty, who give \[benefices\] to unsuitable relatives who would not be worthy to keep pigs! The fourth manner is: giving for the sake of petition or for some other carnal pleasure — as when someone gives because a priest knows how to hunt with him, play chess, or engage in other diversions. And there is also another cunning little trick \[practiced by some\]: the matter is broached or proposed as follows: “How much would it be worth to you, my lord, to give my son a church?” And the lord says: “It would be worth fifty kopy \[a unit of currency\] to give it.” And he says: “Done.” And the lord says: “Well then, I give your son the church — you will give fifty kopy.”

And there are many other devilish stratagems by which the devil deceives both laity and clergy, leading them into simony; such as fathers and mothers, wishing to place their son in a spiritual dignity, who give gifts, serve princes and lords, and purchase bulls from popes.
