I say “holding of error,” for without that one cannot be a heretic; and because holding cannot occur without assent, there can be no heresy in a person unless he consents to the error. And so heresy, like every mortal sin, has its nest and foundation in the heart, that is, in the heart’s will — as the Savior says, that out of the heart proceed evil thoughts, murders, adulteries, fornications, thefts, false witness, and blasphemy.\[2\] Behold, he says that blasphemy, which is heresy, proceeds from the heart. From this you understand that neither any man nor God himself can make a person a heretic without his own consent. Second, from this you understand that heresy does not reside solely in the rational spirit, which by its will stands against the truth of Holy Scripture; for since every mortal sin resides in the rational spirit and every heresy is a mortal sin, therefore every heresy resides in the rational spirit.

Furthermore, it was said that heresy is obstinate holding — that is, holding from which a person is unwilling to withdraw for any period of time or ever. For if a person held an error, believing it to be the truth, yet held it in such a way that as soon as he recognized that what he considered truth was in fact error, he would immediately count it as error — then he is not a heretic. Therefore St. Augustine says that however much some may have gone astray, if they earnestly seek the truth and, upon finding it, immediately abandon the error, such persons are by no means to be called heretics.
