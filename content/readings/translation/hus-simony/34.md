And beyond this right to the enjoyment of the world, he has yet another right: to serve the holy Church, to govern it, to teach it, and to direct it according to Holy Scripture. But this is something other than secular lordship, in which men — and especially churchmen — easily go astray. For the office of the pope, as also of the apostles, is to preach the word of God, to administer the holy sacraments, and to pray earnestly to God on behalf of the people; but to govern the wealth of this world belongs to the lower, secular estate. Therefore the pope should note how Christ and Peter took care not to become entangled in the management of worldly goods; for when one man said to him, “Master, tell my brother to divide the inheritance with me,” Christ replied to him: “Man, who made me a judge or an arbitrator over you?” And again, when someone said to him, “I will follow you wherever you go,” Jesus said to him: “Foxes have holes, and birds of the air have nests, but the Son of Man has nowhere to lay his head.” And he also said to Pilate, when Pilate said, “Your own nation and the chief priests have handed you over to me; what have you done?” Jesus answered: “My kingdom is not of this world. If my kingdom were of this world, my servants would certainly have fought that I not be handed over to the Jews. But my kingdom is not from here.”
