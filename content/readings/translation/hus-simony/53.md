So says that ordinance, so that you may understand that the labor of preaching, celebrating Mass, consecrating, anointing, baptizing, hearing confession, and administering the Body of God — that labor is so bound up with the spiritual office, that is, with divine power and divine ministry, that it neither ought nor can rightly be sold for money. Although people ought to support priests in that labor, the priests may not sell that labor, nor put a price on it; for in that case every simoniac could excuse himself, saying: I am selling the labor! Therefore the Lord God says through the prophet Malachi:\[95\] “Who is there among you that would shut the doors and kindle my altar willingly? I have no pleasure in you, says the Lord of hosts, and I will not receive an offering from your hand.” Here Gratian, who compiled the Decretum, says: that shutting the doors is not the office of the holy sacrifice but belongs to the spiritual ministry.\[96\] Therefore the excuse by which they defend themselves, saying that they sell the labor, is not valid.

And there is yet another argument against that excuse: Since the bishop sells the labor, they ought to give equal money \[i.e., the fair market price\] for it. And since a parish could buy such labor from another person for much less money, the bishop does wrong by his children in selling them the labor at so high a price.
