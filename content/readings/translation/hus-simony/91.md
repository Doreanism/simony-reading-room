### \[F\]

But since, in the aforesaid simony, they have an excuse, saying that it is the laudable custom to give \[money\] for confession, for burial, for the sacraments, for baptism, and so for the other sacred things mentioned above, the answer to this is that it is not a laudable custom to give money for these things — it is, rather, a simoniac custom, as Pope Innocentius says: that neither before nor after ought anything to be demanded on account of custom, for it is simony. And again the ordinance says that the custom of giving for such things is simoniac heresy and accursed. And again the ordinance says: For burial and for the reception of \[holy\] oil let no one attempt compulsion or extortion, nor let anyone defend his sin under any custom whatsoever; for the antiquity of time does not diminish sins, but increases them. And they think, it seems, that by that reasoning it would be permissible, presuming as they do upon the law of death \[i.e., burial fees\] by long custom, not perceiving that sins are the heavier the longer they hold the unhappy soul bound. — The meaning of this statement is as follows: many have made some sin into a habit, holding it as though it were a law, when it leads them to eternal death; and the longer and the more anciently it holds the soul bound to the devil and fettered, so that being thus bound it can do no good — the heavier is that sin; for antiquity and habit of sin never lighten it, but make it heavier.
