It is clear as to the first that one cannot come to the reforming of the unity of the Ecclesiastical body except through the medium of the primitive Church, to whose form or Ecclesiastical sanctions the reformation of the present Church is ordered — namely, that “you may have one heart, and one soul in the Lord” (Acts 4). For it is in him alone that our schisms are united, as blessed Augustinus says in the first book of the Confessions, addressing the Lord: “You have made us for yourself; our heart is restless, unless it rest in you.” And further, in Book X: “In all these things which I run through, consulting you concerning creatures, I find no place for my soul in which my scattered self may be gathered, save in you. Whatever is not in you withdraws from me.” Of which the Savior signifies the necessity, saying: “Martha, Martha, you are careful and troubled about many things: but one thing is necessary” (Luke 10). On which B. Augustinus in the Homilies says: “The one is set before the many. For the one is not from the many, but the many are from the one. The many things belong to the one, who made them.” Thus he.

In him, the communion of the prelates made is the Church’s union; from him, their division is the Church’s rupture.

This is urged thus. The true universal Church consists in particulars, according to Augustinus in On the City of God, Book XVI, chapter 4. Now the particular Church of particular prelates is spoken of by the Savior (Matthew 18): “If he will not hear you, tell the Church.” Therefore, if the communion of particular prelates in God is the union of the universal Church, so their division from the Lord through sin was the occasion of the Schism.

But, you say, it is harsh to hold such things about Prelates. I answer: would that they themselves, recognizing the crime, held such things about themselves. For with blessed Augustinus, On the City of God, Book 14, chapter 14, I dare to say: “It is useful to the proud to fall into some open, manifest sin, so that those who had fallen by pleasing themselves may become displeasing to themselves. For Peter more salubriously displeased himself when he wept than when he pleased himself when he presumed.” Hence the Prophet says: “Fill their faces with shame, and they shall seek thy name, O Lord” — those, that is, who pleased themselves by seeking their own.
