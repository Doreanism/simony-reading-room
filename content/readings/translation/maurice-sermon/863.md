Wherefore, most reverend Fathers, since you have been made rich in all speech and in all knowledge — as the testimony of Christ has been confirmed in you, so that nothing is lacking to you in any grace — I beseech you by the name of our Lord Jesus Christ that there be no schisms among you, 1 Corinthians 2 \[actually 1 Cor. 1:10\]. Schisms, I say, neither with regard to God nor with regard to neighbor. With regard to God, I say, because they are against nature, according to Augustinus, *De civitate Dei* book 11, chapter 17: Vice is so contrary to nature that it harms nothing but nature. Nor would it be a vice to withdraw from God, except for a nature whose vice it is, since it was made to be with God.

Wherefore, for the exclusion of schism with regard to God, let each one examine his own conscience, and thus set before himself the judgment of his own censure: whether in the secret of his heart he finds that peace which Christ gives; whether no concupiscence of the flesh assails the desire of the spirit; whether he does not despise humble things nor, likewise, covet lofty ones; whether he does not immoderately rejoice in the increase of his own goods; whether he is not inflamed with envy at another’s happiness nor gladdened at an enemy’s misery; whether his justice is not cheapened by the power of flatterers, nor by the favor of kinsmen, nor by the price of a gift.

And when he finds, perhaps, that none of these disturbances is in him, let him with sincere scrutiny inquire what kinds of thoughts frequent him, and whether he acquiesces in no images of vanities. For to be moved by no enticements and tickled by no desires does not belong to this life, which is wholly temptation, and by which he is too thoroughly conquered who does not fear to be conquered by it.
