# A Counsel for Reforming the Ecclesiastical Estate at the Council of Constance

“To tell you openly” \[John 16\], taken originally from John 16, and transumptively from the Office of the Mass.

Most reverend Fathers, Lord Masters, and bases and pillars and stakes of the tabernacle of testimony, figured in Exodus 27.

As I am about to address your beatitude, I find myself caught in a twofold judgment: namely, that upon minds burdened with sorrow a certain torpor of diffidence may creep in as I speak. This is so, first, because of the dignity of my hearers. Blessed Gregorius (near the end of Book VI of the Moralia) calls it a terrifying thing, when he says: “When the mind flourishes in teaching among such persons, ignorance is grave, wishing to teach those who are better. For in this case words lose the force of their rectitude when they do not suit the hearer, since medicines too lose their virtues when applied to sound limbs.”

Second, on account of the depravity of morals. For, as the same author says in the Pastoral Rule, “it is harsh that one who does not know how to govern his own life should be made judge of another’s.”

Third, on account of the slowness of what must be said. For, as Petrus Damianus writes, “when the Word of God is set forth to the ears of the curious, attention is paid to whether there is charm of artful style; the coloring of rhetorical elegance is sought out; and the curious mind pries into the captious circuits of syllogisms and enthymemes.”

Concerning which slowness we have a precept — that in the temple of God a grove of unfruitful loquacity is forbidden to be planted (Deuteronomy 16). And we all undoubtedly know that whenever the stalks of a bad crop thrive, the kernels of the ears swell with less fullness. Yet the Savior, in whose hand are both wisdom and the discipline of work and of knowledge (Wisdom 7), from whom also is our sufficiency (2 Corinthians 3), consoles our poverty, saying, where the theme is: “I have many things to say to you, but you cannot bear them now; but when that Spirit of truth shall come, he will teach you all truth.” Therefore, to obtain his arrival, let us greet the Virgin Mother, full of the same Spirit, saying with serene mind: Hail Mary, full of grace.

## “I have things to say to you,” as above.

According to blessed Gregorius, in a sermon in the Moralia, “in speech, when something is said, it is necessary that time, cause, and person be weighed: whether the truth of the judgment strengthens the words, whether the fitting time demands it, whether the quality of the person does not conflict with the truth of the judgment and the fittingness of the time. For he praiseworthily shoots his darts who first spies out the enemy before he strikes.” Thus Gregorius. And since the degrees of what may be said arise from the degrees of being, on account of the style of truth: hence, because I see you gathered, first for the reforming of the unity of the Ecclesiastical body, second for the excluding of the depravity of right conduct, and third for the raising up of the dignity of the priesthood, therefore I have to speak to you on these three matters. Urged on by the necessity of fruitful fidelity, “that I may have some fruit among you” (Romans 1); by the authority of commanding knowability, which I have from you, “to whom it is given to know the mystery of the kingdom of God”; by the usefulness of courageous utterance, by which the Savior, moved, said: “Simon, I have something to say to you” (Luke 7).
