Hence Augustinus, in *De Civitate Dei* XIV.10, says: “At present one lives well enough if one lives without grave crime. But whoever judges himself to live without sin is acting not so as to have no sin, but so as not to receive pardon.” And further, in Book XXII, chapter 23: “Let us know that, however vigorously we fight against our vices or overcome them, so long as we are in this body they cannot be entirely absent from us. Therefore let us say to God: *Forgive us our debts*.”

I say, second: let there be no schisms among you with respect to your neighbor, since the very law of nature resists such schisms. Of this Augustinus, near the end of Book XII of *De Civitate Dei*, writes: “Nothing is more effective against the vice of discord than the remembrance of that one parent whom God created as one, and from whom a multitude might be propagated, so that through this shared origin unity might be preserved among the many.”

This unity will be seen if, from this sacred council, there are banished the affections of particular causes which generate quarrels, and the hatreds which hinder catholic unity — just as is related to have been done at the opening of the councils under Constantinus, in the first book of the *Historia Tripartita*. Otherwise, *if you bite and devour one another, take heed that you be not consumed through ambition*, Galatians 5, leaving the last things worse than the first.

I have not moistened my lips at the horse’s fountain, nor have I been steeped in wine on twin-peaked Parnassus. For, to use the words of the Prince of the Apostles, *we have not followed learned fables, but have made known to you the power and coming of our Lord Jesus Christ, having been made beholders of his greatness*, 2 Peter 1. Concerning which I had things to say to you with respect to the first point.
