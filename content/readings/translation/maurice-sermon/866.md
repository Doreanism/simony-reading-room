Indeed, the kingdom of Solomon was torn asunder on account of his disordered love of women, which exchanged the worship of God for the temporal worship of the Devil through idolatry. 1 Kings 11. The kingdom of Rehoboam, moreover, was torn from him in \[chapter\] 12 on account of his hatred, etc., by which he despised the counsel of the elders and embraced that of the young men. And the kingdom of Jeroboam was likewise taken from him on account of the disordered price which he received for selling the priesthood. For it is said in the same book, chapter 13, that Jeroboam made priests of the high places from the meanest, that is, from the most worthless of the people. And for this cause the house of Jeroboam was overturned and blotted out from the face of the earth.

It is evident, from the correspondence of the figures to the things figured, that the kingdom of the Church is first torn asunder on account of the whoredoms of its rulers, which are purchased from ecclesiastical provisions, after the example of Solomon. These provisions, I say, are spiritual goods, as will be proved below, which are exchanged for temporal and momentary whoredoms.

Secondly, \[it is torn\] on account of contempt for the sanctions and decrees of the Holy Fathers against simoniacal depravity, and on account of the embrace of novelties, after the example of Rehoboam — by those who assert that simony cannot fall upon the Pope. Truly the lying pen of the scribes has wrought a lie. For if in the new law simony befell Judas, a disciple chosen by Christ, then equal to Peter, who also valued a person in divine things at thirty pieces of silver — how should it not befall the Pope, chosen to the apostolate by men?

But why did it fall upon Judas? Truly, because he held bad faith concerning Christ, as a heretic does. And such are all simoniacs. For Remigius says in the preface to the third Psalm: that Judas did not believe Christ to be the Son of God, but rather some sort of magician and sacrilegious man.

Simoniacs, however, think worse of the Holy Spirit than heretics do, as Augustinus testifies against Maximianus, who said that the Holy Spirit is the servant of the Father and of the Son: the simoniacs call Him the servant of the coin.
