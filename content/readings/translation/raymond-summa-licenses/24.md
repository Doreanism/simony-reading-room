I respond with Laur.: strictly speaking it is not simony, but shameful gain. Arg. *Decretum* Causa 1, quaest. 3, cap. Vendentes.

In sum, note that in all of the above cases the word “money” \[pecunia\] is understood to encompass everything that men possess on earth, whether movable or immovable, as in *Decretum* Causa 1, quaest. 3, c. Totum.

TOtum, quidquid homines possident in terra, omnia, quorum domini sunt, pecunia vocatur: servus, vas, ager, arbor, pecus, quidquid horum est, pecunia dicitur. Ideo autem pecunia vocata est, quia antiqui totum, quod habebant, in pecoribus habebant. \[Everything whatsoever that men possess on earth — all of which they are masters — is called money: a slave, a vessel, a field, a tree, livestock; whatever falls under these categories is called money. It is called “pecunia” because the ancients had everything they owned in the form of cattle \[*pecora*\].\]

Item, in such simonies I believe that the bishop can grant a dispensation to the truly contrite and penitent, since it is not forbidden to him; which suffices.
