And a bishop consecrates a church freely and likewise receives a procuration \[hospitality allowance\]. Extra, de simonia, c. Cum sit.

Solution. I say, with Laurent. and Joan., that if his own means do not suffice him competently and honorably, he may lawfully demand payment in moderation; otherwise he ought to demand nothing, but may accept what is voluntarily offered. *Decretum* I, q. 2, c. Placuit; *Decretum* XXXIII, q. 2, c. Quaesta est; *Decretum* Dist. 18, c. De Eulogiis.

Placuit, ut quoties ab aliqua fidelium ad consecrandas Ecclesias Episcopi invitantur, non quasi ex debito munus aliquod a fundatore requirant; sed si ipse quidem aliquid ex suo voto obtulerit, non respuatur: si vero aut paupertas illum, aut necessitas retinet, nihil exigatur ab illa; hoc tamen unusquisque Episcoporum meminerit, ut non prius dedicet Ecclesiam, nisi antea dotem Basilicæ, & obsequium ipsius per donationem chartulæ confirmatum, accipiat. Nam non levis culpa est illa temeritas, si sine luminariis, vel sine substantiali sustentatione eorum, qui ibidem servituri sunt, tamquam domus privata, consecretur Ecclesia. \[It was decreed that whenever bishops are invited by any of the faithful to consecrate churches, they should not demand any gift from the founder as though it were owed; but if the founder freely offers something from his own vow, it should not be refused. If, however, poverty or necessity restrains him, nothing is to be exacted from him. Nevertheless, let each bishop remember not to dedicate the church until he has first received a written donation confirming the church’s endowment and the arrangement for its service. For it is no light offense rashly to consecrate a church as though it were a private house, without lights or without adequate support for those who will serve there.\]

For if it were made obligatory to bring such offerings at that time, they might come less willingly to the Synod and be more reluctant to attend, since \[such offerings\] are not, in their view, to be reasonably required, nor, when brought voluntarily, to be refused.\]

## §. II.

What of the Chancellor of Paris or Bologna, or the masters of schools, or the teachers of other churches, who out of greed or long-standing custom are sometimes unwilling to grant a license to teach unless something is given to them?

To this I say that neither he who seeks a license ought to give or promise anything, nor should any of the aforesaid exact anything. If this is violated, the exactors ought to be stripped of their ecclesiastical dignities and benefices. Extra, de magistris, cap. 4 and 3, or c. Prohibeas.

Prohibeas, ne in parochia tua prædicantia docendi exigatur aliquid, aut etiam præmittatur. Si quid vero postea solutum fuerit, vel promissum, remitti promissum facias, & restitui, appellatione cessante, solutum, sciens quod scriptum est: Gratis accepistis, gratis date. Sane si quis distulerit Magistros in locis congruis instituere, tibi liceat, appellatione postposita, ibi aliorum instruendis præficere viros pravidos, honestos, & discretos. \[Prohibit anything from being exacted or even promised in your parish for the practice of teaching. If anything has been paid or promised thereafter, have the promise remitted and, with appeal suspended, the payment restored, knowing what is written: “Freely you have received, freely give” \[Matthew 10:8\]. Indeed, if anyone has delayed in appointing masters in suitable places, you are permitted, setting aside appeal, to appoint there prudent, honorable, and discreet men for the instruction of others.\]

Quanta Gallicana Ecclesia Majorum per bonarum scientia & honestate præfulget, continentius nititur cavere, quæ confundere Ecclesiasticam videantur honestatem, tanto vehementiori dignos esse animadversione censemus, qui nomen Magistri scholarum, & dignitatem assumunt, & sine certo pretio Ecclesiasticis viris docendi alios licentiam non dant. Cum autem hæc prava consuetudo a cupiditatis radice processerit, & de admodum Ecclesiasticæ honestatis confundat; mandamus quatenus (consuetudine ipsa de vestris Ecclesiis exstirpata) sub anathematis interminatione hoc inhibere curetis, districte præcipientes, ut quicumque viri idonei, & litterati voluerint regere studia litterarum, sine molestia, & exactione qualibet, scholas regere permittantur: si qui vero hujusmodi prohibitionis, vel præcepti exstiterint transgressores, eas officiis, & dignitatibus spolietis. \[Inasmuch as the Gallican Church shines so brilliantly through the learning and integrity of its great men, and strives more earnestly to guard against what may seem to dishonor ecclesiastical propriety, we judge the more worthy of severe censure those who take on the title and dignity of master of schools and yet do not grant ecclesiastical men a license to teach others without a fixed payment. Since this corrupt custom has arisen from the root of greed and greatly dishonors ecclesiastical propriety, we command that, once this custom has been rooted out from your churches, you take care to prohibit it under threat of anathema, strictly ordering that whatever qualified and learned men wish to govern schools of learning shall be permitted to do so without any harassment or exaction. If any transgress this prohibition or command, strip them of their offices and dignities.\]

And although the disgrace applies to both parties, and therefore the possessor’s condition might seem the stronger — *Digest* de cond. ob turp. Caus. l. 3 — nevertheless relief is here given to the master who promised and paid, so that the promise is remitted and what he paid is restored to him, with appeal suspended and notwithstanding any custom, not for his own benefit but out of odium for the other party. Extra, de Magistris, c. Prohibeas and c. Quanta.
