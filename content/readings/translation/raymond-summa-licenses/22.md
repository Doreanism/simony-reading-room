These matters are proved at dist. 37, c. *De quibusdam*; Extra, de magistris, c. *Quoniam*, and c. *Quia nonnullis*.

*De quibusdam locis* — It is reported to us from certain places that neither masters nor provision for literary studies are to be found. Therefore let every care and diligence be applied by all bishops, subject parishes, and other places where need arises, so that masters and teachers may be appointed who shall continually teach literary studies and the doctrines of the liberal arts; for in these things the divine commandments are chiefly made manifest and declared.

*Quoniam* — Because the Church of God, as a pious mother, is bound to provide that the opportunity of reading and advancing not be taken from the poor who cannot be aided by their parents’ means, some suitable benefice shall be assigned to a master at each cathedral church who may freely teach its clerics and poor scholars — and further: for the license to teach, no one shall exact a price, nor under pretext of any custom demand anything from those who teach, nor shall any one forbid teaching to anyone who is suitable, once license has been sought from them; whoever shall presume to act against this shall be deprived of his ecclesiastical benefice; for it seems fitting that one who, out of greed, seeks to impede the advancement of the Church by selling the license to teach, should not enjoy the fruits of his labor in the Church.

*Quia nonnullis* — Because the opportunity for study and the chance of advancement is denied to some on account of poverty, the Lateran Council provided by a pious constitution that in each cathedral church a suitable benefice should be assigned to a master who would freely instruct the clerics of that church and other poor scholars — so that the need of teachers might be relieved and the way to learning opened to students. But since this is little observed in many churches, we, confirming the said statute, add that not only in each cathedral church, but also in others where resources may suffice, there shall be appointed a suitable master, to be chosen by the prelate together with the chapter or the greater and sounder part of the chapter, who shall freely instruct the clerics of those churches in the faculty of grammar and others in accordance with his ability. Indeed the metropolitan church shall also have a theologian who may teach priests and others in sacred scripture, and instruct them especially in those things known to pertain to the care of souls. Moreover, the chapter shall assign to each of the masters the revenues of one prebend —

— and the same to the metropolitan theologian — not so that he thereby becomes a canon, but that he may receive those revenues for as long as he continues in teaching; but if by chance the metropolitan church should be burdened by two masters, it shall provide for the theologian in the manner described above, and shall arrange for the grammarian to be provided for in another church of its city or diocese that is able to suffice.

## §. I.

*Whether a master commits simony if he exacts something from the poor, or from the clerics of the church from which he receives his benefice.*

I respond: yes — for since this is a spiritual matter and he is bound to perform it by virtue of his office, it is certain that to exact anything on this account is simony. Extra, de simonia, c. *Nemo* \[X. 5.3.1\]. Those who sell in the Temple are those who bestow for a reward what by right belongs to certain persons. Causa 1, q. 3, c. *Vendentes* \[C. 1 q. 3 c. 2\]. Beda agrees with this, at the same question, c. *Non solum* \[C. 1 q. 3 c. 4\].

*Non solum* — They are not only sellers of doves and makers of the house of God into a house of trade who seek sacred orders by paying a price in money, praise, or honor. But also those who exercise the spiritual grade or grace in the Church which they received by the Lord’s gift, not with a simple intention but for the sake of some human recompense — acting against the word of the Apostle Peter: “Whoever speaks, let him speak as the oracles of God; whoever ministers, let him do it as with the strength that God supplies, so that in all things God may be glorified through Jesus Christ.” Therefore all such persons, if they do not wish to be removed from the Church when the Lord comes, let them remove these things from their conduct, lest they make the house of God a house of trade.

By these same authorities I say that a master commits simony who, when a feast is to be observed by right or custom, refuses to grant scholars leave to observe it unless money is given to him; or, conversely, grants leave to observe a non-feast day in exchange for money.

But can he exact a salary from other scholars who are neither poor nor of that same church? Or, can a master at Paris or Bologna lawfully make a collection?

It seems that he cannot, because knowledge is the gift of God and therefore cannot be sold. Causa 1, q. 3, c. *Non solum*, as above \[C. 1 q. 3 c. 4\]; indeed he should rather cast money away, after the example of Socrates, who threw away a great weight of gold, seeing that he could not possess both virtues and riches at once. Likewise, professors of law, even if they may honorably accept what is freely offered, nevertheless dishonorably demand it: ff. de var. & extraord. cog., l. 1, §. *Est enim quidem* \[Dig. 50.13.1 pr.\].

But on the other hand: one who renders service over time ought to receive a benefit in return, 12, q. 2, *Charitatem* \[C. 12 q. 2 c. 32\]. Likewise a master may sell his teaching just as a jurist may sell his counsel, 14, q. 5, c. *Non sane* \[C. 14 q. 5 c. 12\].
