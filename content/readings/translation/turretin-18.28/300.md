# Question XXVIII.

On the Stipends of Ministers and Ecclesiastical Goods.

Whether some stipend is owed to the ministers of the church? Affirmed, against the Anabaptists.

## I.

This question was previously raised by the Anabaptists, who held that the gospel must be preached freely — either by the wealthier sort, who have sufficient means of their own to live on, or even by the poor, who secure their livelihood and necessities through some trade and their own labor after the example of Paul, who practiced tent-making and worked with his own hands (1 Corinthians 4:12). They considered the Reformed ministers to be not true ministers but mere hirelings, who take up the ministry after the manner of a business transaction and are accustomed to selling and hiring out their labor to whoever offers the highest wage. They do indeed acknowledge in the Emden Colloquy, Act 78, sect. 4, that the church is bound to provide necessities for the ministers it has called — but only in the case where ministers lack sufficient means to support their households honorably, whether from an inheritance, trade, craftsmanship, or some other secular calling. Outside that case, however, they hold that ministers are bound to preach out of charity and without charge.
