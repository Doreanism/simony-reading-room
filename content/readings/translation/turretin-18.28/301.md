## II.

The orthodox establish the following position. Not only may a minister live from his ministry, but he ought to. And although in a case of necessity — namely, when he can be supported neither by the church he serves nor by others — he may provide the gospel without charge; and indeed, even outside a case of necessity, he may, for weighty reasons and out of Christian charity and prudence, remit to some church that debt, and thus refresh it by his own alms for a time or permanently; nevertheless he is not bound by divine and human law to do so. Rather, it is the church that is absolutely bound to the procurement and payment of a just stipend.

## III.

The arguments are as follows. 1. From the passage of Paul in 1 Corinthians 9:4–19, where the seat of this doctrine resides, and the Apostle not only claims this right to receive a due stipend — and this authority — for himself and for all ministers, but confirms it by various arguments drawn from both divine and natural law, and from approved examples. The first argument is taken from the Apostles (v. 5–6): “Do we not have the right, as do the other Apostles, to take along a believing wife? Or is it only Barnabas and I who have no right to refrain from working?” The second is from the analogy of the soldier, the farmer, and the shepherd, to whom natural equity and reason dictate that stipends are owed (v. 7): “Who serves as a soldier at his own expense? Who plants a vineyard and does not eat of its fruit? Who tends a flock and does not drink of the milk of the sheep?” The third is from the prescription of the law concerning the threshing ox, argued from the lesser to the greater: since God forbids muzzling an ox while it threshes (Deuteronomy 25:4), and thus prohibits that laboring oxen be barred from their fodder, how much less should the ministers of the church be deprived of the fruit of their labor? For by this law God has in view not so much oxen as men, lest they be cheated of a just wage for their work (vv. 9–10): “Is God concerned with oxen? Or does he say this entirely for our sake? Because the one who plows ought to plow in hope,” etc. The fourth argument is from the comparison of unequal things — labor and wages (v. 11): those who sow spiritual things ought to reap material things. The fifth is from the divinely instituted order in both the Old and New Testaments (v. 13): “Do you not know that those who perform sacred rites eat of the things of the sanctuary, and those who serve at the altar share in what is offered at the altar? So God has ordained that those who proclaim the gospel should live from the gospel.”

## IV.

2. From the command of Christ in Matthew 10:10 and Luke 10:7, where, sending the Apostles to preach the gospel in Judea, he forbids them to carry anything on the road — not even food — but wills that they live at the expense of those to whom they are sent. He gives the most just reason for this, namely, that the laborer is worthy of his food and wages. This shows that the *βιωτικά* necessary for sustaining themselves and their households are no less justly owed to the ministers of the gospel by their hearers than a wage is justly owed to a hired laborer for his work and effort.
