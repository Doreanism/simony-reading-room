But since each minister is the master of the stipend earned by his own labor, it is equitable that he disburse it, as he sees fit, to the poor and to other uses according to his own judgment, not another’s.

## XIV.

Moreover, these stipends may be discharged in various ways: either from the spontaneous offerings of the faithful, who contribute generously from their own goods out of Christian charity and justice for the common use of the Church — as was done by the first Christians in the time of the Apostles and for several centuries thereafter; or from a mutual agreement and contributions pooled from individuals; or from the public treasury, paid out by a Christian magistrate; or from tithes; or finally, gathered from the annual income and proceeds of fields and estates donated and bequeathed to the Church, and from other ecclesiastical goods. Whichever of these modes is employed, it matters little or nothing at all to the substance of the matter, provided that sufficient stipends are paid to the ministers of the Gospel, and that a measure be observed in them — that they be not too meager and fall below what is adequate for honorably sustaining the pastor and his family; nor yet so lavish as to conduce more to luxury and ostentation than to what is sufficient and necessary. For either extreme is equally dangerous and to be avoided: lest ministers be held in contempt by an ungrateful people on account of too straitened a household, or lest they waste themselves in luxury, idleness, and empty pomp on account of too sumptuous and splendid a provision — as was done in the Papacy.

## XV.

Although a pastor is bound in certain circumstances to minister without charge — if he has the means to do so; when the church is very poor, or newly planted and still tender, and would not receive the gospel unless it were free of charge; or when sectarians do this and reproach our ministers for not doing likewise — it does not follow that this right does not belong to him. But if the church is sufficiently wealthy yet refuses, whether out of avarice or out of contempt and disdain for its minister, to grant the necessary stipend, the minister is permitted to leave that church — after having first tried all other means — and to offer himself for the ministry of another church; for so great a hostility toward the minister, not to say wickedness, warrants and permits his departure.

## XVI.

If one asks what is to be thought of the gratis of the monks, and especially of the Jesuits — which is prescribed to them by Ignatius, the founder of the Order, in Rule 17 — the answer is ready at hand: it is not only gross hypocrisy, but also a bold and cunning license for scraping together everything. For however much they pretend to teach gratis, they are nevertheless most ready to receive the choicest fields, the fattest estates, and enormous revenues offered to them in the manner of a free gift or alms. And so they perform their labor not gratis adverbially — that is, δωρεάν — but gratis nominaliter, that is, for those who acknowledge the benefit with a grateful mind and make return for it.
