## X.

One thing is to lack the right to seek and demand a stipend. Another is to be unwilling to exercise that right for certain reasons. For while the right remains, its exercise and use can here and now be suspended. Paul was able to do the latter and in fact did so for weighty reasons, Acts 20:33 and 2 Thessalonians 3:8–9. But he makes this neither a rule for himself nor for other ministers to follow constantly, since elsewhere he is recorded as having made use of his right.

## XI.

One thing is *misthophoria* \[wage-seeking\], another is *philargyria* \[love of money\]. In Micah 3:11 the priests who taught for reward and the prophets who divined for money are reproved — namely, those who on account of money to be extracted from the wealthy taught falsehoods, who spoke pleasing things and promised a prosperous condition of the commonwealth, so that they might receive more generous stipends; whereas they ought rather to have severely rebuked the sins of their hearers and urged them to repentance by threatening punishment. But from this it cannot be inferred that it is unlawful to receive stipends, because for those very priests who are accused of avarice, God had instituted a liberal provision in his own law.

## XII.

One thing is to embrace the ministry for the sake of a stipend and petty gain. Another is to demand a stipend and to agree upon it for the sake of duly carrying out the ministry. The former belongs to hirelings, who aim solely at making profit from the gospel. But the latter belongs to true pastors. Certainly, if no wages at all were owed them for the work they do; if such a collection were truly voluntary and gratuitous, as alms are — then it would not be lawful to negotiate stipends, and a stipulation of that kind would be sordid and simoniacal. But because they are most justly owed in accordance with the prescription of God and Christ, it is lawful to negotiate them when they cannot otherwise be conveniently obtained; nor in doing so is there any blemish, stain, or suspicion of a mercenary spirit, provided they are moderate, not excessive to the burden of the flock, and sufficient for the honorable support of the pastor and his family. The measure of such stipends ought to be determined by the counsel of the prudent and the common consent of the church. But if any persons out of stubbornness and impiety and an ungrateful spirit — not out of poverty — withhold them, they deserve to have the Word of God not proclaimed to them and to be denounced by the pastors.

## XIII.

If there are any among the ministers who can comfortably and honorably support themselves and their families from their own patrimony, they are not therefore to be deprived of their ministerial stipends.
