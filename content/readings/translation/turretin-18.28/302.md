## V.

III. From the stipends of the sacred ministers in the Old Testament, Numbers 18, verses 8–12, to which the sacrifices, tithes, firstfruits, and other things of that kind were ordinarily allotted, besides certain cities and suburban fields, Numbers 35. Although in the New Testament we are not bound by those laws with respect to the specific material from which, and the manner in which, stipends were given, they nevertheless remain in force with respect to their general principle and analogy, as is clear from the passage cited above in 1 Corinthians 9:13.

## VI.

IV. From the example of Christ and the apostles, who did not refuse to accept from the faithful the necessities for their support. Christ received \[support\] from women, Luke 8:3, as did Paul from the Philippians, Philippians 4:10, 15–16. And in 2 Corinthians 11:8 he says that he received wages (*opsōnion*) from other churches in order to carry out his ministry to the Corinthians, so that he might preach the gospel to them without charge — namely, to avoid the ill will of avarice and base gain with which the false apostles were endeavoring to burden him.

## VII.

V. From the threats that follow upon the withholding of stipends. For the failure to pay stipends to ministers is counted among the gravest sins of injustice toward one’s neighbor and impiety toward God, with which God threatens punishments, and to those who do the contrary he promises his blessing: Malachi 3:8, 10, 12, Nehemiah 13:10–11, Galatians 6:6–7. “God is not mocked,” says the apostle; “whatever a man sows, that shall he also reap.”

## VIII.

VI. From the absurdities and inconveniences that follow. For if the necessity of stipends were denied, it would follow: 1. that in the election of ministers one must look to qualities and prerogatives of the flesh, and that those should be preferred who could support themselves from their own means and preach without charge — with the result that very often the best and most deserving would have to be passed over in elections. 2. That a dangerous distinction would arise among ministers, some of whom out of charity and a consciousness of God would furnish the gospel without cost, while others out of necessity would burden the church — and so the latter would be counted more contemptible, more odious, and less welcome. 3. That stipends would thus be turned into alms, and ministers would be thought to live on alms, which is false, and their condition would be rendered contemptible. Hence the wise provision in the discipline of the Reformed churches of France that fixed stipends be assigned to each pastor, and that no one be promoted to the office without them, article 42 on Pastors.
