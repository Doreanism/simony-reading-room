## XVII.

Regarding ecclesiastical goods, to say something of their origin and growth. It is certain that their beginning dates already from the time of Christ, through the pious and devout offerings of the faithful — and of women especially, whose names are recorded in Luke 8. The administration of these funds was entrusted to Judas, and they were intended to serve partly for the use of Christ and the Apostles, and partly for the use of the poor, as is explained in John 12. After the ascension of Christ, the first church of Christians gathered at Jerusalem — partly to demonstrate mutual charity, and partly to be less exposed to the injuries of enemies and more ready for migrations and flight — pooled its resources into a common treasury, with the wealthier members relieving the poverty of the poorer even at the cost of their own fields (Acts 2 and 4). This community of goods, however, pertained not to possession (*κτῆσις*) but to use (*χρῆσις*), and it obtained neither in the other churches outside Jerusalem nor did it endure there for long; and so individual churches had their own treasuries, into which each would contribute his share, and into which collections would be deposited — as is evident from the Apologies of Justinus \[Martyr\] and Tertullianus.

## XVIII.

It is not established among writers at what time the Church first began to possess fields and landed estates. It is certain that already before Constantinus the churches held lands and properties, and that the Roman church had been enriched above the rest by the most generous offerings — sufficient not only to sustain the clergy and the poor, but also to relieve other churches. But the lavish benefactions by which the Church was enriched to an immense degree first flourished in the age of Constantinus. For this emperor, in his zeal for the Christian religion, restored to the churches what had been seized by his predecessors, and apportioned to them a share of the revenues, tributes, and public grain, and offered most magnificent gifts fashioned from golden and silver statues of idols. But as for the donation of the city of Rome together with the empire of the West, which is attributed to him in Dist. 96, C. Constantinus, it has long been observed and proved by invincible arguments to be false and spurious — by learned men even within the Papacy itself: Petrus de Vineis, Dante, Marsilius Patavinus, Cusanus, Valla, Otto Frisingensis, Volaterranus, Nauclerus, and even Aeneas Sylvius himself. The gift, therefore — whatever it amounts to — ought rather to be attributed to Pipinus than to Constantinus, although even he did not lavish as much or as great as is commonly believed. Far more probable is it that the Popes, when Italy was ablaze with internal wars, had seized no few towns and territories by force and fraud, and thereafter eagerly sought donations from one or another party.

## XIX.
