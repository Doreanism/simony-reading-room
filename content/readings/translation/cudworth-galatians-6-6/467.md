# 6 Let him that is taught in the word make him that hath taught him partaker of all his goods.

In these words the Apostle sets down another rule concerning the maintenance of the ministry and a competent allowance for the ministers of the word; for it appears that the ministry among the Galatians was at that time much neglected — or at least not so respected as it ought to have been.

In handling this rule, I will first show the meaning of the words; secondly, the reasons for the rule; thirdly, the objections against it; and lastly, the doctrine and practical applications to be gathered from it.

## The meaning.

*Let him that is taught)* The word translated *taught* signifies one who is catechized, or taught familiarly by word of mouth or living voice, as when children are instructed in the first principles of religion. But here it has a broader signification (as Oecumenius has rightly observed): it refers to anyone who is in any way taught and instructed, whether in the first principles and rudiments or in points of greater difficulty, whether plainly and familiarly (as catechists are accustomed to do) or more profoundly for the instruction of the learned.

*Taught in the word)* Why is this addition necessary, some may ask — is there any catechizing without the word? *Answer.* The Apostle adds *in the word* to show that he means not so much the doctrine of Christian religion contained in the Scriptures as the doctrine of the Gospel, which by an *exoche,* or peculiar excellency, is called *the word*. Acts 16:6: *They were forbidden of the Holy Ghost to preach the word in Asia* — which is explained in verse 10 to mean the preaching of the Gospel. Thus it is used, Acts 14:25: *when they had preached the word*
