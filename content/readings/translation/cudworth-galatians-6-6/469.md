The reasons for this rule are many and weighty.

I. We are bound (even by the bond of nature) to support our parents if they are in need, because they supported us and gave us our being. Paul says it is an honest and acceptable thing before God for children to recompense their parents and forebears (1 Tim. 5:4). Now if this is so, men are bound by the same right to support their spiritual fathers in Christ, who have begotten them anew through the preaching of the word — as Paul says that he travailed in pain for the Galatians until Christ was formed in them (Gal. 4), and that he begat Onesimus in his bonds (Philem. 10), and in 1 Cor. 4:15: “In Christ Jesus I have begotten you through the Gospel.” Hence Paul commends the Galatians (Gal. 4:15) in that, if it had been possible, they would have plucked out their own eyes and given them to him. And surely we owe to our spiritual parents and fathers in Christ not only these temporal trifles, but even ourselves, as Paul says to Philemon in verse 19: “Albeit I do not say unto thee that thou owest unto me even thine own self.”

II. It is a law of nations, and a conclusion grounded on common equity, that those who watch, labor, and spend themselves like a candle to give light to others — and do so for the common good of all — should be maintained out of the common stock by all. And the Lord charges all twelve tribes, even all Israel (Deut. 12:1\[9\]): “Beware that thou forsakest not the Levite, so long as thou livest on the earth.”

III. Every trade, calling, and condition of life is able to sustain those who live within it, as experience shows; therefore we must not suppose that the ministry, being the highest calling, should be so base or barren that it cannot competently maintain those who give themselves to it.

IV. Ministers are the Lord’s soldiers, his captains and standard-bearers, and therefore are not to go to war at their own expense; they are the Lord’s laborers in his vineyard, and therefore are worthy of their wages and ought to eat of the fruit of the vineyard; they are the Lord’s shepherds, set over the flock of Christ to feed his sheep, and therefore ought to eat of the milk of the flock.
