There is good reason for this precept. 1. Because the graces of God exhibited to us in the preaching of the word are so precious that they cannot be valued at any price; all the earthly things a man can desire are not to be compared to them, as the wise man says. Therefore Peter said to Simon Magus: “Your money perish with you, because you think that the gift of God may be obtained with money” (Acts 8:20). 2. Because it is not in man’s power to sell them, seeing he is not lord over them but steward and dispenser of them (1 Cor. 4:1). 3. Because that which God has given freely ought not to be sold basely; for by that means what God would have free is made saleable — which kind of trafficking is practiced in the Church of Rome at this day in their indulgences, wherein they sell pardons for the remission of sins and set even heaven itself to sale for a small sum of money. Third, this text must be expounded in such a way that it does not contradict other precepts in this commission, lest Christ be contrary to himself — which he would be, if it were unlawful for preachers to take anything for their pains. For in the tenth verse he says: “The workman is worthy of his meat”; therefore he may receive it. And in verse 11 he enjoins them to inquire in every town where they came who is worthy, and there to abide. Therefore they were not utterly barred from taking all necessaries, for they might take their lodging, food, and raiment — with these they were to be content, and more than these they might not take, even if it were offered or pressed upon them. Thus Elisha refused the gift offered him by Naaman \[the Syrian\] for curing his leprosy, lest God’s gifts should be made a gain, or lest that which was given to set forth God’s glory directly should become an occasion for satisfying men’s greedy desires.

## Objection IV.

Matt. 10:9: The apostles are forbidden by Christ to possess gold, silver, etc. *Answer.* We may not take the letter of the text precisely, for if we do, we make the apostles’ practice contrary to Christ’s precept — and Christ would thereby condemn himself, for he had a bag, which Judas carried. He had besides his upper garment (John 13:4) and a coat without seam (John 19:23). Peter had sandals (Acts 12). Paul had a cloak (2 Tim. 4). Indeed, Christ himself afterwards said to them: “But now he that has a bag, let him take it, and likewise a scrip,” etc. (Luke 22:36).
