## §9. Application.

Here we see that there are two classes of people in the Church: some are teachers, others are to be taught — called in Scripture teachers and hearers. The basis of this distinction rests on the good pleasure of God, who has ordained that human beings should be taught by other human beings, partly because of human weakness, which could not endure the glory and majesty of God speaking directly, and partly so that nothing might be attributed to the excellence of the instrument in the conversion of people, but that God might have all the glory of it — as the Apostle says, 2 Corinthians 4:7: “We have this treasure in earthen vessels, that the excellency of that power might be of God, and not of man.” There is set down in Deuteronomy 5:23 and following a notable foundation for the institution of the holy ministry of human beings in place of God’s living voice from heaven. And it was one of the ends God had in giving his law with such great majesty — to teach us that it is for our good that he does not instruct us with his own living voice from heaven and speak to us in his own person, and that therefore we should be content, indeed desirous rather, to be taught by human beings. For when the people requested that they might hear the voice of the Lord no more, but that Moses might speak to them, the Lord answered, “They have done well to say so; I will raise them up a Prophet from among their brethren like unto thee, and I will put my words in his mouth, and he shall speak unto them all that I command him” (Deuteronomy 18:17–18). This rebukes the curiosity of those who, not contenting themselves with the word of God delivered through the ministry of human beings, desire that God would speak to them with his own mouth from heaven. For the Israelites found by experience that it was a fearful thing, full of horror and astonishment, to hear the voice of God; therefore they requested to hear it no more, lest they die (Deuteronomy 5:25). But the Anabaptists object that under the New Testament, Jeremiah 31:34, John 6:45, and Hebrews 8:13 say they shall not teach every man his neighbour and every man his brother, saying, “Know the Lord.” Therefore, they argue, the public ministry is now unnecessary. The answer is that these words must not be understood absolutely, but comparatively, and indicate not \[the same degree of external teaching as\] was under the Old \[Testament\].
