Therefore those who were taught in the word were required to maintain their teachers through generous contribution, lest they should starve. But now that the Church has been greatly enriched, the ministers may be sufficiently maintained from Church goods without such contributions.

Moreover, it is far more fitting for ministers to be maintained by fixed stipends arising from goods proper to the Church than by voluntary contribution, as the following considerations make plain, showing that fixed stipends cut off several inconveniences that in voluntary contributions either cannot be or are hardly avoided. First, flattery and the suspicion of flattery — the appearance of holding certain persons in high esteem for the sake of personal advantage. Second, the poorer sort are in no way put to shame by this means, as they would be in contributions unless they gave proportionally to the rest. Third, deceit and dissembling — making as though one received little, when in truth one has much — is eliminated by a fixed stipend. Fourth, the ill-disposed would not so easily dismiss their minister and seek a new one who would preach for less, or give nothing at all, if they were sharply reproved and stung for their sins. Fifth, ostentation in some, in giving much and despising those who give less. Sixth, suspicion of covetousness and filthy lucre in ministers, who seem to receive from those to whom they ought rather to be giving. Seventh, the disgrace of the ministry, in collecting themselves, or sending others to collect, the people’s generosity from door to door. Lastly, a fixed stipend comes nearer to the order appointed by God for the maintenance of the priesthood under the Levitical law.

In the next place, I will answer the common objections raised to the contrary by those who think it as easy a matter to say the Service in the Church as to do service in the house; to stand at the altar of God as to follow their master’s plough; to preach in the pulpit as to talk in the tavern.
