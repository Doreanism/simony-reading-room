V. Ministers are to devote themselves wholly to reading, exhortation, and doctrine, and to continue therein (1 Tim. 4:13, 15, 16); they are to give themselves entirely to the building up of the Church and to the fighting of the Lord’s battles, and therefore are not to be entangled with the cares of this life (2 Tim. 2:4). Therefore they are to have their pay and their allowance, so that they may attend to their callings without distraction.

VI. It is the ordinance of God (as Paul says, 1 Cor. 9:14) that they who preach the Gospel should live by the Gospel; therefore those who do not do their utmost, and do not enlarge their generosity to uphold and maintain the ministry in good estate, resist (insofar as it lies within them) the ordinance of God; moreover, they waste and make havoc of the Church of God, and are guilty of the blood of all who perish for want of instruction.

I add further that it is the will of God that ministers who labor in the word and doctrine should be plentifully and liberally provided for — yet with moderation, that they do not draw all men’s wealth into their purses, as the Pope and Church of Rome have done into their coffers, of whom it is truly said: *Religio peperit diuitias, sed filia deuorauit matrem* \[“Religion gave birth to riches, but the daughter devoured the mother”\]. This I prove from the Levitical law: for the whole land of promise, being no larger in compass than Wales, or the fourth part of England, yet yielded unto the Levites, at the appointment of God — besides the share they had from the sacrifices, besides tithes, firstfruits, etc. — forty-eight cities with their suburbs.

It may here be asked whether ministers of the word are now to be maintained by common contribution and the generosity of the people, or not.

Answer: We must consider that if ministers are sufficiently provided for by set stipends — as by some foundation, or by the revenues of the Church — men are not bound to contribute to them and make them partakers of all their goods; although in regard to gratitude they owe to them even themselves, as Paul tells Philemon — but only to such as have no other wherewith to maintain themselves. And we must consider the reason why Paul commands all who are instructed in the word to make their teachers partakers of their goods — namely, because in Paul’s time, and long after, the Church was not endowed with lands or goods
