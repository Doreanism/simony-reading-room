For it is not only the seed by which we are begotten and born anew, but the food by which we are nourished: both milk for the babe — that is, the novice in religion — and solid food for the one who is mature. Being therefore perfect nourishment, the bread of life for the hungry and the water of life for the thirsty, what more could be needed? Beyond this, no word or writing possesses the property of fire save only the word of God: to dispel the darkness of ignorance by enlightening the mind with the saving knowledge of truth, and to inflame the affections with a zeal for God’s glory by burning away the corruption of nature. Again, Divinity is the mistress; all the liberal arts, languages, histories, and the like are but handmaids to attend upon her. Now, when the mistress is speaking, it is good manners for the maid to hold her peace. To this word alone the Prophets were bound by their commission: “Whatever I shall command you, that you shall say.” And the Apostles likewise: “You shall teach what I have commanded you.” Christ himself taught nothing but what he had heard and received from his Father (John 8:28). Paul delivers nothing but what he received from Christ (1 Cor. 11:23), and nothing beyond what Moses and the Prophets had written (Acts 26:22). Paul commands Timothy to charge the pastors of Ephesus that they teach no divergent doctrine — whether in matter or manner, in substance or circumstance. Yet here we must beware of extremes, for in certain cases it is lawful in preaching to make use of the testimony of profane writers and citations from the Fathers.

## I.

When we have to deal with heathen men, who will not be so much moved by the authority of the Scriptures, we may convict them by the testimony of their own writers — as Paul did with the Athenians, Epicureans, and Stoics, by the testimony of Aratus (Acts 17:28), and with the Greeks by the testimony of Epimenides (Tit. 1:12).

## II.

In preaching to a mixed congregation, where some are infected with Popery or some other heresy and will not receive the doctrine delivered, nor yield to the truth, unless it bears the consent of the Fathers of the Church.

## III.

In treating some controversial point of divinity, to show that the doctrine we teach is no new doctrine, but that which was taught in the Primitive Church — especially in speaking to the weak, who have not yet departed from the old superstition in which they were reared.
