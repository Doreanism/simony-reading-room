Rather, the point is that there will be no need for this kind of teaching — catechizing people in first rudiments, as in teaching them what God is. This is understood synecdochically \[as a part standing for the whole\]: not of perfect and absolute knowledge, for “we all know in part” (1 Corinthians 13:12), but of initial and inchoate knowledge, which will be made complete in the life to come.

Furthermore, from this distinction it follows that hearers must not meddle with the public duties of the ministry, for every person is to abide in the calling in which God has placed them and to live contentedly therein (1 Corinthians 7). For no one may take this honour to himself, but he who is called, as was Aaron (Hebrews 5:4). For although it is true, as Ambrose writes, that in the beginning of the preaching of the Gospel and the founding of the Church of the New Testament all Christians did teach and baptize indiscriminately, yet afterward, when the churches were established, this is no longer lawful, nor is it now. And though there is neither male nor female in Christ, but we are all one in him (Galatians 3:28) — namely, in receiving the Gospel — yet in dispensing it there is great difference, since it is unlawful for a woman to preach or to teach publicly: “I permit not a woman to teach” (1 Timothy 2:12); “Let women keep silence in the churches, for it is not permitted to them to speak” (1 Corinthians 14:34; Revelation 2:20). This condemns the fantastic opinion of the Anabaptists, that all people may speak publicly without distinction, according to the impulse of the Spirit and the measure of his gifts.

Again, when Paul says “he that is taught in the word” should communicate to “him that taught him in the word,” he shows what the duty of the minister is who looks to live by his ministry — namely, not to feed his congregation with philosophy, or fables, or lying legends; not to preach scholastic disputes, Thomism, Scotism, tedious questions, trivialities, Popish decrees, or human constitutions; nor to tickle the itching ears of his congregation with the fine ringing sentences of the pagans — “for what is the chaff to the wheat?” — but he must preach the word of God. For there is no other word or writing in the world that carries a promise of being “the power of God unto salvation” (Romans 1), able to “make men wise unto salvation” (2 Timothy 3:15), “to give an inheritance among them that are sanctified” (Acts 20), to be “lively and mighty in operation, sharper than any two-edged sword, entering through even to the dividing asunder of the soul and the spirit, the joints and the marrow, and a discerner of the thoughts and intents of the heart” (Hebrews 4:12), and that can “make the man of God absolute to every good work” (2 Timothy 3:17) — none but this word
