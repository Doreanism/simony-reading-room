It is a maxim not only of canon law, but one grounded in the law of nature, that *beneficium postulat officium* \[a benefit demands its corresponding duty\] — or more plainly, a benefit requires an office, and diligence in the performance of that office. Those therefore who care not so much for the feeding of the sheep as for the feeding of themselves, and who fear not so much the loss of the flock as the loss of the fleece, do not only violate the law of God, but even the law of nations and the law of nature.

Again, from this I gather that those who labor in the word and doctrine may lawfully receive wages, even if they have sufficient means of their own to maintain themselves — for such is the very equity of this text. For if those who are instructed in the word are to make those who instructed them partakers of their goods, then it is lawful for those who instruct and teach to receive something in recompense of their labor, seeing that the laborer is worthy of his wages. Yet this one caveat must be remembered: that if the receiving of wages is a hindrance to the Gospel (as it would have been in Paul’s case), it is not lawful — for we may not so use our liberty as thereby to hinder the free course of the Gospel.

We may here further perceive the great want of devotion that is in most men of these days. For as the cry of the poor in the streets and at our doors is evidence that there is no mercy, no bowels of pity and compassion: so, in that there are so many needy, poor, wandering Levites who would gladly serve for a morsel of bread or a suit of clothing, there is abundant proof that there is very little devotion in men for the maintenance of religion — especially in those who are so strait-laced and short-sleeved in bestowing anything for the good of the ministry, and yet in the keeping of hounds and hawks, and worse pursuits, in maintaining players, jesters, fools, and the like, are very lavish and profuse at great cost. This has been the practice of the world and the contemptible estate of the ministry in former times. Four hundred false prophets were richly provided for at Jezebel’s table (1 Kgs. 18:19), whereas the true prophets of God in the meantime were forced to hide their heads, having scarcely bread and water, and that not without danger. Well, her practice shall condemn a number of professors; nay, our forefathers’ zeal and forwardness — notwithstanding that they lived in blindness — shall condemn our coldness in this regard.
