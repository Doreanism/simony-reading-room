This does not prove that the minister ought to live by the labor of his hands. First, Paul himself received a contribution from the Philippians when he was absent from them (Phil. 4:16): “When I was in Thessalonica, you sent once and afterward again for my necessity.” Therefore, if Paul received support from other churches where he did not labor, it is lawful for ministers to receive from those whom they instruct. Second, consider the reasons why Paul would not take wages from the church of Corinth and certain others: (I) lest he should be a burden to them — “We worked with labor and travail day and night, because we would not be chargeable to any of you” (2 Thess. 3:8); “In all things I kept, and will keep myself, that I should not be grievous to you” (2 Cor. 11:9). (II) That he might set a precedent and example for others to follow in his steps — “Not because we have no authority, but that we might make ourselves an example to them to follow us” (2 Thess. 3:9). (III) That he might make plain what his purpose was in preaching the gospel — not to seek his own advantage, but the salvation of his hearers: “I seek not yours, but you” (2 Cor. 12:14); “We do all things for your edification” (v. 19); “Not that I desire a gift, but the fruit which may further your reckoning” (Phil. 4:17). (IV) That he might confound the false apostles, who did not teach freely but took wages for their labor, whereas he took none — or lest they should slander him and his ministry if he received wages, charging that he did it for filthy gain: “But what I do, that I will do, that I may cut away occasion from those who desire occasion, that they might be found like us in that wherein they glory” (2 Cor. 11:12). Therefore Paul’s example is altogether beside the point and proves nothing. For, first, he himself did accept wages; second, when he refused it, it was upon special consideration; and third, he not only permitted but also commanded that ministers should be maintained.

## Objection III.

Matthew 10:8: “Freely you have received, freely give.” Therefore, just as it was unlawful for the apostles to take any reward for their labor, so it is for preachers today. *Answer.* First, this text is specially to be understood of the extraordinary gift of working miracles, which Christ, having freely bestowed upon them, would have them use freely — not seeking thereby to enrich themselves by exacting or receiving anything, nor to win reputation and glory among men. Second, if we understand it of the whole ministry, as well of preaching as of working miracles, it is to be taken as a precept forbidding only filthy gain — that they should not make merchandise of the word of God, setting it to sale by preaching the word with the purpose of benefiting themselves, feeding their bellies, or gaining a name and reputation in the world.
