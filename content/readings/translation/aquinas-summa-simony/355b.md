To the third objection it must be said that money is not exacted from one who is absolved as a price for absolution — for this would be simoniacal — but rather as a penalty for the preceding fault for which he was excommunicated.

To the fourth objection it must be said that, as has been said, custom cannot override the natural or divine law by which simony is forbidden. And therefore if certain things are exacted by custom as the price of a spiritual thing, with the intention of buying or selling, it is manifest simony — especially if they are exacted from an unwilling party. But if they are received as a kind of stipend approved by custom, it is not simony — provided, however, that the intention of buying or selling is absent and the intention is referred solely to the observance of the custom, and especially when someone pays voluntarily. In all these matters, however, one must carefully guard against anything that has the appearance of simony or greed, according to the Apostle’s word, 1 Thessalonians 2: “Abstain from every form of evil.”

To the fifth objection it must be said that before someone has acquired a right to a bishopric or any dignity or prebend through election, provision, or collation, it would be simoniacal to redeem with money the obstacles raised by adversaries; for in this way one would be using money to prepare a path toward obtaining a spiritual thing. But after a right has already been acquired by someone, it is lawful to remove unjust impediments by means of money.

To the sixth objection it must be said that some hold that it is lawful to give money for marriage because in it no grace is conferred. But this is not entirely true, as is said in the Third Part of this work. And therefore it must be said otherwise: that matrimony is not only a sacrament of the Church but also an office of nature. And therefore to give money for matrimony insofar as it is an office of nature is lawful; but insofar as it is a sacrament of the Church, it is unlawful. And therefore the laws forbid that anything be exacted for the nuptial blessing.

II. On this matter Petrus de Palude says, in his commentary on the Fourth Book, distinction 5, article 3, that here there is no room for the redemption of harassment. His reasoning rests on a distinction among those who harass. Some are those who can harm by deed but cannot benefit by right. From such persons it is lawful to redeem one’s harassment, as is evident when someone elected to a bishopric redeems himself from factual troublemakers who have no right to confer anything. Others, however, are those who can both harm by deed and benefit by right.
