5. Moreover, sometimes a person is received into a monastery through simony and takes a solemn vow there by making profession. But no one ought to be released from the obligation of a vow on account of a fault committed. Therefore he ought not to lose the monastic state that he acquired simoniacally.

6. Moreover, external punishment in this world is not inflicted for an interior movement of the heart, the judgment of which belongs to God alone. But simony is committed by intention or will alone; hence it is also defined through the will, as was said above. Therefore a person ought not always to be deprived of what he acquired simoniacally.

7. Moreover, it is a far greater thing to be promoted to higher offices than to remain in what one has already received. But simonists are sometimes, by dispensation, promoted to higher offices. Therefore they ought not always to be deprived of what they have received.

On the contrary, it is said at C.1 q.1 c. *Si quis episcopus* \[Decretum, C.1 q.1 c.1\]: “Whoever is ordained shall gain nothing from an ordination or promotion accomplished through trafficking; but let him be a stranger to the dignity or office that he acquired by means of money.”

I reply that no one can lawfully retain what he has acquired against the will of the lord. For example, if a steward were to give something from his lord’s goods to someone against the will and ordinance of his lord, the one who received it could not lawfully retain it. Now the Lord, whose stewards and ministers the prelates of the churches are, ordained that spiritual things should be given freely — according to Matthew 10: *Freely you have received, freely give*. And therefore those who obtain any spiritual things through the mediation of a gift cannot lawfully retain them.

Moreover, simonists — both those who sell and those who buy spiritual things, as well as intermediaries — are punished by other penalties: namely, infamy and deposition if they are clergy; and excommunication if they are laity; as is found at C.1 q.1 c. *Si quis episcopus*.

To the first objection, therefore, it must be said that one who simoniacally receives a sacred order does indeed receive the character of the order, by reason of the efficacy of the sacrament; yet he does not receive the grace, nor the exercise of the order, because he received the character, as it were, by stealth, against the will of the principal Lord.
