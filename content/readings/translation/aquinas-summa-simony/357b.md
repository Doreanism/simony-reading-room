For a consecrated chalice cannot be sold at a higher price by reason of its consecration; and an estate that carries the right of patronage cannot be sold at a higher price by reason of that right of patronage; and things that are purely spiritual are altogether unsaleable, as is evident.

After this rule, a distinction must be drawn: certain things are a mixture of spiritual and temporal in which the principal object of interest is what is temporal; while in others the principal object of interest is what is spiritual.

In accordance with this distinction, it is said that those things in which the principal object is temporal may be placed under formal agreement, whereas those in which the principal object is spiritual may not be placed under formal agreement. The reason for both conclusions is one: that the accessory follows the principal — so that the accessory is not priced in either case, but accompanies the principal as something concurrent.

VII. From these two conclusions, an account must be given of all the objections and cases arising on both sides. From them it is clear, first, why cantors for the chapels of the Pope, lords, and churches — as also organists — may lawfully hire out their services by formal agreement. For the principal thing sought from them is human work, not anything spiritual (although human work is engaged for spiritual ends): what is sought in them is voice, artistic skill, and the like, which are sensible and temporal things.

It is clear, second, why advocates and doctors of any faculty may lawfully sell their services. For the principal thing sought from them is human assistance, whether toward justice or toward knowledge: just legal counsel renders temporal assistance that is monetarily assessable; and a doctor renders great temporal assistance to a student through explaining, applying, and the like.

It is clear, third, why it is not lawful to bargain for celebration of Mass, prayer, administration of the sacraments, the divine office or \[office\] of the dead, preaching, holy oil, chrism, the blessing of those being married. For in all these, the principal thing sought is spiritual; and whatever temporal element intervenes in them is not assessed except by reason of the spiritual.

Nevertheless, because in matters that are accidental even the wise can be mistaken, as Aristotle says, it must be noted that it is one thing to speak of the celebration of Mass absolutely, and another to speak of the obligation to celebrate Mass in a given place, a certain number of times per week, at a particular hour, and so forth. These conditions are accidentally attached, and are temporal; and therefore they may be placed under formal agreement. So that, although a priest cannot hire out the act he performs in celebrating, preaching, administering sacraments, and the like — since, as has been said, it is assessed and given a price by reason of the spiritual, which is the principal object sought — he can nevertheless hire out or sell his obligation to keep and present his person in a given place, a certain number of times per week, at a certain time, and so on: because these things are temporal and constitute notable, accidental constraints on his personal freedom that are accidentally joined to the act of celebration and the like. This does not apply, however, to one who does not hire himself out but celebrates spontaneously whenever occasion arises: for there is nothing notably temporal that intervenes accidentally in one who celebrates in this way spontaneously, and therefore, if he were to make an agreement about a price, he would be entering into an agreement about something spiritual. Because, therefore, in these monthly or annual obligations to celebrate and the like, these temporal accidents intervene, they are said to hire out their services lawfully. This is not conceded for those who are not bound by these accidental conditions.

VIII. Hence, in reply to the objections on the other side, each is answered separately. To the first it is said that although both labor and human service in the administration of the sacraments are something temporal, yet because the basis of their valuation is what is spiritual, it is not lawful to sell them — as is evident in the case of holy oil and chrism; for each of these is something temporal, yet since it is not valued except by reason of the spiritual, it is not lawful to sell them.
