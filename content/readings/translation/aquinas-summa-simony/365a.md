### Commentary of Cardinal Caietanus.

In the sixth article of question one hundred, a difficulty arises, chiefly in the reply to the fifth objection: namely, whether a mental simonist can in good conscience retain whatever he acquired simoniacally. The reason for the difficulty is this: on the affirmative side stands the Author himself in the text, in the reply to the sixth objection, saying that one is not bound to renounce a benefice acquired with a simoniacal intention. And there is the legal text at *Extra*, de Simonia, c. *Mandato* \[X 5.3.32\], from which the Author drew this reply.

On the negative side, however, there is a rational argument. For the general rule is that when both the giving and the receiving of a temporal thing are unjust, what is so acquired cannot be retained with a good conscience, as is clear above at q. 32, art. 7. Now it is established that in an acquisition proceeding from a simoniacal intention on both sides, both the giving and the receiving are iniquitous: because the one party unlawfully gives the benefice, money, or service out of a simoniacal intention, without any agreement; and the other iniquitously receives what he receives with a similar simoniacal intention. Therefore nothing so acquired can be retained with a good conscience.

This is confirmed. For it seems altogether remarkable that, where mental simony exists on both sides between two parties — for example, because a bishop grants a cleric a benefice principally on account of the hope of receiving money, and the cleric is also ready to give money to the bishop principally on account of the benefice, and without any agreement the consummated effect follows on both sides — it is remarkable, I say, that in this and similar cases the bishop is bound not to retain the money he received because he received it simoniacally, and yet the cleric can retain the benefice; since the cleric could more readily have donated the money, as the true owner of the money, than the bishop the benefice, since the bishop is only a steward of benefices.
