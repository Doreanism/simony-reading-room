To the argument that a prince intends his action to be efficacious: it is replied that this is true on his part, as agent. But from this it does not follow that he removes impediments that exist on the part of the recipient — impediments that are known to him only as a private person, or as a party to the act. For a prince is a voluntary agent, and does not always act to the full extent of his power, but only to the extent he wills. In the present case, moreover, there is neither appearance nor presumption that he wills to relax the common law with respect to the recipient. Perhaps he is ignorant and believes everything to be valid. Or, even if he has knowledge, since no suspension of the law with respect to that person has been sought from him, he leaves the recipient subject to the disposition of law — leaving the one who buys and receives a benefice to care for himself. Or he simply gives no thought to the matter at all, being occupied with other things, or having grown accustomed to acting badly.

Those who commit simony with the Pope are therefore bound, both in the forum of conscience and in the judicial forum, to resign and to restore whatever they have received; they are excommunicated and incur all the penalties of law — unless the Pope expressly declares his will to suspend the law in this regard, or grants them a dispensation after the fact.
