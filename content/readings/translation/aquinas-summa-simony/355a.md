The sacraments, however, are dispensed through the ministers of the Church, who must be supported by the people, according to the Apostle’s word, 1 Corinthians 9: “Do you not know that those who serve in the temple eat of what belongs to the temple, and those who serve at the altar share in the offerings of the altar?” Therefore it must be said that to receive money for the spiritual grace of the sacraments is the crime of simony, which no custom can excuse, because custom cannot override natural or divine law. By “money” is understood everything whose value can be assessed in money, as the Philosopher says in Ethics IV. But to receive certain things for the sustenance of those who administer the sacraments, in accordance with the ordinance of the Church and approved customs, is neither simony nor a sin; for it is received not as the price of a transaction, but as a stipend of necessity. Hence on the text of 1 Timothy 5, “Let the elders who rule well,” etc., the Gloss of Augustinus says: “Let them receive the sustenance of necessity from the people, and the reward of stewardship from the Lord.”

To the first objection, therefore, it must be said that in a case of necessity anyone can baptize. And since one must never act sinfully, it amounts to the same thing if a priest refuses to baptize without payment as if there were no one to baptize. Therefore the one who has charge of the child in such a case could lawfully baptize the child himself, or have him baptized by any other person. He could, however, lawfully purchase the water from the priest, since that is purely a bodily element.

If, however, it were an adult who desired baptism and death were imminent, and the priest would not baptize him without payment, he ought, if possible, to be baptized by another. But if he could not have recourse to another, he should in no way give a price for baptism, but should rather die without it; for what is lacking from the sacrament is supplied to him by the baptism of desire.

In the second article, in the reply to the first objection, a doubt arises whether in the case described in the text — where an adult at the point of death has no one willing to baptize him without money — there is room for the redemption of one’s own harassment. The reason for the doubt is this: if there is such room, it follows that one may lawfully give money to a greedy priest to baptize him, not by purchasing baptism but by redeeming himself from harassment. And consequently, he ought not to die without the baptism of water. The text says the opposite.
