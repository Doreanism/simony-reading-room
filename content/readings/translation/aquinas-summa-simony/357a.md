Two things, however, must be noted here. First, that under the term “sustenance” there comes not only food and drink, but whatever is necessary for the food and clothing of himself and those in his care. For a priest ought not to have to beg for the support of his own family members, aged parents, or small grandchildren left in his care by the laws of nature, and the like.

Second, that a sustenance stipend can come about in two ways. The first is freely given, or by the authority of a superior. In this case it does not require that the one for whose sustenance it is given be in need — as is evident from the fact that, according to the laws, even the wealthiest bishop on visitation is owed a *procuratio* \[i.e., hospitality expenses\]. The other way is by formal agreement. In this case it can lawfully accrue only to a poor person who is actually in need of a sustenance stipend. For a priest who has sufficient means to live honestly according to his station, if he seeks a maintenance stipend by formal agreement, is shown to be lying when he agrees to such a stipend as necessary without which he cannot comfortably serve in the priesthood — since without that stipend he can do so comfortably. Hence it is shown that he is agreeing not to a sustenance stipend but to a stipend for his services — the lawfulness of which will be treated in the following difficulty. In this first difficulty, therefore, we speak only of a sustenance stipend.

In reply to the objections on the other side, it is said that placing ecclesiastical ministries under formal agreement can be understood in two ways. The first is simply and absolutely — as when one says, “I want such an amount of money for the ministry,” or “if I am to minister.” Such agreements are prohibited and called simoniacal in law and in the text. The second way is conditionally or with qualification — for example, “If I am to minister in spiritual matters, I want, for my maintenance stipend, such an amount: because otherwise I cannot comfortably devote myself to the ministry.” Such qualified agreements, made by ministers who are in need, are not simoniacal before God, who sees a right heart; nor are they simoniacal before the Church, to which it is or can be apparent both that the minister is in need and that the qualification expressly inserted in the agreement — namely, for necessary sustenance — is present. So much for the first difficulty.

V. To the second difficulty it is said that in ecclesiastical ministries it is lawful, speaking in absolute terms, to hire out one’s services by formal agreement. First, because this is the practice of the Church, as is evident from priests who are engaged to serve as monthly or annual chaplains — for an agreement intervenes that they are to be paid a certain amount each month. Second, because the same reasoning applies to an explicit agreement as to an implicit agreement. But an implicit agreement is found to be approved in law, as is evident from the title *Extra, de Praebendis*, chapter *Significatum*. Therefore it is also lawful to bargain explicitly about the price of services. Third, because the laws and the doctors approve the hiring out of services in matters of this kind. But it is clear that a hire can be confirmed by agreement and contract. Fourth, because the Apostle, speaking on this matter in 1 Corinthians 9, says: “Who ever serves as a soldier at his own expense?” For if no one is required to serve as a soldier at his own expense, one may bargain for a stipend for one’s services, just as a soldier in the armed forces does, following the Apostle’s reasoning. This is confirmed by the fact that the Lord, speaking of the work of the disciples in Luke 10, adds: “The laborer is worthy of his wages.” What could be plainer?

Therefore an agreement may lawfully be made concerning the wages of human work and labor in ecclesiastical offices. This opinion is further confirmed by the argument already given: namely, that a temporal thing may be placed under formal agreement. And although this is true in principle on the side of the thing itself, yet because in outward appearance it has the look of something evil — since in this way the door to simoniacal frauds is opened — the Church has therefore prohibited agreements of this kind.

VI. But this opinion does not seem reasonable. For the Church prohibits agreements because they are unlawful, and not the other way around — as is evident from the fact that the doctors, who agree with ecclesiastical law on this matter, appear to differ on \[the reasons for\] it.
