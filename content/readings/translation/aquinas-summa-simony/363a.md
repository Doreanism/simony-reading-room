### Commentary of Cardinal Caietanus.

In the fifth article, in the response to the third objection, a doubt arises: whether prayers for a benefice with cure of souls, made on behalf of the one requesting it, are prayers on behalf of an unworthy person. The source of the doubt is the divergence of opinions.

Although various writers have addressed this, as you may find in the Lord Iohannes de Turrecremata, on the Decreta, in question 1, chapter Ordinationes, article 3, the whole ambiguity reduces to this: whether in such a petition the vice of presumption intervenes. So that if presumption intervenes in the asking, the prayers are on behalf of an unworthy person; if presumption does not intervene in the asking, prayers on one’s own behalf, otherwise for a worthy person, are not prayers on behalf of an unworthy person. This is sufficiently suggested in the text when it says: “By that very presumption he renders himself unworthy.” Whether, however, the petition itself is simply or regularly presumptuous must be examined. And five summary points now present themselves.

First, that the petition for the simple cure of souls is regularly presumptuous — as, for instance, to seek a bishopric. I said “simple cure of souls” because, as is clear below, parish priests do not have the simple cure of souls; rather, bishops are those who hold the simple cure of souls over the whole diocese. I said “regularly” in order to except the petition for a bishopric in the case of necessity for the Church itself. This conclusion is sufficiently clear from the statements of the doctors, and it is Augustinus’s position in Book XIX of the City of God; and it is also clear below in the treatise on the states of life.

Second, that the petition for the simple cure of souls in a particular case is not presumptuous but holy — namely, when it is clearly evident that unless the worthy person himself seeks that bishopric, the Church will be poorly provided for. For then, if he can do so without scandal, an otherwise worthy person may lawfully petition in order to serve; and especially if he petitions indirectly. The reason is that this is a counsel of charity, not of presumption. This is clear from the fact that that Church would render great thanks to him if it knew that by his deliberate petition it had been snatched from the hand of an imminent wolf. And similarly the Bridegroom and Lord of the Church, Jesus Christ, would acknowledge him as a faithful steward of his affairs.

## Article Six.

Whether it is appropriate that the punishment of the simoniac be deprivation of what he acquired through simony. On the sixth article the procedure is as follows. It seems that it is not appropriate that the punishment of the simoniac be deprivation of what he acquired through simony. For simony is committed when spiritual things are acquired through the intervention of some gift. But certain spiritual things, once obtained, cannot be lost — such as all sacramental characters, which are imprinted through some consecration. Therefore it is not appropriate that anyone be deprived of what he acquired simoniacally.
