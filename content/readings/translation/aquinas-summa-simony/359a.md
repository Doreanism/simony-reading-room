To the first objection, therefore, it must be said that, as Hieronymus says in his commentary on Micah, certain gifts were offered voluntarily to good prophets for their sustenance — not as if to purchase the use of prophecy — which use, however, false prophets turned to profit.

To the second it must be said that those who give alms to the poor in order to obtain their prayerful intercession do not give on the condition of intending to purchase prayers: rather, through gratuitous beneficence, they stir the hearts of the poor to pray for them freely and out of charity. To preachers also, temporal support is owed for the sustenance of preachers, not for purchasing the word of preaching. Hence the Gloss on 1 Timothy 5 — *Elders who lead well*, etc. — says: “It is of necessity to receive that by which one lives; it is of charity to provide it: yet the Gospel is not for sale, so that it should be preached in exchange for these things. For if they sell it thus, they sell a great thing at a cheap price.” Likewise, some temporal support is given to those who praise God in the celebration of the ecclesiastical office, whether for the living or the dead — not as a price, but as a stipend of sustenance. And on the same basis certain alms are received for leading processions in some funeral rites.

If, however, such things are done with a contract intervening, or even with the intention of buying or selling, it would be simoniacal. Hence a \[church\] ordinance would be illicit if it were decreed in some church that a procession would not be held at anyone’s funeral unless a certain sum of money were paid: because such a statute would foreclose the possibility of freely rendering the office of piety to some persons. An ordinance would be more licit, however, if it were decreed that to all who give a certain alms such an honor would be rendered: because by this the way of providing \[a procession\] for others would not be foreclosed. Furthermore, the first ordinance has the appearance of exaction, while the second has the appearance of gratuitous recompense.
