“One who is ordained or provided with a benefice, etc.”: for it is agreed among all authorities, and is stated in the Decretals, *de Simonia*, cap. *Si quis* \[X.5.3.1\], that when someone has been knowingly ordained through simony, the Pope alone can dispense with him.

Note secondly, that the Pope can dispense in matters of penalties with any simonist — this is not in doubt; but whether he dispenses *ipso facto* \[by that very act\] with those who commit simony with him is a question disputed among the doctors. Some say that the Pope is understood to dispense in such a case because the knowledge of a prince \[*scientia principis*\] purges, just as he is understood to dispense with an irregular person by knowingly conferring on him an order or a benefice, because he intends his act to be efficacious. Among those of this opinion is Petrus de Palude, in the *Fourth* \[Book of Sentences\], dist. xxv, qu. iv, art. 1. Others hold the opposite view, because even if the Pope could dispense, he is not understood to dispense merely by communicating in a crime. Among those of this opinion is Panormitanus \[Nicolaus de Tudeschis\], in cap. I *de Simonia*.

It seems to me, however, that this second opinion rests on reason and truth, for two reasons. First, because, since in such a case there intervenes no dispensation or explicit relaxation of the law, the only grounds on which a dispensation could be said to intervene here would be the presumption that the Pope intends by his very act to dispense. But it is false that any such presumed dispensation intervenes here. Therefore no dispensation intervenes here at all. — The minor premise is proved as follows. For a presumption that the prince is dispensing to arise, it is required that sound reason counsel it; for although a prince can dispense from his will alone, yet if his will is not expressed, it ought not to be presumed that he dispenses unless sound reason counsels such a presumption. But sound reason does not counsel this — rather the opposite. For such a dispensation would be to the greatest harm of the Church: for from it would follow that sacrilegious men would be the legitimate pastors and holders of churches; and everywhere iniquitous men, secure \[in their belief\] that possession is licit, would buy benefices from the Pope; and thus the Church would be filled with unworthy prelates and parish priests. What could be more harmful than this? — Nor is it an obstacle \[to this argument\] that we already presuppose the Pope to be iniquitous. For a prince is not to be presumed worse than his own acts demonstrate. A Pope who sells spiritual things and wills that those who buy them possess and enjoy them licitly would be far worse than one who merely sells them: for the latter involves one evil, the former a double one.

Second, because if the person of the Pope knew that he to whom he confers an order or benefice was excommunicated, and without any other absolution confers on him the order or benefice, he does not for that reason absolve him from excommunication: as you have from cap. *Si Summus*, *de Sent. Excomm.*, in the *Clementines* \[Clem. 5.10.2\], where the text states that from the Pope’s knowingly communicating in any manner with an excommunicated person, he is not understood to absolve him unless he expressly declares this to be his will. And the Gloss there, on the words *in any manner*, says that this also applies to participation in divine worship. For if the Pope, by knowingly conferring an order or benefice on an excommunicated person, does not absolve him *ipso facto* but leaves the recipient subject to the disposition of law; by the same reasoning, he does not relax the disposition of the common law by the fact that he confers a benefice on someone who, in receiving it, is by law excommunicated and rendered incapable, etc. — And this is confirmed: because dispensations of this kind, since they deviate enormously from the law and the good of the Church, are of the strictest legal construction and are least to be presumed.

VI. To the arguments of the other opinion it is replied that, although the knowledge of a prince qua prince — which is public knowledge — sometimes excuses (and not always, as is clear from the chapter cited), yet the knowledge of a prince qua private person does not appear to excuse, unless it appears otherwise from some other source. Hence if the Pope knowingly assigns the benefice of an excommunicated person, and he knows this only as a private person, I am not certain that it is to be presumed he is dispensing with the recipient — as is clear from the chapter adduced on the matter of absolution, which is not so odious as dispensation. But even if it were presumed that he dispenses, it does not follow by analogy in the case at hand. For in our present case the Pope would sin mortally in dispensing with an unworthy man to be a pastor; whereas in dispensing with an irregular person, he need not sin. Hence it is clear that the cases are not analogous, nor is there the same reason for presuming a dispensation in the one case as in the other.
