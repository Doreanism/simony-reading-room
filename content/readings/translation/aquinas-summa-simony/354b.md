Note thirdly that, since the Pope’s power over the temporal goods of the Church is the power not of a lord but of a steward, as the text says, it follows that the fullness of papal power over the temporal goods of the Church does not exceed the limits of stewardly power — just as in natural things the fullness of the power to heat does not exceed the limits of the power to heat, and in political affairs the fullness of royal power does not exceed the limits of royal authority. Accordingly, the Pope cannot give away the Church’s goods at will; but he can, as one holding the apex of stewardly power, dispose of those same goods far more extensively than any other immediate prelate of a particular church.

And by this the solution becomes clear to the question of merchants — namely whether those who have earned usury from various clerics, but at fixed rates, can come to an arrangement with the Lord Pope: for instance, if the usury amounts to fifteen thousand ducats, to give the Pope three or four thousand and retain the rest with a clear conscience. For it is evident that the merchant would not thereby be absolved in conscience. Both because the Pope is not lord but steward of ecclesiastical goods, and such a composition would not be an act of stewardship, which implies a discerning disposition; and also because even if the Pope were the lord, it is reasonably presumed that he would not be giving freely, but rather ransoming himself from annoyance by taking as much as he could get. The clearest sign of this is that in the composition he strives to recover as much as possible.

From the same foundation — namely that the Pope is not lord but steward — it also follows that he cannot by the fullness of his power give the Church’s goods at will to whomever he pleases, or to his relatives, etc.; but he is bound to dispense them faithfully as right reason dictates, in accordance with: “Now it is required of stewards that one be found faithful.” And again: “A faithful and wise servant, whom the lord has set over his household, to give them their measure of wheat in due season” — and not at will, as much as he wishes.

5. Furthermore, it sometimes happens that someone maliciously impedes another from obtaining a bishopric or some other dignity. But it is lawful for anyone to redeem himself from such harassment. Therefore it seems lawful in such a case to give money for a bishopric or some other ecclesiastical dignity.

6. Furthermore, matrimony is a sacrament of a kind. But sometimes money is given for marriage. Therefore it is lawful to sell the sacraments for money.

On the contrary, it is said in Causa I, question 1: “Whoever has ordained anyone for money, let him be excluded from the priesthood.”
