The second inference is evident because this would be unlawful only if it were unlawful, namely to want something temporal in exchange for something spiritual. II. A second difficulty arises: whether in matters of this kind pertaining to spiritual ministry it is lawful to sell one’s personal labor and hire out one’s services under a price agreement. The source of the difficulty on the affirmative side is that bodily labor, and similarly bodily services, belong to the category of temporal things, not spiritual things. But to sell or hire out temporal things is no sin.

This is confirmed. For if this were intrinsically unlawful, it would not be lawful in some spiritual matters and unlawful in others, but across the whole category of spiritual things it would either be lawful or unlawful throughout. But, as experience attests, it is clearly lawful in many spiritual matters: for a priest lawfully hires out his services in celebrating \[Mass\] three times a week and the like; and the labor he must expend in traveling to a village to celebrate he lawfully sets at a price; a doctor of theology also sells his services and labor, just as an advocate sells just legal counsel. In other spiritual matters, therefore, since they are not different from these, it will be lawful.

The negative side, however, is supported by two arguments. The first: since all ecclesiastical things that are called spiritual — such as the administration of sacraments, the sacrifice of the Mass, preaching, and divine worship — consist of two elements, namely a spiritual and a temporal (for the spiritual in them is grace, truth, and the like; while the temporal is the human action), then if it is lawful to sell or hire these out by reason of the human action, all ecclesiastical things would lawfully be saleable in an absolute sense; so that just as it is lawful to sell a chalice by reason of its material, so, proportionally, it would be lawful to sell all such spiritual things by reason of their human action. This is confirmed from the definition of simony, in which selling what is annexed to spiritual things is included. But it is clear that things simultaneous \[with the spiritual\] are more closely annexed to spiritual things than those to which spiritual things stand as antecedents. But human action in these matters is simultaneous with the spiritual. Therefore it is even less saleable than an ecclesiastical benefice, to which the spiritual stands as antecedent, and which is said to be altogether unsaleable.

The second argument is that, unless a distinction is made between a maintenance stipend and the sale of labor or the hiring out of services — as with an advocate or a doctor — then just as it is lawful for an advocate and a doctor, however wealthy, to sell their teaching and advocacy, so it will be lawful for wealthy priests and preachers to hire out their services, sell their labor, and consequently receive temporal goods from the people not for the sake of sustenance but under the title of wages. And consequently all the laws will collapse.

III. For the clarification of these matters, it must be understood that between a maintenance stipend and a stipend for hired services there is this difference: a stipend for hired services has the character of wages, in accordance with Matthew 20 — “Call the workers and pay them their wages” — whereas a maintenance stipend does not have the character of wages but of necessary or fitting support for ministers. From this it follows that one who gives something temporal, say money, to ministers of the Church for their sustenance is not giving money as the price of their services or labor, but solely to sustain them in food and clothing, so that having nourishment and covering they may devote themselves freely to ecclesiastical offices. But one who gives something temporal to those same ministers in return for their labor renders it as the price of labor and services, as the very name “hiring” indicates.
