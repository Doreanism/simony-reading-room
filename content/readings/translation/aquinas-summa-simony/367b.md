VII. In this opinion, however, I do not stand so firmly on fixed footing that I do not waver. For to the second argument it can be said that the new law in the *Clementines* is an exception from the common law, and was enacted precisely because the common law held the opposite. For this reason it must not be extended, but understood only in its own specific case.

To the first argument, however, it can be said that from so iniquitous a will one ought to presume consistently — that is, that the Pope wills that which follows from his own perverse transaction. Now it is plain that a seller wills to transfer the thing sold to the buyer. A simoniacal Pope therefore intends that whoever purchases from him should truly have the benefice he sells, lest he appear to be a fraudulent vendor.

How much weight these arguments carry, let others judge. I myself confess that if, according to the laws, such knowledge of a prince purges in such a way as to excuse from the penalties of law in the judicial forum (which I leave for the jurists to determine), then one who has purchased a benefice from the Pope in this manner is not bound to renounce it in the forum of conscience: for the penalties of law would be understood to be simply suspended.
