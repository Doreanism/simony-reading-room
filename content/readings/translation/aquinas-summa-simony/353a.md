Therefore, one who sells or buys a spiritual thing shows irreverence toward God and toward divine things. For this reason, such a person sins with the sin of irreligion.

To the first objection, therefore, it must be said that just as religion consists in a certain profession of faith, which a person sometimes nonetheless does not hold in his heart; so also the vices opposed to religion carry a certain profession of unbelief, even though unbelief is not always present in the mind. In this respect, then, simony is called a heresy according to outward profession: because in selling the gift of the Holy Spirit, a person in a certain way professes himself to be the owner of a spiritual gift — which is heretical.

It must be known, however, that Simon Magus, beyond wishing to purchase the grace of the Holy Spirit from the Apostles with money, also said that the world was not created by God but by a certain higher power: as Isidorus says in the *Etymologiae*. And according to this, Simonists are counted among other heretics, as is clear in Augustinus’s *De Haeresibus*.

To the second objection it must be said that, as was stated above, justice and all its parts, and consequently all the vices opposed to them, reside in the will as in their subject. Therefore simony is fittingly defined through the will. The word “deliberate” is added to designate choice, which pertains principally to virtue and vice. But not everyone who sins by choice sins the sin against the Holy Spirit: only one who chooses sin through contempt of those things that ordinarily restrain people from sinning, as was said above.

To the third objection it must be said that the kingdom of heaven is said to be bought when one gives what one has for God’s sake — taking the word “buying” broadly, in the sense in which it is used for merit. ### Commentary of Cardinal Caietanus.

In the first article of Question One Hundred, in the response to the second objection, a difficulty arises concerning these words: “Justice and all its parts, and consequently all the vices opposed to them, reside in the will as in their subject.” For these words seem to go against the Author himself. Since it is clear that he is speaking of the parts of justice not only as integral and subjective parts, but also as potential parts: otherwise he would not be speaking to the point, since he is speaking of simony, which is a vice opposed to religion, which is one potential part of justice.
