And therefore, just as simony is contracted by receiving money or any external thing — which pertains to the gift of the hand — so too it is contracted through the gift of speech or of service.

To the first objection, therefore, it must be said that if a cleric renders to some prelate an honorable service ordered toward spiritual ends — such as toward the benefit of the Church or the assistance of its ministers — by the very devotion of that service he renders himself worthy of an ecclesiastical benefice, just as he would by other good works. In this case, therefore, the gift of service is not to be understood as applying. And it is in this case that Gregorius speaks. If, however, the service is dishonorable or ordered toward carnal ends — for instance, because he served the prelate for the benefit of his own relatives or patrimony, or for something of that kind — it would be a gift of service and would be simoniacal.

To the second objection it must be said that if someone confers something spiritual on another on account of kinship or any carnal affection, the conferral is indeed illicit and carnal, but not simoniacal, because nothing is received in return; thus it does not pertain to the contract of buying and selling on which simony is founded. If, however, someone gives an ecclesiastical benefice to another with this stipulation or intention — that from it provision be made for his own relatives — that is manifest simony.

To the third objection it must be said that the gift of speech means either praise itself pertaining to human favor, which falls under a price, or requests by means of which human favor is obtained or its opposite avoided. And therefore if someone aims principally at this, he commits simony.

Now it seems that one aims principally at this when he heeds prayers offered on behalf of an unworthy person. Thus the very act is simoniacal.
