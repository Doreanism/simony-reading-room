Just as, conversely, we said above that because the will is naturally inclined toward one’s own good according to the higher part of appetite, therefore the will in matters pertaining to ourselves does not require a virtue.

It is clear secondly that because certain virtues, such as liberality, regard operations directed toward another — for instance, the free giving of money to another — in such a way that the passions of cupidity must be restrained, it is therefore also necessary to posit some habitual perfection in the concupiscible faculty.

What is uncertain, however, is in which of these — namely the will or the concupiscible faculty — such a virtue is to be said to reside simply and absolutely as in its subject. This does not clearly appear without a discussion of the proper acts of such virtues. For this reason, the final definition is deferred until the place mentioned above. As for the present text, it can be glossed as referring to those parts of justice that are strictly and properly parts of justice, which Tullius \[Cicero\] enumerated — namely those that have the character of what is owed to another in such a way that without this owing, the moral integrity of conduct is not preserved. Liberality and the like, however, are not of this kind; they partake but little of the character of what is strictly owed, being not a matter of necessity but of a higher good, as appears above in question 80, where the parts of justice are treated.

III. In the reply to the seventh objection, note first that the Pope can commit the sin of simony, just as he can commit adultery and other vices, even though he incurs no canonical censure. And this is clear.

Note secondly that, since the money the Pope might receive is of two kinds — either money of the Church or secular money — whichever he receives as the price of a spiritual thing, he commits simony, because neither belongs to him absolutely. This is evident with respect to secular money. With respect to ecclesiastical money, it is declared in the text: because the Pope is not lord but the principal steward of ecclesiastical funds, and therefore the Church’s money is not his absolutely, so that he could dispose of it at will.

## Article Two.

Whether it is always unlawful to give money for the sacraments. On the second point we proceed thus. It seems that it is not always unlawful to give money for the sacraments. For baptism is the gateway to the sacraments, as is said in the Third Part. But it seems lawful, in certain cases, to give money for baptism — for instance, when a priest refuses to baptize a dying child without payment. Therefore it is not always unlawful to buy or sell the sacraments.

2. Furthermore, the greatest of the sacraments is the Eucharist, which is consecrated at Mass. But some priests receive prebends or money for singing Masses. Therefore it is even more permissible to buy or sell the other sacraments.

3. Furthermore, the sacrament of penance is a sacrament of necessity, consisting chiefly in absolution. But some \[confessors\] who absolve from excommunication exact money for doing so. Therefore it is not always unlawful to buy or sell the sacraments.
