Nor can he recover the money he gave disgracefully, even though the other party unjustly retains it. — If, however, a person is simoniacal because he conferred an order simoniacally, or because he gave or received a benefice simoniacally, or because he was an intermediary in simony: if this is public, he is suspended by the law itself both with respect to himself and with respect to others; but if it is hidden, he is suspended by the law itself with respect to himself only, but not with respect to others.

To the second objection it must be said that no one ought to receive ordination from a bishop known to have been promoted simoniacally — neither on account of his command nor even on account of excommunication. And if one is so ordained, he does not receive the exercise of the order, even if he is unaware that the bishop is a simonist; but he requires a dispensation. — Although some say that if one cannot prove the bishop to be a simonist, he must obey by receiving the order, but ought not to exercise it without a dispensation. — But this is said without reason. For no one ought to obey anyone so as to join him in an unlawful act. Now one who is suspended by the law itself both with respect to himself and with respect to others confers an order unlawfully. Hence no one ought to join him by receiving from him, for whatever reason. If, however, the matter is not established, one ought not to presume the sin of another; and thus one ought to receive the order from him with a good conscience.

If, however, the bishop is simoniacal in some way other than through his own simoniacal promotion, one can receive an order from him if the matter is hidden — because he is not suspended with respect to others, but only with respect to himself, as was said.

To the third objection it must be said that a person’s being deprived of what he received is not only a punishment for sin, but is sometimes also an effect of unjust acquisition — for example, when someone buys a thing from one who cannot sell it. And therefore if someone knowingly and willingly receives an order or ecclesiastical benefice simoniacally, he is not only deprived of what he received — that is, he lacks the exercise of the order and must resign the benefice together with the fruits received from it — but is further punished, because he is branded with infamy; and he is bound to restore not only the fruits actually received, but also those that could have been received by a diligent possessor (though this is to be understood of the fruits that remain after deducting the expenses incurred for their production, with the exception of those fruits that have already been expended for the benefit of the church). — If, however, his promotion is procured simoniacally by others without his will and knowledge, he indeed lacks the exercise of the order and is bound to resign the benefice he obtained, together with its existing fruits (though he is not
