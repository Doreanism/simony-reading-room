## Article Five.

Whether it is lawful to give spiritual things in exchange for the gift of service or of speech. On the fifth article the procedure is as follows. It seems that it is lawful to give spiritual things in exchange for the gift of service or of speech. For Gregorius says in the Register: Those who serve the interests of the Church rejoice that my diocese rewards them. But to serve the interests of the Church pertains to the gift of service. It therefore seems lawful to bestow ecclesiastical benefices in exchange for service received.

2. Moreover, just as one’s intention seems carnal if a person gives someone an ecclesiastical benefice in return for service rendered, so too if one gives it out of regard for kinship. But this does not seem to be simoniacal, because there is no buying and selling involved. Therefore neither is the former.

3. Moreover, what is done solely at the request of someone appears to be done freely, and thus simony — which consists in buying and selling — does not seem to apply. But the gift of speech is understood to occur when someone confers an ecclesiastical benefice at another’s request. Therefore this is not simoniacal.

4. Moreover, hypocrites perform spiritual works in order to win human praise, which seems to belong to the gift of speech. Yet hypocrites are not called simoniacs. Therefore simony is not contracted through the gift of speech.

But against this, Pope Urbanus says: Whoever bestows or receives ecclesiastical things not for the purpose for which they were instituted but for personal gain, whether by gift of speech, of service, or of money, is a simoniac.

I respond that, as was said above, by the word “money” is understood everything whose value can be measured in money. Now it is clear that a man’s service is ordered to some utility that can be assessed in monetary terms — hence even ministers are hired for monetary wages.
