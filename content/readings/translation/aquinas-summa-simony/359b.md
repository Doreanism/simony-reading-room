Therefore if he were to accept something for the use of spiritual power, it would not be understood as hiring out his labor — which he is bound to expend by the duty of the office he has accepted — but would be understood as selling the very use of spiritual grace. And for this reason, it is not lawful to accept anything for any dispensation whatsoever; nor for delegating one’s functions; nor for correcting one’s subjects, or for refraining from correcting them. It is lawful for them, however, to receive procurations when they visit their subjects — not as a price for correction, but as a due stipend.

One who possesses knowledge, however, does not thereby take on an office by which he is bound to render the use of that knowledge to others. And therefore he may lawfully receive payment for his teaching or counsel — not as selling truth or knowledge, but as hiring out his labor. If, however, he were bound to this by office, he would be understood to be selling truth itself: and hence he would sin gravely. As is evident in those who are appointed in certain churches to teach the clergy of the church and other poor \[students\], for which they receive a benefice from the church: from whom it is not lawful to receive anything — neither in order to teach, nor in order to observe or omit certain feast days.

To the fourth it must be said that for admission to a monastery it is not lawful to exact or receive anything as a price. It is lawful, however, if the monastery is poor and insufficient to support so many persons, to grant admission to the monastery freely, but to accept \[payment\] for the sustenance of the person who is to be received in the monastery, if the monastery’s means are insufficient for this. Similarly, it is also lawful if, on account of the devotion which someone shows toward the monastery by making generous alms, that person is more readily received in the monastery; just as it is likewise lawful to draw someone toward devotion to the monastery through temporal benefits, so that he may thereby be inclined to enter the monastery — though it is not lawful to give or receive anything by contract for admission to the monastery, as is held in \[*Decretum*\] I, q. 2, cap. *Quam pio*.
