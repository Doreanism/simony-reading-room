\[The decretal\] speaks of the forum of conscience and expressly states that before God penitence alone suffices. For if it suffices to satisfy the Creator by penitence alone in mental simony, then in order to satisfy God one is not bound in the forum of conscience to anything else — such as resignation, restitution, and the like. Mental simonists are therefore bound, by natural and divine law, to nothing beyond penitence.

IV. Since it is not necessary to be wiser than is fitting, and since that decretal says both things — namely, that before God penitence alone suffices, and absolutely that they are not bound to the resignation of spiritual or temporal things acquired in such a way, not by any pact but with preceding mutual disposition of soul — it must therefore be stated absolutely that mental simonists, that is, those without any agreement, are not bound even in the forum of conscience to make restitution of things acquired through mental and simoniacal disposition, whether those things be spiritual or temporal; penitence alone suffices for them. Hence all simonists by hand, tongue, or service — not acting from a pact but from a principal hope or disposition, giving or receiving such things on account of spiritual goods — sin mortally with the sin of simony and are in a state of eternal damnation so long as they persevere in such evil intent; yet they are not bound to resign or restore what was received in this manner. And this appears to be the common practice.

To the objections urged on the other side, however, from which the truth will become clearer, the following must be said. To the first, which is very difficult, it is replied that a giving or receiving can be illicit in two ways. In one way, according to the very form of the exterior act signified by both the giving and the receiving — as when the act of giving or receiving is in its exterior form a sale, a purchase, and the like. In another way, from the interior disposition alone — for instance, because the exterior act, in its external manner and form, has the character of a gratuitous donation, yet from the interior disposition alone it has the character of a sale. The rule, “when a giving and receiving is illicit, what is thus acquired cannot be retained,” is to be understood of giving and receiving that is illicit in the first way, and not in the second; because in the first way the giving and receiving is illicit per se, whereas in the second way it is illicit per accidens, since the illicitness comes not from the external manner and form but from the bad intention joined to it. And so it is in the present case. For when only mental simony intervenes on both sides, the giving and receiving is illicit not according to its exterior form, but from the evil interior intention joined to it; because the one who gives, gives in the manner of a gift and not in the manner of a sale, even though inwardly he harbors this iniquity; and likewise the one who receives, receives in the manner of a gift, even though inwardly he purchases it from an iniquitous will.

To the second objection it is replied that one false thing is presupposed, namely, that a bishop is bound to restitution of the money he received through mental simony. For it is clear from the text of that decretal that mental simonists on both sides are bound neither to spiritual nor to temporal things acquired in this manner.

To the third objection — which is treated at length by Panormitanus \[Nicolaus de Tudeschis\] in the said chapter *Mandato*, recounting so many reasons for the distinction between a usurer and a mental simonist — setting aside everything that seems to be said less satisfactorily, as you may see for yourself: it is briefly replied that the reason why a mental usurer is bound to restitution of what was given him by the borrower not with the intention of donating, whereas a mental simonist is not bound to restitution of what was donated to him by the mental simonist with an intention of buying or selling, is this: what is given to a usurer is given willingly but involuntarily (for usury is paid involuntarily, by way of redeeming oneself from harassment — it is a mixed voluntary and involuntary act, as is clear from *Ethics* III); whereas what is given to a mental simonist is given voluntarily, since it is given with the intent of buying or selling, for it is plain that both buying and selling are voluntary contracts without any admixture of the involuntary, as you have from *Ethics* V. The entire basis for the distinction is therefore the difference between the voluntary and the involuntary: in the former case the giving is involuntary and must therefore be restored; in the latter it is voluntary and is therefore not subject to restitution.
