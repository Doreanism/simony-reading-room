To the sixth objection it must be said that before God the will alone makes one a simonist; but with regard to external ecclesiastical punishment, one is not punished as a simonist so as to be bound to renounce, but must do penance for the evil intention.

To the seventh objection it must be said that only the Pope can dispense with one who has knowingly accepted a benefice \[simoniacally\]. In other cases, he \[the Pope\] can also dispense — yet only after the person has first renounced what he acquired simoniacally. He then receives a dispensation either small, that he may have lay communion; or great, that after penance he may remain in his order in another church; or greater, that he may remain in the same church but in minor orders; or greatest, that he may even exercise the major orders in the same church, though he may not assume a prelacy.

II. In response to this difficulty, some, making a distinction, say that a mental simonist is not bound to restore what he has acquired, whether temporal or spiritual, in the case of simony *quia prohibita* \[prohibited simony, i.e., simony by reason of positive law alone\], in accordance with the said c. *Mandato*; but in the case of simony *quia simoniaca* \[intrinsically simoniacal, i.e., by reason of divine or natural law\], he is bound even in the forum of conscience to restore what was acquired through mental simony. Their reasoning is that this text speaks only of simony arising from positive law, and therefore ought not to be extended to simony arising from divine law. — Some, however, criticize this response, because the text speaks without distinction.

It seems to me that this response does not hold. For that text speaks of simony also prohibited by divine law — namely, that which is committed for the religious state. For it is established that it is prohibited by divine law for the spiritual state of religious life to be sold, since it is an unsaleable spiritual state instituted by Christ: *If you wish to be perfect, go, sell*, etc. \[Matthew 19:21\].

III. Others say that being bound to restitution arises in two ways: namely, by reason of ecclesiastical penalty, or by reason of natural law. They add that all simonists by agreement are bound by both titles to restitution: namely, by reason of natural law, because both the giving and the receiving were unjust; and by reason of ecclesiastical penalty, because the Church has so decreed. Mental simonists, however, are not bound by reason of ecclesiastical penalty — and this alone is what that text says — but they are indeed bound by reason of natural law to resign and make restitution, both of spiritual and of temporal things, because one who receives anything simoniacally receives it unjustly. And this the text passed over in silence because it spoke in terms of ecclesiastical forum.
