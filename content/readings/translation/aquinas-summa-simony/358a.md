As for the labor one undergoes in traveling to a place of celebration or preaching, this is not to the point. For it is incidental, and can be placed under a price: as can the expenses incurred along the way. The labor that intervenes in the act of celebration and preaching itself, however, is an intrinsic part of celebration and preaching: since God dispenses spiritual things through such human acts.

A Doctor of Theology also differs from a preacher in this respect: the doctor is sought for the temporal benefit of learning in theology, as in the other sciences; but the preacher is sought only for spiritual benefit, as if the preacher were an instrument of the Holy Spirit for purging, illuminating, and perfecting human beings according to the grace of adoption. Hence no one may preach unless sent — according to that saying in Romans 10: *How shall they preach unless they are sent?* And therefore a preacher cannot contract for payment for his work and labor in preaching. Although for this there is also another reason from the same authority: namely, that whoever preaches does so by virtue of a delegated office, and therefore must be content with the stipends set by the one who delegated him — namely, the stipend of sustenance — and cannot hire himself out again to the people, having already hired himself out to the one who delegated him. It does not pertain to the vice of simony, however, if a preacher should prefer to preach in a place where he knows a larger alms has been set, rather than elsewhere: but it does pertain to a defect of charity, by which one attends more to temporal than to spiritual advancement.

## Article Three.

Whether it is lawful to give and receive money for spiritual acts. With regard to the third we proceed as follows. It seems that it is lawful to give and receive money for spiritual acts. For the exercise of prophecy is a spiritual act. But something was formerly given to prophets: as is clear from 1 Samuel 9 and 1 Kings 14. Therefore it seems lawful to give and receive money for a spiritual act.

2. Moreover, prayer, preaching, and the praise of divine utterances are most eminently spiritual acts. But money is given to saints and other holy men in order to obtain their prayerful intercession, according to that saying in Luke 16: *Make friends for yourselves by means of the mammon of unrighteousness.* To preachers who sow spiritual things, temporal support is owed, according to the Apostle, 1 Corinthians 9. To those who celebrate divine praises in the ecclesiastical office and lead processions, something is also given, and sometimes annual revenues are assigned for this purpose. Therefore it is lawful to receive something for spiritual acts.

3. Moreover, knowledge is no less spiritual than \[spiritual\] power. But it is lawful to receive money for the use of knowledge: as it is lawful for an advocate to sell just legal counsel, a physician to sell medical advice, and a teacher to sell the office of teaching. Therefore, by the same reasoning, it seems lawful for a prelate to receive something for the use of spiritual power — for instance, for a correction, or a dispensation, or something of that kind.
