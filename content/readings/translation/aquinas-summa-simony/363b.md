Third, that the petition for the cure — even the simple cure — of souls is not necessarily presumptuous. This is clear because in a particular case it is lawful. And because Isaiah, seeing himself purified, petitioned without presumption: “Here am I; send me.”

Fourth, that the petition for the cure of souls in a qualified sense, or secondarily — such as the petition for a parish, an archpriesthood, and the like — although it smacks somewhat of presumption within the limits of proper governance, yet where ecclesiastical governance is commonly corrupt (that is, where such benefices are not given except to those who petition for them), it lacks presumption, if it is done without prejudice to anyone, and the petitioner is otherwise worthy, and is moved by charity toward the Church. This is clear from the fact that in such corrupt systems of governance, poor provisions are frequently made. And therefore, in this limited cure of souls, worthiness and charity as the motive for petitioning suffice, without its being evident in the particular case that a bad provision is imminent.

Fifth, that when or where ecclesiastical governance of this kind is corrupt, a petition for such a benefice with cure of souls on one’s own behalf — made by an otherwise worthy person, proceeding from human feeling, although it smacks somewhat of presumption insofar as it seeks to have authority — yet since one truly desires and intends to be of benefit, and the danger of poor provision is ever present while such corruption endures, and worthy men are passed over: that presumption appears no greater than venial. And so the prayers are not for an unworthy person simply speaking, but only in a qualified sense.

Nor is it surprising if Augustinus, Gregorius, the Author \[Aquinas\], and others seem to have taught otherwise. For they taught what is to be done within the limits of the laws; we, however, \[treat\] what can be done — and sometimes must be done — amid the corruptions of laws. And again, they \[taught\] what is absolutely lawful; we, however, \[treat\] what is lawful in a case of necessity, whether simply or for the present among such persons.

Moreover, no one ought to be punished for something that was done with the knowledge and consent of another, because punishment is owed to sin, which is voluntary, as is clear from what was said above.
