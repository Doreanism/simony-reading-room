Regarding advocates, it has already been said that they are understood to be selling temporal assistance. And therefore, since they furnish great assistance by way of great counsel in great causes, they wish to be paid handsomely. And rightly so: for such rare assistance, acquired at the cost of so many sleepless nights, labors, and expenses, is worth that much.

IX. Through these considerations the reply to the arguments of the opposing opinion becomes clear, except for the authorities of the Apostle and the Lord. To the first of these it is said that both the Apostle and the Lord are speaking of the stipend of sustenance: as is clear from the Apostle’s conclusion, namely, that it is lawful to live from the Gospel. And that the Lord ordained this, the Apostle himself there testifies, explaining how those words of the Lord, *The laborer is worthy of his wages*, are to be understood in this context. Matthew also clarified this in chapter 10, saying that the Lord said: *The laborer is worthy of his food.* This meaning is also clear from the fact that the Lord, both in Matthew and in Luke, after commanding the Apostles to go without purse or bag, granted them the stipend of sustenance — namely, food and drink — at the hands of those who heard them.

X. Since temporal support may be sought or given in exchange for the provision of spiritual things under three headings — namely, as a price, as a stipend of sustenance, and as a stipend for hired labor — the first heading constitutes simony; the second was ordained by the Lord in both Testaments and can be stipulated by contract with the poor when necessity demands on account of the inhumanity of those being served in spiritual things; the third applies to those \[acts\] in which the principal thing sought is temporal, and incidentally in certain spiritual \[acts\], as is clear from what has been said.

4. Moreover, religious life is a state of spiritual perfection. But in some monasteries, something is required from those who are received. Therefore it is lawful to require something in exchange for spiritual \[benefits\].

But against this is what is said in \[*Decretum*\] I, q. 1: “Whatever is granted by the consolation of invisible grace must never be sold for gain or any kind of reward.” But all such spiritual things are granted through invisible grace. Therefore it is not lawful to sell them for gain or reward.

I reply that, just as the sacraments are called spiritual because they confer spiritual grace, so also certain other things are called spiritual because they proceed from spiritual grace and dispose toward it. These things are administered through the ministry of human beings, who must be sustained by the people to whom they administer spiritual things: according to that saying in 1 Corinthians 9: *Who serves as a soldier at his own expense? Who tends a flock and does not eat of the flock’s milk?* And therefore to sell what is spiritual in such acts, or to buy it, is simoniacal. But to receive or give something for the sustenance of those who minister spiritual things, in accordance with the ordinance of the Church and approved custom, is lawful — provided, however, that the intention of buying and selling is absent, and that it is not exacted from the unwilling through the withholding of spiritual things that are owed, for that would have a certain appearance of selling.
