To the fourth objection it must be said that Simon Magus wished to buy spiritual power in order to sell it afterward: for it is said in \[*Decretum*\] I, q. 3, that Simon Magus wished to buy the gift of the Holy Spirit, and sought to profit by multiplied money from the sale of signs that would be performed through him. And so those who sell spiritual things resemble Simon Magus in intention; whereas in act, it is those who wish to buy them. Those who sell, however, in act imitate Gehazi, the disciple of Elisha, of whom we read in 2 Kings 5 that he received money from the leper who had been cleansed. Hence sellers of spiritual things can be called not only simonists, but also Gehazites.

To the fifth objection it must be said that under the name of buying and selling every non-gratuitous contract is understood. Hence not even the exchange \[*permutatio*\] of prebends or ecclesiastical benefices can be carried out by the authority of the parties without the danger of simony, nor can a settlement \[*transactio*\], as the laws determine. A prelate, however, can by virtue of his office make such exchanges for a useful or necessary reason.

To the sixth objection it must be said that just as the soul lives by itself while the body lives through its union with the soul; so also some things are spiritual by themselves, such as the sacraments and other such things; while others are called spiritual because they adhere to such things. Hence it is said in \[*Decretum*\] I, q. 3, in the chapter *Si quis obiecerit*, that spiritual things do not advance without corporeal things: just as the soul does not live corporeally without the body.

To the seventh objection it must be said that the Pope can incur the vice of simony, like any other person: for a sin is the graver in any person the higher the rank he holds. For although the goods of the Church are his as principal steward, they are not his as owner and possessor. Therefore, if he were to receive money for some spiritual thing from the revenues of some church, he would not be free from the vice of simony. And similarly, he could also commit simony by receiving money from a layman, not from the goods of the Church.
