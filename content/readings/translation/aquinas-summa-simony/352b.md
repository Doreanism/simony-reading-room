Fourth: whether it is lawful to sell things annexed to spiritual things. Fifth: whether only the gift of the hand \[*munus a manu*\] makes one simoniacal, or also the gift of the tongue \[*munus a lingua*\] and of service \[*munus ab obsequio*\]. Sixth: on the punishment of the simonist.

5. Moreover, there are many other voluntary exchanges besides buying and selling — such as barter and settlement. Therefore it seems that simony is defined insufficiently.

6. Moreover, everything annexed to the spiritual is itself spiritual. It is therefore superfluous to add: “or something annexed to the spiritual.”

7. Moreover, the Pope, according to some, cannot commit simony. Yet he can buy or sell something spiritual. Therefore simony is not the will to buy or sell something spiritual or something annexed to the spiritual.

On the contrary, Gregorius says in the *Register*: “No believer is ignorant that to buy or sell an altar, tithes, or the Holy Spirit is the heresy of simony.”

I respond that it must be said that, as was stated above, an act is evil in its kind when it falls upon undue matter. But a spiritual thing is undue matter for buying and selling, for three reasons. First, because a spiritual thing cannot be compensated by any earthly price: as is said of wisdom in Proverbs 3: “She is more precious than all riches, and all things that are desired cannot be compared to her.” And therefore Petrus, condemning the wickedness of Simon at its very root, said: “May your money go with you to destruction, because you thought you could obtain the gift of God with money.” Second, because that alone can be the proper matter of sale of which the seller is the owner, as is clear from the authority cited above.
