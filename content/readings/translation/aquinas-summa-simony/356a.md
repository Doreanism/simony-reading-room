And the reason for the difference is that in giving \[money\] the elect would be preparing a path for himself to acquire the episcopate, which is conferred by the confirmer. The same applies here: because the one who baptizes confers the character on the one being baptized.

Others, however, make a distinction and say that the case can arise in two ways. First, in the case of an adult being baptized for himself. In this case, they agree in saying that he ought rather to die than pay a greedy priest. Second, in the case of an adult acting on behalf of a dying infant — adding to the case the condition that the adult is entirely incapable of baptizing, for instance because he lacks the necessary members. In this case they say that it is lawful to give money to a priest so that he will baptize the infant, not by purchasing the sacrament, but by redeeming the child from harassment. The reason for the distinction is that an adult can be saved through baptism of desire \[*baptismus flaminis*\], whereas an infant is in extreme necessity with no other remedy available. This is the opinion of Panormitanus, in the chapter *Cum in Ecclesia* under the heading *Rubra*, on Simony; and of certain authors cited by Richardus in the twenty-fifth distinction of the Fourth \[Book of the *Sentences*\], article 3, question 1, reply to objection 8. Richardus himself, however, inclines to the opposite view.

III. Among these opinions, the one that seems more consonant with reason is that which holds that redemption from harassment applies here in the case of an infant at the point of death, but not in the case of an adult. The reason that moves me is this: the one to be baptized in this case suffers an injustice from one who is able but unwilling to baptize — for such a person is bound by the necessity of divine precept to baptize, in this situation, one who comes before him in danger of death without a baptizer, just as he would be obliged to feed a man dying of hunger if he encountered him. For it is universally and manifestly true that anyone may lawfully free himself from an injustice he is suffering by paying some price — since this is nothing other than making use of the wickedness of one who is ready, indeed actively committing, that wickedness, for one’s own necessary good — that is, to redeem oneself from harassment. Hence, because between an infant and an adult in this case there is this difference — that the infant suffers an injustice if not baptized, for it loses access to eternal life owed to it through the act of baptism; whereas the adult does not lose that access, since the way of baptism of desire lies open to him (although the greedy man, by refusing to baptize, inflicts an injustice as far as he is concerned on both) — therefore the one who has care of the infant, if he cannot himself baptize (for instance, because he is mute), may lawfully give money to the greedy priest so that he will baptize, not purchasing the sacrament, but removing the obstacle that prevents baptism, namely that avarice insofar as it impedes baptism. Nor is this contrary to the Author’s text, since he does not address this particular case.

And by this the reply to Petrus’s argument is also clear, if it be raised against this position. For the money is not given to the one causing harassment in order that he may confer a right, but in order that he may remove an unjust impediment and consequently confer the right freely, without being impeded. Nor is the case of the confirmer of an election analogous, for there is no necessity there as there is here; nor is the rectitude of the giver’s intention assured there as it is here, since no temporal benefit is being sought.

In articles two, three, and four together, a difficulty arises: whether it is lawful for priests, preachers, and ministers of divine worship to set a maintenance stipend under a formal agreement. The source of the difficulty is that all the laws proclaim the negative \[i.e., that it is not lawful\], condemning such agreements in these matters and imposing penalties if they are made. And in the text, article three, reply to the second objection, it is expressly said: “If agreements of this kind are entered into, or even with the intention of buying or selling, it would be simony.”

On the affirmative side, however, there stands the custom approved by the Church — namely, that priests who agree to serve a chapel for a year enter into a formal agreement for an annual sum of money. Nor are there lacking preachers who, by means of a formal agreement, wish the amount to be paid to them for Lent to be understood as covering their sustenance. The supporting reason may then be stated as follows: to want a maintenance stipend is not to want something temporal in exchange for something spiritual; therefore to want such a stipend by formal agreement is not to sell spiritual things; and therefore it is not unlawful.
