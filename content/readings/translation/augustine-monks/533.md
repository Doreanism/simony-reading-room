Sufficient unto the day is the evil thereof.” Behold, they say, here the Lord commands us to be free from care about our food and clothing. How then could the Apostle think contrary to the Lord, so as to command us to be thus anxious about what we eat and drink and wherewith we are clothed — even burdening us with the crafts, cares, and labors of workmen? Therefore, in the words “he who will not work, let him not eat,” we ought, they say, to understand spiritual works — of which it is said elsewhere: “to each one as the Lord has given. I planted, Apollos watered, but God gave the growth,” and a little later: “each one shall receive his own reward according to his own labor. For we are God’s fellow workers; you are God’s field, God’s building. According to the grace given to me, like a wise master builder I laid the foundation.” Just as the Apostle therefore works by planting, watering, building, and laying the foundation, so “he who will not work, let him not eat.” For what does it profit to eat spiritually — that is, to be fed by the word of God — if one does not thereby labor for the edification of others?
