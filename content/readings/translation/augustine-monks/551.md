The Gentiles without the law, however, who had not yet believed — those to whom the apostle nevertheless testifies that he accommodated himself through compassionate sympathy, not through a chameleon-like dissimulation — that is, in order that he might assist the fleshly Jew or pagan in the same way he himself, were he in that condition, would have wished to be assisted: bearing their weakness in the likeness of compassion, not deceiving them in the fabrication of a lie, as what immediately follows shows when he says: “I became weak with the weak, that I might win the weak.” \[1 Corinthians 9:22\] For he spoke from that principle in order to say all those other things as well. Just as, therefore, what he says — that he became weak with the weak — was not a lie, so too all the rest above. For what is the weakness he says he had toward the weak, if not a sympathy with them so great that, lest he appear a peddler of the gospel and thereby hinder the course of the word of God by bringing it under bad suspicion among the inexperienced, he refused to receive what was owed him by the Lord’s own ordinance? Had he wished to receive it, he would not have been lying, because it was truly owed; and because he refused, he was not lying. For he did not say it was not owed to him; rather, he showed that it was owed, and declared that he had not made use of what was owed nor intended to make use of it at all — becoming weak in the very act by which he refused to exercise his own right, clothed, of course, in so merciful an affection that he reflected on how he would wish to be treated, were he himself so weakened as to be able to suspect, of those through whom the gospel was being preached to him, that if he saw them accepting expenses, they were running a market fair of merchandise.
