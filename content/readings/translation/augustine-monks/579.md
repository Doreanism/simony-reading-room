For if the ancient rulers of this earthly commonwealth are customarily praised by the most illustrious eloquence of their men of letters because they placed the common good of all the people of their city before their own private affairs — so that one of them, a conqueror after subduing Africa, had nothing to give his daughter on the occasion of her marriage unless she were provided a dowry from the public treasury by decree of the senate — with what spirit ought a citizen of that eternal city, the heavenly Jerusalem, conduct himself in his own commonwealth, except so that the very thing he produces by the labor of his own hands he contributes to the common store with his brother, and if anything is lacking to him, he draws it from the common store, saying with him whose precept and example he has followed: “as having nothing, yet possessing all things”?

## 33.

Therefore also those who, having abandoned or distributed either great or modest wealth, wished in pious and wholesome humility to be numbered among the poor of Christ — if they are sound in body and free from ecclesiastical occupations, although the common property and brotherly charity owe them in return the sustenance of their life, since they have furnished so great a proof of their souls and have contributed either a great deal or no small amount from the resources they possessed to meet the needs of the same fellowship — nevertheless, if they too work with their hands, so as to take away from the lazy, who come from a humbler and therefore more hardened way of life, any excuse, they act far more mercifully than when they distributed all their possessions to the needy.
