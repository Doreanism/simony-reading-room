So too, they say, do we: we read with the brethren who come to us weary from the heat of the world, that they may find rest among us in the word of God and in prayers, psalms, hymns, and spiritual canticles. We address them, console them, exhort them, building them up in whatever we discern in their lives to be fitting for their station. If we did not perform such works, we would be receiving even spiritual nourishment from the Lord at our peril; for this is what the Apostle said: “he who will not work, let him not eat.” In this way these men consider themselves to be complying both with the evangelical and the apostolic precept — believing both that the Gospel has commanded them not to be anxious about bodily and temporal need in this present life, and that the Apostle spoke of spiritual work and food when he said: “he who will not work, let him not eat.”
