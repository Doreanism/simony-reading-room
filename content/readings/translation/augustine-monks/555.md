Since, therefore, the opposition of those who contradict is crushed by such weighty and repeated masses of so great an authority, why do they ask me either what kind of work he did or when he did it? One thing I know: that he was not a thief, nor a housebreaker or robber, nor a charioteer, hunter, actor, or seeker of base profit; but he worked, innocently and honorably, at things suited to human needs, such as the work of smiths, builders, cobblers, farmers, and the like. For uprightness itself does not censure what is censured by the pride of those who love to be called honorable but do not love to be honorable. The apostle, therefore, would not disdain either to take up some rustic task or to engage in craftsmen’s toil. For the one who says, “Give no offense to Jews or to Greeks or to the church of God” — whom he could respect in this matter I do not know. If they say Jews: the patriarchs kept flocks. If they say Greeks — whom we also call pagans — even philosophers held cobblers in great honor. If \[they say\] the church of God: that righteous man, chosen to bear witness to a conjugal virginity that would endure forever, to whom the virgin Mary who bore Christ was betrothed, was a carpenter. Whatever, then, people do in these \[crafts\] with innocence and without deceit is good.
