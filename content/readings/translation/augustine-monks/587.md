I call the Lord Jesus, in whose name I say this without fear, as witness over my soul that, as far as my own convenience is concerned, I would far prefer to spend fixed hours each day working with my hands — as is the practice in well-ordered monasteries — and to have the remaining hours free for reading, prayer, or some engagement with the divine scriptures, than to endure the most turbulent entanglements of others’ disputes, whether in adjudicating secular affairs or in cutting through them by interposition. These burdens were fastened upon us by the same apostle, not indeed by his own will but by the will of him who spoke through him; and yet we do not read that the apostle himself underwent them. For the character of his apostolic journeyings was different. Nor did he say: “If you have secular disputes, bring them to us, or appoint us as judges” — but rather those who are of no account in the church, he says, seat these as judges. “I say this to move you to shame. Is there really no one among you wise enough to decide between one brother and another? Instead, brother goes to law against brother — and before unbelievers at that?” He wished, therefore, that wise men who remained in their places, faithful and holy — not those who traveled here and there for the sake of the gospel — should be the examiners of such disputes.
