For when the day of his departure was pressing upon him while he was at Troas, and the brothers had gathered on the first day of the week to break bread, his intention was so great and the disputation so needful that the discourse was prolonged until midnight, as if it had slipped their minds that on that day there was no fast; but when he was going to remain in some place and was disputing daily, who would doubt that he had hours assigned to this duty? For indeed when he was at Athens, because he had found there men who were most zealous inquirers into all things, it is written of him thus: “He was therefore disputing with the Jews in the synagogue and with the Gentiles in the streets and in the market-place every day with all who were present.” For it does not say in the synagogue every day — where the custom was to hold discourse on the Sabbath — but in the market-place, he says, every day; on account of the pursuits of the Athenians, certainly. For so it follows: “But certain of the Epicurean and Stoic philosophers were conferring with him.” And a little later it says: “Now the Athenians and the strangers and sojourners there devoted their leisure to nothing else than to say or to hear something new.” Let us suppose that the apostle did not work during all the days he was at Athens; for that reason his need was supplied from Macedonia, as he says in the second letter to the Corinthians.
