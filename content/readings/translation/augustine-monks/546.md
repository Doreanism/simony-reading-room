The man of God cries out: “The Lord ordained that those who proclaim the gospel should live from the gospel; but I have made use of none of these things” \[1 Corinthians 9:14–15\] — and yet flesh and blood strives to distort what is straight, to close what is open, to cloud what is serene. “He was performing spiritual labor,” they say, “and lived from it.” If that is so, he was living from the gospel. Why then does he say: “The Lord ordained that those who proclaim the gospel should live from the gospel; but I have made use of none of these things”? Or if they wish to interpret even the word “live” as used here according to the spiritual life, then the apostle had no hope toward God, he who was not living from the gospel, because he said: “I have made use of none of these things.” Accordingly, that he might have sure hope of eternal life, the apostle was certainly living from the gospel spiritually. What he then says — “but I have made use of none of these things” — refers without doubt to this present life, which is lived in the flesh, and makes plain what he said the Lord had ordained for those who proclaim the gospel: to live from the gospel — that is, to conduct this present life, which requires food and clothing, from the gospel — just as he said above concerning his fellow apostles; whence the Lord himself says: “The laborer is worthy of his food” \[Matthew 10:10\] and “The laborer is worthy of his wages.” \[Luke 10:7\] This food and this wage, then — owed to the evangelists by those to whom he proclaimed the gospel for the sustaining of this present life — the apostle did not receive, saying: “but I have made use of none of these things.”
