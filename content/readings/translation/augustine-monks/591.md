And they do not consider what difference there is between that prophetic veil and this revelation of the gospel, of which the apostle says: “When you have turned to Christ, the veil will be removed.” For what the veil interposed between the face of Moses and the gaze of the people of Israel signifies, the long hair of the saints also signified in those times. For the same apostle also says that long hair serves as a veil — by whose authority these men are pressed. He says plainly: “If a man wears long hair, it is a dishonor to him.” “That very dishonor,” they say, “we take upon ourselves in recognition of our sins” — making this a screen of pretended humility, under which they display their pride for sale; as if the apostle were teaching pride when he says: “Every man who prays or prophesies with his head covered dishonors his head,” and: “A man ought not to cover his head, since he is the image and glory of God.” He who says “ought not” is perhaps ignorant of how to teach humility! But if in the age of the gospel these men seek this dishonor for the sake of humility — that which was a sacrament in the age of prophecy — let them shear their heads and cover them with sackcloth. But then that spectacle would not be for sale, because Samson was covered not with sackcloth but with hair.

## XXXII. 40.

Now that other argument, which they have newly devised in defense of their long hair, is — if one may say so — how grievously absurd.
