\[The preceding discussion concerns\] whether the words convey the sacramental reality, and similarly concerning the form. He \[the Master\] says consequently in the definition: *conferred with due intention*, because the intention of the minister is required in all sacraments to do what the Church intends, as was stated more fully in the treatment of baptism.

He says *from divine institution, efficaciously*, where two things are touched upon: first, its institution, which, like that of the other sacraments, could only have been effected by Christ, as was stated in dist. 1, q. 2. But when and where it was instituted will be addressed in the third article. Second, its efficacy, by which it is a certain demonstrative sign of its effects for those who place no obstacle. As was expounded more fully concerning the sacraments in general, q. 1 of that distinction.

From all these words it is possible to gather what the name “sacrament of penance” clearly means: the sacrament of penance is the pronouncement of certain words over a man confessing his sins, by a suitable priest, with due intention, efficaciously causing, from divine institution, the absolution of the penitent from sins. And the clauses are clear. For when he says *suitable*, this excludes a priest who does not have authority to absolve the penitent, or who lacks the capacity to use it. As a corollary: penance as a sacrament in its reality is nothing other than the formal words pronounced in the manner described.

## Notable Point 2

Secondly, it is to be noted that while everyone concedes that the sacrament of penance destroys guilt, yet how this destruction is to be understood is a matter of diverse opinion. One view is that of Hugo \[of St. Victor\], who seems to hold that the priest forgives sins evangelically, inasmuch as he declares them bound or loosed. Hence he says that priests remit or retain sins insofar as they judge and declare \[sins\] to be remitted by God or retained. From which it follows that the practice of penance does not remit guilt, but \[that guilt\] has already been remitted by God through prior contrition before the person receives the sacrament in effect, though not before he receives it in vow and intention. And for this he adduces many arguments that are plain there in the text. And this opinion is commonly followed by the ancient doctors.

But Scotus, in q. 4 of this distinction, holds the opposite opinion: that through penance the guilt is remitted by virtue of this sacrament, which guilt would not be remitted without this sacrament in act. Hence he argues against the first opinion, saying: this opinion seems to derogate too much from the sacrament of penance. For even so, by means of the sacrament of penance sin would never be destroyed, but it would necessarily first have to be destroyed through contrition in order that one might worthily receive the sacrament of penance — because no one worthily shows himself cleansed of leprosy to the priest unless he is first free of it. But this is absurd, because then it would follow that penance is not a sacrament of the new law, nor would it have any causal efficacy or dispositional causality toward first grace. Furthermore, it would follow that the sacrament of penance could never be the second plank after shipwreck, that it could never free the shipwrecked from the danger of drowning. Likewise, the reception of that sacrament is an instrument for grace, that is, an efficacious and necessary disposition by divine statute for the reception of grace. But a purely instrumental or dispositional \[cause\] is not a commemorative or ostensive sign of something already past, but of something future. Therefore the conferral of the sacrament of penance, in order that it be worthily received, need not be a sign of a present divine absolution.
