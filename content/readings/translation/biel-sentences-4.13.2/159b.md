Hence between the one ministering and the one receiving there must be a distinction of matter and form. In support of this view, because perhaps in the sacrament of matrimony that distinction cannot be preserved — at least not a real one, as will be said — one may posit a threefold matter of the sacrament of penance. The most remote \[matter\] consists in the sins. The remote \[matter\] is the contrite sinner. The proximate \[matter\] is the confession itself. That matter and form are posited in the sacraments, however, not properly but metaphorically, was stated above, dist. 5, q. 1.

The definition of the sacrament of penance says *under a certain verbal form*, where it touches on the form of this sacrament, which is the priestly absolution. It is not necessary, however, that this form of absolution be as precisely fixed as in baptism or the Eucharist, because it suffices that it express the act of the one absolving, just as with a public judge the words need not be fixed. For one says, as Scotus \[notes\] in q. 4 of this distinction regarding the martyr Theodore: “I order \[him\] to be handed over to the flames.” Another, regarding Lipzianus: “Let \[him\] be punished by the sword” (that is, let him be punished). “Resolved.” Commonly, however, these words are apt: *ego absolvo te* \[“I absolve you”\]. Whatever else is prefaced or follows according to the varying custom of different churches — this is Scotus’s \[view\]. Altarani says that the form of absolution is: *Absolvo te ab omnibus peccatis tuis* \[“I absolve you from all your sins”\]. And this is taken from the words of Christ in Matthew 16 to Peter: “Whatever you loose on earth shall be loosed in heaven.” Absolutions performed publicly, such as *misereatur tui* \[“may \[God\] have mercy on you”\], etc., or *indulgentiam et remissionem te* \[“forgiveness and remission to you”\], are not sacramental but prayers ordered toward the remission of venial sins. And it would not suffice for sacramental absolution to say only these, because through them the priest does not will the absolution to be effected but rather petitions that it be effected. A deprecatory prayer is nonetheless prefaced to sacramental absolution so that the effect of the absolution not be impeded, yet it is not of the essence of the sacrament. And because the principal absolution is the ministry of the priest, it is fitting that something be added that pertains to the authority of the principal absolver, namely: *Ego absolvo te a peccatis tuis in nomine Patris et Filii et Spiritus sancti* \[“I absolve you from your sins in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit”\]. Hence those words seem altogether sufficient and fitting, for they express the act of the minister, the person of the recipient, the sins from which \[the minister\] absolves, and the principal authority. Although according to Scotus only these words are necessary: *absolvo te* \[“I absolve you”\].

The other things that are added to that form by some are superfluous and dangerous, such as: *professis, contritis, oblitis* \[“for those who have declared \[their sins\], are contrite, have forgotten”\], because according to many contrition is not a prerequisite — attrition suffices. So that, as regards the attrited who are not contrite, the effect of absolution would be frustrated \[by such an addition\].

Furthermore, when it is said *I absolve you from sins*, all sins are included and one is absolved from all of them if one places no obstacle. For one mortal sin, at least, is not remitted without another \[being remitted as well\].
