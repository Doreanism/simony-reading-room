Regarding the first point, I give the following rule: A qualified recipient is a man who has the use of reason, who is not physically defective, who is of legitimate birth, who is not barred by excommunication or irregularity, and who is free in his legal status.

I say first, a man, so as to exclude the female sex — and this on account of indecency, the propriety required before those present, and the weakness of deliberative capacity, as established in Book I of the *Politics*.

Second, I say, one who has the use of reason, so as to exclude the mentally impaired, the simple-minded, the insensate, and children.

But could the Pope dispense \[from this requirement\] and ordain children? I reply: I believe he would sin gravely \[in doing so\]. I need not address here whether in fact an ordained child \[validly receives orders\]; yet out of necessity, certain young men are sometimes permitted to be ordained — especially in religious communities, and this with respect to the four minor orders. Today, however, the sacred Council of Trent has established the age requirements for receiving orders, and these must be observed.

But what of major orders? I think that whoever would confer sacred orders on \[such young persons\] would sin gravely.

Third, I say, not physically defective — and I understand this in the sense of serious defect, such as mutilation and the like. The reason is that a minister must be physically capable, and \[serious deformity\] must not be a source of disgrace and scandal to those present.

Fourth, I said, of legitimate birth, and this for the sake of the dignity and fittingness of \[the clerical\] state — unless some other dispensation intervenes.

Fifth, I said, free in his legal status, so as to exclude a serf, who ought not to be ordained. The reason is that by reason of the rank and ecclesiastical status to which one who is promoted to orders is raised, he is in some fashion manumitted and placed outside his lord’s power; and therefore an injury would be committed and injustice done to his lord.

Finally, I say, not barred by excommunication or irregularity. I say this because an excommunicated person, even if he might perhaps receive the character, would not receive the grace, nor the ultimate reality of the sacrament.

Regarding irregularity, one must know that since irregularity is a penalty, it can sometimes befall a person without any fault on his part — as in the case of a just homicide: for example, when a judge sentences and executes a criminal, or an advocate who pleads the case, or a notary who draws up the documents, and the like. Sometimes, however, it can arise through fault — for instance, through simony. Regarding this I say that one who knowingly receives ordination from a simoniac cannot exercise the order he has received without a dispensation from the Pope. A like penalty is incurred by one who fraudulently inserts himself among those called \[to ordination\]. And a similar penalty is imposed on one who ministers in an order he has not received.

Now, second, regarding the minister I give the following rule: The qualified minister is the bishop alone, though by dispensation a \[simple\] priest may serve in his place. I say second, any bishop whatsoever, so that the wicked, or the degraded, or the excommunicated are not excluded: for these can indeed administer the sacrament of order with respect to its first effect, though perhaps not with respect to the second. There is, however, a certain opinion that a degraded or excommunicated \[bishop\], so long as he is tolerated, can administer orders; but so long as he is not tolerated, he cannot. The common opinion of the Doctors, however, does not hold this. The reason is that an indelible power is committed to the bishop at consecration, in the manner described in the preceding question.
