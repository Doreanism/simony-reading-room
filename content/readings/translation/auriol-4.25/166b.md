In the third way, simony can be committed for money — and by “money” I here mean any temporal thing whatsoever, such as a field, a house, or a horse.

Second, I set out propositions. The first proposition is that, speaking of the purchase of sanctifying grace causally understood, and of freely-given grace with respect to the exterior act, sin of simony is always committed. The reason is that when something which cannot be compared or equated \[with anything else\] is exchanged for something else, sin is committed — and it is indeed a sin against justice. But when the sacraments or other such things are exchanged for a temporal thing, something beyond price and beyond equivalence is given for \[something\] that can never equal it — for instance, a sacrament, which the whole world cannot equal, nor even the least degree of spiritual goods. Therefore this is absolutely contrary to the nature of a contract: that which by its very nature ought to be given freely is here made a thing for sale. Hence it is a sin against justice. But against which species of justice? I say that it is against religion, and the same holds for every other grace that is appropriated to the Holy Spirit — and so this sin can be called sin against the Holy Spirit.

The second proposition is that not only can the aforesaid spiritual things not be sold, but neither can the labor engaged in serving at the altar. Nevertheless it must be understood that the one who ministers may rightly receive maintenance suitable to his state, and in such a manner that he does not receive it by way of obligation or contract, but only by way of necessary support. The reason for this propriety is that, although spiritual things cannot be bought, yet the one who is occupied in their ministry cannot attend to procuring for himself the temporal necessities he requires. Therefore, according to right reason, the one who receives spiritual things from another is obligated to render temporal things in return — not by way of contract, but by way of a different, natural obligation, that is, according to the dictate of natural reason. For this reason, one who does not give to the ministers of the Church tithes, first-fruits, and other things necessary for their sustenance commits sin. The reason is that justice requires that to each be rendered what is his own; but to the one who ministers spiritual things, temporal things are owed to the extent sufficient for his maintenance, where he could not otherwise be sustained. Therefore it is an act of justice that the one ministering spiritual things justly receive temporal things, and that the one receiving spiritual things justly render temporal ones in return. From this the answer to the following question is clear: whether it is lawful to give or receive something for sung Masses. For if something is given under the title of a contract of sale or exchange, simony is expressly committed. But if it is given under the title of an act of justice — as one is obligated to render to another what is his own — then money is returned to the celebrant for the sustenance of his life, not as merit or recompense for that spiritual good. And this is how the Apostle speaks in 1 Corinthians 10, from which the response to the principal argument is clear.
