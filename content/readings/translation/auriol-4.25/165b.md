Now, third, one must consider the signs in which the character is impressed. Here one must know that this is easy to see in those orders in which only a single power is impressed: for in those cases the character or grace is impressed at the touch of that material which signifies the reality of the sacrament — as in the touch of the keys at the ordination of a doorkeeper \[*ostiarius*\], and in the touch of the book at the ordination of lectors. But to the acolyte a twofold power is given, by which he has a twofold act: one with respect to the ministers, the other with respect to the matter \[of the sacrament\] — namely, carrying the hosts and the wine.

Regarding this, the Doctors say that the reality of the sacrament is infused at the touch of the more principal \[instrument\] — for example, at the touch of the vessel, but not at the touch of the candle. It seems to me that it is not incongruous that at the touch of each in succession some power should be given, and that both together constitute one total power.

Concerning the subdeacon, it is said that the effect, the reality, and the sacrament are not given to him when he touches the book, but when he touches the paten and chalice together with the bread and wine.

It seems to me that the whole \[ceremony\] is necessary, so that the full sacrament consists in both \[elements\] together — just as was said of the sacrament of penance, which consists in confession and absolution.

Concerning the deacon, some say \[the character is impressed\] at the imposition of hands, and others say otherwise. I say, as I said of the others, that the whole is necessary as one total complete sign.

Concerning the priest, some say that the power of consecrating is given at the touch of the chalice with the paten; but the power of absolving is given when it is said, “Receive the Holy Spirit,” etc. — whence also all those forms, as is evident, are in the indicative mood.

In reply to the argument on the other side: although to consecrate the body of Christ is more excellent than to confer orders, and this \[former act, consecrating,\] is the greater, \[conferring orders\] is nevertheless more difficult \[in what it demands of the minister\]; and therefore for this act \[of conferring orders\] one requires a person not merely singular, as is the person chosen for the other act \[of consecrating\] — who, although not so exalted, is nevertheless more demanding \[a requirement\]. Hence it is that this act must belong to persons who are singular and chosen from among all persons.
