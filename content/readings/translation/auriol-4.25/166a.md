concerning spiritual things. Now spiritual things are: the grace of the Holy Spirit and the virtues; or \[the object of simony\] may be freely-given grace, such as preaching, teaching, performing divine office, and the like. Sanctifying grace — say, the habitual disposition of the virtues — can be considered in two ways: formally or causally. Considered in the first way, grace is regarded according to the substance of the habit, and in this sense no exchange can be made with respect to it; it is not the matter or object of exchange, and consequently simony cannot be committed with respect to it.

In the second way, grace of this kind can be understood causally, \[that is,\] with respect to what confers this grace, as it were effectively. It is in this sense that the sacraments are called “grace,” because they confer grace upon us, insofar as they apply God to us as the one conferring grace — and in this sense grace can indeed be the object of exchange. For since a sacrament is nothing other than a bodily sign, it can certainly be sold and exchanged for something else, as with Baptism, the Mass, and the like; and therefore simony has its place here. Indeed, this is the worst kind of simony.

If, however, we take freely-given grace — such as preaching, teaching, and the like — it may be taken either with respect to the intrinsic act, in which case no sale can occur (for I cannot sell you the habit of my knowledge), or it may be taken with respect to the extrinsic act, such as the utterance of words, bodily movement, and the like. In this latter sense, sale can indeed occur and has its place in such things, and in this way doctrine, counsel, testimony, and the like are sold — and in all of these there can be sin.

Furthermore, it must be understood that some things are called spiritual not because they are grace, but because they are certain annexed emoluments. In this sense, revenues pertaining to maintenance are in some way spiritual, because they are annexed to spiritual things.

So then, to summarize, simony can be understood as committed in three ways: either because sanctifying grace is sold — not formally, but causally, as when the sacraments, which have the function of infusing this grace, are sold; or because freely-given grace is sold — not with respect to the intrinsic act (for it cannot be sold in that way) but with respect to the extrinsic act; or, third, because what is sold is neither sanctifying grace nor freely-given grace, but something annexed — for instance, temporal revenues.

The second distinction concerns simony on the part of the giver: some simony is \[committed\] by word, some by service, and some by money.

To clarify this distinction, it must be understood that the purchase of some spiritual thing can occur in three ways. \[First,\] when it is bought or sold by word — for instance, when it is obtained by prayers. Simony of this kind is called simony “of the tongue,” and this is to be understood as meaning that the spiritual thing is obtained by prayers on behalf of an unworthy person. For if it were sought on behalf of a worthy person, and the prayers were not given primarily for the sake of obtaining \[the benefice\] but only secondarily, simony would not thereby be committed. Hence a precaution is to be observed here: the prayers should not be directed explicitly toward obtaining the thing for the person, but toward setting forth his suitability — so that they function more as an assertion and attestation than as words of entreaty and supplication.
