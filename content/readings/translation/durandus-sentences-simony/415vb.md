## Question Four.

Whether simony is committed in those three customary ways usually assigned: namely, through a gift from the hand, through a gift from the tongue, and through a gift from service.

The second question is whether simony is committed in those three customary ways usually assigned: namely, through a gift from the hand, through a gift from the tongue, and through a gift from service. It is argued that it is not, because simony is committed by the will alone — for it is thus defined as “a deliberate will to buy or sell something spiritual for something temporal” — but someone can have such a will without any of these three means, or any one of them; therefore, etc. Furthermore, simony had its origin from Simon, from whom it took its name; but he offered only money, which is a gift from the hand, as is found in Acts 8. Therefore simony is committed only through a gift from the hand. In opposition is what Pope Vrbanus says, as found in the Decretum, causa 1, question 3, chapter “Saluator,” where it is said that simony is committed through a gift from the tongue, through a gift from the hand, and through a gift from service.

### Response

It must be said that simony, insofar as it is a sin, is committed by the will alone without any exterior act — just as with other sins, since every sin essentially consists in an act of the will. But such simony does not incur a penalty of law; it is purged by penance alone, as is found in the Decretals, De simonia, toward the end. Simony that does incur a penalty of law, however, is committed in three ways: namely, through a gift from the tongue, through a gift from the hand, and through a gift from service. The reason for this is that simony consists in the buying or selling of spiritual things. Now buying and selling consist not only in the giving and receiving of money, but of all things whose price can be measured in currency. For one can buy wine by paying the price in grain or in any other temporal good. But the price of every temporal thing can be measured in currency; therefore, whoever gives something spiritual in exchange for any temporal thing or advantage commits simony. Now a person can expect a temporal advantage from another in three ways: either on the part of the soul, such as friendship or some favor — and in this respect there is a gift from the tongue, which consists in prayers whose granting wins temporal friendship or favor; or on the part of the body, such as some act performed bodily — and in this respect there is a gift from service; or it is something among external goods, whether movable or immovable — and in this way there is a gift from the hand. For this reason, simony can be committed in these three ways, whenever something spiritual or annexed to a spiritual thing is conferred in exchange for any of them. Nevertheless, it must be noted here that a gift from the hand is either of such a kind and of such magnitude that it is presumed to incline one’s affection toward conferring the spiritual thing — and then simony is committed — or it is so small that it is not presumed to incline one’s affection — and then it is not simony. This is indeed true with respect to the judgment of the Church, which presumes in this way from external appearances; but before God and in conscience it is always simony if any spiritual thing whatsoever is conferred on account of a gift, whether great or small.
