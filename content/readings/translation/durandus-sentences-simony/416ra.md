If, however, prayers are made on behalf of a worthy person, in the judgment of men it is probable that the one giving is moved more by regard for the person’s worthiness than by the favor of the prayer. Nevertheless, if he is principally moved by the favor of the prayer or by fear of the one asking, in God’s judgment he commits simony — both the one asked who gives and the one asking, if this is his intention. The same must be understood concerning service, because however honorable it may be, it is simony if an ecclesiastical benefice is given in exchange for it. For a bishop can remunerate a cleric serving him in another way. If, however, a bishop has come to know the worthiness of a cleric through the length of time during which he has served him, he may licitly confer an ecclesiastical office and benefice, provided he has regard for the cleric’s worthiness and not for the service rendered. For Gregorius says in the Register: “It is fitting that those who serve ecclesiastical needs should enjoy ecclesiastical remuneration.” The rationale for all the foregoing is this: the judgment is the same concerning money and concerning every temporal payment that can be obtained through money; but any gift of the hand, whether great or small, any service however honorable, and any personal favor, is a temporal advantage that can be obtained through money; therefore, just as it is the sin of simony if something spiritual is conferred on a worthy or unworthy person for money, so likewise it is the sin of simony if something spiritual is conferred on a worthy or unworthy person for a gift of service, of the hand, and of the tongue, if one has regard for this — and one has regard for this when without these things he would not otherwise have conferred it.

### To the first

To the first argument it must be said that simony, insofar as it is solely a sin, is committed by the will alone; but insofar as it incurs a penalty of law, it is committed in those three ways — namely, through a gift of the hand, a gift of the tongue, and a gift of service. And thus is to be understood the statement of Pope Vrbanus cited in the argument to the contrary.

### To the second

To the second it must be said that although purchases and sales are more commonly made by means of money, they are nevertheless also made by means of other things that can be compensated through money. And therefore, although simony is commonly committed by means of money — which Simon offered because he had it ready at hand — it can nevertheless be committed in other ways by which money can be substituted.

## Question V

Whether a prelate can be excused from the vice of simony by conferring for money a simple canonry or a simple benefice to which neither the care of souls nor a sacred order is attached.
