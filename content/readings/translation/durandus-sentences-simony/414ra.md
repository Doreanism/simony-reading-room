He then draws a distinction concerning simony in the conferral of orders. One person is ordained simoniacally by simoniacal parties when both the one ordained and the one ordaining consent to the simony; such persons must be deprived of their own rank according to the canons. Another is ordained simoniacally by non-simoniacal parties when the one ordained consents to the simony but the ordaining party does not, being ignorant of it; such a person must likewise be removed from his office. Yet another is ordained in a non-simoniacal manner on his own part, yet by simoniacal parties, when the one ordained is ignorant of the simony and the ordaining party commits it knowingly; such persons are mercifully tolerated in their office. He then adds that according to the ancient canons no one ought to be advanced to the subdiaconate before the age of fourteen, nor to the diaconate before fifteen, nor to the priesthood before thirty — and from that age onward one may be elected to the episcopate.

## Question One.

### Whether Every Bishop Can Confer Holy Orders.

Concerning this distinction, the first question concerns those who can confer orders, and the second concerns the impediments that may affect the conferral of orders. With respect to the first question, it is asked whether every bishop can confer holy orders. He argues that not every bishop can do so, as follows: an instrumental agent acts only when it is conjoined to the principal agent; but the minister of the sacraments is an instrumental agent with respect to Christ, who is their institutor; therefore no minister can confer the sacraments unless he is conjoined to Christ. But a heretic is not conjoined to Christ — not through \[personal\] faith, since he is in unbelief, nor through the faith of the Church, since he has been cut off from the Church. Therefore a bishop who is a heretic or has been cut off from the Church cannot confer orders. Furthermore, to promote someone to orders is a greater act than to absolve from sin or from a sentence of law; if a bishop who is a heretic or has been cut off from the Church cannot absolve from sins or from sentences, then neither can he ordain.

In opposition he argues that a priest, however heretical or cut off from the Church, can confect the sacrament of the Eucharist, as is shown in Distinction 33. Therefore a bishop, however heretical or cut off from the Church, can confer orders. The proof of this consequence is as follows: just as sacerdotal consecration is ordered toward the confection of the sacrament of the Eucharist, so episcopal \[consecration\] is ordered toward the ordination of the ministers of the Church.

### Response.

Concerning this question two things must be examined. The first is that every bishop can confer all orders. The second is that only a bishop can confer them, at least the major orders. The first is evident. For a bishop by virtue of his episcopal order possesses, by office, the administration of all the sacraments; in those who possess orders, the higher rank can do whatever the lower can do, and still more besides. Since, therefore, episcopal consecration is the highest consecration conferred in the Church, the power given through it is superior with respect to all others. A bishop can therefore do whatever all those of inferior rank can do in the administration of the sacraments, and yet more: the fullness of the administration of the sacraments resides with the bishop. From this he argues as follows: if not every bishop could confer all orders, this would be either on account of unworthiness of life (because he is wicked), or on account of a defect of faith (because he is a heretic), or on account of a sentence of the Church (because he is excommunicated, suspended, or otherwise cut off), or on account of deprivation of order (because he is degraded). But none of these prevents him from conferring valid orders; therefore, etc. The minor is evident.
