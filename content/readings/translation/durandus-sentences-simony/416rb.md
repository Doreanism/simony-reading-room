For Simon, from whom simony takes its name, wished to buy for money the grace of conferring the Holy Spirit, which he saw being visibly given in the sacrament of confirmation through the laying on of the apostles’ hands. But in a simple canonry and a benefice without the care of souls and without a sacred order, there is no obligation to receive or administer any sacrament; therefore, to sell or buy such a benefice is not simoniacal in itself. Likewise, when a spiritual thing and a corporeal thing are annexed, it is licit through the sale of the temporal thing to transfer the spiritual thing to another, provided that the price is not increased on account of the spiritual thing, as is clear in the case of the right of patronage, which passes with the sale of an estate, as is found at Extra, On the Right of Patronage \[extra de iure patronatus\], chapter “Cum seculi.” But in a simple canonry, spiritual things are annexed to temporal things — namely, the office to the prebend — therefore, since the prebend can be sold, it seems that the canonry can licitly pass with it, provided that the prebend is not sold for more on account of the canonry. Likewise, the spiritual things that precede \[the sacraments\] are no less spiritual than those that follow; but things antecedent to the sacraments, such as sacred vessels, can be licitly sold, provided they are not sold for more by reason of their consecration (as was said in the preceding question); therefore any ecclesiastical benefice, since it is consequent upon the sacraments or naturally apt to follow from them, can be licitly sold provided it is not sold for more by reason of its spirituality. And this is confirmed: because what is purely spiritual may in no way licitly be sold, while what is temporal can be sold entirely; therefore what is partly spiritual and partly temporal (such as a canonry and prebend) can in some way be sold. Likewise, just as to the emperor pertains the dispensation of magistracies, so to the pope pertains the dispensation of ecclesiastical offices; and just as it is not licit to canvass for a magistracy for money — this being the vice of ambitus — so it is not licit to canvass for an ecclesiastical benefice for money, this being the vice of simony. But the emperor selling a magistracy for money does not violate the Lex Iulia de ambitu; indeed, it is commonly held that in the court of the prince the crime of ambitus cannot be committed; therefore, similarly, if the pope confers an ecclesiastical benefice for money, he does not commit simony.

### To the contrary

On the contrary it is argued: because the authority to officiate in the church is more spiritual than the authority to teach, however much the latter depends on human learning; but the authority or license to teach cannot be sold without sin, as is found at Extra, “That Nothing Be Exacted for the License to Teach” \[extra ne pro licentia docendi aliquid exigatur\], almost throughout; therefore, a fortiori, the authority to officiate in the church can in no way licitly be sold. But in a simple canonry there is the authority to officiate in the church; therefore, etc.

### Response

Response. That someone may be excused from simony in the conferral of a simple benefice can be understood in two ways. In one way, that he does not incur the penalty owed to simoniacs by statute of law. In another way, that he neither commits fault nor incurs the penalty of law.
