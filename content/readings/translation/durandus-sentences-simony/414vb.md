Nevertheless, youthful age does not impede reception of orders as though it were contrary to the necessity of the sacrament (as the same authors seem to hold), because someone can have a power whose use he does not yet possess. And therefore, although children lacking the use of reason cannot have the exercise of orders, they can nevertheless receive the power of orders, and upon advancing \[in age\] can afterward exercise it. That last point, however, seems quite doubtful — especially if by “little ones” we understand children who entirely lack the use of reason. First, because it seems quite absurd that such a small child could be made a priest or ordained a bishop. Second, because for the reception of any sacrament there is required a proper intention of receiving if the person is an adult, or an interpretive \[intention\] if the person is an infant. But in such a small child, if he is ordained de facto, there cannot be a proper will to receive the order, since he does not have the use of reason; nor can there be an interpretive one, because although the will of parents, or of those who act in place of parents, can serve as the interpretive will of little ones with respect to what is necessary for their bodily or spiritual salvation — on account of which the intention of parents offering them for baptism, or of the Church, suffices for the reception of saving baptism — nevertheless, the will of parents does not seem to suffice for little ones to receive the sacrament of orders, which is not necessary for salvation but is supererogatory and establishes a person in a grade of dignity. For in this regard it is not readily apparent how the will of parents, or of any others, could be interpretive of the will of little ones, since the little ones may lawfully dissent from such arrangements once they arrive at the years of discretion. And the reason is this: because baptism is ordered to the deletion of original sin, which the child contracts through the act of its parents, it is fitting that the will of the parents suffice for receiving baptism; but this rationale has no place in the case of other sacraments. To the arguments on the opposing side: to the first it must be said that the case of orders and of other sacraments is not alike, especially with respect to women, because the other sacraments are ordered against a defect common to men and women — such as baptism, the Eucharist, penance, and extreme unction — or are ordered to an act common to men and women — such as confirmation, which is ordered to the confession of faith, to which both men and women are bound. But the sacrament of orders establishes a person in a grade of preeminence over others and of teaching, which does not befit women, as has been said. Concerning children, the answer is as given in the body of the solution. To the second it must be said that the argument holds when the agent acts from the necessity of nature, but not when it acts voluntarily, or as an instrument of a voluntary agent — as is the case with the one who administers the sacraments, who acts only according to the form imposed by the principal agent. To the third it must be said that the canons call a woman “deaconess” not from the order of the diaconate but from a blessing, because it pertains to her to read the Homily at Matins, but not to read the Gospel at Mass or to minister at the altar during Mass, which pertains to the deacon. And the canon calls a woman “presbytera” \[meaning\] a widow who has the charge of guarding the possessions of the church, as is apparent from the following chapter, mulieres.
