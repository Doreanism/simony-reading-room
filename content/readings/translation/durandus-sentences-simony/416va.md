For this reason, the pope — or the one with whom he dispenses — is not bound by the statutes of the canons nor does he incur the penalty fixed by canon law. And on this point the question is not doubtful. But if the question is understood in the second sense — namely, whether someone could be excused so as not to commit simony in our case, that is, the culpa \[moral fault\] of simony — then the question is debatable, especially regarding the pope. The procedure concerning this is as follows: first, it is shown in general that the pope can commit the moral fault of simony just as any inferior prelate can. Second, it is shown specifically that in our case the pope would sin by the vice of simony just as anyone else would.

### Article one

The first point is established as follows: every sin is a deviation from some law — natural, divine, or rightly enacted human law. Hence Ambrosius says that sin is “a word, deed, or desire contrary to the law of God.” Moreover, every law addresses those who are subject to it (Romans 3). From this one can argue: every person who acts contrary to a law to which he is subject sins; but every person who sells a spiritual thing for a temporal price sins against the natural and divine law, to which all are subject — the pope no less than others. Therefore, whoever does such a thing sins, and with no sin other than the sin of simony; therefore, etc. The major premise is clear from what has just been said; the minor, from what was said in the preceding question.

### Article two

As to the second point, the procedure is as follows: first the opinion of others is set forth; then, what is believed to be more true. Regarding the first point, it should be known that certain authors distinguish that something can be called simoniacal in two ways. In one way, of its very nature — and it is therefore prohibited in law. In another way, solely from the prohibition of the law. Those things are simoniacal of their very nature which are prohibited from being sold in the New and Old Testaments, such as the sacraments; all other things are simoniacal only because they are prohibited. This distinction is found in the Decretals, under “De officio iudicis delegati,” chapter “Ex parte,” in the Ordinary Gloss, with which Innocentius and Hostiensis concur. Given this premise, it is said

### Opinion

that the pope would sin by the sin of simony in all matters that are simoniacal of their very nature, but not in other matters. Now, to sell a simple canonry or a simple ecclesiastical benefice is not simoniacal except because it is prohibited, as is stated in the same Gloss. For this reason, the pope — who is not bound by any prohibition of positive law — does not commit the vice of simony in this case, not even as to moral fault.

### Refutation of this opinion

But there is a twofold defect here. The first is that the distinction is not sound. The second is that the conclusion is not true. The first point is shown by two arguments and an example.

The first argument is as follows: concerning that which is not evil of its very nature but only because it is prohibited, one can sin only by the sin of simple disobedience, because in such matters the entire basis of the sin comes from the transgression of the prohibition, not from the nature of the act. But the transgression of a prohibition is the sin of disobedience alone. If, therefore, the sale of a simple benefice is not simoniacal of its very nature, it is impossible that it should be anything other than mere disobedience by reason of a legal prohibition.

### Second argument

The second argument is as follows: simony is the sale of a spiritual thing for a temporal price. But legal prohibition does not change the natures of things, so as to make a temporal thing spiritual or vice versa. Therefore, where simony does not exist by the nature of the thing, it cannot exist by reason of a legal prohibition. This is also clear from examples: eating multiple times in a day is not illicit of its very nature but only from the prohibition of law with respect to fast days. Yet one who eats multiple times on fast days sins only by the sin of disobedience alone. Similarly, lying in jest is nothing other than a venial sin; but if the command of a prelate is superadded, forbidding anyone to lie, then by lying one adds to the simple lie nothing other than the sin of disobedience alone. And so it is in all cases, because a human or legal prohibition adds nothing other than the sin of simple disobedience.
