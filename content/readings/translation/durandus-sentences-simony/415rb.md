The major premise is self-evident \[I, q. 1, cap. eos qui per pecuniam\], where it says: “For every owner, if he wishes, sells what he has — whether a slave or anything else among his possessions” — and it applies this to our case concerning simony. The minor is proved as follows: that the prelate has not been appointed by God as the owner of spiritual things but merely as their minister and dispenser — first, by what is found in 1 Corinthians 4: “Let a man so regard us as ministers of Christ and dispensers of the mysteries of God.” And Petrus, the prince of the apostles and vicar of Christ, says in his first canonical epistle, chapter 4: “As each one has received a grace, administer it to one another as good dispensers of the manifold grace of God.” But they received freely, not through a sale, as the Lord said in Matthew 10: “Freely you have received; freely give.” Therefore it is in no way lawful to sell such things. Third, the proposition is evident from the relation of the thing sold to the nature of the sale, as follows: that contract is illicit in which the nature of the thing is repugnant to the nature of the contract; but this is the case in the buying and selling of a spiritual thing; therefore such a transaction is simply illicit. The major is self-evident, because the nature of the thing must be suited to the contract and vice versa. The minor is evident because an accessory follows the nature of the principal: the sacraments, however, are accessories to grace, and therefore they follow its nature. Now the nature of grace is that it be given freely — otherwise it would not be grace, as is stated in Romans 11. But the nature of buying and selling is that it is a non-gratuitous contract. Therefore the nature of grace and of annexed spiritual things is repugnant to buying and selling. It is therefore clear from these considerations that buying and selling \[of spiritual things\] is in itself illicit, and is called “simoniacal” after Simon, who wished to buy the grace of conferring the Holy Spirit, which appeared to be visibly given in the sacrament of confirmation through the imposition of the apostles’ hands, as is recorded in Acts 8. Those who sell, properly speaking, are called “Gehazites,” on account of what is said about Gehazi in 4 Kings 4 \[i.e., 2 Kings 5\]. However, both buyers and sellers alike can be called simoniacs, because Simon wished to buy that grace in order afterward to sell its works. And the same must be said concerning the antecedents to the sacraments — such as sacred vessels and the right of patronage, which is the right of presenting someone to an ecclesiastical benefice. For sacred vessels are in no way to be sold at a higher price on account of their consecration — that is, nothing more should be charged on account of their consecration beyond the value of what is purely material in them, such as the material of gold or silver. And thus, if they are sold on account of the necessity of the church, they may be sold intact; but if \[sold\] to others and not for the use of the church, they must first be broken, lest sacred things be handled by persons other than the ministers of the church. The right of patronage, for its part, cannot be sold by itself nor given in fief; but if a village to which the right of patronage is annexed is sold or given in fief, the right of patronage passes with the whole \[estate\], as is clear from the Decretals \[Extra, de iure patronatus, cap. cum seculi\]; nor should the village or castle be sold for a higher price on account of the right of patronage. As for concomitants — especially those that are inseparable from the administration of the sacraments (such as the bodily labor involved in administering the sacraments or in consecrating churches) — these can in no way be sold without the vice of simony. For the labor of the worker never falls under a sale; only the work itself does, even in purely material affairs. For if a worker — say, a carpenter or a vine-dresser — could perform his works without any labor at all, he could no less sell them; and if, laboring, he performs the aforesaid works and sells them for more on account of the labor, the buyer nonetheless does not buy more on account of the labor but on account of the fruit of the work, to which the worker’s labor contributes nothing \[as a separately priced item\].
