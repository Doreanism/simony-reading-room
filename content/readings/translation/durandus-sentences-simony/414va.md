## Question 2.

Second, we inquire about the defects that can occur in connection with orders. And first concerning the defect of nature, and second concerning the defect of fault. With respect to the first, the question is whether a defect of nature — such as sex or age — prevents someone from receiving orders. This is to ask whether a woman or a child can be ordained. It is argued that they can, because the case seems analogous to that of orders and other sacraments; but both men and women, both infants and adults, can receive other sacraments — such as baptism, the Eucharist, and the like — therefore \[they can receive orders as well\]. Moreover, things that are of the same species can receive the actions of the same agent; but man and woman, adult and child, are of the same species; therefore they can be equally ordained by a bishop. To the same effect is what is stated at 27 q. 1 on the deaconess, cap. Diaconissa, and at dist. 33 cap. presbiteri.

### C

To the contrary: the office of teaching is annexed to orders, but it is not fitting for children or women to teach — for children, on account of their deficiency of reason; for women, on account of the prohibition of the Apostle \[at\] 1 Timothy 2: “I do not permit a woman to teach in the church, nor to have authority over a man.” Therefore, \[they cannot be ordained\]. Moreover, it falls to the ordained to handle the sacred vessels, but this is not permitted to women, as stated at dist. 22 cap. sacratas; and a woman is not to be ordained.

### C

Response. Something can impede reception of orders either because its opposite is required by the necessity of the sacrament, or by the authority of a precept, or by the fittingness and propriety of the one receiving. In the first way, the female sex impedes reception of orders, because the male sex is required by the necessity of the sacrament, the principal cause of which is the institution of Christ, whose prerogative it was to institute the sacraments both with respect to those who administer them and those who receive them. Now Christ ordained only men — at the Last Supper, when he conferred on them the power of consecrating, and after the Resurrection, when he gave them the Holy Spirit, saying “Whose sins you shall forgive,” etc. — nor did he promote his own mother (although she was the holiest of all women) to any grade of orders. The Apostle also, who handed down to us what he received from the Lord (as stated at 1 Corinthians 11), indicates that women ought not to be ordained, nor to hold in the Church the grade of teaching that pertains to the ordained, as was touched on in the arguments above. Nor can it be said that this proceeds merely from the arrangement of the Apostles and not from the institution of Christ, because to take away from someone a dignity that is beneficial and was granted by Christ himself cannot be without prejudice — just as it would be prejudicial in temporal affairs. But the dignity of orders is beneficial for the advancement of salvation in those who use it rightly; therefore, if it had been granted by Christ that women could be ordained, this could not be taken from them without prejudice. It must be held, therefore, that women cannot be ordained by the institution of Christ. The argument from fittingness, moreover, is this: through orders a person is placed in a grade of preeminence over others who are not ordained; but such a grade does not befit women in relation to men — rather, a state of subjection is fitting for them, on account of the weakness of the body and the imperfection of reason. Therefore, \[they cannot be ordained\]. Thus the female sex impedes reception of orders because its opposite is required by the necessity of the sacrament. Youthful age, however (as some say), impedes reception of the major orders because its opposite is required by the necessity of a precept, but does not impede reception of the minor orders except by reason of fittingness alone. The reason for the first is that to the major orders is annexed the vow of continence by statute of the Church, but no one can bind himself to this who does not have the use of reason and discretion; therefore, according to the statute of the Church, no one ought to be promoted to the major orders before the years of discretion.
