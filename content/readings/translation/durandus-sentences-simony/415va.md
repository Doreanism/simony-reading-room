And therefore, if the work of hearing confessions, administering sacraments, and performing other spiritual acts cannot fall under sale without the vice of simony, then neither can the labor involved in such work be sold without simony. To say otherwise is ridiculous, and such piety is merely a cloak of excuse for simoniacs, who can say: “We do not sell the sacraments, but only the labor we expend in baptizing, celebrating Masses, presiding, and administering the other sacraments.” If, therefore, an ecclesiastical minister lawfully receives anything in dispensing the sacraments, he receives it not as a price for the work or the labor, but as sustenance for the minister. And the same must be said of the offices that pertain to sacraments already received, as will be clear in the resolution of the arguments. Now among goods that are called spiritual from the created spirit, some are spiritual in their essence — such as knowledge and acquired virtues. These likewise cannot in any way be sold, for the same reason given above concerning grace and things spiritual in their essence. Others, however, are spiritual by attribution — such as teaching, giving counsel, and bearing witness — and selling such things is not simoniacal. Sometimes, however, it constitutes another kind of sin, and sometimes no sin at all. For when someone is to some extent bound to perform such services, selling them is unjust. For this reason, since a judge is bound by his office to render judgment and a witness, being subject to the jurisdiction of the court, is bound to testify, a judge cannot sell his judgment without injustice, nor can a witness sell his testimony — although either may seek reimbursement for expenses incurred in traveling to and from the place of judgment. From this it follows that, since the seal of a judge is nothing other than an attestation of his judgment, I do not see how a judge may demand anything for his seal, except perhaps the expenses of those who are deputed to this task — namely, the clerks who write and affix the seal.

When, however, someone is not bound by office to perform such services, selling them is not illicit. Hence a jurist may lawfully sell his counsel, and an advocate his representation.

### To the first

To the first argument it must be said that the kingdom of heaven is not properly sold, since God receives nothing from us; rather, we merit it by good works — which meriting Gregorius calls “purchasing,” but improperly. By the same reasoning the answer is clear to the text cited from Daniel 4: “Redeem your sins with alms.”

### To the second

To the second it must be said that, having promised an anniversary prayer — whether public or private — it is not lawful to accept anything temporal as a price, but only as something ordered to the sustenance of the minister. Hence, on the text of \[1\] Timothy, “Let the elders who rule well \[be considered worthy of double honor\],” the gloss of Augustinus says: “They receive the necessities of sustenance from the people, but the reward of their dispensation from the Lord.”

### To the third

To the third it must be said that gifts were offered to the good prophets more for their sustenance — so that they might freely devote themselves to spiritual matters — than as payment for prophetic gifts, as Hieronymus says on Micah. If the prophets received such things otherwise, namely as payment for their prophecy, they sinned. Likewise, the work of preaching can be performed for money — not as a price, but as the object about which the preaching is concerned. For one can preach so that money is given for the construction of a church or for the sustenance of the poor, as with other works of the Church; it is also lawful to accept money for the sustenance of preachers. By the same reasoning the answer is clear to the fourth \[argument\], which argues in a similar way about visitation and correction: because the procuration that is received is not the price of correction or visitation, but the sustenance of the minister. Likewise, when someone is absolved from excommunication, the money received there is not received as the price of absolution, but as a penalty for the offender — in which case, however, one must be very careful to avoid any appearance of greed.
