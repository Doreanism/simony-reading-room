The second part begins at “Concerning Simoniacs.” \[This\] part \[has\] three \[subdivisions\]. First he puts forward the first question and sets out an authority on one side, namely that heretics cannot confer the sacrament of orders. Second he sets out authorities on the contrary side. Third he determines \[the question\] according to the solutions of various \[doctors\]. The second \[subdivision begins\] at “Others, however, on the contrary.” The third at “But here, a certain one determines.” The second principal part, in which he inquires concerning simoniacs, \[he\] divides into three parts. First he shows who are simoniacs. Second, how they can ordain. Third, he sets forth a certain distinction of simoniacal wickedness. The second \[part begins\] at “There is, however, a difference between them.” The third at “Simoniacal heresy, moreover.”

### In Particular

First he speaks and treats first of whether heretics cut off from the Church can confer holy orders, and whether those ordained by them ought to be reordained; and he says that this question is difficult on account of the diversity and contrariety of the doctors regarding it. For some say that heretics cannot ordain, and that those ordained by them do not receive grace; for Innocentius says that they have only the right to baptize, because they cannot give the Holy Spirit, and that those ordained by them do not receive grace and ought not to be received in their orders — though not \[to be excluded from\] lay communion. And Gregorius says that the \[ordinations\] of the Arians are a sacrilegious execration. Cyprianus also says that the sacraments of heretics are empty and sacrilegious. Hieronymus also says that what is offered by heretics is defiled and odious to God. Leo also says that \[sacraments performed\] outside the Church are neither valid nor true sacrifices.

Thereafter he sets out the contrary opinion of those who say that orders can be transmitted by heretics and that those ordained by them need not be reordained; which Augustinus above all appears to maintain, saying that just as they have the right to baptize, so also to ordain — but heretics \[do so\] to their own perdition, Catholics truly unto salvation. Gregorius also says that one who has been ordained once ought never to be reordained.

Thereafter, concerning the point of uncertainty, he touches on four opinions, of which the second and third coincide. The first opinion is that of those who say that heretics, before they have been cut off by sentence of the Church, can ordain, and those ordained by them cannot be reordained; but after they have been manifestly cut off by sentence of the Church, they can no longer ordain nor consecrate the Body of the Lord. And to the authorities of Augustinus they respond that these must be understood as speaking of heretics not yet manifestly cut off by sentence of the Church, and not of others. The second opinion is that of those who say that all sacraments celebrated by heretics even manifestly cut off, \[so long as they are celebrated\] according to the form of the Church, are true and valid; those celebrated by heretics outside the form of the Church are neither true nor valid. And from this the third opinion extends further, saying that sacraments celebrated by heretics in the form of the Church are true and valid in themselves, but as regards their effect they are false and empty in those who administer them wrongly and in those who receive them wrongly. The fourth opinion is that of those who say that heretics who were ordained within the Church retain the right to ordain and consecrate after they have departed from the Church, but those who were ordained while already in heresy lack this right.

Thereafter he treats of simoniacs and says there is no doubt that they are heretics, even if they ordain and consecrate before the sentence of degradation. Simoniacs, however, though properly speaking those are called simoniacs who wish to buy spiritual things — like Simon \[Magus\] — and Gehazites those who wish to sell them — like Gehazi — yet by the common name both these and those are called simoniacs. There is moreover a difference between those who receive ordination from simoniacs: for those who knowingly receive orders from them have their ordination entirely annulled as regards the exercise of orders; but those who do not know that their ordainers are simoniacs, their ordination is mercifully tolerated. Simoniacs, moreover, are not only those who buy consecrations but also those who buy the things that derive from consecrations.
