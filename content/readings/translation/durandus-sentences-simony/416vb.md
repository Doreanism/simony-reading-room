On account of this prohibition, it is judged simoniacal unless it is done with the authority of a superior, whose role it is to judge lest anyone transfer from one church to another out of desire for greater profit rather than with the intention of being more useful.

### Conclusion

The conclusion also is not true, because the sale of an ecclesiastical benefice is simoniacal of its very nature, as will be shown below, and is prohibited by divine law in the Gospel of Matthew 10 — not indeed in itself but in something similar to it. For the authority to elect a bishop and to serve a church is no less spiritual than the authority to preach. But in a simple canonry there is the authority to elect a bishop and to serve a church. Since, therefore, preaching is prohibited from being sold (Matthew 10), similarly — indeed, by an even stronger argument — the authority to elect a bishop or to serve a church, which resides in a simple canonry, is prohibited from being sold.

### What must be said

What must be said, therefore, is that to confer a simple canonry for money is simoniacal of its very nature and not merely from a prohibition of law. This is shown as follows. In a simple canonry there are two things: namely, the office to which one is deputed, and the temporal benefice that is received from it. For the benefice is given on account of the office, for the sustenance of the minister.

Now, this benefice is nothing other than a certain temporal emolument given by the founders of churches and other devout persons to a place and an office for the sustenance of the minister who is to serve in that place. To appoint a minister for such an office and for such a place pertains to the prelate, whose role it is to judge the suitability of the minister. But that the one appointed receives the benefice is not from the gift of the prelate, who has no right in the benefice in this regard, but from the gift of the founders, who gave such a benefice to the minister who would serve in such a place and in such an office. And this is sufficiently suggested in the authority concerning “the most holy bishops and clerics”: “Si quis oratorium donationis collatione,” IX, where it is expressly stated that the founders of churches must supply the expenses for the clergy, but the clergy must be appointed by the bishop, and once appointed must render service to their churches and fulfill ecclesiastical ministry. From this the argument proceeds as follows: to give a spiritual thing for a temporal price is simoniacal of its very nature and contrary to natural and divine law, as is clear from the preceding question. But to sell a simple canonry is to give directly a spiritual thing for a temporal price. Therefore it is simoniacal of its very nature and contrary to natural and divine law; and from this no one can be excused from sin. Therefore, etc. The minor is clear from what has been said: because the prelate does not give any benefice except insofar as he appoints a minister to a place and an office. But the appointment of a minister to divine office is purely spiritual, as we are now speaking of “spiritual” — that which de facto can be sold. Therefore, etc.
