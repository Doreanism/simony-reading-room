But the sacrament of orders derives its efficacy ex opere operato; therefore wickedness of life and defect of personal faith, which pertain to the unworthiness of the minister, do not prevent a bishop from being, by virtue of his consecration, the minister of all orders (as has been shown), and from conferring valid orders, provided he observes the proper form and matter that are required for the necessity of the sacrament. Moreover, for the actual exercise of an act, a habit — which merely determines the mode — is never strictly necessary; what suffices is the power by which anyone can act, as the very name “power” signifies. But grace and faith, through which one lives a good life, are certain habits; therefore they are not required in order to be able to confer orders, but only in order that orders be conferred licitly and rightly. Second, the same minor is evident with respect to the remaining points — namely that an excommunicated or degraded bishop can confer all orders — because if he could not, it would be either because he had been deprived of the power itself, or only of the exercise of right but not of the exercise of fact. It cannot be said that he is deprived of the power, because what is given through consecration always remains as long as the consecrated reality remains; but the power of conferring orders is given to a bishop in his consecration, and therefore always remains and cannot be lost. Nor is he deprived of the exercise of fact, because the exercise of fact corresponds to power — for power is that by which we can act — whereas the exercise of right, beyond power, requires lawful use. Since, therefore, through excommunication and degradation a bishop is not deprived of the power to confer orders, he is consequently not deprived of the exercise of fact; he is, however, deprived of the exercise of right, because he confers orders illicitly and to his own damnation and to that of one who knowingly receives them. This is expressly determined in the text by Augustinus, as found at prima questione prima capitulo quis quidem.

### Second Article.

The second \[point\] is evident — namely that only a bishop can confer orders, at least the major orders — because it is no lesser thing to place commanders over an army than to enroll soldiers; but it belongs to the bishop alone to enroll soldiers in the army of the Church Militant through the sacrament of confirmation, as was shown above in Distinction 7. Therefore it belongs all the more to the bishop alone to place commanders in the Church, which is done through the sacrament of orders, especially with regard to the major orders.

To the first argument it must be said that a bishop, however wicked, heretical, or degraded, remains joined to Christ as principal agent in the administration of the sacraments by reason of the ministerial power he received in his consecration, and by reason of the faith of the Church, provided he intends to do what the Church does — even if he does not believe that it is valid.

To the second it must be said that the case of ordination and the case of absolution from sins and from sentences are not alike, because the ability to ordain another belongs to a bishop by reason of his episcopal consecration, which is not and cannot be lost through any fault or sentence. For this reason a heretical bishop, even one cut off from the Church, can ordain another. But the ability to absolve from sins and from sentences belongs to a bishop by reason of pastoral charge or jurisdiction, which can be taken away by a superior, because the universal care of the Church and jurisdiction over all Christians was entrusted to Peter alone, for himself and for his successors. From them it is derived to others, and by them it is taken away, as the pope — the successor of Peter — judges expedient for the Church to ordain. By his arrangement, a heretic and one cut off from the Church to whatever degree is deprived of pastoral charge and jurisdiction, and therefore cannot \[absolve\] from sins or from sentences.
