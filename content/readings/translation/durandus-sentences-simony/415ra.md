## Question III

Next we inquire about simony, which is a vice that sometimes arises in the conferral of orders. And first, whether it is lawful to sell or buy spiritual things for a temporal price. It seems that it is, because nothing is more spiritual than the kingdom of heaven and the remission of sins; but these can lawfully be purchased for a temporal price. For Gregorius says in a Homily, “The kingdom of heaven is worth as much as you have.” And in Daniel 4 it is said, “Redeem your sins with alms.” Therefore, all the more so, other spiritual things can lawfully be purchased. Furthermore, the entire ecclesiastical office is something spiritual — especially the Mass, and then public and private prayers — but temporal goods are lawfully received for these: for revenues are assigned for Masses to be said in chapels, for anniversaries to be celebrated, and alms are given to good men for private prayers; therefore, etc. Furthermore, prophesying, preaching, visiting, and correcting are spiritual acts; but temporal goods are lawfully received for these; therefore, etc. Proof of the minor premise: temporal goods were given to the prophets, as is clear from 2 Kings 3 \[i.e., 2 Samuel 3\]. Likewise, it is lawful for preachers to receive stipends, as is clear from 1 Corinthians 9. And prelates who correct and visit also receive procurations; therefore, etc.

### To the contrary

To the contrary is what Gregorius says in the Register, which is cited in the decree of Paschalis \[I, q. 3, cap. altare\], where he says: “No one among the faithful is unaware that to give an altar, tithes, and money, and to sell the Holy Spirit, is simony and heresy.”

### Response

It must be said that something can be called spiritual either from the uncreated Holy Spirit or from the created holy spirit \[i.e., the spiritual soul\]. If from the uncreated Spirit, this can be in two ways: either it is spiritual by essence — such as grace and the other gifts of the Holy Spirit — or by attribution, such as the sacraments, which are spiritual through a sine qua non cause, along with their antecedents, concomitants, and consequents. What is spiritual by essence can in no way be sold, neither in law nor in fact. The reason for this is that in every sale the thing sold can be transferred from the seller to the buyer, either in fact or in law; but grace and the other gifts that are spiritual by essence cannot be transferred to another, neither in law nor in fact; therefore they cannot be sold in any way — on account of which simony is always committed in the sale of such things. The sacraments, however, which are spiritual by cause, can indeed be sold in fact, but never in law nor lawfully. The reason for this is threefold, drawn from the three things present in a sale to which the thing sold has a relation: namely, the price, the seller, and the sale itself. First, the proposition is evident from the relation of the thing sold to the price, as follows: that sale is in itself illicit in which the thing sold cannot be compensated by the price (for it belongs to the nature of buying and selling that the thing bought or sold be compensated by the price); but a spiritual thing cannot be compensated by any earthly or temporal price, according to Proverbs 3, concerning wisdom (and with even stronger reason this holds true of grace and spiritual things pertaining to grace): “She is more precious than all riches, and all the things that are desired cannot be compared to her.” Therefore the sale of any spiritual thing for any temporal thing is in itself illicit.
