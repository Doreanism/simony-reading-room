As for the further claim that in sacred Scripture nothing is found to be condemned as simoniacal except the sale of the blessing of the sacraments and the working of miracles, this is false. In Matthew 10 the selling of all spiritual acts is prohibited, to some of which even a simple canonry deputes \[its holder\]. In the Old Law as well it is condemned that anyone should become a minister through money, as is found in 1 Kings 13 \[3 Kings 13 in the Vulgate, i.e., 1 Kings 13 in modern Bibles\], that whoever wished would fill his hand and become a priest of the high places.

### To the second

it must be said that the case of the right of patronage is not analogous to the conferral of benefices. For when the right of patronage passes along with the sale of a manor or a castle, it is not sold directly and primarily, because the sale does not bear upon it but upon the manor or castle. Nor is it sold even secondarily, because the manor or castle does not sell for more on account of the annexed right of patronage — otherwise simony would be involved. But if someone were to sell an ecclesiastical benefice, the sale would bear directly upon that which is spiritual, because a prelate has no other way of giving a benefice except by installing a minister in an office. And therefore if anything were sold, it would primarily be sold by reason of this \[spiritual element\], whatever temporal thing might be annexed. And so the sale would bear directly upon that which is spiritual, which cannot be done without the vice of simony.

### To the third

the answer is clear from the same principle. For the accessories of the sacraments — such as sacred vessels — may be sold licitly, provided they are not sold for more by reason of their consecration, because the sale bears directly upon that which is corporeal. But the office consequent upon a sacrament is spiritual in itself, and therefore it cannot be sold, nor \[can it be sold\] by reason of an annexed temporal benefice that follows from it. Since \[the benefice\] is not transferred except by first transferring the office, it is clear that the sale must primarily bear upon the office, which is spiritual. And through this the answer to the subsequent confirmation is also clear: for when something is primarily corporeal and \[also\] primarily spiritual, it can in some manner be sold by reason of the corporeal element, when the seller can directly and of himself transfer that corporeal thing to another without the mediation of the spiritual element — which is not the case in the matter at hand.

### To the fourth

it must be said that the emperor, in selling a magistracy, although he does not sin against the Lex Julia \[the Roman law against electoral corruption\], nevertheless sins against the natural law, to which he is subject. Similarly, the pope, if he sells any ecclesiastical office whatsoever, although he does not sin against canon law, to which he is not subject, nevertheless sins against divine law, to which he is subject.
