This is in accordance with the explanation given above in the first question, along with the fruits that were received. Nor is one released \[from this obligation\] by whatever accident caused one to lose use of the thing — whether one sold it, consumed it, alienated it, whether the thing perished, or whether one lost it — because one was in default of restitution. It would be different if one had offered it to the owner at the proper place and time, and the owner had then refused to receive it: if it had subsequently perished, one would be excused. He says “to the owner of the thing.” This is necessary when the thing was given to one gratuitously, because then one does not satisfy the obligation by restoring it to the one who gave it, since the thing was not that person’s to give. But if one received it by a contract of purchase or exchange — since, according to Alexander \[of Hales\] in the same passage, one may more readily look to one’s own interest than to another’s — one may restore it to the person with whom one contracted, upon receiving back the price one paid. From the owner of the thing, however, one can demand nothing, neither in the court of conscience nor in the external court, however much one paid for it in the public market. Therefore, let one learn to trade more cautiously. \[This holds\] notwithstanding any contrary custom or statute. And this is true if one bought it from a place of safekeeping. But if one bought it from a place where it was to be returned to its owner — because otherwise the owner of the thing could not recover it, or only at a much reduced price — one could then seek from the owner the price at which it was bought, because one has usefully managed the owner’s affairs. If, however, one derived profit from the thing thus purchased, one is then obliged to offset the fruit of the thing against the expenses and purchase price. And if the thing thus purchased were to perish without one’s fault, or if one were to lose it, one would not be bound in the court of conscience — although in the contentious court one would not be believed, unless it had been publicly established beforehand that one bought it with that intention. All these matters are in the Directorium iuris at the passage cited. He says “to the owner of the thing if one knows him”: because if one does not know who the owner of the thing is, yet knows that it was unjustly acquired, or cannot find the owner after diligent inquiry, then one is obliged to make restitution to the poor, as in the case of uncertain restitution, together with the fruits thereof. He says “notwithstanding any human statute,” as for instance if there were a statute declaring that whoever publicly purchased something in such a public market would not be obligated \[to make restitution\] — for such a statute is iniquitous, since the restitution of another’s property, when one knows it to be such, is a matter of natural and divine law. This is the view of Alexander of Hales, Part 4, q. 86. One who retains it sins mortally, and therefore no contrary statute avails. He says “until \[satisfaction has been made\] to the owner,” and so on, because one is always bound to make restitution until the owner of the thing has been satisfied — whether one sells the thing or gives it away in the former case, while in the latter the second party restores it to his vendor. But once the owner has been fully satisfied, or the owner has voluntarily remitted the debt, one is released from the obligation of making restitution to the owner — though not from the obligation of restitution in respect of the price at which one sold under compulsion, which price or profit is not to be restored to the buyer. For one’s own turpitude stands in the way, but \[it must be given\] to the poor, because one was a possessor in bad faith, according to Raymund. But Hostiensis understands this as a counsel: that the taint \[of bad faith\] is purged as regards restitution, because full satisfaction has been made.

## Proposition 2.

The second proposition: one who receives another’s property in good faith, not knowing nor believing it to have been wrongly acquired — whether by gift or by contract — is not obliged to restore it, provided the ignorance was not gross. Nor \[is one obliged to restore\] fruits received during the period of good faith. This is the opinion of Hostiensis in the Summa, Book 4, on penance and restitution, and others. He says “in good faith,” which is to say, one who does not know nor believe the things to have been wrongly obtained, but presumes they are not another’s — as Panormitanus \[says\] on c. suis de rechi.
