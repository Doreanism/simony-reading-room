And just as a wife may in some necessity spend on physicians of the body even without the husband’s consent, so also in necessity of the soul. It has also been said that “outside the case of necessity” is to be understood whenever it is said — not only of the wife’s necessity, but also of the husband’s, of the children’s, of the household’s, indeed of strangers, who in extreme necessity could and ought to take — that is, to take another’s property and relieve the one in need — provided there is no other who can and will assist. So also, if the husband were bound to make restitution for wrongfully taken goods and could not be induced or compelled to make restitution, the wife could prudently and cautiously make restitution from the husband’s goods, because in this she would be usefully conducting the husband’s affairs, providing for his soul’s welfare, just as she might provide for his body in illness through physicians.

## Fourth Proposition

Proposition 4. A husband who alienates the wife’s dowry or the paternal donation made on account of marriage is bound to restore whatever was alienated. Proof: because the dowry is the wife’s primary patrimony, from which the husband is bound to support the wife and the burdens of marriage. When the marriage is dissolved, the husband or his heirs are bound to restore the dowry. However, the husband gains the fruits of the dotal property on account of the burdens of marriage. Wherefore the offspring of dotal cattle belong to the husband, since they are reckoned as fruit; yet in such a way that he must replenish with the newborn offspring \[the heads of cattle\] that have died. But he who truly gains the fruits must sustain the burdens of marriage; otherwise not. If one asks to whom the dowry is to be restored: he answers that if the dowry was a *dos profecticia* — that is, given from goods provided by the father or grandfather or accepted in their name — it is returned to the father if the wife dies first and left no children; otherwise by custom it remains with the children. But if the husband dies first, it must be returned to the father of the daughter; yet the father cannot demand it without the daughter’s consent. But if the dowry was an *adventicia* — that is, given by the wife herself or by anyone other than the father and grandfather — if the wife dies first, the dowry belongs to her heirs. If the husband dies first, it returns to the wife. And if the dowry consists of immovable property, it must be restored immediately upon dissolution of the marriage. If it consists of movables, it must be restored within a year by the husband, to the extent he is able, having regard that he not be reduced to want.
