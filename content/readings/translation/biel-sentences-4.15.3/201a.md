A possessor in bad faith is one who possesses knowing that the thing belongs to another. By contrast, a possessor in good faith is one who, not knowing that he possesses what belongs to another, holds it with the mind of an owner. He says “whether by gift” — that is, by free receipt — “or by purchase.” He says “provided the ignorance was not gross or affected” — as for instance because it is commonly held that all those things were plundered, or taken in an unjust war, or because the bishop excommunicated as robbers those from whom he received them, or because he bought a chalice or missal or the like from a mercenary soldier. For such a person is bound to make restitution, just as the one described in the first proposition. And note that ignorance of fact, not of law, excuses. For if the recipient knows with probability that the thing was plundered, and does not know that the law forbids buying such things from such a person — since this prohibition derives from natural law — ignorance of it does not excuse. He says “nor \[is one obliged to restore\] fruits received,” because fruits received during the period of good faith need not be restored, nor the price of the thing if it was sold or exchanged, nor the thing itself if it perished, or if one lost it or consumed it. Some make distinctions on these points: during the period of good faith one is not obliged to restore; but once good faith ceases — namely, from the moment one learned the things were wrongly acquired — one begins to be a possessor in bad faith, and is then obliged to restore the thing itself if it still exists, or the gain if one has been enriched by it. One may deduct the expenses incurred in the thing while improving or preserving it during the period of good faith, but not those incurred after good faith ceased, according to the rule given above in the preceding question. One may also, if one bought in good faith, seek the purchase price from the vendor in the contentious court. If the judge thus found the thing was to be restored, \[the buyer\] would not restore it at the sentence of an impious priest or of a judge without coercive authority, though in the court of conscience the vendor would be bound to make restitution — he would have to be induced to do so, yet could not be compelled in the contentious court, unless he had expressly warranted at the time of sale against subsequent claims. It is advisable in such a case that the buyer have himself cited in court by the owner of the thing, and when cited, inform the vendor to defend him, so that the vendor will be bound in the court of law to hold him harmless.

## Third Proposition

One who receives another’s property under probable doubt, and does not omit diligent inquiry into the matter, is bound to make restitution of it. The summary of \[Post.\] at the cited passage establishes that good faith alone excuses one from restoring another’s property. But one who doubts with probability is not a possessor in good faith. Therefore \[one is bound to restore\]. Likewise, ignorance that does not arise from diligent inquiry — gross ignorance — does not excuse, just as it does not excuse from mortal sin, so neither does it excuse from restitution. It does not excuse from mortal sin, because in a state of probable doubt one exposes oneself to the risk of mortal sin; and one who receives another’s property \[in such a state\] sins mortally. According to that \[text\]: “He who loves danger shall perish in it” (Eccl. 5). Nor does it excuse from restitution, as has been said, because a possessor in bad faith is bound to make restitution in the manner of a possessor in bad faith \[as described\] above. He says “in probable doubt,” that is, when one has reason to doubt from probable signs giving rise to a probable apparent suspicion or presumption — signs from which the wise commonly judge the thing to be another’s, by a judgment not of certainty but of presumption. For in such doubt one is closer to the knowing than to the unknowing.
