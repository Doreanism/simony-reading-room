## As to the Third Article, the First Doubt Against the First Conclusion.

## Article 3.

## Doubt 1.

Against the first conclusion: one who steals out of piety in order to give alms is not bound to restitution. Therefore the conclusion is false. The antecedent is proved thus: no one is bound to restore a thing he holds without sin. But one who steals in this way does not sin, which is proved as follows: he would sin especially against the precepts of the second table — “You shall not steal” — not in this case, because all sins against the precepts of the second table are against charity toward one’s neighbor, just as sins against the precepts of the first table are against charity toward God. But theft, especially in the case under argument, is not against charity toward one’s neighbor, for it is consistent with it. For charity toward one’s neighbor is to will for him the love of God, eternal goods, and whatever else promotes beatitude, as was established in Book 3, dist. 28. But one could will this even while taking something from him — especially what is not necessary for his salvation, and especially if it is an obstacle to him, as are superfluous exterior goods, which he is bound to distribute to the needy, as Luke 11 says: “Give what remains as alms.” Those things, then, I can be unwilling to \[allow him to keep\] precisely out of charity. To this objection the reply is that it is wrong to steal from a neighbor, even one who is abundantly wealthy, in order to give alms, except in a case of extreme necessity on the part of the neighbor. But in that case the rationale of theft ceases. For in such a case one may relieve the neighbor’s necessity from the rich man’s goods when one cannot do so from one’s own — as \[Thomas\] himself \[says\] in that place. And for this Augustinus in a homily \[says\]: “Perhaps someone thinks and says: ‘There are many rich, greedy, covetous Christians; I shall not sin if I take from them and give to the poor. For they do no good; they will receive \[their\] reward.’ But a thought of this kind is suggested by the cunning of the devil. For even if one distributes to the poor everything one has stolen, one adds to sin rather than diminishes it.” So Augustinus, 14, q. 5, c. *forte*. For we must not do evil that good may come of it (Rom. 3). To steal is evil, because it is forbidden.

To the argument it is replied that every sin is a sin insofar as it is against charity toward God — as it were a \[kind of\] sin, according to Augustinus; Ambrosius holds that \[sin\] consists in the transgression of the divine law and is contrary to God’s charity, whence what is committed is in the first place a sin because it is forbidden by God, and charitable love of neighbor includes love of God, whence a neighbor cannot be loved charitably without loving God. Nor does anyone who does not keep God’s commandments love God. It is therefore conceded that one who sins against the precepts of the second table sins against charity toward one’s neighbor, but nonetheless even more against charity toward God. And when it is argued that theft is consistent with charity toward one’s neighbor, this is denied. Consequently it is said that to love a neighbor charitably is to will for him God’s love and beatitude. Certain goods God first wills us to will, and all evil God first wills us to refuse. And through this, whoever wills temporal evils for his neighbor, even those not contrary to salvation, does not love the neighbor. So it does not hold
