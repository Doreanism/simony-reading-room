That he is also not bound to restitution is clear, because no one is bound to make restitution of a thing owed to him to one to whom he does not owe it, but is rather bound to restore it to the prior \[rightful holder\]. For it would be futile to restore what ought to be restored to oneself.

That he does sin in certain cases by such receiving will be explained. It is said, \[taking\] what is properly his own, as when he finds a thing unjustly taken from him in the possession of some detainer, whether that detainer took it or it came to him in some other way. It is said, or what is owed to him, as \[goods\] received through usury, extorted through fraud, received through a loan, owed for labor or service, or in some other way, such that if he had sought that thing by lawful judgment and had not been deficient in proof, it would have been adjudged to him -- of all such things the proposition speaks. It is said, he takes, which is to be understood as applying even with a doubtful mind. He says whether secretly or violently and openly.

Not bound to restitution: this is to be understood in the forum of conscience; it is otherwise in the external forum. For in that forum he would lose the thing itself unless he recovered it immediately, before the taker brought it to a safe place. For it is lawful to repel force with force. He would also be condemned for theft or robbery if the proofs by which he might establish the thing as his own were lacking. Likewise regarding usury, a loan, and wages, if he recovered what was owed by his own authority. It is said, he would not be excused from sin.

Not always is one bound to restitution on account of what one acquires with sin, as is evident. It is said, in certain cases. The first is when he could obtain what is his by means of a judge pronouncing sentence, because no one ought to be judge in his own cause. If, however, he could not obtain it juridically due to the negligence of the judge, the power of the detainer, or the lack of witnesses or proof, and could not recover it by petitioning the detainer, but it was certain that the thing was owed to him and he did not exceed what was owed him by receiving it, he would be excused from sin. ## Corollary

One who secretly receives a deposit, or what he had lent, without requiring it of the one with whom it was deposited or who received the loan, sins, because he burdens the depositary, who is bound to make restitution or to show that he is blameless. So also regarding the debtor. He also sins against common justice by rendering judgment for himself, and is bound to relieve the burden of the depositary -- not to restore the thing -- as Thomas says in *Summa Theologiae* II-II, q. 66, art. 5. And because good faith does not permit the same person to make restitution twice, it would nonetheless be safer -- as Antoninus adds -- \[to consider\]: What if several persons participate simultaneously in theft or robbery? In that case it must be considered that it would be right for the one who secretly recovers his own loss to declare to the one who caused the loss, the thief or robber, or to their heirs, by some honorable means, or to give him something if he owes him anything. Better yet, moved by conscience, let him first restore the wrongly taken \[goods\] before secretly recovering \[his own\]. So says Antoninus. The second case is when from such receipt scandal to oneself or one’s neighbor would follow, or grave danger to an innocent person in whose custody the goods may perhaps have been entrusted. For no one ought to enrich himself with peril to himself or loss to another.
