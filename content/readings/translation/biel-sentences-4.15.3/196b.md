## Having Set Forth These Things, a Fuller Response Is Now Given

## First Proposition

If a wife has taken something from the husband’s own property by dispensing it \[without his knowledge\], she is bound to make restitution to the husband, unless the extreme necessity of her children or household excuses her, because the wife has no faculty of dispensing with the husband’s own property. Much less does a husband \[have such a faculty\] with the wife’s property. From this it follows that from these goods the wife cannot give alms, nor convert them to pious uses. He says “unless extreme necessity excuses” for the same reason as was stated above concerning extreme necessity.

## Second Proposition

A wife who alienates or disposes of her paraphernal goods or earnings without the husband’s knowledge, even against his prohibition, is not bound to make restitution, unless the husband’s remaining goods could not suffice for the support of the children in the marriage. This is proved because, with respect to her own goods, the wife is not subject to the husband’s authority, as has been said, and she is her own mistress in disposing of them. This accords sufficiently with S. Thomas, \[Secunda\] Secundae, q. 32, art. 8. Likewise, as long as the dowry suffices for sustenance, the wife is not bound to labor for profit. Therefore, if she labors of her own accord, the profit will be hers, as has been said. But she may dispose of what is her own as she wishes, unless she were prohibited by some law, which does not appear in the present case. And he says “unless the remaining, etc.,” because in this case she is bound to labor for the upbringing of the children of the marriage by natural law, and therefore she cannot expend the earnings on other uses.

## Third Proposition

If a wife secretly retains or disburses something from common goods contrary to the husband’s expressed or presumed will, outside a case of necessity, she is bound to make restitution to the husband, unless she is usefully conducting the husband’s affairs. This is proved because the disposition of common goods belongs to the husband as head and principal governor of the household, and therefore she has no faculty of dispensing with them contrary to the husband’s will, outside the cases designated in the proposition. He says first “from common goods,” beyond what was said in the second proposition. He says “without the husband’s consent,” because with the husband’s consenting she may dispose of common goods. He says “or presumed \[will\],” because if from local custom or other signs his consent can be probably presumed, she may disburse and is not bound to make restitution. For example, if local custom holds that wives give alms of bread, wine, and the like, she may presume to give without asking the husband, because custom grants \[such\] jurisdiction. And although husbands sometimes forbid it, Angelus says in the Summa under the word “alms” that a wife ought to form her conscience according to Rodon., \[since husbands\] do this so that she not give too much, but \[give\] in moderation. And the same is to be understood in its manner of other things customarily done by wives.
