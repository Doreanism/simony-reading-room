It says “movable,” because in immovable property, such as a field or a house, theft is not committed, but rather despoilment or rapine.

It says “corporeal,” because in an incorporeal thing, such as an action or a servitude, theft is not committed, since such things cannot be handled.

It says “fraudulent,” because if someone believed that a thing was his own when it was not, or even if he knew it was not his, yet believed the owner would not be unwilling and had reasonable grounds for so believing, he does not commit theft. For in case of doubt, the owner is presumed to be unwilling.

It says “without the owner’s consent,” which is to be understood both of the proximate owner, that is the immediate superior, and of the remote owner; for if the superior owner were not unwilling, there would be neither theft nor rapine. This is evident in the despoiling of the Egyptians by the children of Israel, Exodus \[12\]. It is therefore required that both be unwilling. For if either one consents, there is no theft. Hence simony or usury is not properly called theft, because when the owner of the thing is willing and his will is known, the one receiving the thing does not commit theft. If, however, he believed the owner to be unwilling, he does commit theft in this manner with respect to intention, though he is not thereby bound to restitution. And the owner is said to be unwilling even when he sees the thing being taken and does not object, out of fear or shame. Otherwise, by not objecting when he could have, he is deemed to consent.

It says “for the sake of gaining,” because if one were to take a thing not with the intention of gaining it, but with the intention of safeguarding it, or of making the owner more cautious, or for some similar reason, it would not be theft.

It says “the thing itself, its use, or its possession,” because it makes no difference whether the one taking another’s thing intends to appropriate the thing itself, or only the use of the thing, or its possession. For whatever of these he intends, he commits theft. And if one wishes to distinguish theft more clearly from rapine, “secretly” should be added, as Saint Thomas adds---though the word “fraudulently” already sufficiently distinguishes them, if less explicitly. This will be discussed immediately below. It is also said “without the owner’s consent”: this is to be understood as referring not to an illicit act but to a lawful act, as was touched upon in the preceding question.

## Second Note

\[It is to be noted\] that in general there are two kinds of theft: manifest and non-manifest. Manifest theft is when the thief is caught before he arrives at his intended destination. Non-manifest theft is when the thief is not caught. There are also certain thefts that receive special names, such as sacrilege, which is the theft of a sacred thing, or of a non-sacred thing from a sacred place. Hence “sacrilege” is said to be quasi “sacri laedium,” or quasi the violation of the sacred law. Sacrilege is committed either against a person, as when an ecclesiastical person is harmed; or against a place, as when the immunity of a church or cemetery is violated; or against a thing, as when some sacred thing is taken from a sacred place, or a sacred thing from a non-sacred place, or a non-sacred thing from a sacred place; as is held in the Gloss on “sacrilegium,” 14 q. 4; and in the chapter Si quis contumax, in the third causa, q. \[2\].
