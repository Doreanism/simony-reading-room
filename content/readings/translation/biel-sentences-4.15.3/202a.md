The second case is when one usefully labors in connection with the plunderer’s goods or the spoils themselves — by preserving or increasing them — because this redounds to the benefit of the creditors, that is, those to whom restitution must be made, provided the benefit is equal to the expenditure.

## Sixth Doubt: Whether those who seize things from a shipwreck are bound to make restitution?

He answers briefly that things thrown overboard from ships, or left aboard a wrecked vessel, are reckoned as abandoned by those who had them, and thus belong to whoever takes them — because what is abandoned is in no one’s possession, and the taker thereby acquires it. When, however, something is not reckoned as abandoned, what follows is stated in the next doubt. When it is not reckoned as abandoned: Hostiensis says, in the Summa, tract. on restitution, tit. on penance and restitution: “Whatever is found from a shipwreck itself, whether received at sea or on shore, must be restored, because it is another’s property.” It must therefore be restored to the finder. Furthermore, one who takes such things, appropriating them to oneself, sins mortally, commits theft, and acts against fraternal charity. Nor does any statute or custom enacted to the contrary excuse from restitution, because such a statute is unreasonable, contrary to divine law and fraternal charity. Moreover, those who seize such things are excommunicated by the very act, as in c. excoi. de rapto, where, from the decree of the Lateran \[Council\], it says: “Let those also who despoil with damnable greed the Christians suffering shipwreck — whom by the rule of faith they are bound to assist — know that they are subject to excommunication unless they restore what was taken.” The gloss there \[says\]: those who act against this are excommunicated by the very law, and are bound within a year to \[pay\] fourfold, and they commit theft. Nor shall the state claim anything for itself from another’s calamity. And some understand the excommunication to be incurred by the very act after an admonition, or if one was in default — when one is able to make restitution and does not. Those who seize and despoil Christians who are navigating at sea, compelling them under duress, are to be excommunicated. From this it is also clear that those who plunder the shipwrecked are punished more severely than those who despoil seafarers.
