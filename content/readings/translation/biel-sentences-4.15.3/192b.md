# Third Question.

Next, thirdly, inquiry is made concerning the various species of restitutions, corresponding to the various species of alienation of goods. And first, concerning the restitution of goods whose alienation does not transfer ownership, as in goods secretly or violently taken from the owner without the owner’s consent, as occurs in theft and rapine. Then, concerning the restitutions of goods transferred with the owner’s consent.

Regarding the first, it is asked whether all thieves and robbers, and their accomplices, are bound to restitution of the individual goods alienated.

The question will be treated in three articles. Having set out preliminary notes, the terms are described. Second, the question will be examined through conclusions pertaining to the title. Third, doubts will be resolved.

## Article One.

## First Note.

Regarding the first: theft is to be noted. According to blessed Thomas, Secunda Secundae, q. 66, art. 3, it is described \[as follows\]: it is the secret taking of another’s property. But because this description is too general if theft is taken in the strict sense---for it applies equally to simony, usury, and fraudulent or unjust commerce---and too narrow if theft is taken broadly for any unjust handling of another’s property, in which sense it is used in the seventh commandment, “Thou shalt not steal.” Hence Augustinus, in his book of Questions on Exodus, says: by the name of theft is rightly understood every illicit usurpation of another’s property. For he who forbade theft did not thereby permit rapine; rather, in the law of the Old Testament he wished rapine to be understood under the name of theft. For he signified the whole by the part: whatever is illicitly taken from one’s neighbor belongs \[to theft\]. 14 q. 5 Penale.

Taking theft in this way, then, the definition does not apply to every form of theft, for it does not apply to rapine if the latter is not thereby distinguished from theft. Therefore Raymundus, whom the doctor commonly follows, defines it differently: theft, taken in the strict sense, is the fraudulent handling of another’s movable corporeal property, without the owner’s consent, for the sake of gaining the thing itself, or its use, or its actual possession.

It says first “handling,” which is put as the genus: this is the real and external apprehension of the thing. Theft is not committed by will or words alone; although the will to steal is a sin, it is not yet called theft. By will alone one transgresses another precept, namely: “Thou shalt not covet thy neighbor’s property.”

It says “of another’s property,” because theft is not committed in one’s own property---which is true, unless another had some right in that thing, for instance if it had been pledged or loaned to another. \[If\] Pacenianus receives it fraudulently and secretly, theft is committed. For the thing is another’s to him with respect to use, because it has been pledged or loaned to another. Similarly, if someone finds his own property in another’s possession (who has no right to it) and takes it, theft is not committed; but he loses his legal right to it, inasmuch as he takes it by his own authority and adjudicates the matter himself. And this is according to the law of the forum, not the law of heaven. Therefore he is not bound to restitution. But of this in the doubts.
