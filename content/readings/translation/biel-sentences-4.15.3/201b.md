From those propositions there follows as a corollary that all who knowingly eat, consume, or convert to other uses anything taken from another’s property — such as property acquired by plunder, theft, or usury — whether they be religious or others, and likewise those who doubt with probability, are bound to make restitution, as Alexander of Hales says at the passage cited above. He adds that, to avoid doubt, lest they expose themselves to danger, they should hold as a rule that whoever has a \[guilty\] conscience about a thing, that it belongs to another, is bound to restore it. And this is true of the thing itself in its individual identity when it was plundered or stolen — in which case, that is, title does not pass, and therefore restitution of the identical thing is required. But if title does pass — as in loans by usurers — and the one retaining the profit, such as the usurer, has other goods justly acquired that are not subject to the obligation of restitution, then one is not obliged \[to restore\] from one’s own property, because by receiving it one is not rendered unable to make restitution from what is justly held. But if one has no other goods justly acquired, but all that one has is subject to the obligation of restitution — because one owes as much or more in restitution than one possesses — then, in this case, what one received from such a person renders one obligated to make restitution, because his poverty — the penalty he has received — renders him unable to make restitution. Regarding this, the question arises as to what of the wife and household who knowingly eat, are sustained by, and consume such things. To this, \[Post.\], Hostiensis, and others give the distinction just set out above. But suppose the husband has nothing except stolen goods. First it must be said that the wife ought not to leave her husband on account of the marriage debt to which she is bound — unless perhaps the husband were to compel his wife to steal or plunder. Second, that sometimes she ought to live from her dowry if possible, or from her paraphernal goods, or from extra earnings, or by begging from close friends and strangers, or by some other honorable means. If she cannot provide for herself by these means, or if the husband by his cruelty does not permit her to eat from other sources, then she is in a case of extreme necessity, and may then live from the plundered and wrongly acquired goods — but most sparingly, only as much as the preservation of life and bodily health requires, cutting out all excess — because in such a case even stealing would be possible without sin. And because it is most difficult in those circumstances to observe the precise measure of necessity, it is useful to take \[from such goods\] with fear and sorrow, to do penance, and to maintain at least some intention of making restitution at another time.

There are four cases in which one may live from such plundered goods. The first is when one usefully manages the affairs of the plunderer — namely, by inducing the robber or usurer toward restitution — and for as long as there is hope of correction, during the time when one presses him at opportune hours for amendment. And as long as a person remains in this life, one must not despair of him, because what does not happen in a year may come about in a moment. Therefore, if one has not ceased to correct the husband at opportune hours, and to pray for him, and to bring his cause before the Lord, one may with a sound conscience make use of those goods as necessity requires — not only extreme necessity, but even need short of the extreme, though not to the point of excess. So too, religious preachers, rebuking and laboring to bring such a person back — whether in public preaching or in private admonition — may, during the time of this correction and labor, be refreshed from wrongly acquired goods; yet with such moderation that by reason of this one does not become unable to make payment
