## For the Resolution of These Matters, Note First

There are three kinds of goods among those in matrimony. Some are the husband’s own. Some are the wife’s own. Some are common to both. Common to both are those which they earn by shared labor, such as the fruits of the dowry and the donatio propter nuptias \[“gift on account of marriage”\]. The dos \[“dowry”\] is that which is given to the husband by the parents of the wife, or by the wife herself, or by others, for the bearing of the burdens of matrimony. But the donatio propter nuptias is what the bridegroom gives to the bride when he receives her vow, and similarly if he did not receive a dowry, as in Sext., *illud de dona. inter virum et uxorem*, c. *nup.* The husband’s own goods are all other goods which the husband has or acquires by his own industry and labor, or otherwise, without the wife’s collaborative effort, such as also paternal goods or hereditary \[goods\], apart from the dowry and the donatio propter nuptias. The wife’s own goods are the paraphernalia — that is, whatever a woman holds apart from the dowry (from *para*, meaning “beside,” and *ferna* meaning “dos,” hence “beside the dowry”) — such as hereditary successions, bequests or gifts made to her. Angelus says in his *Summa*, under the word *Uxor* \[“wife”\], that a wife sometimes works in her husband’s goods for her own use — for example, in the husband’s wool in order to make a garment, or in his linen in order to make household linens; in that case she earns \[the fruit\] for her husband. Nor are those things any the less his on that account, just as if she had done no work on them; nor can she claim the price of her labor from her husband. Or she labors in the husband’s goods or in some others’ \[goods\], not for common use but so as to make a profit for herself; in that case the profit does not accrue to the husband — it accrues to her, not to him — unless she did not bring a dowry sufficient to maintain herself and the common children along with the husband, and the husband has no other sufficient goods. But if she brought a dowry sufficient to maintain herself and the common children, or at least herself, when the husband has other sufficient goods, she is not required to surrender the profit to the husband; rather she may labor in those goods or in others so as to have the price of her labor, and she acquires it for herself. And this is evident, because a wife is not bound to labor for her husband if she has a dowry sufficient for bearing her share of the burdens, or otherwise has maintenance by right of bequest. Nor can the husband compel her by withholding maintenance, as Panormitanus \[holds\] in c. *lfas. de resti. spolia.* But if the wife did not bring a dowry sufficient to sustain the burdens of matrimony, she ought at least in part to labor for the support of those burdens. If she refuses, the husband may deny her maintenance. The civil laws appear to hold that a wife who is maintained should let \[her earnings\] accrue to the husband’s \[estate\] — those \[earnings\] which ought to fall within the husband’s domain — understanding this as regards the rendering of the conjugal debt, while she labors for her own use in the husband’s goods; otherwise it does not hold. So when it says she is “almost a member of the husband’s household,” this is to be understood of the husband’s dominion not in an absolute sense, but insofar as she is under his care, ought to be governed by him, and as regards the conjugal debt, because she does not pass into the husband’s legal power.
