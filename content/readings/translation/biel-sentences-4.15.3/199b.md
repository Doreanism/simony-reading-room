The fourth case is when a sin of mortal peril threatens, as when one acts not according to \[one’s\] conscience, not believing it lawful for oneself but counting it as theft, or because one exposes oneself to the danger of perjury if, by seeking it, one would only worsen one’s situation by confirming one’s right under oath. If any of these cases intervenes, it is not lawful \[to take\], and one sins by taking -- yet one is nonetheless not bound to restitution. If from such taking there looms peril or temporal or spiritual harm to one’s neighbor, one is bound to restore the harm, not the thing taken. Outside these cases, one neither sins nor is bound to make restitution, even if a prelate has commanded under pain of excommunication that whoever removed anything of such goods from a detainer should make twofold restitution, because since one may lawfully retain it for oneself in recompense, it has already become one’s own and is no longer another’s. Nor if the prelate in his sentence, by which he would excommunicate receivers, were to name explicitly those who took \[things\] with the intent of recompense or otherwise took at all -- because then the sentence has the effect of correcting an intolerable error, and therefore one is not bound \[by it\]. Likewise it does not bind one who does not reveal that another has taken something for the purpose of recompense, provided it is certain that the thing is owed to him; it is otherwise if he is not certain. Likewise, if someone finds a thing stolen or unjustly taken from another in the possession of a detainer and restores it with the true intent \[of returning it to the rightful owner\], he is not bound to make restitution to the detainer, nor does he commit theft, because it is done neither fraudulently nor for another’s gain, provided it can be done without peril. He also usefully manages the business of the thief, who is bound to ratify \[the act\]. So too, one commits neither theft nor sin who steals away Christians captured by unbelievers and frees them, because the unbeliever holds them unjustly. The same is to be said of one who frees those unjustly detained by a Christian. Raymundus and Hostiensis say that in time of truce, no one in the possession of things detained by the other \[party\] ought to touch anything or alienate anything; one who has freed a Christian captive from an unbeliever is bound to make restitution -- not that he should restore the Christian person himself to peril of his soul, but the equivalent value \[of the person\]. If, however, the unbeliever compelled the Christian to circumcision or idolatry, the Christian is now wholly freed, \[as in\] dist. 54, *nulla*. And therefore one who frees him neither sins nor is bound to make restitution, but rather merits reward. So too, if someone were violently using his maidservant and would not desist, if she could not otherwise escape such evil, she could lawfully flee and leave all her goods, as Antoninus says. ## Doubt 4

which takes place in many ways, it has been said in the preceding question: concerning those who participate in theft or robbery, where it must be considered \[that\] this can occur in two ways. Either some persons associate with robbers not in order to rob, but to defend the robbers or to intimidate the despoiled so that they do not defend themselves -- and without whose presence the robbers would not rob -- all such persons are bound to restitution in solidum \[jointly and fully\]. So also \[says\] Angelus in his Summa under the word *furtum*, §1: if such persons go along with the intent of keeping the peace, by which fear is instilled in the despoiled so that they dare not defend their goods, unless they had signified this to the same \[parties\], because they give an efficient cause. He cites Innocentius and Hostiensis, as in *sicut digni*, on homicide, and *Directum, restitutio*, §3. In the second way it can happen that persons associate with each other in order to rob.
