## Fifth Proposition

Proposition 5. A son under family authority, even while still under the father’s power, who takes something from the parents’ goods outside of extreme necessity and against their express or presumed will, commits theft and is bound to restore the same. This is evident, because he takes another’s property against the owner’s will. And he is bound to restitution: for the parents’ goods are the property of others with respect to the children, as regards ownership and administration, nor do the children have any right in them, even though they may expect a future inheritance. He says “against express or presumed will,” because the will of the parents who have administration \[of the goods\] is present and there is no theft. And he says “of those having administration,” because the mother’s consent does not suffice, since she herself does not have the power of dispensing with the husband’s goods or the common goods. And he says “presumed will,” when the son from certain signs and conjecture believes the parents consent — as when they know and do not object, or such is the common custom of the locality or household. The father, however, cannot take from paternal property; much less can the emancipated son. But you might ask: what if the son has a peculium? Can he not dispose of that as he pleases?

that a *peculium* means a small sum of money or a small patrimony. And for the present purpose, it designates whatever a son under family authority acquires while living under the father’s power — whether he acquires it from the father or from elsewhere. It is fourfold: namely, *profecticium*, *adventitium*, *castrense*, and *quasi castrense*. The *profecticium* is what proceeds — that is, derives — from the father’s property or on occasion of it. Here \[the term\] “from the father” is taken broadly, so as to extend to everything that proceeds from the father as its primary or immediate cause, such as money or some property of the father conceded by him to the son, and the fruit or profit therefrom proceeding or acquired — and this without the labor and industry of the son. For instance, if he leases to a tenant farmer for a certain share of the crop a holding given to him by his father, or lends money to a merchant, and what profit he has therefrom: these things that thus proceed will be *profecticia*. But what the son acquires from the aforesaid goods through his own labor or industry — for instance, by personally cultivating the field or conducting trade with the said monies — the profit attributable to the property is *profecticium*, while the profit attributable to the labor and industry of the person earning it will be *adventitium*.
