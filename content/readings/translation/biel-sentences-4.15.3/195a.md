The \[canon\] adds in the cited chapter that if any priests or clerics presume, whether during life or at death, to give \[such a robber burial rites\] or to attend his burial, or attempt to receive his alms, and are found to be participants in this rapine, they are to suffer the loss of their order and be irrecoverably deprived of any ecclesiastical benefit. Hostiensis understands this to apply when the person has been repeatedly admonished, or when the disturbance was brief; having been absolved by his own priest he is to be buried; the heirs are to be compelled by ecclesiastical censure to make satisfaction according to the deceased’s resources, under the same title (*In lfis.*). And the same applies if, when at the point of death, he promised to make satisfaction should he recover but died before satisfying. Likewise, nonetheless, he would not deny penance to one who held some temporal property, even if he could not make satisfaction during life, provided he promised to make amends and to give \[satisfaction\] should he come into better circumstances. And all these things are to be understood of notorious robbers, not of concealed ones.

## Although Theft and Rapine Differ in Species, in the Moral Order or in Terms of Sin There Is Little Difference for Present Purposes; It Is Certain They Are Prohibited by the Same Commandment. Yet They Designate Diverse Species That Differ Accidentally, and to Set Out Such Redundant Legal Distinctions Here I Judge Superfluous.

## Last Note.

Last to be noted: there are two kinds of participants with thieves and robbers. Some participate in the act of stealing or despoiling; some participate in the property taken by theft or rapine. The former are those who provided the occasion by commanding, counseling, etc., without which the act of theft or rapine would not have been committed. The latter are those who did not provide the occasion by their act, but participate in the stolen goods — receiving them from the robbers, buying, using, consuming them, and the like. And this may happen knowingly or unknowingly. So much for Article 1.

## As to the Second Article, the First Conclusion.

## Article 2.

## Conclusion 1. Every thief and robber is bound to restore to the owner of the property each and every thing taken by theft or rapine, if restitution lies within his power.

Proof: whatever anyone has acquired unjustly, by fraud, or by violence, against the owner’s will, he is bound to restore to the owner, if possible. But every thief and robber is of this description; therefore he is bound. The first principle is evident from what was noted first. The major premise was proved in the preceding question; the minor is also evident from the definitions of theft and rapine given in the preceding article. The phrase “to the owner of the property” and “if restitution lies within his power” was clarified in the preceding question, where it was shown what must be restored and to whom; and the present conclusion is to be understood subject to the qualifications noted there.

## Conclusion 2. One who participates, whether in the act or in the stolen property, with a thief or robber, is bound to restitution — yet in different ways according to the different modes of participation.
