It is said “not out of malice” because, however small the thing taken may be, if the taking nonetheless proceeds from an intention to harm one’s neighbor or from a desire to possess another’s property, and one presumes that the removal of even so slight a thing is against the owner’s will, one is not excused from mortal sin. For even the mere will and coveting of a neighbor’s goods is a mortal sin and is contrary to the tenth commandment: “You shall not covet,” etc. Yet rapine and theft differ in the nature of their sinfulness, since all else being equal rapine is a graver sin than theft. Hence Augustinus, in his *Questions on Exodus*: “It is punishable to take something secretly, but far more severely punishable to seize it openly” (*Decretum* C.14, q.5, c.*Penale*). The gloss there \[says\]: “punishable, that is, worthy of punishment.” For the robber offends more than the thief, for two reasons. According to blessed Thomas, *Summa* II–II, q. 66, art. 9. First: since both theft and rapine are sins insofar as by them another’s property is taken against the owner’s will, the more unwilling the owner, the more grave the sin of taking. Now violence, which constitutes rapine, is more directly opposed to the \[owner’s\] will than ignorance, which is the basis of theft. Second: because through rapine not only is material harm done to someone, but it also tends toward a kind of disgrace and injury — that is, contempt of the person harmed — which outweighs the fraud and deceit that are present in theft. And a third reason may be added: because there is greater audacity and obstinacy in rapine than in theft, and thus a more intense will to take another’s property, and consequently a graver sin in rapine. For the intensification of an evil will aggravates sin, just as, on the other side, the intensification of a good will increases merit. For there is greater audacity and more intense will in a public sin than in a hidden one. He who is less deterred from the will \[to take\] wills it more forcefully, clinging to it more firmly, ready to defend that will against all opposition — and this is the nature of rapine. For the robber is not held back by shame, because he steals openly; he is ready to defend himself, because he steals by force; and he has taken on a harlot’s brow, incapable of blushing, which aggravates the sin. Nor does it hinder \[this conclusion\] that the robber is more ashamed of theft than of rapine. We are more ashamed of greater disgrace; and so disgrace seems greater in theft than in rapine, and therefore theft seems the greater sin. But as Thomas says: since people, being attached to outward things, take more pride in the external virtue that is displayed in rapine than in the interior virtue that is destroyed by sin, they are therefore less ashamed of rapine than of theft — because theft is attended by a kind of fear and pusillanimity, which they detest more than audacity. A fourth reason is also added: rapine is more severely punished than theft, both by civil laws and by canon law. For by canon law one is excommunicated by the bishop in whose diocese one committed the rapine (*De rapto*, cap. 1, q. 2), nor is one absolved unless one first makes amends by restoring what was taken, if one is able.
