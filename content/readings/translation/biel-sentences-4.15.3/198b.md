From the foregoing it follows that a wife cannot give to the children of a prior marriage anything from her husband’s goods or from the common goods, except by way of alms, and much less to other friends. And this is true as regards the offspring of a prior marriage, according to Avilés, when such children have no legal claim against the mother. If, however, she has such an obligation -- either because she is obligated to support them while they are under age, or because both spouses hold their goods jointly, or for some other just cause -- the husband is bound to support them, since the mother with all her burdens passes to the husband, according to the custom of some. Yet since the husband holds the administration \[of the common goods\], the wife ought not to support them against his prohibition, except in extreme necessity. She may, however, seek to have the husband compelled to this by a judge. If nonetheless she were to support them in that case, I believe she would not be bound to restitution, because the support is owed to the children, and she is not giving them what belongs to another but what is already owed to them. If the children have no legal claim against the mother -- that is, because neither the mother nor the husband is bound to support them -- the mother ought not give them anything. And if she were to give from the common goods, she would be bound to restitution, unless it were the custom that in common goods not the husband alone but also the wife holds administration; then the wife could give just as the husband could, for in a common thing each has an equal right, as the *Digest* on partnership (*pro socio*) throughout. This is to be understood of a common thing with respect to administration. In these and other matters touching dowry and peculium, agreements, customs, statutes, and municipal ordinances have much effect, which, as has been said above, prevail over the common law in these matters. If you ask: what if a woman committed theft before marriage or after, and the husband shared in the theft -- or the husband committed theft and the wife participated? I respond, following Avilés, that where by regional custom husband and wife share debts arising from contract or wrongdoing, if the stolen thing still exists in their power, the wife may restore it even against the husband’s opposition. The same would hold even if there were no such community between them, for whoever holds stolen goods must restore them to the owner. If \[the goods\] have been consumed, the wife may, so long as the husband does not expressly oppose it, and by usefully managing the husband’s affairs and freeing \[herself\], restore the equivalent from the common goods. But if the husband prohibits it, Pisanus says he does not believe she ought to make such restitution, since she does not hold the administration of goods but the husband does. If, however, she makes restitution from the common goods in good faith, I do not believe she would sin mortally if she did so secretly and without scandal to others, since here there is no fraudulent handling nor intent to profit.

## Third doubt

Whether one who takes his own thing, or something owed to him, or its equivalent, from the one holding it -- whether violently or secretly -- commits theft or robbery and is bound to restitution. He responds by a single proposition. One who takes his own thing, or what is owed to him, from a detainer who has no right in it, commits neither theft nor robbery, nor is he bound to restitution, although in certain cases he is by no means excused from sin.
