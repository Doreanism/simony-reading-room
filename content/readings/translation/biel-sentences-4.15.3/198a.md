And it is of no benefit to the son except that it is not confiscated \[by the state\], in the case where the father’s other goods are confiscated.

The *peculium adventitium* is what comes from elsewhere: such as from a donation of the mother, of friends, or of anyone else whatsoever, not on account of the father; or what he acquires through industry, or what comes by chance or fortune — but not on the occasion of military service or public office. Excepted here is what is donated by the emperor, empress, or king. In this *peculium adventitium* the son under family authority acquires ownership — that is, the property — while the father holds the usufruct as long as he lives, unless he has remitted it, or unless someone gives or bequeaths it to \[the son\] on the condition that the usufruct does not belong to the father, or unless the son succeeds together with the father to the inheritance of a brother or sister. But when the father dies, that peculium belongs entirely to the son and he will not share it with his other brothers.

The *peculium castrense* is what is donated to a son under family authority by parents or others on the occasion of military service and not otherwise, or what he himself acquires in military service, which would not have been acquired had he not been a soldier — because what was given or acquired during military service \[even\] to one not engaged in military service does not belong to the *peculium castrense*.

The *peculium quasi castrense* is what the son under family authority receives from public office — as if he were an advocate, physician, teacher of the liberal arts, notary, official, rector, or the like, who receive public salaries or stipends. \[The author\] adds this note: because what common notaries acquire does not belong to the *peculium quasi castrense*; but notaries of lords and officials, who ought to receive a salary from public funds, \[do fall under it\]. It must also be added: what \[is received\] by donation of the emperor, empress, or king, as above. And in both the *peculium castrense* and the *peculium quasi castrense*, the son under family authority has full ownership, property, and also usufruct, and is regarded as a *paterfamilias*. He may make a will, give alms, and dispose of \[goods\] for vows. The father likewise has no right whatsoever in them. All the foregoing concerning the peculium has been established by the laws, which if you wish you may seek in the Summas or among the legists.

From these things it is evident that a son under family authority who takes something from the *peculium profecticium* — whether with respect to the substance of the thing or with respect to the usufruct — or from the *peculium adventitium* with respect to the usufruct, takes the father’s property, and consequently is bound to restitution. Not so if he takes or disburses something from the *peculium castrense* or *quasi castrense*. And this \[obligation\] holds with respect to the first two types when the father has not consented, tacitly or expressly. And the fifth proposition is to be limited in the same way as the third above, concerning the wife and common goods, with its corresponding declaration.
