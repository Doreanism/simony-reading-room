## Third Note

\[It is to be noted\] that rapine is understood in multiple senses. In one way, in the broadest and most improper sense, for any undue usurpation of a thing, whether temporal or spiritual. It is taken in this sense in Philippians 2, where the Apostle says of Christ: “He did not think it robbery to be equal to God,” because he did not attribute to himself an equality with God that was undue---for he was and is equal to the Father according to his divinity, and true God of true God. But the first parent wished to seize this, as Augustinus says: “They wished to seize divinity and lost happiness.” And Christ paid the penalty for this rapine, as he says in the Psalm: “I restored that which I took not away.”

In a second way, rapine is understood broadly, yet less broadly than the first, for any unjust usurpation or retention of another’s temporal property by any means whatsoever, whether manifest or concealed---and in this sense it comprehends under itself theft, rapine in the strict sense, usury, and the like. Hence Ambrose: “If anyone accepts usury, he commits rapine; he does not live life,” in the book De bono mortis. And Augustinus, 14 q. 5 Si quid: “If you have found something and have not returned it, you have committed rapine.” And blessed Piero, \[as\] found in dist. 42 § ordinandus: “He is convicted of seizing another’s goods who strives to retain for himself more than is necessary.” This is to be understood of what is superfluous to one’s nature and station. And in that sense rapine is not distinguished from theft even in the broad sense.

In a third way, rapine is understood in the proper and strict sense, as it is distinguished from theft in the strict sense. Thus rapine is described as: the illicit usurpation of another’s property effected by violence for the sake of gain.

It says “of another’s property,” because if someone were to take by violence his own property from one who had taken it from him, while that person was still carrying it away, or after the thief or robber had reached his destination and settled there, it would not be rapine, as will be said.

It says “by violence,” to distinguish it from theft, in which another’s property is taken by fraud.

It says “for the sake of gain,” for the same reason as in the definition of theft; because if someone were violently to wrest a sword from the hand of a madman in order to prevent homicide, it would not be rapine. Similarly, if someone takes another’s maidservant in order to fornicate with her, it is not rapine, though it may be called rape, which is a species of lust.

It says “illicit,” because when things are violently taken from enemies in a just war, or similarly when a debt owed by someone is collected from an unwilling person by public authority, rapine is not committed.

And from this the twofold difference between theft and rapine is clear. First, because theft is committed secretly, rapine openly. This is indicated in Book 5 of the Ethics, ch. 4. Second, because theft is done by deceit, but rapine, as has been said, by violence. Rapine is also taken in the strictest sense, as distinguished from predation: in that case rapine concerns immovable property, while predation concerns movable property.
