And this occurs in two ways: either they go as a united band -- say, under one leader -- to commit despoiling together. Then each is bound to restitution of the whole in solidum, because each gives an efficient cause to the entire despoiling. In the other way, they do not run thus united under one lord or leader, but each seizes or steals on his own. ## Doubt 5.

Concerning those who participate in a stolen or despoiled thing, not in the act of despoiling. He raises the fifth doubt about those who receive such goods and whether they are bound to restitution. For the resolution of this, it must be noted that those who receive such stolen or seized \[goods\], or \[goods\] acquired unjustly in any manner whatsoever, such that those who acquired them are bound to their restitution -- whether the receivers receive them by gift or by contract, as for example by sale, by exchange, or the like -- can stand in three different situations. For either they knew at the time of reception that the thing had been unjustly acquired in the aforesaid manner, or they simply did not know, or they were in doubt. And I said advisedly, unjustly acquired such that the acquirers are bound to restitution, in order to exclude that which is unjustly acquired to whose restitution the acquirer is not bound, as in the case of base profit, for instance from prostitution, acting, and the like. For if the first receiver of what was unjustly \[gained\] is not bound to restitution, neither are others who received from him. This being premised, he proceeds by three propositions.

## Proposition 1.

First: every person who knowingly receives a thing unjustly acquired, whether by gift or by contract, is bound to restitution of the same thing if he has it, or of its value if he does not have it -- to the owner of the thing if he knows him, or to the one from whom he received it -- notwithstanding any human statute enacted to the contrary, until the owner of the thing has been fully satisfied by the receiver himself or by another. So \[says\] Thomas, q. 3, art. 5; and Hostiensis in the Summa. And he proves this, because everyone who knowingly holds another’s property is bound to restore it, and such is everyone of whom the proposition speaks. The major premise was proved in the preceding question. The minor is manifest. It says in the proposition, *every receiver*, because it makes no difference whether the one who receives the unjustly acquired thing -- whether from the first transferor, such as a thief, robber, or usurer, or from one who bought or received from that person -- is second, third, fourth, or any number whatsoever to infinity, according to Hostiensis; because in whatever way the unjustly acquired thing comes to him, he always holds what belongs to another. Therefore he is bound to restitution. It says, *a thing unjustly acquired*, which is to be understood, as was said, of a thing to whose restitution the unjust acquirer was bound, because it is otherwise with base profit. It says, *knowingly*, because it is otherwise if he was ignorant or in doubt, as will be said below. It says, *whether by gift*, as if the thief gave it gratis, *or by contract*, whereby he renders an equivalent to the one from whom he receives, as when he buys or receives it by another exchange. It says, *of the same thing if he has it*, because then he is bound to restore the identical \[thing\] as was said above in the preceding question.
