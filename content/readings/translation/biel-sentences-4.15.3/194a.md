Fourth, it is to be noted that since rapine, like theft, denotes the taking of another’s property against the will of its owner---that is, with the owner unwilling---the species or modes of rapine may be distinguished according to the modes of the involuntary, as set out in Book 3 of the Ethics. For the voluntary is either simply voluntary or, in a qualified sense, voluntary by addition, as is clear there. And correspondingly with the involuntary. Hence the taking of a thing is simply involuntary when it is done by an elicited act of will; and such a taking, insofar as it is open and unjust, is manifest rapine, because it is effected through manifest violence. Sometimes, however, the taking is involuntary only conditionally, because the one suffering it would not wish it to occur if he could prevent it without greater harm---as when subjects endure unjust oppressions from their lords. And this kind of extortion does not escape the character of rapine. And in accordance with this, rapine is committed in various ways: manifestly, as by brigands and public robbers and highway ambushers who wander about and seize whatever they can; it occurs in shipwrecks; it occurs in tolls; it occurs in levies, tallages, and similar impositions; it occurs in wars and battles; it occurs in the various extortions of both lords and officials---concerning the restitutions for which we must now examine.

## Fifth Note

\[It is to be noted\] that all rapine, and likewise theft, is a sin, because they include injustice in their very definitions. For, as has been said, if something is justly taken, whether the owner is ignorant of it or knows of it and is unwilling, it is called neither theft nor rapine. Hence, as blessed Thomas says, Secunda Secundae, q. 66, art. 8: rapine denotes a certain violence and coercion by which what is one’s own is taken away contrary to justice. Now, no one has a just \[power of\] coercion in human society except public authority. Therefore a private person not exercising public authority, if he takes anything by coercion or violence, acts unjustly and commits rapine. To princes and rulers of communities, however, public authority is entrusted for this purpose: that they be guardians of justice. Therefore it is not lawful for them to use violence and coercion except according to the tenor of justice, and this either by fighting against enemies or by punishing wicked citizens. What is taken away by such violence has not the character of rapine, since it is not done contrary to justice. But if any persons unjustly take the goods of others by public authority and by force, they act unlawfully and commit rapine. But if you proceed to ask whether all rapine and theft are mortal sins, the answer is yes, by their very genus. This is evident, because they are contrary to an express negative precept of the Decalogue: “Thou shalt not steal,” where theft is taken broadly for every unjust taking of a thing, whether by fraud secretly or by violence openly, as has been said. And thus by that precept all theft and all rapine are forbidden, and consequently it is mortal---according to the definition of mortal sin given by blessed Augustinus, as is found in \[Causa\] 2. Nevertheless, if something very small were to be handled by stealth and not out of malice, one would be excused from mortal sin, as Saint Thomas says.
