When someone purchases a thing for exactly what it is worth, the price equals the thing purchased; when for less, the price falls below it; when for more, the price exceeds it. But nothing can be made equal to the Word of God, nothing can be subjected to it as a means of exchange, and nothing can be set above it. All things can indeed be subjected to the Word of God, because all things were made through him; yet they are not subjected to him as though they were the price of the Word — as though someone might hand over some thing in order to receive it. Nevertheless, if this manner of speaking is in any way admissible, if some rational principle or custom of speech permits the word, the price for obtaining the Word is the purchaser himself, who gives himself for himself to this Word. When we buy anything, we seek something to give in return, so that by handing over the price we may obtain the thing we wish to purchase. And what we give is outside us; or if it was with us, it passes outside us when we give it, so that the thing we purchase may be with us. Whatever price a buyer finds, he must find something such that he gives what he has and receives what he does not have; yet that from which the price departs remains, and that for which the price is given draws near. But one who wishes to obtain this Word, who wishes to have it, should not seek outside himself what he has to give — let him give himself. And when he has done so, he does not lose himself, as one loses a price when one buys something.

## CHAPTER TWO.

2. The price of the Word is man himself. The Word of God, then, is set before all: let those who can purchase it do so; and those who are devoutly willing are able to do so. For in that Word there is peace: “and on earth peace to men of good will” (Luke 2:14). Therefore, let one who wishes to purchase it give himself. This is, so to speak, the price of the Word — if it can be called such in any manner — because the one who gives does not lose himself, and he gains the Word for which he gives himself, and gains himself in the Word to whom he gives himself. And what does he give to the Word? Not something alien to the Word, in return for which he gives himself; rather, that which was made through the Word itself is restored to it so that it may be remade. All things were made through him. If all things, then certainly man as well. If heaven, if earth, if sea, if all things that are in them, if the whole of creation — then certainly most manifestly that one who, made in the image of God, was made as man through the Word.

3. The Word of God: a form that is itself unformed. Form without time and place. We are not now treating, brethren, of how that which was said can be understood: “In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God.” It can be understood ineffably: it is not through human words that one comes to understand it. We treat of the Word of God, and we say why it is not understood.
