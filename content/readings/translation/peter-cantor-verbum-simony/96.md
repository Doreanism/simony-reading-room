Likewise the Lord: “Seek” first and above all “the kingdom of God, and all these things shall be added unto you” (Matthew 6). He does not say: seek these things and seek those things, but seek this above all, “and those things shall be added to you.” Likewise: “His left hand is under my head” — not above it — “and his right hand shall embrace me” (Song of Songs 2). The right hand is eternal blessedness, and all the things that prepare the way and path toward it; as it were, let the left be for the right’s sake, not the right for the left’s. Hence: “Let not your left hand know what your right hand does” (Matthew 6).

From this also arises the distinction that is established between the Old and New Testaments: because the latter promises eternal things, while the former promised temporal things. The reading \[of the New Testament\] is also raised at its end, as a sign of this, while the reading of the Old is lowered.

Likewise: For this reason the sandals of apostles and bishops have soles below and openings above — as if to say: preaching, and whatever spiritual thing is attached to it, should never touch or regard earthly gain, nor be hidden beneath the earth in the vessel of worldly fear, but should regard heavenly things alone, freely and without hope of any earthly benefit. Likewise: For this reason a censer has solidity at its base but holes and openings at the top — namely, so that the smoke of prayer may ascend, not descend in regard to some earthly benefit. If it should look downward, the sandals and censer are inverted.

And note that spiritual acts are done for three kinds of cause: the cause on account of which \[*causa propter quam*\], which ought to be God alone; the cause without which \[spiritual acts\] could not be done; and the cause without which they would not be done. If a spiritual act is done for a temporal purpose only and not for God, it is execrable and diabolical. But if it is done for God’s sake and for the sake of earthly benefit without which it would not be done — when it could be done \[without that benefit\] — then the cause “without which” turns into the cause “on account of which.” “For my cloak is too short, etc.” (Isaiah 28) — “and my bed is too narrow, etc.” (ibid.) — “You cannot serve God and mammon” (Matthew 6) — “How long will you limp between two sides?” (3 Kings 18). You cannot “enter the land by two roads” (Sirach 2). The third cause, however, necessity arising from human frailty, excuses. “For the laborer is worthy of his food” (Matthew 10). Likewise the Apostle in the second Epistle to the Corinthians 4: “not adulterating the word of God.”

Augustinus: \[In contrast to those\] preaching for profit, like false preachers, or for vainglory — “but out of sincerity and from God,” for God’s sake alone, “and we speak before God” (2 Corinthians 2) and act — so that by “word” every good and spiritual work is understood. Likewise: There are two ends between which every act ought to be enclosed — fear of punishment (which is not altogether an undue end, since it excuses and removes one from sin; nor altogether a fully due end, since it does not by itself bring \[the act\] to full merit) and love of God, namely the right eye. The left eye looks to punishment; the right eye to loving God. Where, then, is a third eye — for loving money — on account of which you act? Likewise, these are the ends, the two millstones, of which the Lord speaks in Deuteronomy, chapter 24: “You shall not take in pledge”
