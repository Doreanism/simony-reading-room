and he had come to the mountain of God, Horeb, the Lord appeared to him in a flame of fire from the midst of a bush; and he saw that the bush was burning yet was not consumed (Exodus 3). As he drew near to see that great vision, the Lord said to him: “Do not come closer, but remove the sandal from your foot, for the place where you stand is holy ground” (Exodus 12). If, therefore, Moses was forbidden to approach a burning bush that was merely a figure, how much more are you forbidden to approach the true body of Christ, unless you have first cast off from yourself and from your affections all dead and carnal works? Likewise: since the Lord forbade the stranger, the foreigner, and the unclean person from eating of the paschal lamb, how much more should a sinner — not yet made a citizen and member of God’s household — be kept from the true Lamb, the stained from the spotless? Likewise David: “Who shall ascend the mountain of the Lord, or who shall stand in his holy place? He who is clean of hands and pure of heart,” etc. (Psalm 23). Likewise: if you have not yet watered and kissed the feet of Christ with tears of repentance as Mary Magdalen did (Luke 7); if you have not yet sat down “to eat the bread of sorrow” (Psalm 126); if you have not yet humbled yourself to kiss the feet of the Lord; if you have not yet risen to the strong works, as it were to the kiss of the Lord’s hands — how do you presume to approach the kiss of his head, indeed the consecration, reception, and kiss of the whole Christ? “These things come in due order and by degrees, just as do the ranks of ordination.” In this sacrament, if any degree is omitted, one sins more gravely than in omitting a degree of holy orders. Likewise Solomon: “If you sit at the table

of a rich man, know that you must set before him such things as have been set before you” (Proverbs 23, after the Septuagint). The Lord has set before us countless courses and gifts, yet all of them are contained under three headings. He set himself before us as the price \[paid\] for us on the cross; as the \[viaticum, the\] provision \[placed\] on the altar; as the future reward in heaven. The first we set before him in return “if we have crucified our flesh with its vices and concupiscences; if we have been crucified to the world and the world to us” (Galatians 5–6); “if we have denied ourselves and daily taken up the cross of Christ and followed him” (Luke 9); “if we bear on our body the marks of the Lord Jesus” (Galatians 6) with the Apostle; if we have mortified our flesh for Christ; if to die for him has been granted from above; if we have suffered with our neighbor; if we have lived according to the Gospel. The second we set before him if we have fed Christ himself — not in his own person, but in his members — by distributing to the poor whatever alms we give, whether bodily or spiritual, for “whatever we have done for one of the least of his members, we have done for him” (Matthew 25). The third we cannot set before him, “for he has no need of our goods” (Psalm 15), but he himself will set it before us in the future; yet his inheritance is ours, for the sake of which he sanctified himself — the inheritance of which the Father says to the Son: “Ask of me, and I will give you the nations as your inheritance” (Psalm 2).
