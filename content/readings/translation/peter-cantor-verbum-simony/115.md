“Benjamin is a ravenous wolf; in the morning he devours the prey, and in the evening he divides the spoils” (Genesis 49). For Paul himself ordained certain men and, according to the distinct ranks of persons, assigned them diverse offices in the Church — as he did for Timothy, Titus, and others. Observing this delightful variety and beautiful house of the Lord, David said: “The queen stood at your right hand in gilded clothing, surrounded by variety, and thus clothed about with golden fringes and varieties” (Psalm 44). But what makes this variety praiseworthy is the variety of chants, readings, responsories, antiphons, and ornaments. Most of all, the variety of harmonious persons and offices renders \[the Church\] most delightful and beautiful — a variety of ministers ought to be established corresponding to this variety \[of offices\]. When these things are observed in the Church, one will rightly and truly be able to say of her: “You are beautiful and comely, O daughter of Jerusalem, terrible as an army set in array” (Song of Songs 6). Likewise in the Epistle of James, chapter 3: “Do not become many teachers, my brothers” — the interlinear gloss says: that is, ministers of many churches — “knowing that we shall receive the greater judgment.” For by promiscuous actions the offices of churches are thrown into confusion. Likewise \[in the appendix to the Third Lateran Council, under Alexander III\]: if it is forbidden for one church, one prebend, one bishopric to be divided among many, then by the contrary analogy, neither should many be assigned to one person — unless perhaps on account of a shortage of ministers and a multitude of cases — and this on account of the shameful and ignominious enormities that arise therefrom. Rather, let one belong to one, not many to one. If your father was content with one wife, why are you not content with one church? Consider what the Apostle says: “He who serves the altar shall live by the altar” (1 Corinthians 9). He does not say: “He who serves altars shall live by altars”; nor does he say: “He who serves altars shall live by the altar”; nor does he say: “He who does not serve the altar or altars shall live by the altar or altars.” For he says: “He who does not work shall not eat” (2 Thessalonians 3): and so, where one does not work, or when one does not work, let him not eat either. If someone objects that he serves in several churches, I reply that he cannot serve several churches at one and the same time, and thus he should receive a benefice from each church in which he serves at least in proportion to the time \[spent there\] and according to how he serves. Let each person consult his own conscience, having understood the formula of the words of the one granting and the one receiving an ecclesiastical benefice, \[and consider\] how he ought to conduct himself in the church. For a book is handed to him and it is said: “I give you this church, or prebend, for the honor of God and the service of this church” — as if to say: “Be assiduous in the office of this church.” In some churches this formula is expressed more explicitly: “I give you this church, or prebend, for the honor of God and the service of this church, that you be resident and a permanent dweller in this church.” Likewise, those men seem to interpret badly: “Seven women shall lay hold of one man” (Isaiah 4).
