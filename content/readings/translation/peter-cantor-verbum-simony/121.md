Himself sold,
he desires to sell all things.

(For things that begin with an evil principle are scarcely exercised in a good manner or brought to a good end.) “That man is a thief” (John 10), because he calls what is another’s his own — that is, he calls God’s sheep his own — and yet the care of souls is entrusted to him; if not: like a hidden plunderer, he usurps for himself what is not his, namely the patrimony of the Crucified and the goods of the poor. “He is also a robber” (ibid.), because he has stolen God’s sheep. If he has the governance of souls, he kills himself; even if he does not, he both destroys himself and violently seizes and detains the goods of the poor. “But he who enters through the door” (John 10) — that is, through Christ, sincerely — “not seeking his own things, but those of Jesus Christ” (Philippians 2), “he is the shepherd of the sheep.” “To him the doorkeeper” — that is, Holy Scripture, which leads to Christ and to his eternal blessedness —

or the Holy Spirit, “who teaches all truth” (John 14), “opens” a wider way to blessedness, \[namely\] the hidden meanings of the scriptures, of questions, confessions, and counsels. “For he who follows me does not walk in darkness” (John 8) — whether of ignorance or of sin. “And the sheep” — that is, those predestined to life, his subjects — “humbly and devoutly hear him, obeying his voice, because he preaches Christ and nothing else.” And this pastor “calls his own sheep by name” (John 10) — he summons, promotes, and exalts them in the offices and dignities of the Church, each by name, namely by the name of merit, predestination, or spiritual grace (Philippians 4). Of names of this kind it is said: “Rejoice, because your names are written in the book of life” (Numbers 4). Likewise: Moses called each one by his own name — not by the name of the crowd, not by the name of money paid, not by carnal affection, not by any private condition such as birth or the like. “For whoever does the will of my Father who is in heaven” — whether Greek, Scythian, or whatever barbarian — “this person is fit for the kingdom of God” (Matthew 7).

And the poet:

Chosen from every shore
he advances, and seeks character, not the cradle.

(Claudianus, book ii, In Praise of Stilicho.)
