# CAPUT XXXVII.

## On the Four Kinds of Spiritual Things.

There are four kinds of spiritual things. First, there are spiritual things through which the Holy Spirit is had or is presumed to be had, such as virtues and miracles. Second, there are also spiritual things through which the grace of the Holy Spirit is conferred on someone, or, once conferred, increased in him, such as the ecclesiastical sacraments (see notes) — except matrimony — orders, and also ecclesiastical offices. Third, there are spiritual things annexed to spiritual things, that is, attached to ecclesiastical persons or churches, such as dignities, tithes, first-fruits of the field, and the revenues of the church. Fourth, vessels and vestments consecrated to the church are called spiritual things. The first may be obtained by petition, both in law and in fact. The second, in law, though not in fact — for they are sold, but this engenders simony. The third may be obtained by petition in law, except in cases to be discussed elsewhere. The fourth: if they can be melted down, once melted they may be sold to anyone; but if not, then only to ecclesiastical persons, not to laymen. For we are forbidden in the fifth distinction of the consecration \[rite\] to reduce to common use things once dedicated to God, even the consecrated stones of a church.

That temporal things may be offered for spiritual ones seems to follow from Numbers, chapter 31, at the end: The sons of Israel came to Moses and said: “For this reason we offer as gifts to the Lord, each of us what he was able to find as booty in gold: anklets, bracelets, rings, armbands, necklaces, that you may intercede for us before the Lord.” Therefore, for trentals, anniversaries, and things of this kind, is it lawful to exact and give money? It is not so. Payment of this kind is understood from what follows: “Moses and Eleazar the priest received all the gold that was offered, and having received it brought it into the tabernacle of testimony, as a memorial of the sons of Israel before the Lord.” From this it is clear that money may be given to the church as a memorial and an incentive toward it, as a sign of the devotion of one seeking the suffrages of the Church, and as an example to others; and it is not given in exchange for spiritual things. For in the same way Peter in heaven prays for someone when he sees that person drawing near to God in devotion and work, just as the Church \[prays\] for a sign of the devotion and work of one who thus seeks its prayers and suffrages. Therefore, on that reasoning, is it lawful for me to give to the Church so that I may be inscribed in it and receive its revenues? It is not so. For a different reasoning on either side reshapes the compact. For the suffrages and prayers of the Church are necessary for salvation; but salvation is possible without ecclesiastical revenues; and one who gives less or nothing ought to be equally admitted to the suffrages of the Church as one who has given more, provided he has sought them devoutly and from \[genuine\] affection — otherwise it will appear to be shameful trafficking and venality within \[the Church\]. Second, money is given to the church out of necessity for the support of the ministers’ natural \[needs\] and for the provision of the church’s lights, and things of this kind, without which the divine office cannot rightly be celebrated therein.
