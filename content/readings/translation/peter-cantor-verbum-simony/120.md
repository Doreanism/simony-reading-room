But just as several sick persons are not committed to one physician, nor several wards to one guardian without difficulty and danger, so much the less ought several churches to be committed to one man, where the peril of souls is at stake — there \[it is a matter only\] of bodies and possessions. Likewise: upon such persons falls the curse of God spoken of in Deuteronomy, chapter 28: “You shall take a wife, and another shall sleep with her” — shall rest, sing, and minister in the church. What follows does not apply to them, but is quite the contrary: “You shall plant a vineyard and not gather its vintage; you shall sow a field and not reap its harvest” (Deuteronomy 28). These men, on the contrary, do not plant, do not tend, do not stake the vineyard, nor do they put dung on it — and yet they harvest it and drink of its wine. They do not sow the field, and yet they gather its fruits; so that they may also be likened to the one of whom the Lord says in the Gospel: “You are a harsh man; you reap where you did not sow, and gather where you did not scatter” (Matthew 25). Likewise: if scandal is caused in a church when someone who ought to read or sing in it is absent for a single day, how much greater \[the scandal\] if a person has been absent for a year, or even a decade, or longer, from the church in which he ought to minister? But the greatest offense and absence is this: that one man reads or sings twice in a single night in some church — how much greater, then, ought the offense to be when one man is assigned to two churches, since he cannot read, sing, and minister in both on the same day or night? But in a certain noble church — what is more shameful — there are five or more very wealthy dignities in revenues and receipts, for the collecting of which no service is rendered in it, whether by a vicar or by anyone else. I say not merely that singing and reading are absent, but not even attendance at its councils is given. Indeed, not one of the five persons is found present in it even once a year. Likewise: such men are more detestable than Lamech, who was the first to introduce bigamy (Genesis 4). For these men are found to be not merely bigamists, but trigamists, and tetragamists, and pentagamists, and beyond. And such a man — whatever he leaves as heir to one of his churches from all that has been gathered in it — will have difficulty distributing and leaving to each of his churches what belongs to it. Likewise, Isaiah, chapter 5: “Woe to those who join house to house” — coupling church to church — “and field to field unto the boundary of the place” — the larger one, that is: “Will you alone dwell in the midst of the land?” As if to say: Are you alone wise, or do you alone know how to sing in the church, that you therefore usurp many of them?
