For he is a hireling who praises God for something other than God himself. Likewise: “We will praise you when you have done him good” (Psalm 48). Gregorius: In God’s sight the sacrifice of confession made by temporal prosperity is despised. Hieronymus: One who confesses thus with the mouth eats the iniquity of his heart, which he digests in the torments of hell. Likewise the Lord in the Gospel: “You seek me not because of me, but because I fed you with loaves of bread” (John 6). Likewise: “The kings of the Gentiles lord it over them, and those who have power over them are called benefactors” (Luke 22) — on account of the benefits conferred upon themselves. “But you are not to do so. For even the pagans and tax collectors do this” (Matthew 5). Likewise: To the condemnation of this \[practice\] serves the example of clerics who, playing dice, rushed disorderly and indecently to Vespers when they heard that a monetary payment for singing Vespers would be distributed in the church before the service. Likewise: the example of one who petitioned in church that the feast of St. Stephen be kept as a double feast with silks and ecclesiastical chants — but who obtained his request only after promising an annual meal and refreshment for the clerics, and moreover that the monetary payment for the night office of that night be doubled; so that they thus celebrated the feast of the doubled coin rather than the feast of St. Stephen. Likewise: the example of the weeping and wailing mourners of the Lombards, hired solely for pay to weep and lament at the funerals of the dead. To these are like certain men in the church who sing psalms and chant for a fee, of whom each might say:

*O money, money, to you he renders this honor.*
(Juvenal, *Satires* 5)

Brothers — or rather, those for whom money is discipline, the coin is dean, and even god — who are idolaters, adoring the coin rather than God: they will sing to God only on account of the coin. Likewise the Apostle, first Epistle to the Thessalonians, chapter 5: “Test all things; hold fast what is good; abstain from every form of evil.” Augustinus: Not only from things illicit in themselves, but also from those things which, though it is lawful both to do them and to leave them undone, appear evil to those of lesser understanding — from these too “abstain,” “providing honorable things, not only before God but also before men” (Romans 12). But it is a base and evil appearance that scandalizes a neighbor by the left hand, while enticing someone with the right. If you object that it presents a good appearance because many and frequent \[clerics\] thus sing psalms and chant and serve God in the church — hear Augustinus condemning this when he says: It is better that spiritual things be entirely omitted than that they be performed, handled, or touched impurely and insincerely. In the carrying out of ecclesiastical offices, the Lord approves not the frequency of the many, but the devotion of souls.
