Likewise: How great the purity and sincerity of canonical election ought to be is evident from the words and figures of Truth itself. See how purely and sincerely Joshua was elected, how purely and sincerely other judges \[were chosen\] (1 Samuel 11): Saul too, although reprobate, \[was elected\] as king — but given to the people in the wrath of God, even because they had requested him (2 Samuel 5). See most holy David, most sincerely “chosen from the nursing ewes” (Psalm 77), and anointed king with the horn of oil. See Gideon reject those who drank like cattle in a manner fitting the belly-hungry, and those given to pleasure; and choose the few and sober (who would go forth in virtue to battle against the Amalekites, lapping up the ground) (Judges 7). See in Numbers how men from twenty years of age and upward were chosen in virtue to fight against the Midianites (Numbers 1): how much more ought prelates, manly in age, in spirit, and in gravity of morals — who would advance in virtue to battle against invisible enemies — be chosen in the Church? Likewise, \[consider\] the word of choosing. For to “elect” is to read out the better from among the good. One who elects otherwise rather dejects and casts down than elects. Now, in order that simony may be more openly censured, let us suppose some kind of definition by which its roots may be made known, of the following sort. Simony is whenever something is done by a man whereby a spiritual benefice is given not freely, or less freely. I say “is done,” not “exists.” For blood relationship exists in nature, from God; it is not made by man. But when a spiritual benefice is given on account of nature rather than grace, “flesh and blood reveal this” (Matthew 16) to the one who gives it, not the Spirit — nor is this simony, but a sin of another kind and a mortal one. Whether, however, one who thus receives a spiritual benefice in preference to a more worthy person in the church is bound to resign it, I do not determine. For the greatest havoc will arise in the church if this be said. I say “is done” — \[that is,\] some undue thing is done, such as a sordid gift or service that has been rendered or is to be rendered. Due service is excluded, as when someone who is called to share in the solicitude of a bishop serves the Church at the bishop’s expense, so that the bishop may thereby be assured of his dignity, knowledge, virtues, and good morals — to one so endowed he confers an ecclesiastical benefice, and not therefore less freely. By the adverb \[“freely”\], however, there is excluded the expectation of a gift, or of service, or of a spiritual benefice to be conferred or already conferred, whether or not a compact has intervened. “For God looks upon the heart” (1 Samuel 16); but a compact and a condition sine qua non \[that\] the priestly office could not be exercised in the church is not excluded. For a bishop or priest makes a compact with a patron concerning the provision of the necessities of nature for the ministers of the altar before the dedication of the church. This definition encompasses the simoniacal plague and the Giezitic leprosy, but not the Korathite violence, which breaks down the door of the church. This \[Korathite violence\] is worse than the first two plagues. I would rather be a thief than a robber, a simoniac than a Korathite \[who proceeds\] by the prayers and extortions of princes.
