Now if in the Synagogue and under the figure no interchange of offices could exist, no mixing of order or alternation, how much more in the Church, where the truth resides, ought this interchange and indistinction of offices, personages, and dignities to be prohibited and eliminated — something that has so disfigured and confounded her that it is no longer known how, or under what title, such creatures should be addressed.

Likewise: if offices, dignities, and personages were kept distinct, \[the Church\] would truly be “a terrible place” (Genesis 28), “arrayed like an army in battle order” (Song of Songs 6), against which “the gates of hell would not prevail” (Matthew 16). But if in a physical battle line and in material warfare it is not permitted that the same man be a front-rank soldier and an armor-bearer and a constable and a commander of the army, a seneschal, the same man guardian of the right flank and the left, the same man of the northern and southern wings — how much more in the Church and in spiritual warfare, where one must fight not merely against visible enemies but against invisible ones, namely demons and vices, ought offices not to be confused? On account of this undivided state of personages and offices, the Church was easily triumphed over by the devil, the world, and vices; hence she may say: “Though an army encamp against me, my heart will not fear” (Psalm 26) — on account of my confused, undistinguished, and disordered members. Likewise Augustinus: “If under secular law one who sought two or more guardianships is held suspect, and on that account was excluded from all — how much more ought one who in the Church covets and usurps multiple offices to be excluded and held suspect, lest in attempting both offices at once he perform neither well?” Likewise: Jethro, even though a gentile, is recorded in Exodus and Deuteronomy to have said to Moses — who was judging between one case and another from morning until evening, held in such esteem by all that he had no time for himself — “You are wearing yourself out with foolish labor” (Deuteronomy 1); “divide your burden among many,” etc. On which Augustinus says: “It seems to me that a mind too much occupied with the many affairs of men is in some measure rendered less available to God; it becomes the fuller the more freely it is extended toward heavenly and eternal things. How much more, therefore, does he become empty of God who is intent on many offices?” For,

*The mind spread over many things has less power for each.*

On account of this Solomon says: *My son, let not your deeds be many* (Sirach 11).

*The wise man sets a limit even to honorable pursuits.*

(Juvenal, *Satires* VI.)

Moses therefore, at the counsel of Jethro, appointed some as officers over tens, others over fifties, others over hundreds, others over thousands — pentacontarchs, others as chiliarchs — and all without overlap (Exodus 18). Why, then, in the Church does the same man hold the deanery and the archdeaconry, or two archdeaconries, and the like?
