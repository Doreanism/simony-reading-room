I believe that if this cause were brought before a just judge, he would assign to such a person at least a portion of the revenues sufficient to live on. For it is unjust that someone should have the full integrity of spiritual duties — such as singing in the choir, entering the chapter, ministering at the altar — yet not have the full integrity of the things annexed to them. Such revenues may not be spent even on pious causes. From the common portion of the church, indeed, an annual alms of this kind could be assigned to monasteries or other religious houses; but from the spiritual and particular portion belonging to someone in the church, it could not.

Moreover, since a mental compact proceeding from both parties makes an entry vitiated, it makes the entry more vitiated on the part of the one conferring the ecclesiastical benefice. For it is in his hands and his will to give it or not. He is the door through which entry is made well or badly; let him take care that he not be corrupted, lest the gift be less freely given. If, however, the recipient had in his will to give a gift, whether personally or through another, but had not yet done so, and the prelate conferred the ecclesiastical benefice upon him sincerely and without any hope of a gift, the recipient shall receive it while doing penance for his corrupt will — and his entry shall not be vitiated, since the integrity of the door remains. **105** For he received freely and the other gave freely. And even if he had a corrupt and, before God, simoniacal will, by repenting of it, since he did not carry it into effect, he acquires or possesses nothing simoniacally. It is as if I had in my will to buy your daughter from you as my wife, yet you gave her to me freely — I would have her as a wife freely and without purchase. Likewise: if a theft from the church were secret in his private judgment — that is, in the judgment of confession — it is simply a theft, and what was taken is ordered to be restored by the confessor; therefore a secret, mental simony — judged by the interior forum of the church, not the exterior — is simony, and a benefice thus obtained, namely furtively and secretly, must be resigned. For the confessor as interior judge ought to judge that it must be restored, since simony has been committed before God; but the judge of external causes will not judge this to be simony unless the accused has publicly confessed it or been convicted of it. Likewise: suppose someone coming to a church or monastery says: “I offer myself and my goods to this church or monastery, to serve in it perpetually.” If he is received without distinction, his entry will be sound. But if more than what he offered is demanded of him, or if a distinction is drawn between his goods and his person — so that his goods are the reason he is received — his entry will be vitiated. If, however, the distinction is drawn between his goods and his person in such a way that he himself is the reason his goods are received along with him, then the entry will be sound and licit.
