“And he goes before them” by counsel toward perfection, and by the merit and example of his life. “And the sheep follow him” (John 10) — even into the kingdom of God, by obeying and persevering to the end — “because they know his voice, wherever he goes” (Revelation 14). “But a stranger” — one in whom there is not the voice of Christ but of temporal gain, even if he otherwise appears to be a good man — “they do not follow, but flee from him, because they do not know the voice of strangers” (ibid.). Now just as Christ is “the door” for entering into the Church both militant and triumphant, so too is he “the way — I am the way” in example, “the truth” in promise, and “the life” in reward (John 14) — making a bridge and a way for us into the present and the future Church. Hence the Epistle to the Hebrews, chapter 9: “Christ, appearing as the high priest of the good things to come, through a greater and more manifest tabernacle not made with hands” — that is, “not of this creation” — etc. There are four bridges, ways, or means of entering into the Church, into the heaven of the saints through Christ. The first was the burning of the censers of Aaron, the flowering and bearing of fruit on his staff, by which it was shown that he was called by the Lord. Concerning his calling we read in Numbers, chapters 17 and 18, and in the Epistle to the Hebrews, chapter 5: “Nor does anyone take this honor upon himself, but only one who is called by God, as Aaron was.” I do not speak of a miraculous calling, but of the signs of works, knowledge, and virtues, by which one shows that he is pleasing to God. The second bridge was the voice from heaven, which declared that the Son had been glorified by the Father: “This is my beloved Son, in whom I am well pleased” (Matthew 3). By this calling Nicolaus was called. The third bridge was the casting of lots, by which “Matthias received the place of ministry and apostleship from which Judas transgressed, that he might go to his own place” (Acts 1). And it is notably to be observed that the apostles chose only two from the disciples of Christ for the office of the apostleship — those whom they recognized as more suitable — and they did not presume to name either of them as apostle, but left this to divine grace. And had Simon Peter chosen the seven deacons as stewards of the goods of the poor (Acts 6) out of private love and carnal affection, the disciples so chosen — being useless to him — would have merely petitioned \[for their own interests\]. Now, however, the casting of lots has been removed from use on account of its proximity to sortilege; it has been succeeded by canonical election, which, unless it be pure, sincere, and holy, will be at variance with Christ and sacrilegious. Granted that nowadays lots are not trusted — even for the blind judgment of electing someone to an archdeaconry and other such worldly offices in the Church, lest an unworthy person be elected — how can they be trusted for the perpetrating of homicide through foreign and blind judgments?
