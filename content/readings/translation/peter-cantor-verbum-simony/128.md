# CAPUT XXXVIII.

## In How Many Ways Is Simony Committed?

Simony is committed by breaking the door that is Christ, in three ways: by gift of the hand, of the tongue, and of service. This distinction can be derived from the Moralia of Gregorius (Book IX, ch. 17), on the passage in Jeremias (ch. XLVIII): “Cursed is everyone who does the work of God deceitfully” — that is, for the reward of a material thing, or for a word of praise, or for the favor of human judgment. For there is the coin of vainglory, the coin of pride, the coin of greed, and the coin of base servility. But regarding this last, the question arises: does it produce simony even without an explicit compact? Yes, indeed. But what of service rendered in an honorable manner? The philosopher seems to address this, saying (Seneca, Letter 42): “Therefore in those things which we pursue, toward which we strive with great effort, we ought to examine whether there is any benefit in them at all, or whether the disadvantage outweighs it.”

Some things are superfluous, some not worth their cost. But we do not foresee this, and those things seem free to us which cost us most dearly. Our foolishness is revealed in this: that we think only those things are bought for which we pay money; we call those things free for which we spend ourselves — things we would not purchase if we had to give up our house or our pleasant and fruitful estate for them. Yet toward these things we are most ready to press on, with anxiety, with peril, with the sacrifice of honor, freedom, and time. So little does anyone value himself. Let us therefore do in all our counsels and affairs what we customarily do when we have gone to the seller of some commodity. Let us see at what price this thing we desire is offered. Often the highest price is one for which no money is paid. I can show you many things which, once acquired and possessed, have wrested our freedom from us. We would be our own masters if these things were not ours. But \[the philosopher\] seems to speak of labor that follows upon a benefice already conferred, not of labor that precedes it. Likewise: the coin of greed, with an explicit compact and without one, together with the corrupt hope of expectation and intention on the part of both giver and receiver, produces simony. Does the coin of service therefore likewise \[produce simony\] apart from a compact? Yes, indeed, if the service rendered is base. If honorable — for instance, if the one conferring the ecclesiastical benefice looks in the recipient at grace, learning, probity, that is, at Christ rather than at temporal advantage rendered to himself through the service performed — then there is no simony; nor should the recipient render that service with the intention of receiving a temporal reward. Likewise the question arises: does a mental compact, with the hope of both parties, giver and receiver, which is at least undue before God, constitute a fraudulent entry into simony and produce a vitiated entry into a spiritual benefice?
