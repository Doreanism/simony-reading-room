Likewise, Epistle 87, to the Hebrews: “We have an altar from which those who serve the tabernacle of the body have no right to eat” (Hebrews 13) — that is, those who do not mortify and “crucify their flesh with its vices and concupiscences” (Galatians 5); those who “do not open their hand to the poor,” etc. (Proverbs 31). Likewise: who ought to approach the Eucharist for its consecration or reception, and how worthily and fearfully they ought to do so, the Apostle declares in 1 Corinthians 11, saying: “When you come together, it is no longer” (that is, it is not fitting) “to eat the Lord’s Supper,” unless you are first tested and prepared, as is shown below in the same passage. Likewise: if when we pray, “admonished even by saving precepts, we scarcely dare to say: Our Father” (Matthew 6) — unless we are his sons by adoption, and unless we have fulfilled all the petitions that follow — how much more difficult is it to dare to consecrate and receive him whole, since here greater devotion and purity of conscience is required? And so this prayer is placed before the reception of the holy Eucharist. Likewise: take care lest you gather and receive this heavenly manna beyond one gomor — that is, the measure of sobriety — lest it breed worms in your conscience through sins (Exodus 16). Take care that “in a clean and new linen cloth” (Matthew 27) and a pure conscience you wrap the body of the Lord. Take care that “you lay it in a tomb in which no one has yet been placed” (John 19): or if the devil was once lodged there, lay the Lord only in your clean tomb once the devil has been cast out from it.

Take care “not to put new wine into old wineskins” (Matthew 9) corrupted by sins. Take care to carry the heavenly ark of the Lord upon a new cart (1 Samuel 6). And if you yoke cows to it, yoke such as these: namely yourself and others who have not learned to turn their necks back after the calves (1 Samuel 7) — that is, after their affections and carnal desires — but who, proceeding on a straight path, though lowing, carry the ark of the Lord into the promised land, into the heavenly house of Aminadab. Likewise, Psalm 117: “He has established a solemn day in the thickets, even to the horn of the altar.”

# CHAPTER XXXI.

## Against the Geryon-like monsters with two bodies, two-headed in holding multiple churches.

Not unlike — indeed not out of harmony with — the plurality and the two-faced or three-faced nature of masses is the plurality of persons, dignities, and offices around one and the same person, whether in a single church or in different ones. For there are certain men who, like Geryon, like Briareus — two-bodied, three-bodied, monstrous beasts of many heads — contend to be assimilated to God, who is three in persons yet one in substance. For each of them, though one in substance and person, nevertheless desires to be many persons in the personages and dignities of the Church.
