In the second, a compact is admitted — namely, that a priest may make an agreement with a patron concerning the provision of the necessities of life. Likewise a priest lacking an ecclesiastical benefice may make an agreement with another to minister in his church, while he is away at school, covering sufficient necessities for carrying out his office — that is, he should have not only enough to live on but enough to feed the poor, so that what he preaches in word he may confirm in deed. Similarly in the third case a compact is admitted; yet when it occurs in either the second or the third case, it must be arranged so that on account of it the weak are not scandalized. A shameful, dishonorable, and grasping compact is always excluded. Moreover, since ecclesiastical offices belong to the category of spiritual things, just as a priest who sells his office — for instance by hiring out himself, his labor, his works, and the instruments of the church, even the nuptial blessing — commits simony. So too does one who accepts a poor benefice insufficient for his person on account of a temporal emolument to be attached to it, who would never have submitted his neck to the yoke, burden, and duties of that office unless for the sake of extrinsically added or promised increase of revenues — he is branded and I mark him not only for the venality of the office but for the venality of his own person in carrying it out. For it is more shameful to sell oneself than to sell something external. On the same point, Causa 1, q. 2: “With what pious affection of mind,” etc. Whether one who has thus obtained a dignity or ecclesiastical benefice is obliged to resign it is a question. Now just as a vocal compact in spiritual matters is a mark and effective cause of venality and simony before men, so too a mental compact is such before God and the angels, who speak only in mental terms. Simony therefore consists in the expectation of receiving a gift and in the intention of giving one, and in the hope of both parties — that is, of the one giving the ecclesiastical benefice and hoping for a return, and of the one receiving or hoping to receive the ecclesiastical benefice. To give is sometimes the same as to receive. Whether one who has thus obtained an ecclesiastical benefice is required to resign it is also a question. But since expectation and intention of gifts both produce simony, a question arises about the difference between simony and the Gehazi-leprosy in this case. Simony is committed in the will — both in the intention of the one who resolves to give and in the expectation of the one who hopes to receive; but the Gehazi-leprosy consists only in the act, namely when something is given or received. Likewise: if only expectation intervened on the part of the one receiving the ecclesiastical benefice — if he \[the recipient\] knew nothing by inference that the other expected a return, even though the recipient himself had formed no intention to give a gift — his entry into that benefice is shameful, because it was not freely conferred upon him. But is he required to resign it? If, however, the \[grantor\] did not know that he expected a gift, and if the prelate was in need, he will be able and ought to give to him and assist him.
