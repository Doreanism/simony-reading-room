“He who attends to many things gives less attention to each one.”

“Divide your burden among many,” etc. By analogy: a third kind of unity is therefore also derived from this, namely unity of dignities, offices, and revenues. For if the consequent is denied, so is the antecedent. If a plurality of actions is denied and forbidden, then so too is a plurality of dignities and the like. To hold several dignities or revenues is the antecedent to exercising many activities within them. “For he who does not work, let him not eat” (2 Thessalonians 3). And indeed before a secular judge, one who seeks the guardianship of two wards is regarded with suspicion, lest, in seeking both, he perform neither well. If, therefore, it is forbidden that “your acts be in many things,” the antecedent is still more strongly forbidden — namely that “your acts be in many” churches, revenues, and dignities. Take care lest you hold things from all or from many churches, revenues, and the like in such a way that you do not even possess yourself.

# CAPUT XXXIV.

## On Evil Uniqueness

There is also a fourth kind of unity, namely an evil one, by which a person seeks to stand apart from others, setting up his own standard of justice, “thinking himself to be something, when he is nothing” (Galatians 6) — exceeding proper measure, or arrogating to himself what he does not have: singular, wild, one-horned, proud. Of such a one it is said: “Woe to the solitary man, for when he falls” — falling through pride — “he has no one to lift him up” (Ecclesiasticus 4). Likewise: all the questions that can be raised about Jacob’s double marriage of wives and about the handmaidens brought in to him (Genesis 29), and against such persons — Jacob is nevertheless excused, on the grounds that he married two women to provide offspring for the worship of God and to multiply the people of one faith. Let those persons themselves consider whether they have taken on several churches and dignities for the spiritual fruitfulness and benefit of the Church, rather than for temporal income and profit. If they have taken these on in order to multiply the people in the worship of God, to multiply children and the fruit of good works therein. This hardly seems credible, given that Rachel — that is, some church richer in revenues and temporal fruits — is preferred over Leah, who is yet more fruitful in spiritual fruit. For Leah is despised, while Rachel is chosen. And Rachel said to Leah: “Give me some of the mandrakes of your son Reuben. She replied: Is it a small thing that you have taken my husband from me, unless you also take the mandrakes of my son? Rachel said: He shall sleep with you tonight in exchange for the mandrakes of your son” (Genesis 30).

Yet those who are at present attached to two churches care not to sleep, rest, read, or sing even one night in one of them — let alone for “the many mandrakes and revenues which they claim.” But the Church would be more fruitful if individual ministers were assigned to individual churches.
