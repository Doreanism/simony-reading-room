Consider that throughout all Israel there was but one temple, one tabernacle, and even in the courtyard of the temple under the open sky but one altar of offerings (3 Kings 6). They offer sacrifices; the Lord will not receive them.” Following the example of the single temple, therefore, in each city there ought to be a single church, or, if the city were populous, a few, yet all under one greater church. For the multiplicity of chapels has given birth to illegitimate ministries and many other monstrosities and irregular practices.

# CHAPTER XXX.

*Against those who celebrate or receive the body of Christ unworthily.*

Because the aforementioned plurality, multiplication, and novelty of Masses proceeds from the greed and irreverence of those who celebrate them, it remains to speak of those who celebrate and receive the body of Christ unworthily. Writing therefore against unworthy celebration, as well as against unworthy reception, the Apostle in his first letter to the Corinthians 11 says: “Let a man examine himself, and so let him eat of that bread and drink of that cup. For he who eats and drinks unworthily,” etc. Augustinus \[says\]: by celebrating this mystery without devotion, or otherwise than it has been handed down by the Lord or by the Church, or by remaining in a will to sin (Aug., *De eccles. dogm.*, c. 53), “he eats and drinks judgment to himself, not discerning the body of the Lord” — that is, not distinguishing it with due reverence from other food, out of the habit of celebrating or receiving the body of Christ. Likewise, Gregorius speaks of anyone who celebrates or receives the body of Christ unworthily, just as the Lord says of Judas: “But behold, the hand of him who betrays me is with me at the table” (Luke 22). For whoever presumes to violate that inviolable sacrament of the Lord’s body, following the example of Judas, betrays the Son of man. One who celebrates not according to the rite of the Church, or who receives unworthily, is such a traitor.
