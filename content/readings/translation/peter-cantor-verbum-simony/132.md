Item: Whether it is simoniacal for an abbot to purchase a mitre, letters of emancipation, and such things in order to avoid being subject to the bishop. It seems that it is, since these things are annexed to spiritual matters — which is indeed the case. Yet it is lawful for anyone to redeem his own rights even in spiritual matters. Hence: spend money to purchase paradise for yourself. Item: The question is raised whether it is simony to give a procuration or revenues to a chapter so that it may celebrate the feast of some saint in the double rite, whereas it had previously celebrated it only in the simple rite, or also for singing a new, unaccustomed hymn, and such things. There is not complete purity here either. The blessed Thomas of Canterbury, in order to banish simony entirely from his household, when he appointed Master Arnulph as chancellor, bound him by oath that he would accept nothing — neither by compact nor without compact, not so much as a hand-cloth \[canipulum\] — for administering the office of the chancellery.

# CAPUT XXXIX.

*On the punishment of simoniacs.*

There follows \[a discussion of\] the punishment of simoniacs. Gehazi, on account of the enormity of his crime, was struck with leprosy (2 Kings 5). Simon was condemned to hell. In the present life the sentence of his condemnation was pronounced: “Your money be with you in perdition” (Acts 8). A certain man, who for the ransom of the Church’s liberty sold rights and ecclesiastical benefices to princes, was deprived of his accustomed grace of preaching. Another, while conferring orders and saying “In the name of the Father and the Son,” failed at the third sign. For he fell mute when he was bound to invoke “the Holy Spirit” — as if God were saying to him: “Freely you have received, freely give” (Matthew 10).

## The accessory \[is punished\] just as the principal.

Hence the deacon Cyriacus, when gifts were offered to him so that he would come and heal the daughter of King Sapor, said: “Far be it from me that the grace of Christ should be for sale through me! Go ahead,” he said to the messengers, “I will not mount a horse, just as Christ did not, but I will follow you on foot.” A certain devout woman also, who ate virtually nothing and was often rapt into ecstasy and angelic visions, lost her former grace, her spirit of prophecy, and her converse with angels when, under the pretext of building a chapel and purchasing fields for the use of that monastery, she began to receive gifts from those who offered them. And others have been gravely punished by God in many other ways.

# CAPUT XL.

*On the exchange of spiritual things.*

There follows \[a discussion of\] the exchange of spiritual things, which draws near to the contract of buying and selling, and thus appears to incur the mark of simony. The exchange of goods in certain provinces where coin is scarce takes the place of the contract of buying and selling, so that goods are received for goods, and livestock for livestock. So too the exchange of spiritual things seems not very unlike buying and selling, and appears to constitute simony.
