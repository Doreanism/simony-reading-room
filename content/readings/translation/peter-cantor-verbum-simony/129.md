Suppose an elderly uncle gives his church to his upright and even honorable nephew, but would not have done so except in the hope of receiving the revenues and income of that church for as long as he lives, and the nephew would not have accepted the spiritual \[benefice\] unless those attached fruits were given to him — then the giver (and if he who thus holds an ecclesiastical benefice) is obliged to resign it. It is settled, however, that if an oath has been or is to be interposed over the concession of the receipt of revenues, and thus by means of a vocal compact, such an entry is vitiated. But what of the election of someone, if he has sworn or promised to swear that he will observe the customs of that church and the like? “Blessed is the man who is fearful in such matters” (Proverbs 28). Likewise: what if someone has conferred the administration of an ecclesiastical dignity upon another, who would not have conferred it, nor would the other have accepted it, unless he had granted him the revenues thereof for three years in order to marry off the sister of the one receiving that benefice — so that a spiritual thing is received and a temporal one given? Likewise: what of one who resigns a church or prebend so that a prelate may confer it upon his nephew, compelling him by oath not to offend his uncle, **104** but to minister to him from the incomes the necessities of life — which many approve, and count as ungrateful the one who refuses to provide necessities to an impoverished uncle, as though the latter had received the benefice with that hope and intention, and the former had conferred it with the same intention? Likewise: if a prelate would not otherwise grant ecclesiastical benefices to his young nephews, and if the flesh urges him ever so slightly to do so, unless he receives the revenues and fruits — in all these cases the question arises whether the entry has been vitiated and whether \[the parties\] are simoniacs. Likewise: one who sells one of two things — a spiritual benefice and its annex — leaves neither one unsold; and one who buys one leaves neither one unbought; therefore one who gives one gives the other as well, and one who receives one receives the other. But suppose a prelate confers an ecclesiastical dignity upon someone, which he would not have conferred otherwise, unless he had pledged its fruits to purchase or redeem fields or revenues of the church, or had granted the revenues for a period for building or \[other\] expenses; then he receives one but not the other — or rather not immediately. The same objection applies to annual, biennial, triennial \[grants\] and the like, all of which appear to constitute fraud against the law of simony. Here some draw a distinction, saying that the sequestration of ecclesiastical goods made by common authority of the Church and for her benefit is licit, but that which proceeds from the bishop’s private advantage and greed is simoniacal. Likewise: by this same rule — “whoever sells one \[sells both\],” etc. — others are shown to be receiving simoniacally, and at least unjustly, the fruits of someone’s prebend for a year along with another spiritual thing to which those fruits are annexed, whether in that year the instituted canon has served in the church or not. If he has not served, then those receiving the fruits thus deprive that church for a year of both its servant and its service.
