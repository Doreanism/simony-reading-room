The return from the left horn to the right signifies the gathering of the remnant to the faith, which will take place in the time of Elijah and Enoch, which \[the Mass\] anticipates and hastens — or rather figures as already accomplished — thus returning frequently from horn to horn; yet it does not yet signify or represent the host as sent. Item: through the Mass is \[signified\] the Passion of the Lord, who from the bosom of the Father (John 1), as if from the right horn of the altar, came into the sinister and malignant world (John 5). When his hour came to pass from this world through the Passion to the Father (John 13), he was sent as a host for us into heaven; and ascending on high, Christ led captivity captive (Ephesians 4). Such persons destroy this principal figure of the Mass, or show it to be false and empty, when they return from horn to horn — from right to left and from left to right — before the Mass has been celebrated. Item: the visitations or insertions of Masses were instituted not by Church authority but by greed. Evidence of this is that the more popular Masses are inserted, and no others — such as the Mass of blessed Linus or of Sebastian, and the like. Item: since no other ecclesiastical offices are inserted, why is the office of the Mass inserted out of greed? For the multiplication of prayers is not the insertion of offices; prayers may be multiplied in any of the hours without those hours being thereby inserted. Item: a Mass so inserted is like the idol of Nebuchadnezzar described in Daniel at the first vision (Daniel 2), whose head was of gold, its chest and arms of silver, its belly and thighs of bronze, some parts of the feet of iron, and some of clay. Item: good examples are to be taken from good \[men\]. We know that the conventual churches do not have this custom of inserting Masses. Item: the example of the obligation of the first Mass owed to certain monks and the second to the priests; therefore it was the greed of the monks that compelled such a priest to celebrate twice in a day. Item: the example of the matron to be purified, who at the offertory of the second Mass — the one for the faithful — made her offering at the priest’s instigation, not at the first, even though a monk was lying in wait to collect the offerings of the first introit, and so went away disappointed. Item: the example of the soldiers offering resistance by making offerings one by one at each introit; one of whom said to the priest thus inserting a Mass: “Even if you begin single Masses in this way until vespers, I will not cease from making an offering until he \[the priest\] has consecrated the holy Eucharist.” Item: the example of the priest who, not yet vested in the chasuble, began to multiply introits so that the gathered people would not depart. Item: souls are injured in this way. They are defrauded of what is owed and promised them, for if the authority holds that the more often and more specially the sacrament of the altar is offered for them, the greater refreshment they receive — \[then they are cheated\]. Item: since it is established in the Church that special prayers avail more than general ones, if therefore the trental of one person or an anniversary is combined and aggregated with the trental of another, an injury will be done to the other — indeed to both — since each soul, by promise, expects to be helped not merely by a general prayer but also by a special one for each.
