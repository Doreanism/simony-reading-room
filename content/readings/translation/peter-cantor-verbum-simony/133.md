Therefore \[simony arises\] also between those who resign, even without the knowledge of the giving bishop — in which case (that is, the bishop being unaware of this compact of exchange) he will not give the benefice to his nephew in a simoniacal manner, yet that nephew will acquire it simoniacally. Item: By an exchange of this kind, especially of tithes redeemed from lay hands and of revenues deriving from lay investment funds, any ecclesiastical benefice whatsoever could be obtained; and thus all the prebends of some church could be acquired through lay exchange without simony — which is plainly false. Item: Deuteronomy 22: “If a man has taken a wife and seeks occasion to put her away, once the elders of the city have learned of this, they shall punish him according to the law and adjudge her to him as wife, so that he may not put her away at any time during his life.” By analogy, therefore: if a man has lawfully received a church and seeks occasion to abandon it because another is fatter and richer, he ought to be punished according to the penalty established in the canons and to remain in it for all the days of his life.

Note, however, that an exchange both of persons and of benefices, made by the authority of the Church and for its benefit, is permitted; whereas one made for temporal advantage — where one’s own utility is attended to more than the Church’s — appears to carry a blemish. Nevertheless I do not wish “to set my mouth against heaven” (Psalm 72) by asserting that this is simoniacal, since the supreme pontiff Alexander II granted that an exchange of this kind may be made by dispensation — I do not know whether in writing or without writing. Item, the Lord: “Freely you have received, freely give” (Matthew 10). And likewise, the same Lord elsewhere: “If you are invited to a dinner, recline in the lowest place, waiting until it be said to you: Friend, go up higher” (Luke 14). And the Apostle: “No one takes honor to himself,” etc. (Hebrews 5). From these three authorities it is gathered that “grace, unless it is given freely, is not grace” (Romans 10). But will there be a middle way between “being called by God” and “taking honor to oneself”? (Hebrews 5.) One who errs from both sides is at fault. Far be it! For you are not called by God unless you ascend through him, unless you receive freely — that is, as one who attributes nothing to his own merit — rather than taking honor to yourself. “Entering by another way than the door” (John 10), ascending by another path, you are called by another than God. But what if the breaking down of the door lies only in the will and not in the deed? Repent of the will and retain the benefice received — even though it was received with a bad will, it was nonetheless freely given. For I will not accept your book less \[freely\] if you have given it to me as a gift, even though I had intended to buy it. It is established from all this, therefore, that a compact concerning the augmentation of a pension — especially one made between claustral religious (who have no cure of souls) and a perpetual vicar established or to be established — constitutes simony if the question is between a secular cleric and a perpetual vicar.
