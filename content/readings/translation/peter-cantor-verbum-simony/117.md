Similarly the Apostle assigns diverse offices to the individual members in the human body. Thus in the same passage: “If the whole body were an eye, where would be the hearing? If the whole were hearing, where would be the smelling?” etc. And he immediately adds: “The eye cannot say to the hand: I have no need of your works; or again the head to the feet: You are not necessary to me.” So too in the household of the father of a family, there are diverse offices serving human need. For he appoints one as swineherd, another as oxherd, another as coachman, another as courier, and so forth, according to each person’s own capacity, lest any damage to his property be incurred. How much more, then, is a distinction of offices and dignities to be made in the spiritual body (that is, in the Church), so that charity may bind all together and spread more widely? Hence that excellent versifier \[writes\]:

Eyes as sentinels for the head, ears for harmony,
Feet as guides, and hands that accomplish all things.

# CAPUT XXXII.

## Against bigamists and Lamechites holding several churches.

It is a shame that certain bishops borrow eyes from other bishops. And since there are four bishops who have but two eyes between them, they have been made not only blind or one-eyed but even monstrously bereft of eyes, when they ought to be creatures full of eyes before and behind, as one reads in Ezekiel (ch. 1). For each ought to be an Argus, so as to see spiritually and make others see. For as the poet says:

Argus bore a head encircled with a hundred eyes.
(Ovid, Metamorphoses I, fable 10.)

Such men are worse than Julius Caesar. When Lucanus had enumerated Caesar’s individual vices, he said of him in one phrase:

Caesar was all things.
(Lib. III, Pharsalia.)

As if he had usurped to himself all the dignities of Rome. For he was consul, quaestor, etc., and this in the same city. Still worse, then, is he who wishes to hold all honors in different places at once. \[He is\] a beast of many heads, resembling that monster over which theologians dispute — which from the navel downward appears uniform, but above is manifold. And so those lie who say that Gaul alone is free of monsters. Note that nothing deforms the body or the head more than absent eyes; no parts are more necessary to the body. It is also doubtful whether this person of many faces ought likewise to have a plurality of seals, or different seals. And how he should write, or how one should write to him — whether with the title of dignity: whether all the names of \[his\] dignities should be appended, or only one; or his proper name without the names of dignities, in which case he might perhaps take offense.
