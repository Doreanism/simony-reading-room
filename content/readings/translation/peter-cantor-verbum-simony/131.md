From all this it is clear that no monastery ought to be founded or established without a fixed number of fields, livestock, and persons. For once these things increase, it will be very difficult to avoid the snares and temptations of simony. What if I give something to the bishop’s household servants so that they will not harm my reputation with him but will keep silent and refrain from working against my receiving an ecclesiastical benefice? To have the pledge of their silence — they being counselors to the bishop — makes the entry corrupt. What if they are not the bishop’s household servants, and yet I give to them so that they will not besmirch my reputation? The entry will not be corrupt on that account. But what if I give something to the bishop’s doorkeeper so that I may enter his chapel to be ordained? No \[simony is committed\], since the order is already owed to me by reason of the examination and my enrollment in the register that has preceded it — even if the examination or enrollment has not yet taken place, yet if I enter for this purpose with the hope of being ordained because I am worthy of ordination, the entry will not be corrupt if I give something to the doorkeeper for that reason, since the care of ordinations is not his concern. Simony extends its branches from the altars where it sits rooted and nests all the way to the doors and the entrance of the orders to be conferred. For a chaplain stands at the side of the ordaining minister so that, once the order is conferred, he may immediately seize the shears and the hand-towel from his hands, alleging custom as his justification; he even complains that he does not receive a separate hand-towel from each of those ordained; and often the sacrilegious hand \[of such a one\] snatches even what the widow had borrowed. Furthermore, the arch-cock \[archigallus, a satirical name for the archdeacon\], as the price of presenting a candidate for ordination as subdeacon, demands three solidi; for deacon, five or four; for priest, seven — as a meal-fee. Just as buying and selling is the principal form of simony, so too is the accessory form, along with everything attached to the ecclesiastical office which is owed as of right to the one to whom the principal thing is conferred. Therefore, to sell the seal, the hand-cloth, the \[benefit of\] justice, the wax, and such things — unless poverty excuses it — is simony. Hence Gregorius \[says\] (Homily 39, on Luke 19), on the verse: “And entering the temple he began to cast out those who sold and bought therein.” Many, while they receive a place among the sacred orders through the religious habit, convert the office of religion into the commerce of earthly trade.

“He sells the dove in the temple” (John 2), who bestows for payment what rightly belongs to certain persons. “He sells justice,” who preserves it only upon receipt of a payment. “He buys in the temple,” who, when he denies to his neighbor what is rightly owed him, and pays patrons a fee to support an unjust cause, purchases sin. To these it is said: “You have made my house a den of thieves” (Matthew 21; Luke 19). For through malice and cupidity they kill where they ought to give life. Item: The question is raised whether buying that common form of fraternity from which one is expelled if his promise is not fulfilled is simony. It seems that it is, since spiritual benefits are annexed to it — such as solemn Masses, suffrages, and the prayers of the faithful — though this we do not settle definitively.
