not wishing to reveal the counsel of the Fathers, the young man hit upon a new stratagem and replied to his mother that he had heard that henceforth there would be several wives for one man. Upon hearing this, his mother gathered the matrons to the door of the Capitol and cried out: “Not two for one, but one for two!” (Macrobius, Saturnalia, bk. 1). But \[the problem here is\] not “two for one” but “two for one man” — indeed today \[there are\] more than two, as in the church of Saint-Merry and many others. It should also be noted that in secular jurisdictions a single soldier cannot perform military service for two lords without keeping safe his prior allegiance, and thus the jurisdiction of the other is diminished. How much less can anyone divide himself so as to serve God in several churches, when he ought to present himself wholly and always so as to minister in each one individually? Someone also reproaches another for having taken on the care of more peoples than he himself had persons in his own parish. “I have heard,” he says, “that when a certain dean was sharply rebuking his subordinate for the negligences he committed at all hours, day and night, that subordinate replied in turn: ‘What right have you to rebuke me, when in so many churches where you hold dignities and from which you receive revenues and proceeds every day, you commit so many defaults and negligences by your absence?’ So that dean fell silent and afterward dared not rebuke him or anyone else for such default and negligence, sensing a greater cause for reproach in himself.” I have also heard that when two canons belonging to two churches were \[in question\] — one being a dean in one church and the other a dean in the other — it was asked which was the spiritual father of the other and which was the subject of the other. The answer was that for as long as they are in one of those churches, the one who is dean in the church where they happen to be is the spiritual father of the other, and the other his subject.

But if they are not in any of those churches but perhaps in another or in others, which is the father and which the spiritual son? Such paternity and such sonship is contrary to nature and monstrous. That physician too would be held in ill repute who took on the care of twenty or thirty sick persons in the same village, let alone in different villages; how much more ought he who has taken on the cure of so many souls — not in one village but in different villages, not in one region but in different regions — be struck with a mark of infamy. Likewise John in his Gospel: “The Good Shepherd lays down his life for his sheep; but the hireling, and he who is not the shepherd, whose own the sheep are not, sees the wolf coming and abandons the sheep and flees” (John 10). If he sees the wolf coming, then he is present. Likewise: if he flees, then he is present. Often too the hireling does not flee but leads the sheep away and brings them back to the folds, and preaches on occasion — therefore the hireling sins less than the man whose church it is, since that man is always absent. But by what name shall that one be designated who is always absent? He is not a shepherd, because he does not feed the sheep; he is not even a hireling, because the hireling is always present, whereas this man is not.
