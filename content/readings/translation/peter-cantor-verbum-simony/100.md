of whom it is written: “The zeal of thy house hath consumed me (Psalm 68),” he says to them: “My house shall be called a house of prayer, but you have made it a den of thieves (Matthew 21).” In a den both plunder is seized and the seized plunder divided. A thief, moreover, is one who, in exchange for the spiritual things of the Church, pursues temporal gain — who also pursues bodily those who give nothing and offer nothing, seeking how he may receive and snatch gifts from them; and who spiritually slays those who do give, since while he schemes after the injury and robbery of his neighbors’ goods, he sits, like a thief, in his den. Likewise Augustinus (*Tractate 10 on John*): “All who seek in the Church what is their own, and not what belongs to Jesus Christ (Philippians 2),” make it a house of commerce. Of this same matter Augustinus also says: let no one do anything in the oratory except that for which it was made — hence also its name. Likewise: inflamed with this same zeal for the house of the Lord, Peter, in order to imitate his master in punishing the madness and heresy of simony, said to Simon Magus, who supposed the grace of the Holy Spirit to be for sale: “May thy money perish with thee (Acts 8).” He spoke this as an imprecation \[optatively\], caught up into the lofty judgment of God, foreseeing the plague this man would bring upon the Church. With the same zeal he struck down Ananias and Sapphira, so that they were carried out dead before him, saying to them while they still lived: “How did you dare to lie to the Holy Spirit?” Likewise: it is notably worthy of note that the Lord, still a youth, possessing no authority of public administration, but quite abject and lowly among them, “drove out those who were selling and buying (John 2),” etc. In this he gave to all a form and example for accusing, rebuking, and expelling, as far as we are able, every species and plague of simony — above all the selling of the Eucharist. Likewise: we read that he drove out the buyers and sellers from the temple twice, giving us the form and example that not once only, but always, as often as we see the dove being sold, we must rebuke and challenge the seller and the buyer, so that this plague may be driven from the Church (John 2 and Matthew 21). Likewise: if the Lord thus corporally punished the figure of the sale of the dove, how incomparably more will he punish the very reality and leprosy of simony? Likewise: “If then, when the whip was made of cords (John 2),” the sale of the dove was punished, how much more ought it to be punished now — not merely with a whip, but even with nails, swords, and all other weapons, and with every force be combated? Likewise: how great is the enormity of this crime is clear from the fact that, while the Lord waited with patience for men ensnared in other crimes to come to repentance — as in the case of Judas the betrayer — **76** instructing us that we should not accuse, expose, or prosecute the sins of our neighbors by an irregular judicial process, but rather conceal and bear with them, as he himself bore with the betrayal of Judas; this one offense alone he could not endure without punishing it immediately and corporally, whereas he corrected and punished all others by word alone. Likewise: in driving out those who sold doves and overturning the tables of the money-changers, whom the avarice of the priests had placed there so that they might lend on credit to those who did not have offerings, in order that these might find them more readily at hand.
