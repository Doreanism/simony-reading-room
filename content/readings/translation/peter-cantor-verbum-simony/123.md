But now we see very many making this thing a benefice — that is, they do not seek to raise up in the Church those whom they have known to be of greater benefit to the Church, but rather those whom they love with private affection, or to whose services they are bound, or for whom some great person has interceded, and — to say nothing of worse things — those who obtained clerical status by gifts.

It must also be added that nowhere do we read the Lord to have gnashed his teeth and burned with anger on account of any crime as \[he did\] over the monster of simony. For, about to fight against this monster so as to cast it out of the Church, “having made a whip of cords” (John 2), armed and laying aside, as it were for a moment, his natural meekness according to his humanity, “he drove out those selling doves in the temple and overturned the tables of the money-changers” (John 10). As if to say: through me one must enter into my house. “For I am the door” (John 10) — you do not approach churches, or spiritual things, or things annexed to spiritual things, through yourselves. “For thus you make my house, a house of prayer, a den of thieves and a house of commerce” (Matthew 21). If therefore the Lord blazed and gnashed so against the figure, how much more against the truth and the monster of simony — which, to be cast out of the Church today along with the monsters it could \[already\] cast out, would require not merely cords but swords, staves, and thunderbolts falling from heaven? The Lord, expressing canonical election as the imitator and successor of the aforesaid three modes of choosing, says in the Gospel: “Come after me,” etc. (Luke 9). “Fulfilling the precepts” without which there is no salvation, “crucifying your flesh with its vices and lusts” (Galatians 5). Because, “If anyone wishes to come after me,” it is necessary “that he deny himself, and take up his cross, and follow me” (Luke 16). Thus a prelate of the Church must be chosen from among those crucified in this way. And because the aforesaid qualities common to all are not sufficient for those to be chosen, he adds: “I will make you” — through authority, the infusion of gifts and virtues, so that one who presides in dignity may preside in the uprightness of morals and excel in knowledge. And so, when the aforesaid \[qualities\] have been fulfilled and conferred, “I will make you become,” through the ministry and canonical election of the electors, “fishers of men” (Matthew 4) — of souls, not of money. Those elected otherwise, or who have obtained a spiritual benefice \[by other means\], are like the priests of Jeroboam, who made priests of idols from the lowest and most base of the people: “Whoever wished filled his hand and became a priest of the high places.
