Yet they have much to fear, since while they are still members of the devil they are present at the celebration of the body of the Lord, when it was not lawful even for the catechumens, though sanctified, to be present. Likewise: “Appoint a solemn day among the dense \[branches\] even to the horn of the altar” (Psalm 117). Note the firmness and stability of mind in what is good. “A day” of virtues, so that you may say: “This is the day which the Lord has made” (Psalm 75). — “And a solemn one,” so that “the remnants of thought might keep feast to the Lord,” that is, in serenity of conscience, and this “among the dense \[branches\]” of all the articles of faith, of virtues, of good works, of tears — which make this drink sweeter — of penitential fasting, for you who desire to draw near “to the horn of the altar” (Psalm 117), that is, to the celebration or reception of the body of Christ. Likewise, Exodus 12. If the Lord commands that the paschal and symbolic lamb be eaten “roasted with fire and with wild lettuces,” and this by those “with loins girded, holding staffs in their hands”: how much more does he command that whoever approaches the eating of the true paschal Lamb must come with bitterness of heart and penance, purified also in the furnace of the Holy Spirit — chaste, without reproach of conscience, strong, clothed entirely in virtues, so that even on his feet he is not unshod (whence: “feet shod in readiness for the gospel of peace” \[Ephesians 6\]), but is firmly grounded in faith, virtues, affections, and good works. Likewise, to inspire awe in those who approach the Eucharist, there avails the example of that holy sinner of Egypt, who, doing penance in the desert, was visited by the Lord in this sacrament once in forty years by the abbot Zosimas of the hermits of that desert (*Life* in Sur., vol. II). To him she first made her confession; and when he came to her a second time to give her the Eucharist, at the coming of the Lord’s body she was dissolved entirely in tears. For although she had wept every single day, she wept then more, and nearly to the point of the flooding of her eyes.

Whence \[Hildebertus writes\]:

*She, her sins now done, came to the cup of piety,*
*She offers herself wholly in tears and is joined to the chalice.*

(Hildebertus of Le Mans, bishop)

Likewise, concerning those who approach this altar unworthily, the Lord makes complaint, saying: “And they gave me gall for my food” (Psalm 68) — the bitterness of their sin — “and in my thirst” — with which I thirsted to drink of them and to incorporate them into myself — “they gave me vinegar to drink” — the sharpness of their depravity. But one must note that the Lord, “when he had tasted the vinegar, would not drink it” (Matthew 27) — so likewise he does not incorporate such persons into himself, even if they receive the Eucharist sacramentally, those who are of the Church in name but not in spirit.

**85** Likewise Augustinus \[says\]: whoever has not first been crucified with Christ to the flesh, the world, and its concupiscences (Galatians 5), before approaching the celebration or reception of the body of Christ, crucifies Christ again insofar as it lies in him, and is to be numbered among those of whom the Apostle says: “Again crucifying the Lord to themselves and making a mockery of him” and “being against him (Hebrews 6).”
