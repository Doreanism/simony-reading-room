When someone was writing to such a beast of many heads and had prefixed in the salutation the names of all \[his\] dignities and prebends, he heard from someone else: “One more thing should be added: ‘Serf and peasant of Sancerre.’” This person of many faces will also need to have a multiple wardrobe and many breviaries on account of the diverse liturgical uses of the churches over which he presides. But what is to be done if in this church all those wearing such-and-such vestments are excommunicated? What if in that church everyone is excommunicated who wears garments that the other church forbids? This person is also the many-formed monster of Horace, whose likeness cannot be found in the natural world. Hence:

If a painter wished to join a horse’s neck

(Horace, Ars Poetica.)

The same author elsewhere:

The jackdaw will provoke laughter,
stripped of its stolen plumage.
(Epistles I, ep. 3.)

For it is theft, or rather plunder, to seize and usurp so many goods belonging to the poor. Against such men the Lord speaks in the Gospel of Luke: “Into whatever city you enter, choose who in it is worthy, and remain in his house, and do not pass from house to house (Luke 10).” If therefore a guest of one night must be chosen as worthy, and one must not pass “from house to house,” how much more must he who ought to be permanent resident and minister in one and the same church \[remain in his place\]?

# CAPUT XXXIII.

## On holy uniqueness.

This monstrous plurality of dignities and the like, gathered around the same person in the same or in different churches, is also condemned by a threefold and holy uniqueness — lest such a man be able to say with David: “Look upon me and have mercy on me, for I am alone and poor” (Psalm 24). If therefore, by analogy, you set riches against \[this argument\], I shall reply: that it is possible to be poor amid riches. The Apostle did not dread riches, but their effects. The first uniqueness is that of faith, sacraments, and morals, by which the distracted man should take care against heresies and schisms: “One faith, one baptism, one Lord,” etc. (Ephesians 4). “One thing I have asked of the Lord,” etc. (Psalm 26). “My dove is one” (Song of Songs 2). The ark was completed in one cubit. The second uniqueness is that of action. Hence Solomon: “My son, let not your deeds be many” (Sirach 11).
