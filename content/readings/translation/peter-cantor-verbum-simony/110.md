Likewise: Mark \[the Evangelist\] cut off his own thumb so that he would be disqualified from the priesthood, fearing lest he be forced into ordination as a priest — such was his reverence for celebrating the body of Christ, which he had nevertheless received sacramentally many times.

But now those to be ordained do not remove their natural members; rather they bring lawful and unlawful \[offerings\] so that they may be ordained, and more for the sake of gain than of devotion and divine service. Likewise in the *Lives of the Fathers*: a certain elder among the hermits, when he was chosen by them as a priest and had been ordained by the bishop of Egypt, said: although I am a priest, I will not celebrate \[Mass\] in my life — judging himself unworthy to celebrate the body of Christ, even though, not celebrating it himself, he would receive it from another. But now some celebrate the body of Christ far too frequently, as though it were a daily feast, out of greed, while others do so far too rarely out of sloth. Likewise in the Psalm: “Send out your light and your truth; these have led me and brought me into your tabernacles”; “I will go in to the altar of God” — celebrating or receiving the body of Christ — “to God who gives joy to my youth” (Psalm 42), that is, grants me glad youth, namely the youth of grace, in present reality, and the youth of glory in hope. The youth of nature too must be attended to in one who is to be ordained, unless gravity of character and holy life has supplied for the deficiency of age. Moreover, one who celebrates and stands at the altar while celebrating is as it were in paradise, forgetful of all that lies behind. Likewise: “I will wash my hands among the innocent,” and thus innocent and clean, following the example and imitation of those who are as angels, “I will encompass your altar, O Lord” (Psalm 25), so that I may celebrate your body. Likewise, when Joshua was in the territory of Jericho, meditating on something, “he saw an angel with a drawn sword,” to whom he said: Who are you? “Are you ours, or of our enemies?” Who, answering, said: “I am the prince of the host of heaven. Remove the sandals from your feet, for the place where you stand is holy ground” (Joshua 5). Consider therefore, you who approach the altar to celebrate and receive the Eucharist, that “the place where you stand is holy ground; a place of awe, the house of God and the gate of heaven” (Genesis 28). Fear “the angels ascending and descending there upon a ladder” with a drawn sword, lest they cut down your pos-

terity (3 Kings 21); fear lest “the axe be already laid to the root of the tree” (Matthew 3), because you approach unworthily. Fear the choir of angels standing by, bearing witness against you and ready to avenge their injury and the Lord’s wrath, if you approach unworthily; or \[fear them as\] bearing the sacrifice of praise of your prayers up before the sight of God on high, if you offer worthily. Whence \[Hildebertus writes\]:

*At the hour when the priest stands suppliant at the altar,*
*The very Son is immolated to the Father, yet remains.*

*The heavens open, the celestial choir is present, the depths are joined to the heights, the one who acts and the act become the same.*

(Hildebertus of Le Mans, *On the Mystery of the Mass*)
