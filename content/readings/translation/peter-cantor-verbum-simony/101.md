He showed also that every occasion of simony, and every facility for receiving even trifling gifts in exchange for spiritual things, must be rooted out and extirpated from the Church. Likewise: what is more dangerous than, as if blind, to allow God himself to be sold more shamefully than Judas sold him, while we clearly discern venality in the other sacraments but not in this one? As when some priests in a single day celebrate two complete masses, while others, ashamed and afraid to celebrate two in full, multiply one — doubling it, tripling it, and manifolding it — that is, celebrating it with many faces \[i.e., repeating it with different introits\]. Likewise: what is more shameful than, if no offering is made at the first face and first introit, to assume another face — indeed a third and a fourth — in order to elicit an offering? These men are like those who sing tales and deeds of valor. When they see that a song about Landric does not please their audience, they immediately begin to sing about Narcissus; and if that too gives no pleasure, they sing of another. The reason why the other ecclesiastical offices remain in their simplicity, purity, and original institution and are not doubled, whereas this one alone is doubled against its institution, is plainly this: because of the offering that is brought for it, and not for the others.

Likewise: that we may press further on the root — that is, the extirpation of simoniacal greed — “So will I bless thee in my life, namely in thy name,” not in the name of money, not of greed, “I will lift up my hands (Psalm 62):” he lifts up his hands in the honor and glory of the Lord who, persevering in good, seeks not his own glory, nor the world’s, but the Lord’s. As if he were saying: I begin from myself, “because I crucify myself with \[my\] vices and concupiscences (Galatians 5),” I preach the Crucified, I adore the Crucified, and — what is more — I purely consecrate the Crucified, receive him, and distribute him to others to eat. How then shall I, with hands crucified to the world and consecrating thee, O Christ, lift them up to any price save thee, the price of the world? How to gain — above all, to sin? Likewise, Simon Magus, who wished to be a Christian and to work miracles for the sake of profit, was repelled by Simon Peter. To avenge this repulse he said, as it were, to Peter: you repel me, and I shall triumph over you — indeed, over the whole body of the Church (Acts 8). “Even upon the very altars I shall place my throne, my couch, and my seat (Isaiah 14),” and my dominion; so that even with the angels present and as it were gathered in one corner of the altar to consecrate the body of the Lord, I, in another corner — with the minister of the altar, or rather with my own — shall consecrate it for a price. Greed holds dominion over this sacrament, which is nonetheless beyond all price. Likewise: even if I am driven from the altar, it is not for that reason that simoniacal greed, my daughter — daughter of the high priest — will be any the less \[present\]; rather, she will burst all the more into the Holy of Holies, setting everything upon the altars for sale. And what is wicked: from those very offerings altars are erected, holy places adorned, and monasteries of religious built — thus abusing this apostolic precept: “asking no questions for conscience’ sake (1 Corinthians 10)” — although the Apostle was speaking only to those who sometimes ate with idolaters in order to preach to them.
