Because of this sin the house of Jeroboam sinned, and was overthrown, and wiped from the face of the earth (1 Kings 13).” But was this \[act of Jeroboam’s\] simoniacal? It is certain that selling the legal priesthood was simony. Whether \[selling\] the priesthood of idols \[was simony\] is a question — unless perhaps we say that simony was present there for the reason that even “priests of the high places,” on account of the dignity of the priesthood, were necessarily obliged by their office to preach and to have uprightness of morals, just as true priests are.

Likewise the Lord in Luke, chapter 12: “Who do you think is the faithful servant,” or “steward and prudent?” (Luke 12) “Faithful” — fulfilling those things that are necessary for salvation: whence the Apostle says, “Entrust these things to faithful men who will be capable of teaching others also” (2 Timothy 2). “Capable” in life, knowledge, and eloquence. “And prudent” — so as not to “give what is holy to dogs” (Matthew 7). “Whom God has appointed” — by his authority and the ministry of the electors, that is, by canonical election. Otherwise \[such a one\] is not sent by the Lord, of whom, and of those like him, it is said: “As many as came” — that is, not sent by God — “are thieves and robbers” (John 10). There follows: “Over his household,” so that as he presides in dignity, so he may preside in the uprightness of morals. “That he may give them” — he is appointed over them not to receive from them milk and wool, oil and lees, but rather “to give them a measure of wheat,” \[that is,\] of the divine word, “according to the capacity of his hearers, and this in due season. For everything has its season. There is a time for silence, and a time for speaking” (Ecclesiastes 3). For preaching, if it is constant, becomes cheap. But those who are otherwise constituted over the Lord’s household are like the priests of Bel (Daniel 14), who, with the true door “Christ” broken through, enter — insofar as it lies in them — by furtive little doors (that is, by sordid gifts, counted money, carnal affection, and the like) to the altar, into the Holy of Holies, eating and devouring and carrying off the offerings of princes and peoples.

Against such persons may also be set what is said in Deuteronomy, chapter 20: “What man is there who has built a new house and has not dedicated it? Let him go and return to his house, lest he die in battle and another dedicate it. What man is there who has planted a vineyard and has not yet made it common, from which all may be permitted to eat? Let him go and return to his house, lest he die in battle and another enjoy the use of it. Or what man is there who has betrothed a wife and has not taken her? Let him go and return to his house, lest he die in battle and another take her.” But these men receive churches and ecclesiastical benefices and do not dedicate them by any work or any service, and so they die in battle. Often too they plant good conduct and doctrine in themselves, but have not yet made it common to others by example and by the word of preaching; and therefore let them return to their own house and church, if they have not done these things, before they are chosen for another.
