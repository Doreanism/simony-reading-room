# CHAPTER XXVIII.

## Against the Sale and Multiplication of Masses.

Toward rooting out the sale and multiplication of masses — which the sale of altars and the greed of ministers has engendered — \[let it be argued thus\]: if Christ was immolated for us but once, let him also be represented as once immolated, by his sole vicar on any given day (Hebrews 9).

Likewise: if Christ, offered once, was a sufficient sacrifice and redemption of the human race, it suffices for a single priest to represent once a day him who suffered once and was immolated once. “Whatsoever is more than this cometh of evil (Matthew 5).” Likewise: those who consecrate twice in a day “are again crucifying in themselves the Son of God, and making him a mockery (Hebrews 6).” Likewise Augustinus: receiving the Eucharist as a daily communion I neither praise nor blame; but I leave it to each one’s conscience — provided he is not conscious of any \[grave sin\], provided \[he brings\] a troubled spirit and a contrite heart (*On Ecclesiastical Dogmas*, c. 55). If therefore Augustinus barely grants a single reception in a day, and only to one thus humbled, with a contrite and devout heart — would he, if asked, have granted to anyone the right to consecrate twice in a day? By no means! — but he would have repelled such a one with all his force, and perhaps struck him with anathema. Likewise: to receive the Eucharist belongs to all Christians; to consecrate, to a few; therefore it is greater to consecrate than to receive; but it is detestable and exceedingly disordered to repeat \[communion\] in a single day — that is, to have the body of the Lord given to anyone more than once in a day; therefore it is disordered and perilous to repeat in a single day what is the greater thing, namely to consecrate twice — especially when necessity does not compel it. Likewise: just as in Exodus the children of Israel were commanded that for each person only one omer of manna should be gathered daily, except on the sixth day: “for then they should gather double portions of food, that is, two omers per person (Exodus 16),” so that the omer gathered on the day of preparation would suffice for the Sabbath, on which they were not permitted to gather or to do any work. But whoever had gathered more than an omer, and beyond what was sufficient for eating, found what he had collected rotten and full of worms — so it is to be feared lest the sacrament of the altar, consecrated twice in a single day by one and the same \[priest\], rot — not in the sacrament itself, I say, but in the conscience of the one who consecrates it — on account of his sins and crimes in thus doubling it.
