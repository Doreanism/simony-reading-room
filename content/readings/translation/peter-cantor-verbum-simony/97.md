Since, then, the first millstone is not meritorious without the second — indeed, without it, it is in a sense forbidden — all the more is the third millstone of concupiscence, or a fourth millstone of vainglory, forbidden from every work, so that not even the third may be mingled with either of the two. For just as a divided mind does not obtain \[its request\], so neither does a work thus divided, in which there is no division under God or for God’s sake, but against God — so that it is no longer a division and participation, but a total seizure of that work by the devil. But if a work of the right hand, performed for the cause or love of acquiring money or receiving it anew, begets simony: what if it is done out of fear of losing money already possessed, or of receiving \[new money\]? Will it be simoniacal? It is agreed that it is a lesser evil. Likewise: it is read (Exodus 2) that Amram, a man of the house of Levi, fathered a son, Moses, by his wife; and seeing that he was beautiful, hid him for three months. You too, therefore, hide your works from cupidity and vainglory, lest they be seized by the Egyptians and submerged, and lest, made womanish by mingling with the left hand, they be reserved for the work of the devil. Likewise: “Enter your chamber and, having closed the door” against cupidity and vainglory, “pray” by acting well, “to your Father” (Matthew 6). Likewise: since a twofold invitatory is read — the psalm and the signal bell — a third is found in the Church more efficacious than the others: the gift. Likewise: The Lord commands the Passover to be celebrated “in the unleavened bread of sincerity and truth” (1 Corinthians 5). Augustinus: Without any corruption of vices, in the truth of good works, which belong to the right hand. If the left hand is mingled with these, the Passover is then celebrated with the old leaven. The priest celebrates the Passover as often as he approaches the altar to immolate the Paschal Lamb. Likewise: “Therefore I loved your commandments above gold and topaz” (Psalm 118). Augustinus (on this psalm, verse 1): Not on account of gold and topaz, or any temporal thing — as a Jew needed to be enticed by temporal things — how much less so a Christian, if he performs spiritual acts for temporal things, who is bound to love God’s commandments above them, not for their sake. Whose life ought to be a cross and martyrdom, so that he attends to this: “You shall weep and mourn” (John 16) — not earthly consolation — so that he may await the eternal blessedness to be conferred on him in the future. Likewise Augustinus (*Enchiridion* ch. 66 and book II of *On the Merit and Remission of Sins* ch. 34): For this reason the Lord did not will to remove punishment by baptism as he removed guilt — lest belief in God be made too comfortable, motivated, that is, by the removal of punishment rather than by love of faith and justice.
