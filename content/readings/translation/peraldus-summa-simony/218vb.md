# On Simony

And on the eight things that contribute to its detestation: Chapter 6.

Concerning which we shall proceed in this manner. First we shall speak of simony. Second, of the sin of those who build up the interests of their relatives. Third, of the sin of choristers. Fourth, of the sin of mercenaries \[hired clergy\]. Fifth, we shall touch upon the signs of avarice that appear in the Church. Sixth, of the sin of those who unworthily confect \[the Eucharist\]. Seventh, of the vice of proprietorship among those in cloistered life. Eighth, of the avarice of those who knowingly conceal \[sins\]. And of simony we shall speak first.

## On Simony

First we shall set forth what pertains to the detestation of simony, and second the divisions and principal species of simony. There are eight things that contribute to its detestation. The first is that saying of Pope Damasus: “All crimes, in comparison with the heresy of simony, are reckoned as virtually nothing.” In this saying simony is shown to be detestable in two ways: because it is shown to exceed all other crimes, and because it is compared to heresy. In many other places as well, this sin is called a heresy. Hence Pope Nicolaus \[II\]: “We decree, establish, and confirm that the threefold heresy of simony stands condemned.” And on Matthew 21, “And he overturned the chairs of those selling doves,” the Gloss says — on the phrase about those who sold doves, etc. — the Interlinear Gloss says: “He specifically condemns the heresy of simony.” And in Acts 8, where Petrus says to Symon Magus, “Repent of this wickedness of yours,” the Interlinear Gloss says: “that is, of the twofold evil of covetousness and of perverse belief.” And in that passage it is shown that simony consists in placing something of inestimable value at a base price, and in separating the body of the Church from its head as heretics do, and in reckoning — whether in thought or in deed — that the gift of the Holy Spirit is subject to the dominion of money.
