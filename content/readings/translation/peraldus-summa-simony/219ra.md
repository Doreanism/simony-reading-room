so in simony one acts against grace when he purchases the work of grace, since he has been redeemed by grace. And just as on the Day of Judgment the sodomite will be struck speechless — according to the interpretation of the name \[Sodom, meaning “mute”\] — because he was an enemy of nature, which had been his spouse (for as far as it lies in him, the sodomite destroys the human race), so too the simoniac will be struck speechless, who was an enemy of the grace by which he had been redeemed. And just as according to the law of nature sodomy is judged either the greatest sin or one of the greatest, because it is against nature, so in the age of grace, according to the law of the Gospel, simony is judged a sin of the greatest magnitude, because it is properly contrary to grace. For it directly contravenes that saying in Matthew 10: “Freely you have received; freely give.” Hence simoniacs are rightly symbolized by the Sodomites who sought to break down the door of the house of Loth, of whom it is said in Genesis 19: “And they were on the very point of breaking down the door” — that is, the law. The third thing that contributes to the detestation of simony is the great zeal that we read the saints had against this sin. Hence in 4 Kings 5 \[i.e., 2 Kings 5\], Heliseus said to Giezi: “The leprosy of Naaman shall cling to you and to your descendants forever.” And the Lord in his first coming is not recorded to have burned with anger against any other sin as he did against this one. Hence he is recorded twice to have driven out the buyers and sellers from the Temple: the first expulsion is found in John 2, the second in Matthew 21. For he had come to be judged, not to judge, and to bear sins with gentleness, not to punish them; and yet on account of this sin — indeed, on account of a \[mere\] figure of this sin — he refused to maintain his gentleness, but as if in wrath, with a whip made of cords, he overturned the tables of the money-changers and the chairs of those selling doves. And although his first coming was one of mercy, nevertheless on account of this sin justice intervened. Hence Bernardus \[says\] to Pope Eugenius, speaking of the plague of simony: “Look to the Master,” he says. “He does not prepare his ears to listen but a whip to strike. He neither speaks nor accepts words, for he does not sit judging but pursues and punishes. For he does not conceal the cause, saying: ‘You have made my house of prayer a house of commerce.’ Therefore do likewise. Let such traders blush before your face, if possible; if not, let them not bring their coins but rather lose confidence. Let them hide their silver from you, knowing you more ready to pour it out than to receive it.” On account of the zeal the Lord had against this sin, it is written in the Psalms \[Psalm 69:9\] in his person: “Zeal for your house has consumed me.” Even the sin of Iudas, God was willing to tolerate patiently, but this sin he would not tolerate. Petrus also had great zeal against this sin when he said to Symon Magus: “May your money go with you to perdition,” as is recorded in Acts 8. How great a zeal the Church also has against this sin is sufficiently clear to those who possess expertise in canon law.
