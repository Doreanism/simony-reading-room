## Sixth, what contributes to the detestation of simony is this,
that this sin renders a person hateful to God and to men. Whence Ecclesiasticus 20: “He who assumes power for himself unjustly shall be hated.” The Gloss says: “By God and by men.” For frequently, even though the simony is secret, it nevertheless renders a person hateful to men. For God punishes him in this: that he has no favor among men.

## Seventh, what contributes to the detestation of simony is this,
that it corrupts the root. Whence it renders everything that follows poisoned or at least useless. To this may be referred that passage in Hosea 9: “Their root is dried up; they shall by no means bear fruit.”

## Eighth and last, what contributes to the detestation of simony is this,
that simoniacs are saved only with great difficulty. And this for two reasons. First, because of the magnitude of the sin. Second, because they must resign the benefice that they acquired simoniacally. Whence they are like the devil’s fish-traps. A fish that has entered a trap does not easily return to the place through which it entered, and it does not exit by any other way except as a catch. So too a person does not easily renounce a benefice that he holds simoniacally, and he is not saved in any other way. Whence he already has, as it were, one foot in hell. Therefore a person ought to take great care how he enters the church. For unless he watches himself at the entrance, he virtually sets his foot in hell. Therefore Ecclesiastes 4 says: “Guard your foot when you enter the house of the Lord.” One who buys an ecclesiastical benefice is like someone who would buy the privilege of having a great mass of earth fall upon him, crushing him and not allowing him to rise. Concerning the difficulty of being saved that exists in simoniacs, that passage from Jeremiah 17 may be cited: “The sin of Iuda is written with an iron stylus, engraved with an adamantine point upon the breadth of their heart.” By Iuda the clergy are understood, whose duty it is to devote themselves to the confession \[that is, the proclamation\] of divine praise. To them belongs the sin of simony, which is indicated to be written as though indelibly — by the fact that it says “with an iron stylus,” which is a fitting instrument for writing, and by the fact that it is written on something like an adamantine fingernail, that is, something hard and smooth. For writing made on such material lasts a long time. But writing that is indelible can still be lost; therefore he adds: “Engraved upon the breadth of their heart.” And he shows that this writing is on the tablet of the heart, which cannot be lost by them or abandoned. To this same difficulty pertains what Petrus said to Symon Magus in Acts 8: “Repent of this your wickedness, and pray to God, that perhaps this thought of your heart may be forgiven you. For I see that you are in the gall of bitterness and in the bond of iniquity.”
