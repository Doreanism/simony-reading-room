The fourth consideration that contributes to the detestation of simony is the manifold insult that this sin inflicts upon God. And we shall set forth at present twelve of these insults.

## The first is:
that it sells God — and this in two ways. First, because it sells the grace of the Holy Spirit. Hence, on that passage in John 2, “To those who sold doves,” etc., the gloss says: “The Holy Spirit appeared in the form of a dove. Thus the dove signifies the Spirit, whom simoniacs sell. But the Holy Spirit is God; therefore simoniacs sell God.” They also sell God in another way: by selling Christ in the sacrament of the altar when they celebrate for money. Nor is this a small insult. There is no one so poor in this world who would not be greatly outraged if someone wished to lead him to the marketplace and there put him up for sale like a beast of burden. Why, then, is it not an insult to God when he is put up for sale by those who above all ought to honor him? It should be enough for such people that Christ was sold once for their sins. These aforementioned \[simoniacs\] appear to be worse than Iudas in five respects. They sell the immortal and already glorified one, whom Iudas sold as mortal and in a state of humility. But the insult done to someone in a state of honor is greater than that done in a state of humility — just as an affront to a knight is greater than one offered to a squire. Nor do they sell him only once, as Iudas did, but more than a hundred times. They also sell him for a lesser price than Iudas did. They even believe him to be God — which Iudas did not believe when he sold him for thirty coins. Iudas, moreover, when he repented of this sin, led by remorse returned the thirty pieces of silver he had received for Christ; but simoniacs cannot be brought to repentance so as to restore what they have acquired through simony.

## The second insult is
to regard God himself as a seller, which is no small affront. For if some king or count of great liberality were reproached for selling his garments or his arms, he would consider it a great wrong. What wonder, then, if he who is the most liberal bestower of all things — who made everything he made in order to give it away, some things so that he might bestow \[his gifts\] upon them, and other things so that he might bestow them \[as gifts\], who gives his gifts to the good and the wicked, to the grateful and the ungrateful, to friends and enemies, who asks that petitions be made to him — \[what wonder\] if he takes it ill? Petrus alluded to this insult when he said to Symon: “Because you thought the gift of God could be obtained with money” \[Acts 8\] — as if to say: you regarded the giver as a seller. And in John 2 the Lord says: “Do not make my Father’s house a house of trade.” He who regards God as a merchant makes God’s house a house of trade.

## The third insult is:
that \[simony\] equates the gifts of God with a paltry price: it equates with base profit that which is precious in God’s sight, that which is the price of the kingdom of heaven — and in this it also shows contempt for the giver himself.
