## On the divisions of simony

After these matters, we must set forth the divisions that serve to make simony detestable and that pertain to simony. Of these we shall present only three. The first division is this: among those who commit simony, some commit it by buying, as Symon Magus did — these are properly called simoniacs. Others commit it by selling, as Giezi did — and these are called Giezites. Sometimes, however, both groups are broadly called simoniacs. The second division is according to the diversity of gifts by means of which simony is committed.

## On the fourfold simony

Simony is committed by means of a fourfold gift: namely, when someone obtains something spiritual through money, through flattery, through carnal entreaties, or through servile compliance. Concerning the first, we read in 3 Kings 13 \[1 Kings 13:33\]: “Whoever wished, he filled his hand and became a priest of the high places.” And it continues: “On account of this the house of Hieroboam sinned and was overthrown and blotted out from the face of the earth.” If the selling of an idolatrous priesthood was so avenged by the Lord, how will the selling of the true priesthood be avenged? Flattery, indeed, is greatly to be feared, for it penetrates a person’s heart and draws him in imperceptibly — which is why it is called oil in the Psalm \[Psalm 141:5\]: “Let not the oil of the sinner anoint my head.” And elsewhere \[Psalm 109:18\]: “It entered like water into his inward parts, and like oil into his bones.” And let his speech be a curse. For flattery is both oil and a curse: oil insofar as it delights, a curse insofar as it prepares torments. Hence the Gloss on Malachias says: “Things that wrongly delight are called a curse, because they prepare torments.” From carnal entreaties, moreover, one must diligently guard oneself. Hence Bernardus says: “Where it is not lawful to give, what place is there for the one who asks? One person asks on behalf of another; another perhaps asks on his own behalf. If you ask on behalf of another, you are suspect; if he asks on his own behalf, he is already condemned.” The Lord gave us an example of not yielding to carnal entreaties in John 2, where he says to his mother, who wished him to perform a miracle: “What is that to me and to you, woman?” — indicating that he did not have from her the power to work miracles. And in Matthew 20, he did not grant the request of the mother of the sons of Zebedaeus, who asked that he give this dignity to Iacobus and Iohannes — that one might sit at his right hand in his kingdom and the other at his left. We also have an example of not asking for a benefice to be obtained from temporal \[powers\] in Genesis 18, where Abraham asked God not to destroy Sodom. For he did not wish to ask God specifically about his nephew Loth, that he might be delivered from the conflagration. And the cantor of Paris, of blessed memory, used to say that he would scarcely have asked God to obtain a benefice for his nephew — he who refused to ask God to deliver his nephew from the fire.
