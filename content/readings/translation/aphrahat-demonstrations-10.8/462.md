He who takes from it cannot consume \[the whole\], and when he has given to \[others\], nothing will be lacking. If you catch fire from a blaze with a lamp, so that you may kindle several other lamps from it, the blaze from which you take it is not lessened, nor does the lamp from which you light many others fail. One man cannot carry off the entire treasure of the king, nor can a thirsty man, while drawing water, drain the spring; nor can a man stationed on the highest peak reach what is near and what is far with his eye alike; nor, if he were to stand and number the stars of heaven, could he attain to the powers of the heavens: so neither can he who approaches the worship of God carry off the whole \[treasure\]; and although he should take very much, the thing will not appear to have been diminished, nor, if he should give away all that he has received, will it in any way have been used up or wholly consumed. And remember, dearly beloved, what I wrote to you in the first discourse on Faith, namely that he who has received freely ought in turn to give freely, just as he received, as the Lord says: “Freely you have received, freely give”; for if anyone should wish to retain what he has taken, even what he has received will be taken away from him.
