Yet I do not approve of an oath \[as a condition of membership\] because of the danger of perjury. Furthermore, if it has been ordained that all the brethren shall assemble once or twice a year to deliberate about the salvation of souls, to hear the word of God, to celebrate Masses and prayers for the living and the dead, and to kindle devotion — and for this purpose they each year contribute a fixed sum from which they may be refreshed and provide for the poor — this is good, provided their intentions are pure and fixed principally upon God. For, as Augustinus says: “Far be it that those things which we do or have for a good and lawful purpose should be imputed to us if some evil should happen from them beyond our intention.” *Causa* 23, q. 5, c. *De occidendis*.

*De occidendis*... “Far be it that those things which we do or have for a good and lawful purpose should be imputed to us if, through them, any evil should befall anyone beyond our will. Otherwise we should keep no household or farm implements, lest someone use them to kill himself or another; no trees should be planted, lest someone hang himself from one; no window should be made, lest someone throw himself headlong through it. What more shall I recount, since I could never finish by recounting? For what is there among the lawful and good usages of men from which harm cannot also be inflicted?”

## §. XII.

## Question: If a layman gives something to one who has no right in an election but who is maliciously impeding it, so that he will desist from such obstruction — is this simony?

It seems clearly that it is. *Extra de simonia*, c. *De hoc*. Likewise, because both parties had the will \[for the transaction\]: the layman at least had the will to sell, and the cleric to buy. *Causa* 1, q. 1, c. *Qui Audit*; *Causa* 1, q. 3, c. *Salvator*.

On the contrary, it seems that it is not simony; for he gave in order to redeem himself from harassment, which appears to have been lawful. *Extra de simonia*, c. *Dilectus filius*; and the Apostle, Ephesians \[5\]:16: “Redeeming the time, because the days are evil.”

Solution: the matter is doubtful. I believe, however, that simony has been committed, because no right had yet been acquired by him; but once a right had been acquired by him, I would say otherwise — which is the case treated by the last-cited authorities.

*Tanta*: “The stain of this crime is so great that even slaves against their masters, and all manner of criminals, are admitted as accusers. Likewise, every sinner may sing Mass except the simoniac, whom anyone — that he may be removed from the order wrongly received — may accuse, even a harlot.”

And \[a person in such a case\] ought rather to allow himself to be excommunicated than to receive orders from such a bishop. Arg. *Extra de sententia excomm.*, c. *Inquisitioni*.

*Inquisitioni*: “Responding to your inquiry, we believe a distinction must be drawn: whether one of the spouses knows for certain of an impediment to the marriage on account of which he cannot engage in carnal intercourse without mortal sin — even though he cannot prove it before the Church — or whether he does not know it for certain but only believes it. In the first case he ought rather humbly to endure the sentence of excommunication than to commit the mortal sin of carnal intercourse...”

What if \[someone\] did not know that \[the bishop\] was a simoniac, even though he was?

Say that, however hidden it may be and however ignorant \[the recipient\] may be, he does not receive execution and needs a dispensation — and this in detestation of the crime. *Causa* 1, q. 1, c. *Si qui a Simoniacis*.

And although I say thus of one who was ignorant, it is otherwise with one who was coerced: for he does receive execution. *Causa* 1, q. 1, c. *Constat*.

*Constat*: “It is established that many have suffered violence and been unwillingly drawn in and, though resisting, ordained by heretics. But if such a person, after such an ordination, did not participate when they performed the sacraments, and did not share in communion with them, and immediately when they departed renounced their wicked assembly \[and\] returned to the Church — such a person may have some grounds for excuse. But those who returned after a month or more, knowing themselves to have been ordained by heretics — even if they received nothing from them — are guilty of usurping a dignity.”

And the reason is that the will of one who was coerced is more clearly established than that of one who was ignorant. *Extra de simonia*, c. *Sicut tuis*; and above, same section, §. *Quid*, at the end. This, however, is to be understood of one who was conditionally coerced; for one who was absolutely coerced does not receive the character. *Extra de baptismo*, c. *Majores*, §. *Item quaeritur*, v. *Propter quod*.

*Majores*: “Propter quod \[on which account\], some not unreasonably distinguish between an unwilling man and an unwilling woman, and between the coerced and the coerced, \[saying\] that one who is violently dragged by terrors and torments, and receives the sacrament of baptism lest he incur harm — such a person (like one who approaches baptism in a feigned manner) receives the impressed character of Christianity, and he, as one conditionally willing even though not absolutely willing, must be compelled to observe the Christian faith. In this sense must be understood the decree of the Council of Toledo, which says that those who were already in times past compelled to Christianity (as was done in the days of the most religious Prince Sisebut) — since it is now established that they have been associated with the divine sacraments, and have received the grace of baptism, and have been anointed with chrism, and have been made partakers of the body of the Lord...”
