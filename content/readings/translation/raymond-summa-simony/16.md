The same note is sounded in the third book of Kings \[1 Kings\]: that the wife of Jeroboam, when his son was ill, went to Ahijah the man of God, and brought him ten loaves of bread, a cracknel, and a jar of honey — for these are to be reckoned as provision for the prophet rather than a gift for the prophecy. Yet those accustomed to going to soothsayers (for there were many diviners and soothsayers in Israel) may well have thought the same of prophets too, out of the custom of evil, and wished to offer to holy men what they were accustomed to offer to diviners. Hence in the fourth book of Kings \[2 Kings\] we read that Elisha came to Damascus, and Benhadad king of Syria was sick, and they told him, saying: The man of God has come here. And Benhadad said: Take gifts with you, and go to meet the man of God, and inquire of the Lord through him, asking whether I shall recover from this illness. So Hazael went to meet him, bringing with him gifts, and all the good things of Damascus, forty camel-loads. But it is not to be believed that he \[Elisha\] accepted \[them\], just as we read of that same Elisha that Naaman, cleansed of his leprosy, offered him gifts, saying: I pray you, accept a blessing from your servant. And a priest may hire out his labor either by the day or by the year, just as hired priests do. Extra, Ne praelati vices suas, c. Querelam.

If, however, he has provisions, he is nonetheless not bound to do so, and must either sing \[Mass\] freely or refrain; otherwise he would appear to act out of avarice. And although this might perhaps not be simoniacal (since no one is bound to serve at his own expense — Causa 12, q. 2, c. Caritatem; extra de simonia, c. Cum sit), yet it is base gain, or at the least bears the appearance of evil, from which the Apostle teaches us to abstain. And \[see\] Causa 1, q. 1, Emendari.

Once one Mass has been celebrated, whoever celebrates a second for money or for the flattery of men does not escape condemnation, without exception. De consecratione, Dist. 1, c. Sufficit.

\[From the canon\] Sufficit \[It suffices\]: It suffices for a priest to celebrate one Mass in a day, because Christ suffered once and redeemed the whole world. It is no small thing to celebrate one Mass, and he is very blessed who can celebrate one worthily. Some of us, however, celebrate one for the dead and another for the day, if necessity requires. But those who, for money or for the flattery of worldly people, presume to celebrate several Masses in a single day, I do not think will escape condemnation.

Here the question arises consequently about processions that conventual and other churches hold at the obsequies of the deceased, given or promised a fixed sum of money, which they would not otherwise hold.

To this I say, saving better judgment: if they have not made a statute about this, \[the answer is\] the same as above in the first question, and by the same legal authorities. If, however, a statute has been made, and in these words or their equivalent — that whoever leaves \[a bequest\] to the convent shall be entitled to processions — then, unless they were already otherwise bound to do this, it is not simony; provided, however, that their intent was to receive it on account of the labor involved. Arg. extra de simonia, c. Cum sit Romana, and similar \[canons\]. If, however, the statute was made in these words or their equivalent — that no procession shall be held for anyone unless he has left the convent ten \[solidi\] — the statute is not valid, because it removes the office of piety, namely that no procession at all shall be held freely or for a smaller sum. Arg. extra de jurejurando, c. Sicut 1 and 2.

\[From the decretal\] Sicut ex litteris \[As from the letter\]: As we have learned from the letter of your discretion, R., prior of your monastery, inflamed by the heat of anger, confirmed by oath that he would no longer remain in the cloister of that monastery to which he had bound himself by the bond of profession, \[dwelling there\] with monk G. (and further below): We reply that it was not lawful for the said monk to make a rash oath, especially since it would break the fidelity of his profession and void the purpose of his stability. And therefore let your solicitude make provision for him, that such a penance be enjoined upon him that any other \[monk\], deterred by his example, will fear to do anything similar. Let him, however, carry out his penance in the cloister which he could not lawfully abjure, with perpetual stability.

\[From the decretal\] Sicut nostris est auribus \[As it has been intimated to our ears\]: The church of Todi being vacant, its canons and a bishop with them, while he was still in minor orders, made certain oaths to the detriment of episcopal rights, before any discussion of the election of a pontiff had been undertaken (and further below): For his ill-considered oath, we have caused a fitting penance to be imposed upon the bishop (and further below): Because what is attempted against the utility of the Church is to be called not oaths but rather perjuries. We command that you inquire diligently of the canons themselves concerning the truth of the aforesaid matters, and if you find them to be true, that whatever you find to have been attempted in such a manner to the detriment of episcopal rights, you reduce to its proper state, appeal notwithstanding.

What of confraternities constituted in such a way that no one is received unless he promises, or even swears, to give ten \[solidi\] annually?

I say that if they are established for an honorable purpose — for instance, for feeding the poor, ransoming captives, or similar causes — it is not simony, and they are not to be condemned. Indeed, the Council of Gangra declares that anyone who despises such confraternities is to be anathematized. Dist. 42, c. Si quis despicit; and the universal Church approves.
