Simony is so called from Simon Magus, the first in the New Testament to perpetrate this crime, because he wished to purchase the grace of the Holy Spirit from Saint Peter; just as Gehazi in the Old Testament wished to sell the grace of healing to Naaman the Syrian. Hence, properly speaking, the seller of spiritual things is called a “Gehazite” after Gehazi, and the buyer a “simoniac” after Simon; but in common usage both are called simoniacs.

## §. II.

## The Ways in Which Simony Is Committed.

Simony is committed in three ways, as Gregory says on that passage of Isaiah 33 — *Blessed is he who shakes his hands free of every gift* — noting that it does not say merely “of a gift” but “of every gift”: for one thing is a gift from the hand, another from service, another from the tongue. A gift from the hand is money. A gift from service is service wrongfully rendered. A gift from the tongue is favor. *Causa* 1, q. 1, c. *Sunt nonnulli*.

There are some who do not receive monetary rewards for conferring orders, yet who bestow holy orders out of human favor or praise, and from the very act of bestowing seek only the return of praise. These men, assuredly, do not give freely what they received freely, since they seek the gift of favor in exchange for the service of the sacred office they render. Hence, when the Prophet described the just man, he said: *Blessed is he who shakes his hands free of every gift* — not merely “of a gift,” but “of every gift” — for a gift from service is service wrongfully rendered; a gift from the hand is money; a gift from the tongue is favor. Therefore, he who confers holy orders then shakes his hands free of every gift when, in matters divine, he seeks not only no money but not even human favor.

If, however, there is doubt whether something was given freely or by agreement or with corrupt intent, three things are chiefly to be considered: the character and standing of the giver and receiver — whether, for instance, it was given by a poor man to a rich man or the reverse, or by a rich man to one of equal wealth — the size of the gift and whether the thing given is of great or small value, and the time of the donation — whether it is made in pressing necessity or at another time; and according to these circumstances, a presumption is to be formed for or against \[simony\]. Simoniacs who have ordained or been ordained simoniacally are deprived of their proper rank according to the ecclesiastical canons. Simoniacs also who have been ordained simoniacally by non-simoniacs are likewise removed from the office wrongfully received. But those who have been ordained non-simoniacally by simoniacs we mercifully permit to remain in office by the laying on of hands, out of necessity for the times. (But this is to be understood of those who are ordained by simoniacs whom they did not know to be simoniacs: these are rendered simoniacal not by guilt of crime but by the ordination of a simoniac. Concerning them Pope Urban says:)

POPE URBAN. If any have been ordained non-simoniacally by simoniacs, and if they can prove that at the time of their ordination they did not know those men to be simoniacs, and that those men were at that time held to be Catholics in the Church, we mercifully sustain their ordinations — provided their lives are commendable. But those who have knowingly permitted themselves to be consecrated — or rather execrated — by simoniacs, we declare their consecration to be wholly void.

## §. III.

## Cases in which a gift from the hand, a gift from service, or a gift from the tongue may lawfully be given for spiritual things.

Now there are cases in which a gift from the hand may lawfully be given for spiritual things, sometimes a gift from service, sometimes a gift from the tongue.

From the Council of Toledo XI: Whatever is bestowed by the consolation of invisible grace must never be sold for money or any kind of reward, since the Lord says: *Freely you have received, freely give* (Matthew 10); and therefore, whoever henceforth, being placed in an ecclesiastical order, shall receive any price or reward — unless voluntarily offered — for baptizing and confirming the faithful, for chrism, or for promotion in holy orders, in pursuit of such gain, etc.

A gift from the hand may be given in five cases. The first: if it is offered freely, not in exchange for a spiritual thing. *Causa* 1, q. 1, c. *Quidquid invisibilis*.
