And thus Joannes and many others judge, with whom I agree. Hugutio, however, understands all of this \[the foregoing passage\] to pertain to those ordained by heretics. Some, however, say that if \[a candidate\] cannot prove that the bishop is a simoniac, he must receive orders from him if the bishop so commands, even though he knows him to be a simoniac; but that he cannot exercise \[the orders\] without — as some say — a dispensation from the Pope, or, as others say, from any superior, which must be sought in secret so that the bishop may not be defamed. This \[is said\] of a simoniac in his \[own\] order; it is otherwise with respect to another kind \[of simoniac\], according to the distinction made above, same section, §. *Circa paenam*.

What if \[someone\] believes the bishop to be a simoniac on account of the report of certain persons?

I respond: he ought not to credit it; but while the belief persists, he ought not to receive orders from him, and he should lay aside the error. However, in the case of \[an order\] already received from him \[the bishop\] at a time when he had heard nothing about him, he may minister. *Extra de simonia*, c. *Per tuas*, 2.

## §. XIV.

Suppose that someone offers his goods to the Church purely and simply, yet with the principal intention that through the temporal goods he offers he may be able to acquire spiritual \[benefits\]; and the clerics who admit him as a brother would not admit him if they did not receive temporal advantages — will there be simony?

To this Innocentius responds that without doubt both parties — the one and the others alike — are judged guilty before the strict Judge, who is the searcher of hearts and knower of secrets. Hence some say that, since \[the canon\] does not expressly declare them simoniacs, this is not simony; yet they are bound to an equal penalty on account of intention joined with consequent act. Arg. *Extra de Bigamis*, c. *Nuper*.

*Nuper*: “Your fraternity has lately brought to us for counsel (& below)... We respond to this question as follows: that with those clerics who, insofar as lay in them, have contracted matrimony with second wives, it is not permissible to dispense as \[one would\] with bigamists — even though they are not bigamists in truth — not on account of any defect of the sacrament, but on account of the disposition of intention joined with consequent act.”

Others, who hold that intention alone makes a man a simoniac, say plainly that this is simony. *Causa* 1, q. 1, c. *Quicumque studet*, and p. 7, c. *Reperiuntur*.

*Quicumque studet*: “Whoever strives to purchase holy orders by giving a price... is not a priest, but in vain merely desires to be called one.”

## Just as intention alone makes a man a usurer.

*Causa* 14, q. 3, c. 1, or, *Si faeneraveris*.

*Si faeneraveris*: “If you have lent to a man — that is, if you have given your money on loan from whom you expect to receive back more than you gave — not the money alone, but something more than you gave; whether that be wheat, or wine, or oil, or anything else whatsoever; if you expect to receive back more than you gave, you are a usurer, and in this are to be reproached, not praised.”

All of this, however, is understood when the Church or monastery is well-provided; otherwise, if the monastery were so meagre or oppressed by poverty that its revenues barely or insufficiently sustained its present members, they might \[in such a case\] not only have such an intention, but I believe they may also openly say: “We do not have enough for ourselves; we gladly receive you \[into\] spiritual \[fellowship\], but we cannot \[sustain you\] in temporal matters unless we have additional resources” — and then let him offer himself and his goods. *Extra de simonia*, c. *Tua nos*.

*Tua nos*: “Your prudence has led you to seek our counsel (& below)... We respond to your devotion as follows: if anyone bestows or offers his goods to the clerics with a condition or pact, that he may thereafter retain them in exchange for a prebend and be admitted as a canon, such an offering or reception cannot take place without the vice of simony, since in such matters every pact or agreement must cease, in accordance with the canonical sanctions. But if he offers purely and without any pact or condition, humbly asking that he be admitted as a canon and be permitted to retain his goods for his prebend, and the clerics of the same Church consent purely — such a reception may take place without scruple of simoniacal wickedness. Although we have said this is how the matter is to be answered, because it has been given to us to judge only of manifest matters, yet if he who makes such a gift is led by the intention of acquiring spiritual benefits through the temporal goods he offers, and the clerics who admit him as a brother would not admit him if they did not receive temporal advantages — without doubt both the one and the others are judged guilty before the strict Judge, who is the searcher of hearts and the knower of secrets.”

But what is said in *Extra de simonia*, c. *Quoniam*, v. *Paupertatis praetextu*, is to be understood when this was said in fraud. Hence it would be advisable to counsel those entering \[such arrangements\] that in this matter they should correct their intention, so that they give principally for the sake of God and in order not to be a burden to the house. Arg. *Causa* 16, q. 6, c. *De lapsis*.

But are those so received bound to renounce \[the spiritual benefits\], and those who received \[the goods\] bound to make restitution of what they thus received? No; because this kind of mortal simony is expiated by condign penance. *Extra de simonia*, c. *Mandata*.
