The Savior foretold in the Gospel that toward the end of the age false apostles and false prophets would arise and lead many astray... Whoever, therefore, sells or buys ecclesiastical goods — which are gifts of God, since they are given by God to the faithful and by the faithful to God, and are received freely from him, and therefore ought to be given freely — for his own profit, esteems with that same Simon that the gift of God may be possessed for money. Therefore, because he bestows or acquires these same goods not for the purpose for which they were instituted, but for his own profit, by the service of his tongue, or undue compliance, or the giving of money, he is a simoniac; since the chief intention of Simon was solely the cause of money, that is, avarice — which is idolatry, as the Apostle Paul says, etc.

Those who buy and sell in the temple Christ drives out, because he condemns those who grant the laying on of hands for a gift, as well as those who strive to purchase the gift of God.

\[From the decretal\] Cum in Ecclesiae corpore \[In the body of the Church\] (and further below): It is exceedingly horrible that in certain churches venality is said to hold sway, so that something is required for the installation of bishops or abbots, or any ecclesiastical persons whatsoever in their seat, or for the introduction of priests into a church, as well as for burials and obsequies of the dead, for the blessings of those to be married, or for other sacraments. Many believe this to be lawful because they judge the law of death to have prevailed through long custom, not heeding that crimes are so much the graver the longer they have held an unhappy soul in bondage. Lest, therefore, these things be done henceforth, we strictly forbid that anything be demanded for the installation of ecclesiastical persons in their seat, or for the institution of priests, or for the burial of the dead, or for the blessing of those to be married, or for the conferring or conferral of other sacraments. If anyone shall presume to act contrary to this, let him know that he shall have his portion with Gehazi.

\[From the decretal\] Ad aures nostras \[To our hearing\]: Know that it has come to our attention that when C. of Senevilla had been bound by the bond of excommunication on account of a debt he owed, he satisfied his creditors; but when those who had pronounced the excommunication demanded ten pounds for his absolution, he was unable to obtain the benefit of absolution. Since it is therefore unworthy and contrary to ecclesiastical reason that the benefit of absolution be purchased, we command your discretion that, if the aforesaid C. has paid his debt, you cause him to be absolved freely, if those who excommunicated him have not done so when asked.

If, however, laymen are accustomed by pious custom to offer something at burials, or at the blessing of those to be married, or at the obsequies of the dead, and similar occasions — even though these things cannot be demanded by priests, nor should the sacraments be delayed on their account — after the fact, once the bishop has learned the truth, they ought to be compelled to observe the praiseworthy custom. Extra de simonia: Ad Apostolicam.

\[From the decretal\] Ad Apostolicam \[To the apostolic\] hearing: It has come by frequent report that certain clerics demand and extort money for the obsequies of the dead, and for the blessings of those to be married, and similar occasions; and if by chance their greed has not been satisfied, they fraudulently interpose fictitious impediments. On the other hand, certain laymen, out of the leaven of heretical wickedness, strive to break a praiseworthy custom introduced into holy Church by the pious devotion of the faithful, under the pretext of canonical piety. Wherefore, concerning these matters, we forbid wicked exactions to be made and command that pious customs be observed, decreeing that ecclesiastical sacraments be conferred freely. But those who maliciously strive to change a praiseworthy custom are to be restrained by the bishop of the place, once the truth is known.

Likewise, no priest should, for entreaty, payment, favor, or kinship, conceal from the bishop the public sin of his parishioner, or bring a penitent who is doing penance insufficiently to reconciliation and bear witness to his reconciliation, or out of any grudge remove a penitent who is doing penance worthily from reconciliation; for both are simoniacal. Extra de simonia, c. Nemo Presbyterorum.

\[From the decretal\] Nemo Presbyterorum \[Let no one of the priests\] receive any gift or any temporal benefit — or rather, any spiritual detriment — from any public sinner, so as to conceal his sin from the bishop or his ministers; nor, out of regard for any person through kinship or familiarity, communicating in another’s sins, shall he hesitate to make it known to the bishop; nor shall he bring any penitent, or one who is doing penance insufficiently, by favor or partiality to reconciliation and bear testimony of reconciliation to him; nor by any grudge remove a penitent who is doing penance worthily from reconciliation. Both are simoniacal.

## §. XI.

## May a priest sing Mass for money?

I say that if he is bound by his office to this Mass, or to these obsequies, or the like, to demand anything for it is simoniacal. Causa 1, q. 3, c. Vendentes.

If, however, he is not so bound, and lacks provisions, he may lawfully receive \[something\]. Causa 1, q. 1, c. Judices.

\[From the canon\] Judices \[Judges\]: Its judges judged for bribes, its priests responded for hire, its prophets divined for money, and they leaned upon the Lord, saying: Is not the Lord among us? No evil shall come upon us. Therefore on your account Zion shall be plowed like a field, and Jerusalem shall be like a watchman’s hut in an orchard. Let no one doubt that Jerusalem was overthrown on account of the crimes that are read in this chapter (and further below). Peter could indeed have sold to Simon Magus what he sought — or rather, he could have feigned to sell it (for the Holy Spirit can neither be sold nor bought) — but he condemned the money offered along with the one who offered it.
