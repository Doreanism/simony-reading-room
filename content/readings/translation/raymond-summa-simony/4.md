(& below) As for the text, *Blessed is he who shakes his hands free of every gift* — this was said of those gifts which are accustomed to attract or corrupt the mind of the recipient; for if even the elect person himself should offer to his ordainer or consecrator a medicinal remedy, or some wine, or other things of modest value which ought not to incline or move the will of the recipient, the Roman Church has not been accustomed to interpret this as making the recipient delinquent, nor the giver.

The second case is for spiritual works to which the person is not bound by office. *Causa* 32, q. 2, c. *Caritatem*.

*Caritatem*... Let the visitors of churches and their clerics, who toil with them not through the parishes of their own city, receive at your arrangement some subsidy for their labor. For it is just that those should receive a stipend who are found to lend their service in due season.

The third case also: for certain spiritual works that are owed — for example, when a bishop consecrates a church or visits his diocese, he may exact a moderate procuration. Extra *de simonia*, c. *Cum sit Romana*; *Causa* 10, q. 3, c. *Relatum*; Extra *de praescript.* c. *Cum ex officii*.

*Cum sit Romana* (& below): We respond to your consultation that you may not and should not exact anything for the consecration of churches, except the procuration.

*Cum ex officii* \[Since from his office\]: \[The Archbishop of Sens,\] as he was passing through the diocese of Paris while making his visitation of the province of Sens pursuant to his official duty, found it necessary to stop at the churches of the Abbot of Saint-Magloire, the Prior of Castres, and certain others of the same diocese; and when he requested from them the procuration due to him, they inhumanly refused it, alleging in defense of their position only that they did not recall ever having paid such procuration to his predecessors, nor that it had ever been demanded of them. (& below) We therefore, holding their aforesaid allegation to be of no force whatsoever, since prescription cannot run against the procuration owed by reason of visitation, just as it cannot in any manner run against the visitation itself — even if another might prescribe against him on both counts — command that you cause the sentence which the aforesaid Archbishop pronounced against such contemnors according to the ancient custom of his province to be observed until fitting satisfaction is made; because, according to the Apostle: *He who sows spiritual things, it is no great matter if he reaps temporal things, since no one is compelled to serve at his own expense.* If they wish to object

against the Archbishop’s position and prove anything further alleged above, we shall cause justice to be shown to them.

The fourth case: for eternal life and the remission of sins. As in Daniel 4: *Redeem your sins with almsgiving.*

The fifth case: to ransom from an unjust vexation which is being inflicted upon one in a matter of spiritual right. Extra *de simonia*, c. *Dilectus filius*, and the Apostle: *Redeeming the time, for the days are evil.*

*Dilectus filius*: R., a Provost, has set forth to us that when the Archdeacon of York \[Eboracum\] did not cease to oppress him with many burdens, he promised to give him a certain sum of money so that he would desist from injuring him. But the said Archdeacon, after receiving the money, did not cease from molesting him regarding his dignities and benefices. Therefore we command your fraternity that, if the matter is as stated, you compel the Archdeacon to restore what he received (if it is established that he has acted contrary to his promise).

Concerning a gift *from service*: distinguish, for service is either bodily or spiritual. In the first case, if the service is honorable, it may \[be given for spiritual things\] — as if one goes to Rome on behalf of a church, or serves some bishop or church in lawful and honorable matters, provided it is without any agreement. If it is dishonorable, it may not — as above, in the same section.

A gift *from service* is service wrongfully rendered. If it is spiritual service, one should not render such service in exchange for a benefice or other thing to be given to oneself, unless such service has been imposed upon the benefice itself — for example, if a certain prebend carries the obligation that whoever holds it must sing a daily Mass of the Blessed Virgin Mary. Extra *de praebend.* c. *Significatum*.

*Significatum est nobis* \[It has been signified to us\] that you gave a prebend in your church by common consent to a certain priest, on condition that he should celebrate Mass of Saint Mary daily (unless prevented by bodily infirmity), and that he should not leave your church nor delegate the anniversary of that prebend to anyone without the common consent of both the provost and the chapter. This arrangement we confirm to this extent: that the aforesaid priest, unless prevented by bodily infirmity, must diligently render his service to the said church, and as frequently as he can (preserving his honest and due devotion) celebrate the solemnities of the Mass; nor is it lawful for him to withdraw this service, whether on grounds of his own will or by transferring himself to another church.

As for giving something for spiritual or temporal things — for instance, a chalice, vestments, and the like — some say this is not simony unless it is found to be prohibited, as in the case of a church, a cemetery, and chrism, or blessed oil. *Causa* 1, q. 1, *Placuit* 2, *Dictum* 2, & *Statuimus* 1; Extra *de simonia* c. *Ad nostram*.

*Placuit* \[It has been decreed\]: that no bishop shall demand anything beyond the procuration for the little balsam which, once blessed, is given through the churches for the sacrament of baptism, since individual \[bishops\] are accustomed to exact a tremissis for it; lest perhaps we, selling what is consecrated for the salvation of souls through the invocation of the Holy Spirit, be ourselves damned — just as Simon Magus wished to purchase the gift of God with money.
