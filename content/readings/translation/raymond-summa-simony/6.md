SOME TIME AGO a certain cleric S., procurator of the Prior and monks of Acre, and cleric P., representing the cause which was being contested between them and the said P. and his brother, whose representative he claimed to be, over the church and chapel of L., came into our presence and agreed among themselves (though not with our mediation) that three silver marks should be paid to the said P. for his expenses, and that the same P. should withdraw from the suit and cease from molesting the monks. When they asked for that composition to be confirmed by Apostolic authority, we did not think it fit to admit it, on the grounds that it appeared to contain the corrupt character of an illicit agreement. \[And below:\] He therefore, acquiescing in our admonitions and renouncing the said suit on behalf of himself and his brother, resigned into our hands the authentic instruments which he held concerning the matter; and we, sending these to the Prior and monks, commanded that they should endeavor to provide the said P. and his brother with some benefice, from which they might obtain greater benefit by Apostolic intercession than ought to have accrued to them from that illicit agreement, etc.

Concerning this matter, however, some distinguish: that both before and after election or ordination, a bond \[*cautio*\] may lawfully be sought which is established by law or by approved custom. \[See\] Distinction 23, c. Quamquam; Distinction 63, c. Quia sancta.

ALTHOUGH all who are entrusted with holy orders are bound by canonical rules, it is nonetheless expedient that those whom ecclesiastical discipline approves for promotion should pledge their vows of promise under a bond. For what is particularly pledged is wont to be feared more than what is concluded in a general undertaking. And therefore it has pleased this holy Council that each one who is about to ascend to ecclesiastical grades should not receive the honor of consecration until he has promised by a written declaration that he will guard the Catholic faith with sincere contrition and devotion of heart, and ought to live justly and piously, etc.

BECAUSE the holy Roman Church, over which we preside by God’s authority, suffers violence from many parties upon the death of the Pontiff — such violence being inflicted because the election and consecration of the Pontiff is made without the knowledge of the Emperor, and imperial envoys, sent by canonical rite and custom, are not present to prevent scandals — we will that when a Pontiff is to be installed, the bishops and all the clergy shall gather and elect him in the presence of the senate and people, and that the one who is to be ordained shall thus, having been elected by all, be consecrated in the presence of imperial legates; and that no one shall dare, on pain of peril to himself, to extort oaths or promises by some new invention, except those which ancient custom requires — lest the Church be scandalized and imperial majesty be diminished.

From a superior, any bond may be sought, provided it is honorable. \[See\] Cause 10, q. 3, c. ult., i.e., Illud; Extra *de electione*, c. Significasti.

WE WISH YOUR HIGHNESS in no way to be ignorant of the fact that the bishop of the city of Syracuse, among other things which he pledged in a bond of his written undertaking, is recorded to have promised specifically under penalty of law that he would never demand more than two solidi in the name of *cathedraticum* from any priest of his diocese, nor in any other way act to its detriment. Your Highness should take care not only to have the said bishop observe this, but also to see that it is maintained with fitting diligence for all the bishops of Sicily; and also that no excessive banquets be held, but that a suitable meal be prepared, proportionate to the means of each place, when the bishop comes for confirmation — for we do not wish, nor do we permit for any reason, that the poor be burdened by this pretext.

YOU HAVE SIGNIFIED TO US, dearest brother, that kings and the greater men of the realm have been moved to astonishment that the pallium was offered to you by our apocrisiaries on such a condition, should you exhibit the oath which they had brought us in writing. \[And below:\] Let them rather wonder in this matter at our Lord Jesus Christ, who when he committed the care of his sheep to Peter, set a condition, saying: *If you love me, feed my sheep.* If the maker and knower of consciences and of secrets used this condition — not once only, but a second time, and even to the point of sorrow — with what solicitude ought we to impose so great a prelacy of the Church upon brothers whose consciences we do not see? etc.

Others say that before the election no bond or agreement can be sought, and if one is given, it is simony, because it appears to be done so that \[the candidate\] may be elected. \[See\] Cause 8, q. 3, c. Talia.

THAT HE IS NOT TO BE HELD a simoniac who after his election has sworn an oath for the protection of the Church’s interests is easily proved. For it is one thing to swear an oath before the election, or to enter into an agreement to do so in order to be elected. It is another thing, without any preceding agreement, to offer such an oath after the election. The former deposes \[the candidate\] from office as a gift rendered; the latter generates no impediment to consecration. Concerning the former case, Pelagius writes to the clergy of Catina.

SUCH LETTERS we sent long ago to our brother and co-bishop Eucarpius concerning the visitation of the church of Catina, so that from them you might have been able to know our judgment. Nevertheless, because we have learned also from your account that certain persons there are stirring up dissensions not with good intent but with base designs — men who, knowing that they cannot attain the episcopate on account of age, knowledge, or the merits of their life, wish only to disturb the Church so that the future bishop may promise them immunity for what they have presumed, and episcopal ambition may become security for plunder — we have thought it necessary to warn you also that you should hasten with God’s favor to bring to conclusion the decree made against the deacon Elpidius, subscribed by all whom your account has named, together with the same deacon. \[And after a few words:\] When therefore our son the magnificent man Len the Praetor came here, he reported to us concerning each of the matters that have been raised there, and how the votes of nearly the whole city were in agreement upon the deacon Elpidius.
