Likewise, there are two cases in which, by the common law, persons are not simoniacs nor are they required to resign. One is if my enemy were fraudulently to give money for my election, intending that the election be subsequently annulled and I left dishonored. The other is if, against my will and with my express opposition, someone secretly gave or promised money to a bishop unwilling to confirm the election, or to those collating \[the benefice\]. Extra de Simonia, cap. *Nobis*, and cap. *Sicut* 2. And we hold this to be true unless I should afterward have acquiesced — by paying the promised money or returning what had been paid — as in that *Sicut* 2.

## §. X.

## What of certain customs regarding the procuring of feasts, etc.

Likewise, it is asked whether customs regarding the procuring of feasts after the reception \[of orders or religion\], or regarding the exactions and extortions that many persons practice at the consecration of bishops, the benediction of abbots, and the ordination of clerics — saying “so much is to be given to the notary, so much to the doorkeeper, so much to the cantor,” and the like — whether, I say, these customs constitute simony?

It seems not; for to receive \[something\] for expenses and the support of the tabernacle is not simony. Causa 1, quest. 1, cap. *Judices*. Likewise, custom makes that no offense which would otherwise be an offense. 32, q. 4, *Objiciuntur*.

Jacob’s four wives are cited as an objection: that when it was the custom it was not a crime, just as the Patriarchs lay with their wives’ handmaids to receive seed from them, not in the lust of satisfying pleasure but in the providence of propagating succession. Likewise the Apostles, when their hearers admired their teaching, rejoiced together with them, not in eagerness to obtain praise but in charity to sow the truth.

And this seems to be expressed in Dist. 63, cap. *Quia*, and 1, q. 3, c. *Quaesitum*.

On the contrary: in such matters custom does not diminish sin but increases it. Extra de simonia, cap. *Non satis*.

*Non satis*... (And below.) We therefore prohibit that any money be required of those who wish to enter the religious life. Nor may any priory, or any chaplaincy of monks or clerics, be sold by annual lease. Nor may any price be exacted from him to whom the administration of these is committed, in exchange for that commission. Therefore whoever shall have presumed to act contrary to this decree — both the one who gave and the one who received, or consented — let him not doubt that he shall have his portion with Simon. Likewise, for burial, for the receipt of chrism and oil, let no exaction of any price be attempted; nor let anyone shield his guilt under the pretext of any custom: for the long duration of time does not diminish sins but increases them.

Likewise, no one should receive food or a pasty. Causa 1, quest. 3, cap. *Si quis objecerit*. And all these are expressly forbidden in Extra de simonia, c. x., or c. *In ordinando*.

*In ordinando*... (And below.) Just as the minister or notary at an ordination ought not to sell his voice or his pen. We therefore wholly forbid that the one being ordained give anything for his ordination, for the use of the pallium, or for charters and pasties.

Solution. If such things are exacted or extorted and paid, then both those who received and those who gave are simoniacs and are condemned along with Gehazi and Simon, no custom notwithstanding. Extra de simonia, cap. 2 already cited, and cap. *Sicut pro certo*.
