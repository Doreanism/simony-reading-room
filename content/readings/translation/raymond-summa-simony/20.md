## Greg. IX.

“If anyone shall have ordained, or presented someone for ordination, receiving from him a promise or oath that he would not trouble him regarding his provision: the ordainer shall know himself suspended from the conferral of orders, the presenter from the execution of orders for three years, and the ordained from the order so received — until they shall have merited to obtain dispensation in this matter from the Apostolic See.”

Note in summary that the Council of Chalcedon judges as simoniacs even the administrators of ecclesiastical property, defenders \[*defensores*\], and those subject to a rule, if they are ordained through money; and also all intermediaries. By the name of administrator \[*procurator*\] is understood any administrator of ecclesiastical affairs: for example, a provost \[*praepositum*\], or an *oeconomus* (that is, a *vicedominus* — that is, one who presides over the bishop’s affairs). By the name of defender, an advocate or *syndicus*, namely one who presides over the church’s legal affairs, and a *castaldus* or *major domus* — that is, one who has charge of the bishop’s external affairs — and the bishop’s judge, as in the church of Bologna. By “those subject to a rule” is understood an archpriest, archdeacon, canon, monk, and any person bound to an ecclesiastical office.

Concerning intermediaries the matter is plain. *Causa* 1, q. 1, *Si quis Episcopus*, and *Causa* 1, q. 3, *Salvator*.

## Urban. II.

\[*Salvator*:\] “… Otherwise, why would the holy council of Chalcedon, consisting of six hundred thirty bishops, prohibit a procurator or defender of a church, or anyone subject to a rule, from being ordained through money, and anathematize the intermediaries of so great a crime, unless it judged those persons to be simoniacs? And if the aforesaid soldiers of the Church are so struck down on account of this crime, no wise person will deny that those who do not serve the Church are, on account of this same cause — that is, of buying or selling — to be struck down far more severely. Moreover, our blessed predecessor Paschalis affirms in his book *On Consecration* that whoever shall have sold one of two things \[the spiritual and the material\], without which the other does not come about, leaves neither one unsold; and by this it is understood that one who sells or buys a thing belonging to the church is a simoniac. By the name of procurator, the aforesaid council understands any administrator of ecclesiastical affairs — for example, a provost, an *oeconomus*, a *vicedominus*; by the name of defender, an advocate or *castaldus*, and a judge; and by ‘those subject to a rule,’ an archpriest, archdeacon, canon, monk, or any person bound to an ecclesiastical office.”
