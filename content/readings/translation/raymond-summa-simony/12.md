Your beloved son A., your messenger, set forth on your behalf that when you were visiting the diocese of Canterbury and making visitation in the monasteries and religious houses you discovered simoniacal wickedness, such that in them many were received for a price who ought rather to have been received freely, indeed even invited to the observance of the religious life; you therefore doubted whether, because the multitude is found at fault, any abatement should be made of rigor. We respond to your inquiry as follows: if an accusation has been canonically instituted before you against those stained by this defilement, once the crime has been publicly established by judicial procedure, you are to exercise the punishment of canonical severity both upon those who gave and those who received; but if this has become known to you only through inquiry, you are to remove from their places those who were received in such houses through simoniacal wickedness and direct them to stricter monasteries for the performance of penance; and upon abbots, abbesses, priors, whatever prelates and their officials you are to impose a fitting penance, and until they have completed it, you are to suspend them from the execution of sacred orders; enjoining your bishops to take care that this form be observed throughout their dioceses. That which was offered freely without any fixed sum may, however, be gratefully accepted.

The simoniacal corruption has infected so many nuns that scarcely any are received as sisters without a price, using the pretext of poverty to conceal this vice. We utterly prohibit this from occurring henceforth, ordaining that any who hereafter commit such wickedness — both those receiving and those received, whether subject or superior — shall be expelled from their monastery without hope of restoration, to be confined in a place of stricter rule for the performance of perpetual penance. Regarding those who were received in this manner before this synodal statute, we have decided to provide as follows: removed from the monasteries in which they took perpetual vows, they are to be placed in other houses of the same order; but if perhaps they cannot conveniently be placed elsewhere on account of excessive numbers, lest they wander damnably in the world, they are to be received in those same monasteries by fresh dispensation, with their former places changed and inferior ones assigned. We decree that this is likewise to be observed regarding monks and other religious. Moreover, lest they be able to excuse themselves by simplicity or ignorance, we command that the diocesan bishops cause this to be published throughout their dioceses each year.

But if \[the simony was committed\] without their knowledge, whether before or after the Council, let them renounce as above; and afterwards they may be newly elected, with their places changed as above. Extra de simonia, c. *Ex insinuatione*.

Through your communication it has come to our attention that your father, by the interposition of money, once acquired for you the benefit of a prebend; and when you reached the age of discretion and had received the sign of the Lord’s Cross, you freely resigned the said prebend into the hands of the provost and the brethren. But they, taking pity on you, newly elected you as their canon, such that by this election you would obtain the last place in choir and in other matters (and further on). Response: being content with the indulgence which your brethren are known to have mercifully shown you, you must dare to claim nothing for yourself in that church by reason of the first reception.

Likewise in the first case, if no stricter rule is found, I believe with Johannes \[Teutonicus\] that after renunciation they ought to be transferred to another monastery of the same rule; but if that cannot conveniently be done, let it be done at least temporarily. Argument from Dist. 81, c. *Valet*; and afterwards let a new election take place as above — and this especially where the matter concerns penance.

Sometimes a change of place is useful for one converted for the salvation of his soul; for often when place is changed, the disposition of the mind is changed as well. It is fitting that a person be bodily uprooted from the very place where he was enslaved to temptations. For the place where anyone has lived wickedly sets before the mind’s eye what he often thought or did there.

Likewise I believe that the bishop, or even the chapter, can dispense with such persons according to the distinction set forth above — provided they were not themselves participants in the simony; for then, since they ought to be punished on that account (Extra de simonia, c. *Dilectus* 1), they are not to obtain a privilege from that same fact; indeed they appear to have lost the power of election, and others who have not sinned have acquired it. Argument from Extra de electione, c. *Bonae memoriae*.

To the \[late\] Archbishop of Mainz and the canons of Mainz: since your votes were divided in various directions, with some seeking the Bishop of Worms and others choosing your archbishop, then provost of St. Peter’s in Mainz, as pastor for themselves — we gave a mandate to the Bishop of Palestrina, legate of the Apostolic See, that if it was established that the said bishop had received the Church of Mainz in temporal or spiritual matters, or had administered temporal or spiritual affairs in it, he should declare the postulation made of him void (and further on); then he was to inquire into the truth of the election of that archbishop, and if it were established to him that there was duress, which he and his \[followers\] feared — notwithstanding that after an appeal interposed to us, proceedings had been taken in that election, since the others who interposed an appeal to us lest proceedings be taken in the election without him and his associates did not consider it necessary to defer to it; so that they could not use this objection against him, because one who acts against the law invokes the aid of the law in vain, nor could it seem to be objected against him by them that he had been elected by fewer votes according to their assertion, since they themselves, despising the appeal legitimately interposed to us and receiving the aforementioned bishop without our permission, had rendered themselves unworthy — he should confirm the election of the archbishop (and further on). Although the matters objected against the said Bishop of Worms and his supporters were notorious, the said legate nonetheless received many witnesses produced by the archbishop’s party for greater caution.
