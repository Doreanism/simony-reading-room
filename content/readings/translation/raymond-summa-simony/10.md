\[*Ab excommunicatis:*\] Those ordained by bishops who were once excommunicated but formerly catholic — provided they did not receive those orders simoniacally, and provided it is established that those bishops themselves were not simoniacs, and provided further that the candidates’ more devout life and distinction in learning appeared to commend them to advancement — you may permit, upon imposition of such penance as you judge fitting, to remain in the orders they received. We do not, however, grant them leave to ascend to higher offices, unless the greatest necessity and utility demand it and their holy manner of life shall have merited it. (But that text of Gregorius is understood to refer to those excommunicated by name, whose ordinations are invalid if their condemnation was not unknown to those being ordained.)

Otherwise, a dispensation in orders may be granted by the Pope, from the fullness of power, even in the case of those who knew the simony was being committed. *Extra de Simonia*, c. *Nobis*; *Causa* 1, q. 1, *Erga simoniacos*.

\[*Nobis:*\] It has been reported to us on your behalf, etc.

\[*Ad ultimum:*\] To the last matter which we find at the end of your consultation, we have judged it proper to reply that a person who has been elected as a prelate of any church and, as stated, has been rejected for simoniacal wickedness, cannot by right obtain any dispensation whatsoever from his own bishop.

\[*Erga simoniacos:*\] We decree that no mercy is to be shown to simoniacs in preserving their dignity; but in accordance with the sanction of the canons and the decrees of the holy Fathers, we utterly condemn them and by Apostolic authority ordain that they are to be deposed. It is to be understood \[that this applies\] unless they were drawn in by force.

\[*Si quis neque:*\] If anyone — not distinguished by holy character or outstanding in governing clergy and people — has, when pressed by importunity, shamelessly received the priesthood of Christ, already defiled by some crime, out of an unjust love of his heart or through sordid entreaties, or through attendance, or manual service, or a fraudulent small gift, pursuing episcopal or priestly dignity not for the profit of souls but blinded by the greed of vainglory; and if in his lifetime he has not voluntarily relinquished it, and unexpected death has not found him penitent — without doubt he shall perish forever.

\[*Præsentium portitorem:*\] The bearer of the present letter, whom you have reported to have obtained the dignity of priesthood against his will through money given not to the bishop but to a certain one of his princes, owing to the negligence of his parents: although the holy canons testify that he ought to be deposed, yet because he committed this offence unknowingly and under compulsion, and because he has spontaneously petitioned to be deposed from that same order — applying the bowels of mercy out of the consideration of discretion (which is the mother of all virtues) rather than from the rigour of the canons, we advise your fraternity that he may remain in that same order; yet in such a way that, if the church he serves does not suffer from a shortage of priests, he shall remain suspended from the priestly office; but if perchance the utility of the church shall require him to take up pastoral charge, let it be lawful for him, by concession of his bishop and in obedience to the brethren, to exercise the priestly office.

If, however, the money was not given to the bishop but to a subordinate, without the bishop’s knowledge, the bishop is able to dispense. *Causa* 1, q. 5, c. *Præsemium*, as above.

## §. VIII.

But can a bishop dispense with such persons?

Some say, without distinction, that he cannot. Arg. *extra de Judiciis*, c. *At si clerici*.

\[*At si clerici:*\] If clerics have been convicted or have confessed before a secular judge of a crime, they are not on that account to be in any way condemned by their own bishop; for just as a sentence pronounced by a judge who is not one’s own does not stand, so neither does a confession made before him. But if clerics have confessed to crimes in court before the bishop, or have been convicted by legitimate proof — provided these are such crimes as warrant suspension or deposition — they are not without reason to be suspended from their orders or permanently removed from the ministry of the altar. For adultery and other lesser crimes, however, the bishop can dispense with clerics after completed penance.

Others say, and more correctly, that if the crime was committed without the knowledge of those ordained, once the person has simply renounced the exercise of orders, the bishop can dispense. *Extra de Simonia*, c. *De simoniace*.

\[*De simoniace:*\] Concerning those ordained simoniacally, we cannot give you a definitive answer without learning more fully how they were ordained; since some, though ordained simoniacally in a certain species of simony — namely when they themselves were ignorant of it — can (because they are not simoniacs) remain in their orders.

I understand this to apply when the money was not given to the bishop himself; for if it had been given to the bishop, he himself could not \[dispense\] on account of his own dishonor, but only the Pope. *Causa* 1, q. 1, c. *Erga*, and c. *Si quis neque*, and *Causa* 1, q. 5, c. *Præsemium*.

\[*Quicumque sane:*\] Those who, while still children, obtained churches or portions of churches through money by the cupidity of their parents — once they have entirely relinquished them, if they wish to live canonically in those churches, we grant as a great mercy that they may remain there, and we do not remove them from sacred orders on this account, provided they are otherwise found worthy. Those, however, who when of mature age bought them out of wicked cupidity on their own initiative — if they wish to live canonically in other churches, retaining their own orders, we permit as a great mercy that they may minister there. But if perchance they cannot be transferred elsewhere and promise to live canonically in those same churches, let them be content with minor orders and not ascend to sacred orders — the authority of the Apostolic See being in all things preserved.
