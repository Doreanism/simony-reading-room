## §. VI.

But what of money or property received simoniacally? To whom is it to be restored?

I answer that all such things must be restored to that church in whose injury they were given, from whomsoever they were received — whether cleric or layman, whether principal party or intermediary, or anyone else. *Extra de Simonia*, c. *De hoc autem*, and c. *Venitas*.

\[*De hoc autem:*\] As to the fact that the Bishop of Osma is said to have promised a benefice to an archdeacon and a priorate to a certain cleric before his own election, in order that each might consent to him: we reply to your prudence that if it is manifest that the same archdeacon and cleric received those promises for that reason, or if they have confessed it in court or been lawfully convicted, they are to be permanently deposed from the ministry of the altar. If, however, the fact is manifest but they have not been convicted or confessed, yet suffer under public infamy, canonical purgation must be enjoined upon them; and if they fail in it, they are to be punished as perpetrators of so great a crime. Moreover you should prevail upon the king and his princes to restore without diminution to the church of Osma whatever they received from the aforesaid bishop in order to give their assent to his election, since they cannot retain it without grave peril to their salvation.

Objection. Shall restitution be made to the one who gave? Solution. Some say that this was done dispensatively. Others distinguish — and I agree with them — that if the one who gave the money remains in the world, restitution is not to be made to him but to the church, as stated above; but if he passes to a religious life, or if he was already a religious person and transfers from one monastery to another, the money should be restored outright, and the properties — at least as regards the usufruct — to the monastery he is entering, so that as it bears the burden it may also have the benefit. And this is how the contrary opinion speaks. A parallel case: *Causa* 16, q. 6, c. *De lapsis*, and 27, q. 1, *Si quis rapuerit*.

## §. VII.

What dispensation may be granted to a simoniac?

It remains to consider what dispensation may be granted to a simoniac: the matter is distinguished on both sides. For there are four cases in which dispensation is utterly forbidden. Hence the verse:

Whether order is given twice, or baptism repeated,
Whether order is sought in order to overturn the state of faith,
Or whether it is conferred so that Simon may accomplish both,
And a dispensation be granted — the hope is held to be wholly void.

*Causa* 1, q. 7, c. *Saluberrimum*; *de consecr.* dist. 4, c. *Qui bis*; *Causa* 1, q. 7, c. *Convenientibus* in book 3 *de Sacram.*

Furthermore, simoniacs: *Causa* 9, q. 1, *Ab excommunicatis*.

\[*Saluberrimum:*\] For it is most wholesome and full of the special benefit of salutary penance that priests, deacons, or clerics of whatever order who wish to be seen as corrected, and who desire to return to the catholic faith which they had long ago abandoned, should first without ambiguity acknowledge their errors and the very authors of those errors as condemned by themselves — so that, once their perverse opinions are also extinguished, no occasion for despair may remain, and no \[church\] member may be violated by association with such persons, since their own profession will have come to stand against them in every respect. Regarding them, we also prescribe that the constitution of the canons be observed: let them count it a great benefit if, with all hope of advancement taken from them, they remain permanently in whatever order they are found — provided they have not been defiled by a repeated anointing.

\[*Convenientibus:*\] Those assembled at Nicaea in Bithynia, etc. Furthermore, simoniacs are not held to a subversion of the faith, nor are those ordained within the catholic Church set in arms against the faith. For although some ordain through money, they do not attack the faith, nor do they preach the grace of the Holy Spirit as something to be sold. Hence, when by true repentance they have detested their heresy, they are not forbidden by the foregoing authorities to be received back in their orders; yet by that chapter of the Nicene Synod beginning *Si quis per pecuniam*, etc., and by that other of the Seventh Council beginning *Si quis omnem traditionem*, and by the authorities of Innocentius and many others, they are ordered to be irrecoverably condemned.

\[*Si quis rapuerit:*\] If anyone shall seize, solicit, or carry off a woman who is an assistant, a deaconess, or a nun, etc., her goods and those of everyone defiled by complicity in such a crime shall be claimed for the religious house in which such a woman was living, by devout bishops, stewards, and provincial governors and their officials; but those \[who committed the crime\] shall be subjected to capital danger. The woman, moreover, is to be sought out everywhere and delivered with her possessions into a more secure monastery; but if she was a deaconess having legitimate children, the lawful portion shall be given to her legitimate children.
