With what pious affection of mind the Roman Church compassionates your desolation, dearest brethren, etc.… Therefore beware, brethren, how ambiguous is the counsel you seek: which counsel we find confirmed by no reason in accordance with the proposal of your will; yet if he whom you have found is so opportune to your necessity — provided that all pact be absent and all convention cease, and no division be made of your church — let him come, let him begin to serve God freely, and devoutly discharge the duty of his governance; and thereafter the Roman Church permits you, as by way of subsidy, to bestow upon him some gifts from his own church as the consolation of the brethren.

But after the election this is certainly possible, because then he \[the superior\] has no hope of obtaining anything from them; provided it is not made on the basis of a prior agreement. Causa 8, q. 3, c. *Artaldus*.

Concerning the second case, it is recorded in the acts of Urban II.

Artaldus, Bishop of Arles and suffragan of the church of Narbonne, came to Rome to the Lord Pope Urban to be consecrated; for his own archbishop refused to consecrate him, because after his election, for the sake of preserving the goods of the church, he had sworn to the canons. He was therefore consecrated by the Lord Pope Urban, having first been cleared of this kind of oath: “Concerning the oath which I made to the canons of our church — after my election I made no prior agreement to the effect that I should be elected. The Archbishop of Narbonne withheld canonical consecration from no one but me, and that on grounds of a criminal charge, to my knowledge; nor am I conscious of any crime on my part by reason of which he would repel me from the priesthood.”

From a superior, however, an honest security may be sought both before and after the election. Dist. 28, c. *De Siracusane*; and Dist. 100, c. *Optatum*: and in this they agree with the earlier opinions; and this last opinion is the more pleasing.

The pallium you desired we were unable to confer on you now, because we found the page of your profession to contain less than is proper — in that you made no mention therein of the holy universal councils in which the Symbol of our faith is contained, nor of the constitutions of the decretals of the Roman pontiffs according to custom; nor had you fortified it with your own subscription, nor had you sent anyone to confirm it by oath.

## §. V.

## On the Punishment of the Simoniac.

Regarding the punishment of the simoniac, make the following distinction: for simony is either in \[holy\] orders or in \[ecclesiastical\] benefice. In the case of orders, \[the simoniac\] does not receive \[the right to\] exercise \[ministry\], but is suspended by the law itself both with respect to himself and with respect to others, whether the matter is occult or manifest — just as a notorious fornicator \[is suspended\]. Extra de simonia, c. *Tanti*; and Dist. 32, c. *Verum*; and thus is understood Causa 1, q. 1, c. *Reperiuntur*; and this \[is so\] because the character \[of orders\] is obtained by stealth.

Very many are found who, perishing through the traffic in gifts, wish to purchase the grace of the Holy Spirit; while they offer a base price in order to acquire the sublimity of the pontifical order… We apply to this cancerous wound the cautery that still remains, decreeing absolutely that whoever hereafter is found to have offered any reward for receiving the dignity of a divine gift, shall from that moment know himself condemned to the reproach of anathema and cut off from participation in the body and blood of Christ, from which time it is established that he has committed an execrable crime against Christ. But if any accuser comes forward against him, let the one who has obtained this order through the giving of gifts both be deprived of the honor he has received and be shut away in a monastery under perpetual penance; and let those who on this account have been receivers of gifts, if they be clergy, be punished by loss of their honor; but if they be laymen, let them be condemned to perpetual anathema.

Likewise \[the simoniac\] is punished by deposition and infamy, if the crime has come to the notice of his judge in any way, even by inquest or denunciation. Causa 15, q. 3, c. *Sane*; Extra de accusatione, c. *Inquisitionis*.

Whoever has obtained this holy and venerable see of the bishop by the intervention of money, or whoever is discovered to have received anything in order that he might ordain or elect another, shall be dragged down from the rank of the priesthood upon the accusation brought in the manner of a public crime and of treason against majesty; and we decree that such persons be not only deprived henceforth of this honor, but also condemned to perpetual infamy — so that an equal punishment may attend both, whom a like crime stains and makes equal.
