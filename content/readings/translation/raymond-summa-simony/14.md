*Sicut pro certo* we have learned, in many places and by many persons — selling doves in the temple, as it were — shameful and wicked exactions and extortions are made for the consecration of bishops, the benediction of abbots, and the ordination of clerics; and it has been fixed how much is to be paid to this person or that, and how much to yet another — and, to heap up a yet greater damnation, some persons strive to defend this greed and wickedness by a custom long observed. Wishing, therefore, to abolish so great an abuse, we wholly condemn this custom (which ought rather to be called a corruption), firmly decreeing that for these \[offices\], whether about to be conferred or already conferred, no one shall presume to exact or extort anything under any pretext whatsoever; otherwise both the one who received and the one who gave such condemned payment shall be condemned together with Gehazi and Simon.

If, however, the person elected or ordained, or another acting on his behalf, not under compulsion but freely offers something, some say that if it is done pursuant to a prior agreement it is simony, but otherwise it is not. Others — and more correctly — distinguish whether it is given principally on account of that \[benefit\], or not, such that he would have given it in any event. In the first case it is simony, because to give is to give at any time whatsoever, and to receive is to receive at any time whatsoever. q. 1, c. *Eos*, in fine; and 1, quest. 3, c. *Si quis praebendas*.

A correction was ordained: that those who are baptized \[as had been the custom\] should not put coins into the font, lest the priest appear to sell for a price what he received freely.

Likewise, those are not excused from the crime of simony who, changing the name of the thing and the time of payment, called the coins — which they first named chrism-coins because they were given for chrism and at its reception — second, Easter-coins, and third, the custom of mid-Lent. Extra de Simonia, c. *In tantum*.

*In tantum* — with sins demanding it, simoniacal wickedness has corrupted the hearts of certain people (And below.) For it has come to our attention that whereas some of your suffragans formerly used to receive for chrism a fixed sum of money, now, fearing no canonical penalty and wishing to evade your correction, they anticipate the time of payment, receiving in mid-Lent what they were accustomed to receive after Easter, and in order to disguise the reason for receiving it they vary the name of the coins — coins they formerly called chrism-coins, then Easter-coins, they now call the custom of mid-Lent. (And below.) Since the sale is more expressly manifested when one receives the price before conferring the precious thing — whereas grace to be conferred ought to be conferred freely — we command you to correct the aforesaid excesses accordingly, restraining your suffragans and their officials from so illicit an exaction, lest the fault of others be imputed to you as a penalty on account of your negligence.

You should also endeavor to abolish that wicked custom in your province, by which archdeacons demand that a mark of silver, and lesser deans a white cow, or a fixed sum of money, be given to them for the investiture of churches.

Likewise, by the preceding question understand the solution of another: whether on the basis of some other custom certain things can be exacted for the benediction of those marrying, for the burials or funerals of the dead, for chrism or holy oil, or for any ecclesiastical sacrament by which grace is conferred therein — which I add on account of matrimony, since in it (as it is only a work of nature and a natural contract) grace is not conferred, and therefore money can be given for it as such. Extra de sponsal., c. *De illis*.

The divine Apostle Peter (whose throne Your Holiness has received by lot) deposes those who have laid on hands for money, or who lay them on, as being \[like\] Simon Magus... For Peter says to Simon: “Thou hast neither part nor lot in this matter, for thou hast thought the gift of God can be possessed by money.” For the twenty-ninth canon of the holy Apostles, and the Acts of those same Apostles, and the third and fourth books of Kings, pronounce utterly alien to the priesthood anyone who has at any time given or received money in connection with \[the imposition of hands\], whether before the laying on of hands, or in the laying on of hands itself, or after; for to receive is to receive at any time whatsoever.

If anyone shall have acquired prebends, a priory, a deanery, or any ecclesiastical honor or promotion, or any ecclesiastical sacrament — such as chrism, holy oil, the consecration of altars or churches — by money, through the execrable ardor of avarice, let him be stripped of the dishonorably acquired honor, and let buyer, seller, and intermediary be struck with the mark of infamy; and let nothing be exacted from or presumptuously given by anyone, for food or under the pretext of any custom, before or after; for it is simoniacal — but let him freely and without any diminution enjoy the dignity and benefit conferred upon him.

In the second case it is not simony. Extra de Simonia, c. *Et si questiones*, and *Dilectus* 2 in fine. Yet everyone ought to abstain not only from evil but from every appearance of evil, according to the Apostle: for even if the person being ordained, elected, or having been elected does not give with a corrupt intention, nor does another receive it thus; yet by reason of time — as if given at the very collation itself, or a little before or after — or by reason of place, person, or the thing given, \[a suspicion\] arises

Concerning those, however, who after taking an oath promise to marry certain women and then, without having known them, abandon the land and remove themselves to other regions — we wish to make known to you that the women themselves will be free (if no further step has been taken in the matter) to transfer themselves to other vows, provided penance for the perjury has been received, if they were the reason the marriage was not consummated.

This I also say concerning a consecrated chalice or similar things which can be sold without risk of simony, even in the same form, to other churches in cases of necessity — which you will find below, de Immunit. ecclesiast.
