Likewise, one who receives a benefice simoniacally is obliged to renounce the benefice and to restore all fruits both received and capable of being received. Causa 14, q. 6, c. *Si res aliena*; and Extra de restitut. spoliat., c. *Gravis*.

Likewise, he cannot reclaim the money he gave — not because the other party received it rightly, but because the \[simoniac\] gave it shamefully. Causa 14, q. 5, c. *Non sane*.

Not everything taken from an unwilling person is thereby unjustly seized. But when judgments and testimonies, which are not to be sold whether just or genuine, are sold unjustly and falsely, money is seized far more wickedly; because it is also wickedly given, even when given willingly. He is, however, accustomed to demand back the money as wrongfully taken from him who has purchased a just judgment, because it ought not to have been for sale. But one who gave money for an unjust judgment would indeed wish to reclaim it, were he not afraid or ashamed of having bought it, etc.

If, however, someone is a simoniac because he has conferred an order simoniacally, or because he gave or received a benefice simoniacally, or was an intermediary in simony — if the matter is occult, he is suspended by the law itself with respect to himself alone, just as any other criminal \[cleric\]; but not with respect to others. Argument from Extra de simonia, c. *Et si quaestiones*, and *Veniens*, and c. *Ex suae fraternitatis*.

And understand \[the obligation to restore\] the fruits as referring to those that remain after deducting the expenses incurred in seeking, gathering, and preserving the fruits, which are deducted both from the bona fide and from the mala fide possessor. Dig. de pet. haered., *Si a patre*, §. *ult*.

I further understand this to apply to those fruits which he has not spent in the service and utility of the church, but on other uses; for even one who presumptuously undertook to manage my affairs in order to defraud me has an action against me — not for what he is out of pocket, because he presumptuously undertook my affairs; but for that by which I have been enriched, as Dig. de neg. gest., *Si pupilli*, §. *Sed et si quis*.

I understand the same to apply to the one with whose knowledge and consent the simony was committed, or who did not renounce it as soon as he knew. Argument from Extra de simonia, *Sicut tuis*, near the end of the chapter.

Otherwise, if he were a bona fide possessor, he is liable not for the fruits already consumed, but for the existing ones only. C. de rei ven., *Certum*. This punishment, moreover, is for clerics; laymen, however, are to be excommunicated. Causa 5, q. 1, c. *Reperiuntur*, and *Si quis Episcopus*.

If, however, the matter is manifest, he is suspended by the law itself both with respect to himself and with respect to others. Extra de simonia, c. *Accusatum*, and c. *Quoties*.

\[*Quoties*\] (and below): It has come to our notice that you were ordained through simoniacal heresy; for this reason we were obliged to forbid you to celebrate the solemnities of the Mass, until we could more surely discern what the matter is.

F., a priest, came before us and set forth that the Abbot and brethren of S. R. refused to receive him as a monk until he agreed to give thirty solidi; and once the agreement was made, on the following day they clothed him in the monastic habit, and these same monks demanded thirty solidi, the abbot ten, and the household twelve for sustenance (asserting this to be the custom of the monastery). Since therefore an act of this kind appears pernicious, we command you, if you find it to be so, to compel the Abbot and monks to restore to the aforesaid F. the money so unworthily received; and, suspending the Abbot and the senior persons of the monastery from the exercise of their office for the excess of such great wickedness, to enjoin on the said F. that in another monastery, in the monastic habit, he endeavor to serve the Lord.

From the letters of your brotherhood, and from the confession of the priest present, we have learned that when his archdeacon was unwilling to present him for the order of priesthood on any other terms, he paid six solidi to the same, and so was presented by him and received the order itself. Wherefore we have thought fit to reply to you as follows: that if this is not public but secret, you should privately confer with the same priest, earnestly warning and inducing him — without any compulsion — to assume the habit of some religious order and perpetually to abstain from the office which he illicitly acquired. Otherwise it will not be safe (since his transgression is secret) to coerce him against his will from that same office; yet you should not fail to impose upon him secret penance, as shall seem good to you.

If, however, the matter is manifest, he is suspended by the law itself both with respect to himself and with respect to others. Extra de simonia, c. *Accusatum*, and c. *Quoties*.
