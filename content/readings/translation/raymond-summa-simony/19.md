\[continuing from the previous page, on canons seeking to install their nephews:\] “let what is mine please you, and what is yours will please me.” They make their pact through these or equivalent words. There is yet another mode: someone says, “It is the custom that, just as you were heard in one petition, I ought to be heard in another.” Again, another says, “As long as I live, no one shall be made a canon in this church unless the one for whom I have interceded is first made a canon, or at least made a canon along with the others.” Is simony committed in such cases and similar ones?

I reply: In the first and last cases, simony is committed; for the canon says: “Let all pact be absent, let all convention cease.” *Causa* 1, q. 2, c. *Quam pia*. In the second case, if the petitions are spiritual, there is no simony. Arg. *Dist.* 63, c. *Quia*.

For all who serve one Lord in one church ought to enjoy equal freedom; since “weight and weight, measure and measure,” etc. Extra, de constit., c. *Cum omnes*.

\[*Cum omnes*:\] “Since we are all one body in Christ, and individually members of one another, the greater ought not envy the lesser, nor the older the younger; but all who serve one God in one church should enjoy equal freedom; since weight and weight, and measure and measure, both are an abomination before God, and the Gospel’s householder recompensed the first workers of his vineyard and the last with equal wages…”

Nothing more honorable is to be shown to any one person such that others are thereby troubled in conscience. *Causa* 16, q. 7, c. 1, *Decimas*.

\[*Decimas*:\] “We decree that these tithes shall be under the hand of the bishop, so that he who presides over the others may distribute them justly to all, and not show more honor to certain persons such that others are troubled in conscience; but let all things be held in common: for it seems dishonest that some priests should have abundance while others suffer want; but just as there is one catholic faith, so it is necessary that he who is the provider of the place, even if there be many churches, should nonetheless distribute faithfully to all.”

And according to the Apostle, 2 Corinthians 8: “The relief of some ought not to be a tribulation for others.”

If the petitions are carnal, or proceed from some pact already made or now in the making, it is simony. See above, same \[title\], §. *Committitur*.

What of bishops and other prelates who receive the fruits of vacant churches or other spiritual benefices for some years, and then confer \[those benefices upon someone\], retaining for themselves the fruits and revenues so received, or retaining for themselves a portion of the fruits?

To this I make the following distinction together with Innocentius: whether the one who gives the spiritual benefice was receiving its revenues before the donation, or not. For if he was not receiving the revenues before the donation, but another was, and by the donation it comes about that he himself receives those revenues — there is no doubt that simoniacal corruption intervenes in this. But if he was receiving the revenues before the donation, I believe a distinction must be drawn: whether, before the donation, he arranged that the revenues should be retained for a time for a just and necessary cause; or whether he made an agreement with the one who received the benefice, or with any mediator, that, once the office was granted, he himself would retain the revenues. The first alternative I believe to be licit; the second I say is not licit, lest we open a path to those who strive to cloak their wickedness.

\[*Ut nostrum*:\] “… As to the matter of impropriety \[*inhonestatem*\]: it was not licit, by any pact or agreement, under the prescribed form or tenor, to grant a bare office while retaining the revenues for oneself — since the Lateran Council prohibits anyone from presuming, when granting an ecclesiastical benefice, to retain a portion of its revenues for his own use; and elsewhere it is found to be required by canon that ecclesiastical benefices ought to be conferred without diminution. Hence we believe a distinction must be drawn: whether the one who gives the spiritual benefice was receiving its revenues before the donation; for if before the donation he was not receiving the revenues himself, but another was, and by the donation it came about that he himself received the revenues — there is no doubt that simoniacal corruption intervenes in this. But if he was receiving the revenues before the donation, we believe a distinction must be drawn: whether before the donation he arranged that the revenues should be retained for a time for a just and necessary cause; or whether he made an agreement with the one who received the benefice, or with any mediator, that once the office was granted, he himself would retain the revenues. The first alternative we believe to be licit; the second we say is not licit, lest we open a path to those who strive to cloak their wickedness.”

\[Lateran Council:\] “We furthermore prohibit the imposition of new taxes on churches by abbots, bishops, or other prelates, and the increase of old ones, and that they presume to appropriate a portion of revenues for their own use; but let them, with good will, preserve for those below them the freedom which their superiors wish to preserve for themselves. If anyone acts otherwise, let what he has done be held void.”

## §. XV.

Someone lacks a canonical title \[for ordination\] and wishes to be ordained; the bishop refuses to ordain him because he lacks a title. He asks a certain priest to present him under the title of his church; and when that priest refuses to do so, he promises him that, if the priest presents him in his church, he will never seek any portion \[of the benefice\]. And so, with that priest presenting him, he is ordained. The question is whether he has committed simony.

The answer is that unless dispensation has been mercifully granted to him, he ought neither to ascend to higher orders nor to minister in the order he has received, according to the strict rule of the canons. Extra, de simonia, c. *Per tuas*, §3.
