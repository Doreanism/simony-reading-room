WE DECREE that just as nothing is to be received for the dedication of churches and the conferral of holy orders, so also priests who are to receive chrism shall give nothing for balsam or for the purchase of lights. Let bishops therefore buy balsam from the revenues of the Church, and let each provide for the lights to be prepared in his own churches.

IT HAS COME TO OUR ATTENTION \[...\] we command your fraternity that you in no way exact money for the exercise of ecclesiastical ministry, for the assignment of vicars, or for chrism.

Regarding the gift *of the tongue*, a distinction is drawn by Huguccio and many others: either one is elected or ordained by his own petitions, or by the petitions of others. In the first case, however worthy he may be, it is simony. \[See\] Cause 8, q. 1, c. In Scripturis; Cause 1, q. 1, c. Ordinationes.

IN YOUR WRITINGS you narrate that you sought rest most earnestly, but by this you show that you have come suitably to pastoral solicitude: because just as the position of governance is to be denied to those who desire it, so it is to be offered to those who flee it, and as it is written: Let no one take honor to himself, but he who is called by God as was Aaron, etc.

ORDINATIONS which are made with the intervention of payment, or of petitions or service rendered to some person with that intent, or which are not made by the common consent of clergy and people according to canonical sanctions, and are not approved by those to whom consecration belongs — we judge to be invalid: for those who are ordained in such a manner do not enter through the door, that is, through Christ, but, as Truth itself testifies, *they are thieves and robbers.*

In the second case \[petitions by others\], either those petitions are carnal or spiritual. If carnal, and they are made on behalf of an unworthy person, it is simony, for the petitions then take the place of payment. \[See\] Cause 1, q. 1, c. De ordinationibus; and Cause 1, q. 1, c. Sunt nonnulli.

WE HAVE FORBIDDEN under strict prohibition the acceptance of anything for ordinations. It is therefore necessary that you neither consent to nor permit anyone to be brought to holy orders through a gift, through favor, or through the supplication of certain persons.

If however the petitions are carnal yet made on behalf of a worthy person, simony is not committed, provided the ordinator or elector has regard not to the petitions but to God and to the merits of the person. \[See\] Extra, *de aetate et qualitate et ordine praeficiendorum*, c. Tuam; Cause 1, q. 1, c. Latorem.

WE COMMEND your diligence in the Lord, which you are known to have applied to reforming the monastery of Saint Quiricus. We command that, if you cannot better provide that monastery with a suitable person, you install as abbot the monk whom you have commended in your letter for his integrity and industry, and through whom the state of that place is believed to be reformable — having absolutely no regard to the illicit petition which certain laymen were making, but acting solely from the duty of your own office of provision, etc.

THE BEARER OF THESE LETTERS we made an acolyte through the intercession of your holiness, and we have sent him to your service, so that if he shall have served more abundantly in winning souls, he may be able to advance further.

There are others who dissent on the first point, saying that one may lawfully petition on his own behalf, after the example of Peter saying to the Lord: *Behold, we have left all things and followed you; what therefore shall be ours?* I distinguish — subject to better judgment — that one cannot petition for himself a church, dignity, or other benefice having cure of souls attached to it, and in this I agree with Huguccio, through the laws cited above and Extra *de jure patronatus*, c. Per nostras.

YOU ASKED to be informed by our letters whether a cleric may present himself (if he is suitable) to a vacant church in which he holds the right of patronage. Since no one ought to thrust himself into the office of ecclesiastical prelacy, we answer that no one can present himself for the personatus of any church, however suitable he may be, and whatever studies and merits commend him.

A simple benefice, however, if one is in need and considers himself worthy, he may request without fear of sin or simony. \[See\] Extra *de rescriptis*, c. Ad aures; and *de praebendis*, c. Non liceat; and in this I agree with others.

IT HAS COME TO OUR EARS that deans, archdeacons, precentors, or others endowed with ecclesiastical dignities very often, when seeking letters concerning lesser benefices, suppress the name of their dignity and call themselves by a simple name, as though they held no personatus. \[And below:\] We therefore respond to your consultation as follows: since it is not our intention that persons abounding in many revenues should, by means of our letters, disturb poor clerics concerning lesser benefices, we do not wish letters in which the plaintiff suppresses the name of his dignity to have force.

LET NO BISHOP ordain clerics and provide them no means of support; rather let him choose one of two courses: either let him not make clerics, or if he does, let him give them that from which they may live.

If however one is not in need \[and petitions\], he sins, as is clear from the decree Ad aures just cited, and from Cause 1, q. 2, c. Clericos, and Cause 12, q. 1, c. Illi autem qui.

THOSE CLERICS ought to be sustained by the revenues of the Church who have no support from the goods of their parents and kinsmen. Those who can be maintained by the goods of their parents and their own resources, if they receive what belongs to the poor, assuredly commit sacrilege, and by the abuse of such things eat and drink judgment to themselves.

THOSE HOWEVER who are so weak that they cannot renounce their possessions — if they leave to the dispensator what they were about to receive, to be distributed to those who have nothing, they possess their own goods without sin: because they too in a certain sense leave their own things when, content with their own resources, they consider that nothing of those \[ecclesiastical goods\] is owed to their labor or to their order, and \[so\] receive \[nothing of those goods\].

## Whether Spiritual Things Can and Ought to Be Redeemed?
