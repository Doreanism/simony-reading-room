» It is manifest that simoniacs, as the foremost and chief heretics, are to be rejected by all the faithful; and if, after being admonished, they do not repent, they are to be suppressed by the secular powers as well. » All crimes, in comparison with the simoniacal heresy, are reckoned as virtually nothing.

It must be considered first: what simony is, whence it takes its name, by what means it is contracted, with what penalty a simoniac is punished, and what dispensation may be granted in his regard. Finally, we shall address certain doubtful cases.

## §. I.

## What Simony Is, and Whence It Takes Its Name.

1. Simony is a studied desire, or willful intent, to buy or sell something spiritual — nor is it necessary to add “or something annexed to the spiritual,” for what is annexed to the spiritual is itself spiritual. Causa prima, question third, chapter *Si quis objecerit*.

» IF anyone objects that it is not consecrations themselves that are being purchased, but the things that flow from consecration, he is shown to be utterly foolish; for since the Church, or a bishop, or an abbot, or any \[ecclesiastical office\] whatsoever profits nothing without material resources — just as the soul cannot live bodily without the body — whoever sells one of these two \[i.e., the spiritual office or its material endowment\] does not thereby leave the other unsold, since neither proceeds without the other. Therefore let no one purchase a prebend, or a church, or anything ecclesiastical; nor \[let there be\] any promise or agreement made before or after whereby such \[a purchase\] is to be discharged.

It appears, however, that \[the word “annexed”\] ought to be added: for the right of patronage is prohibited from being sold, because it is annexed to something spiritual. Extra, *De iure patronatus*, chapter *De iure*.

» REGARDING the right of patronage (and further on): We command that, if R. purchased that right — since it is unseemly for the right of patronage, which is annexed to something spiritual, to be sold — you declare that contract null and void.

But this is said for the reason that this spiritual right — namely, the right of patronage — falls upon the person of a layman.

This definition, or description, is proved by Causa prima, question first, chapter *Qui studet*.

» WHOEVER endeavors to purchase the gifts of God for a price, and \[to remain\] in holy orders, it is beyond doubt that he can in no way henceforth remain in or be restored to \[his office\]; for what else is it to acquire the gift of the Holy Spirit for a price than a capital crime and the simoniacal heresy?...

It appears, however, that mere intention or desire — even without being deliberate or manifested — makes \[a person\] a simoniac, just as it makes a usurer, as in Luke 6: “Lend, expecting nothing in return,” and Extra *de usuris*, chapter *Consuluit*.

» YOUR devotion consulted us whether a man ought to be judged in the court of souls as a usurer who, not intending to lend otherwise, lends money with the design that, even though no express agreement be made, he might perhaps receive back more.... In truth, since what is to be held in these cases is plainly known from the Gospel of Luke, where it is said: “Lend, expecting nothing in return,” such men, on account of the intent of gain which they harbor (since all usury and surplus is prohibited by the law\*), must be judged to act wickedly, and are to be efficaciously moved in the court of souls to make restitution of what has been accepted in this manner.

To this point, distinguish with Vincentius and Ambrosius: Some things are prohibited because they are simoniacal — such as selling or buying the sacraments — and briefly, those things which were simoniacal in the Old and New Testaments: in these, mere intention suffices. Other things are simoniacal because they are prohibited, namely those that are simoniacal only by constitution of the Church — as when someone resigns a church on the condition, or by agreement, that it be given to his nephew or to another: in such cases, mere intention does not suffice unless it proceeds to an act.

2. Likewise, by the terms “buying” and “selling” understand every non-gratuitous contract, except the contract of exchange — concerning which hold as follows: that an exchange of dignities, prebends, or other ecclesiastical benefices cannot be effected by the authority of the parties themselves without the danger of simony, any more than can a translation. Extra *de transactionibus*, chapter *Super eo*, and *de rerum permutatione*, chapter *Cum olim*.

» CONCERNING the question you raised, whether a transaction could be made regarding an ecclesiastical benefice that had been brought to litigation: we give this reply, that a transaction cannot be made over a sacred and litigious matter. For we believe that sacred things, when they are to be possessed in exchange for something given, retained, or promised, bear the character of simony. But if the litigants settle the matter freely and amicably among themselves, we by no means say this is contrary to the sacred canons.

» WHEN formerly master S. and B. had exchanged an archdeaconry and a prepositura with one another (and further on): Although the said master had from the outset freely confessed to the exchange, the said B. denied it altogether, claiming that he had resigned the prepositura without any condition whatsoever and had afterward obtained the archdeaconry following the resignation of the said master (and further on): Since it was established to us that the said B. had given his consent to the exchange, and the said master publicly admitted his fault, we took care to deprive the one of the prepositura and the other of the archdeaconry, pronouncing a definitive sentence against them in this matter.

A bishop, however, if he has examined the case and found it necessary, may by his office transfer persons and effect an exchange. Extra *de rerum permutatione*, chapter *Quaesitum*, and chapter *Cum universorum*.

» IT was asked on your behalf whether exchanges of prebends may be made, since the exchange of dignities had been prohibited by the Council of Tours... (and further on): Hold therefore as a general rule that exchanges of prebends cannot be made as a matter of right, especially when a prior agreement is involved, which in matters spiritual or connected to things spiritual always carries the taint of simony. But if a bishop has examined the case and found it necessary, he may lawfully transfer persons from one place to another, so that those who are less useful in one place may be able to employ themselves more usefully elsewhere.

» *CUM UNIVERSORUM* (and further on): We were informed by the report of canon G. that, after he and clerk L. had begun to treat between themselves of an exchange of their prebends, which they believed to be of mutual benefit, eventually L., having assigned the prebend of G. himself to a certain kinsman of his, refused to resign his own prebend to G. as he had promised (and further on): Since the laws come to the aid of those who have been deceived, and not of those who do the deceiving — even though they could not on their own authority have exchanged ecclesiastical benefices — yet in order that pardon may be granted for their simplicity, we command that, if it is established that the said G. was deceived in this manner, \[you\] remove from his prebend the kinsman of the said L., or any other person holding it unlawfully, and cause it to be restored to G. himself.
