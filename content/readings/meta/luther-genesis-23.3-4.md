Luther's commentary on Abraham's words to the sons of Heth—"I am a stranger and a sojourner among you"—uses the patriarch's humble request for burial rights as the occasion for an extended treatment of simony. Luther draws on the canonists' definition of simony and the proper use of ecclesiastical revenues, contrasting Abraham's approach with the corruption of Church officials who improperly acquire benefices, burial privileges, and property through simoniacal means. The passage engages extensively with the canonical tradition, Gratian, and the abuses of the papal church in the administration of sacramental and temporal goods.
