The opening chapter of Augustine's *Sermo CXVII*, a sermon on John 1:1–5 ("In principio erat Verbum, et Verbum erat apud Deum, et Deus erat Verbum") preached against the Arians. §1 ("Verbum Dei ut intelligatur, quo pretio comparandum") opens with an argument about the incalculable worth of the divine Word: every saleable thing is either equal to, subject to, or exceeds its price, but to the Word of God nothing can be made equal, nothing can be subjected to it, and nothing can be set above it — an argument of immediate relevance to the theology of simony, which treats spiritual goods as though they could be priced. §2 ("Pretium Verbi ipse homo") turns this into a soteriology: the price the Word demands is the self, offered not as a loss but as a gain, since Christ's humanity was made through the Word and is restored to it. §3 ("Verbum Dei forma quædam non formata") concludes with a meditation on the Word as the uncreated form of all forms — immutable, without time or place, the ground and summit of all created things.
