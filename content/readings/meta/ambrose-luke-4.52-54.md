Three sections from Book IV of Ambrose's *Expositio Evangelii secundum Lucam*, drawing the lessons of Naaman's healing (2 Kings 5) into a teaching against simony. Commenting on Christ's command in Luke 4 — "Gratis accepistis, gratis date" — Ambrose insists that no price may be exacted in the dispensation of the sacraments and that the grace of healing is no man's to sell. The reading culminates with a denunciation of Gehazi, whose hereditary leprosy (4 Kings 5:27) Ambrose reads as the figure of all who, like Simon Magus, would profit from sacred ministry: such men are made foreign to the Kingdom of God, "neque ebriosus, neque idolis serviens regnum Dei possidebit" (1 Cor. 6:10).
