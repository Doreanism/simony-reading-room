Gabriel Biel's treatment of the intention required for valid baptism, presenting the latter conclusions of the sole question under Distinction V. Drawing on Alexander of Hales, Thomas Aquinas, Richard of Middleton, and Scotus, Biel argues (Conclusio III) that the minister's intention must extend not only to the baptismal act as such but to its effect and terminus; (Conclusio IV) that specific actual intention is not required—it suffices to intend what the Church intends in general, so that infidels and heretics may validly baptize provided they intend to do what Christians do; (Conclusio V) that virtual intention (neither merely habitual nor fully actual) is what is strictly required; and (Conclusio VI) that when multiple ministers jointly baptize a single person, each must complete the entire form—including ablution and words with intention—for the sacrament to be valid, as illustrated by the hypothetical of a mute and a one-handed man.
